Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

at the inauguration of the Basilan Circumferential Road

[Declared in Brgy. Tumahubong, Sumisip, Basilan, on March 21, 2016]

We are here today to witness another testament to our commitment: That no Filipino will be left behind on the Straight Path. Our aspiration: Whether Moro, Lumad, or Christian, to keep pace with the progress we are enjoying. Today, we are inaugurating the Basilan Circumferential Road, which has long been a dream of many of our countrymen, and is finally being utilized.

This road construction began in the year 2000, but was delayed for a long time due to the unrest in your province and other factors. It was allegedly deliberately hindered by rebel groups. Because, aside from making it more difficult to evade the law, their influence would weaken as our Bosses feel the true care of the State.

Previously, traveling on your circumferential road was difficult because of the bumpy sections or parts that weren't even roads at all. Because of the long travel time, even passenger vehicles reportedly only made one trip per day. During the rainy season, the slippery and muddy roads were even more dangerous, and some parts were impassable. In addition, there were also criminals carrying out theft, ambushes, and even carjacking. Consequently, the flow of your commerce and services was disrupted. Your poor condition just got worse.

I was a little surprised earlier. In our travels across the entire Philippines, this is the first time I've heard of us using chains on our roads in the Philippines. We last saw those chains in America. Snow chains. Here, I guess they're mud chains. [Laughter] It's really unusual.

On the Straight Path, we ended the suffering caused by what you were used to. Led by our uniformed personnel, we strengthened security in your communities. Likewise, we allocated P1.83 billion for the concreting of more than 51 kilometers of road and the construction of four bridges. With the help of the project, the former 3 hours and 45-minute journey on the circumferential road now takes only two hours or less. Our public transport vehicles also travel here more frequently. With faster and safer travel, our farmers can choose markets to sell their crops, depending on where they can maximize their profits. The same goes for businessmen whose products now reach remote barangays.

We thank those behind this project. First, to the DPWH, led by Secretary Babes Singson. [Applause] Secretary Babes has truly cemented in the minds of Filipinos the new image of the DPWH: To implement projects that immediately bring benefits to our Bosses. We also salute our soldiers and police who not only ensured security while the project was being completed but also lent their strength and arms in repairing the road. We thank Gov. Mujiv Hataman of ARMM for his good governance, identifying and addressing the needs not only of Basilan but also of ARMM. [Applause] Under his excellent leadership, the economy and investment in your region continue to grow. Of course, to our Bosses from Basilan, thank you very much for your cooperation with the Straight Path, including Gov. Jum Akbar and Cong. Jim Hataman. [Applause]

From the very beginning, our success today is just a part of our extensive strategy to uplift your province. Aside from this circumferential road, the construction of Transcentral Road Phase 1 is ongoing, and Phase 2 is also up for bidding. Perhaps you are more familiar with the transcentral road than I am, but we have a circumferential road that traverses the entire province, what about the center, might that be left behind? That’s why there’s a transcentral road. We are concreting the road from the city of Lamitan to the city of Isabela in Phase 1, which will continue to the municipalities of Maluso and Sumisip. Once completed, the travel time from Sumisip to Isabela City will be reduced by one hour, and the travel time from Maluso to Lamitan City will also be reduced by 45 minutes. Aside from the roads mentioned, we have also completed almost 20 kilometers of road that is part of the Al Barka-Tipo-Tipo Road. [Applause]

It is clear: With good governance, we have been given the ability to improve our service to our countrymen. To be honest, from 2005 to 2010, our predecessor allocated only P1.85 billion for the construction of roads, bridges, flood control projects, and other infrastructure here in Basilan. From 2011 to 2016, we increased this—in the DPWH alone—to P14.85 billion. This is only for Basilan. [Applause] Throughout the country, we have built and repaired 18,547 kilometers of national roads including 107,579 linear meters of bridges. You can see: With the maintenance of peace and order, your government can focus not only on one sector, but also on our other needs.

Let me go back to our circumferential road. I think, our opponents will say that the approaches—there are bridges, the approaches are on either side. There are still four that we need to pave. They will say, “Is this complete or is it still lacking? There are still parts that are not paved.” Let us just remind them of what the engineers taught us: The approach is heavy-duty. On the road, the durability can be like this, but the approach is different when going up and down from the bridge. Now, what is called needs to “settle,” it needs to be “compacted.” Included in the project, once the time for compaction is finished, we will also pave it. And that piece of approach, which is less than a kilometer in total, if we had completed the 131 kilometers of the whole thing, that less than a kilometer approach would be very easy, even if only Babes and I were to do the approach. [Applause] But the cement mixer, is Mujiv. [Laughter]

Here in Basilan, we are also strengthening agriculture and our countrymen who depend on it. That land that was once uncultivable and unplantable due to unrest, is now a livelihood source for many Basilan residents. Along with this, we allocated P274.61 million for the construction of farm-to-market roads and irrigation systems. For the fisheries industry, we allocated P67.7 million for the construction of municipal fish ports and community fish landing centers. The report to us: in Sumisip, Lamitan, Maluso, Akbar, Hadji Muhammad Ajul, Lantawan, Tabuan Lasa, Ungkaya Pukan, and Tipo-Tipo.

To ensure that the progress of our industries is not intermittent, we have the Sitio Electrification Program. Through this program, more than 97 percent of the sitios in Basilan now have electricity. The remaining five sitios without electricity, according to the Department of Energy and the National Electrification Administration, will be energized before the end of March. [Applause] Likewise, all identified sitios without electricity in the country in 2011, which reached 32,441; will also have electricity before the end of March—and there are only ten days left in March—or the two Secretaries will resign. But with their competence, they might finish it before the end of March.

Let me be clear: During our term, we will not bequeath guns and violence to the next generation of our Moro brothers and sisters. It is a great pity that we are pushing for a just and equitable law for lasting peace in Mindanao. It went through a long process and other procedures before reaching Congress for deliberation and passage. You are all witnesses, but the enactment of our BBL seemed to be stalled only because of two senators. One of them is running for office now. Judge him for yourselves. [Applause] The committee report was released a long time ago, and even in the last days of the session, the line-by-line interpellation by one senator who is not running for office now was not finished. Judge for yourselves: Do they want to pass or block the law based on their actions?

We already have a draft of the proposed law; the identified law will simply be refiled with the next administration. They will not be starting from scratch; they have already come a long way. If this is focused on and personal interests are not allowed to prevail, I do not see any reason why the next administration cannot immediately enact it into law. My appeal: Let us continue to pursue the path towards lasting peace. Let us not lose interest and determination to pass it for the benefit of the majority.

It is clear that on the Straight Path, what we deliver is: peace, aid, and skills towards a better future. We are not content to simply say, “We did something, at least.” For us, continuous effort is needed and we must be able to say, “We maximized the opportunity for every Filipino.” Through the Payapa at Masaganang Pamayanan (PAMANA) Program, we focused on communities that have long been trapped in conflict, and now we are opening up opportunities for them to progress. With its help, we funded various livelihood projects in Basilan, built trading centers, and constructed barangay health stations.

Here in Basilan, I was again surprised to learn how many beneficiaries there were of the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program in June 2010. Like in Cavite and Batangas, those benefiting from Pantawid Pamilya here in Basilan during that time was zero. There were no 4Ps beneficiaries in 2010 when we first took office. Under Daang Matuwid (Straight Path), we raised that from zero because we now have 31,449 beneficiary households in Basilan. [Applause]

You know, throughout the country, less than 800,000 households benefited from Pantawid Pamilya. This year, we will be able to close the target of 4.6 million beneficiary households. [Applause] The report we received shows that the initial results of this program are very good: 7.7 million Filipinos have crossed the so-called “poverty line” with the help of the program.

All of these achievements, we were able to accomplish because of our contributions and cooperation. We were also able to do all of this without adding to the burden of our Bosses; except for the Sin Tax, which helps improve our health services.

The question is: Is there anyone with sound mind who would say, “Let’s not continue the Daang Matuwid?” On May 9th, we will once again face a crossroads, where we will choose the next leaders of the country. For me, if the question is who has the ability, experience, and conscience, and will undoubtedly continue our achievements: That is the Matuwid (Righteous) Team of Mar Roxas and Leni Robredo. [Applause] They are the untainted team, the ones who will continue our progress; enforce the law and promote the welfare of the nation before self.

My Bosses, there are only 101 days left in our term, and I can say that in every moment, from the beginning until now, I have strived to be true to all of you. We have risen from a long period of falling. Now that we can run and accelerate, it is only right that we continue to move in the same direction. Let us continue, expand, and defend the Straight Path towards a peaceful and prosperous future.

Let me, in closing, tell you two things. Earlier, on the road we passed, someone made a poster and it said, “Sir, we still need more livelihood projects here.” Understood. As we were arriving here, I saw how many islands and beaches you have. White sand, the water is beautiful. And in tourism, we have peace here, let's invest in tourism. Let's not forget: every foreign tourist we can invite here in the Philippines is equivalent to one job. Those who join the tourism industry don't need to be highly educated. It's like, they can quickly have a livelihood.

Look at Tawi-tawi: In 2008, their tourist arrivals exceeded 200 people for the whole year. In 2014, it was 16,540. And of course, to reach Tawi-tawi, you have to pass through Basilan, pass through Sulu, you know that when Basilan and Sulu are in the news, something usually happens, right? Before reaching those tourism sites, there are exciting moments here first. But imagine if all these places were peaceful. Imagine, everything that the Almighty God has given us is there and can be enjoyed by everyone.

There's more: Last Saturday, when I went out, I went to a restaurant. The waiter told me, “I hope you get an extension, even just for three years.” I looked at him and said, “Brad, you know, we reached a point in our history back then, I think you weren't even born yet, where there was a person who finished two terms and then asked for more.” How much did he ask for? He should have finished in 1973, [but] he finished in 1986. He was heading towards an eight-year term—it was in our Constitution back then, two four-year terms—there was an extension of 13 years. And we can't say that those 13 years were a voluntary step down.

My fellow citizens, I am truly happy to be here. Look at the air here. In Luzon, it's almost like Tagaytay again, a bit cool. [Applause] Of course, now our minds will be troubled again, “Where will we go on our honeymoon when the time comes?” [Laughter] If God will still grant me the chance.

But this opening of the road, truthfully, is just the beginning. But to start that, it took 16 years. We did it in six years, to finish it. And not just for you in Basilan. The long-considered idea is now being realized.

The question is: When you are in Basilan, you have no right as a Filipino; when you are in Apayao, your votes are few, sorry about that. That shouldn't be the case. We insisted on basing things on what needs to be addressed, not on the number of votes you will bring [to] whoever the candidate is, but rather, what are the needs of our fellow Filipinos? [Applause]

There are only 101 days left. I thank you; you helped me in 2010, and you are helping me while I am in office so that we can achieve many things. But I will just repeat to you: This is just the beginning; it is in your hands. Is what we did in these six years correct, which is why we called the next election a referendum? Or will we listen to all those who say they will further improve the situation, especially those who are claiming ownership of 4Ps now.

I was just wondering the other day. I said, “Please look at everything they’re saying about the 4Ps.” They showed me, just a sample, from 2013 to 2016, you’ll be surprised by the number of critics, those who wanted it stopped, those who called for an investigation, and all sorts of things they said. I assume you all have internet access here, even if only occasionally. Just look at what they’re saying, or else you might say I’m the one attacking. That’s not true. I’ll let them speak for themselves to discredit themselves. Because someone said, “That 4Ps program needs to be expanded. Let’s make it 5Ps.” [Laughter] “And while we expand it,” he said, “I’ll lower your taxes, while we increase the benefits.” There are many housewives here, I presume. If your husband said, “I’ll reduce your budget, but we’ll eat more every day.” How many housewives here would agree? How many housewives here would say “That can be done”? Let’s just leave it at that; it’s good that we have media, we even have new media—social media. Let’s let all those who are competing to show their platforms speak. And let’s let them slip up and reveal the truth.

But you will shape the future. We are called the “Father of the Nation.” I hope I can look back on this and say that this was where it all began, and that progress and the improvement of our society, and responding to the needs of every Filipino, will continue. I hope we don’t make a mistake in the coming election. We might suddenly say, “Those six years of the Straight Path were just a vacation. Let’s go back to real life.” The Filipino people don’t need to return to a sad fate. The Almighty God has shown us so much that He truly loves us. As they say, “God provides mercy, man provides the work.”

On May 9th, let us not forget: Our vote is our present life, but more importantly, it’s the legacy we leave to the next generation. You are my Boss from the beginning. You are my Boss until now. You will tell us where we are going.

Good day. Thank you all very much.
