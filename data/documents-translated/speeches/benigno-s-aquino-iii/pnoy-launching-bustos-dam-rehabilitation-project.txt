Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

Upon the launch of the Angat Afterbay Regulator Dam (Bustos Dam) Rehabilitation Project

[Declared in Brgy. Tibagan, Bustos, Bulacan, on April 15, 2016]

Today is a new testament to our vow: To bequeath a Philippines that is far better than the one we inherited. We are pushing the limits of every program and project to deliver genuine service to our Bosses.

Before we proceeded here to Bustos, we led the groundbreaking of the Bulacan Bulk Water Supply Project in San Jose del Monte. This is the first awarded Public-Private Partnership project of our administration for the water sector. I was told that it was promised to you in 1992, 24 years ago, or almost a generation that you waited before it was realized. And of course, one generation made the promise, and a different generation is implementing it. Let me repeat: The promise during that time was that you would benefit from Angat.

Let me just explain how complicated your situation was before we took office: The Angat Dam, of course, is in Angat; Angat, of course, is in Bulacan. But the water from it isn't being utilized by Bulacan. Before, because you didn't have access to Angat, you were forced to get water—or up to the present, you are getting water from deep wells—which could result in what is called “land subsidence” or the sinking of the land. And of course, saltwater is also seeping in.

In summary: When things are good, those who promised are there for you, like the ones who made the promises are with you. In times of hardship, Bulacan is left on its own. Under the Straight and Narrow Path, we are correcting the situation.

This January, we signed the contract for the Bulacan Bulk Water Supply Project, and preconstruction activities have already begun. It was supposed to start in July, but it is currently underway. The aim of these projects is, first of all, to replace the water source that is no longer deep wells. We are now moving towards what is called “surface water.” The result of this: more assured water supply, stronger pressure, and lower water costs. Currently, the average paid by your OSEC to the water districts is around P20. The winning bidder will charge P850 per cubic meter. That's our first benefit: the price will go down and the supply will increase. It will also provide an alternative water source for residents who previously relied solely on deep wells. Once this is completed, the number of beneficiaries will be: 3 million Bulakeños—and this will be the template. Throughout the Philippines, it's the same: Small water districts need to rely solely on deep wells. Deep wells deplete our so-called “natural aquifer,” saltwater enters, the land can sink—it's really not sustainable. We need to switch to surface water. The problem is, water districts can't fund the reservoirs they need. So, this Bulacan Bulk Water will be the template for the entire country.

Aside from this project, we are also launching the Angat Afterbay Regulator Dam (Bustos Dam—it's behind us) Rehabilitation Project, which will improve the irrigation system here in Bulacan.

As they say, this Bustos Dam was built in 1926. When it was built, my parents weren't even born yet. The dam was fitted with a steel gauge in 1967 to increase its water storage capacity. But because it was made of steel, it rusted, deteriorated, and also because its service area expanded, even reaching Pampanga.

Due to the damage caused by typhoons, the gates rusted, so the steel gates were replaced with rubber ones in 1997. It wasn't long before these rubber gates also developed holes. You'll see the rubber gates later, and you'll notice they look similar to the concrete they're resting on. For a long time, only repair works and vulcanizing were done to cover the holes because there were allegedly no funds to replace the gates themselves. Perhaps the tire vulcanizing shops benefited from this. But according to the assessment in 2011 and 2012, they need to be completely replaced.

We studied what needs to be done for its rehabilitation and improvement: We will replace the six rubber gates and install sprinklers for the rubber gates to extend their lifespan. We will start the rehabilitation this May, and we aim to complete [it] by August 2017. Once completed, it is expected to irrigate the entire 26,791 hectares of the current service area of the Angat-Maasim River Irrigation System, from only 23,068 hectares irrigated during the last cropping season. An estimated 23,700 farmers from Bulacan and Pampanga will benefit from this.

We are also aware that during the rainy season, Bulacan is one of the most affected by flooding. We addressed this so that your livelihoods would not be affected: We built infrastructure and projects to alleviate the hardship it causes.

The amount we allocated for flood control projects here in Bulacan from 2011 to 2016 totaled P3.41 billion. This includes the ongoing Valenzuela-Obando-Meycauayan (VOM) flood control project. The story from Manong Willie is that, in the past, even just high tide or a little rain would immediately flood the barangays. Under your flood control project, we are now constructing a flood control wall to avoid inconvenience. And the report from Manong Willie is that currently, this flood wall is already being utilized.

As part of our goal to keep you safe from the danger of flooding, we are also implementing Project NOAH. Here, we have installed two Automated Rain Gauges and ten Water Level Monitoring Stations. Malolos, Meycauayan, and San Jose del Monte are also covered by FloodNET, which provides rainfall forecasts and warnings so that our citizens can evacuate immediately in times of danger.

These programs are just some of what we are doing in Bulacan. In the past almost six years of traversing the Straight and Narrow Path, your province has truly come a long way.

Of course, none of this would be possible without the cooperation of our agencies. Special mention to the diligent and reliable Secretary Babes Singson of the DPWH, [applause] MWSS Administrator Gerry Esquivel, [applause] NIA Administrator Florencio Padernal, [applause], as well as the contribution of the private and public sectors, and also your support and trust, my Bosses. So to all of you, thank you very much. [Applause]

Perhaps some are asking: How did we achieve these accomplishments we mentioned? It's simple: What we did was restore a government that truly cares for and nurtures its people. We didn't waste any opportunity, we focused on your needs, and we addressed them, not tomorrow, but immediately, now. The good thing is: We are able to deliver benefits to our Bosses without adding to their burdens, except for the Sin Tax.

However, all our hard work, as you know, could simply vanish, especially if the next leaders of the country mismanage things. In the upcoming elections, it is in your hands whether what we have started will continue and expand.

As Father of the Nation, it is my obligation to guide you on the right path. My only request is: Let us carefully examine the candidates for the country. In my opinion, looking them in the eye, I can tell you who has integrity, ability, and truly prioritizes the nation before self. It is no other than the righteous tandem: Mar Roxas and Leni Robredo. [Applause]

You are all witnesses, all our projects are interconnected and focused on a single goal: to further improve the standard of living of our Bosses. Now, we are at a higher starting point. We can dream again, and we have a greater capacity to make it a reality. With our continued contribution and care, I am confident that we can also achieve our lofty aspirations.

Later, as you exit, MIA will have displays. Please look at the before and after. The irrigation canal in the past, they used a backhoe, dug it, and then left it to chance. It was as if we turned the irrigation canal into a planting area. Now, it is being properly aligned, well-maintained, and we are focusing the water where it is truly needed.

Here, with our new administrator—you know, I’ll admit—one of those we appointed before him, I wasn’t really happy with. Let me give you an example of why we weren’t happy: During their anniversary one year, they showed me that for rehabilitation [and] maintenance, their goal was this, and they achieved double the goal. Shouldn’t that be praised? The problem is, the new one, was lucky to reach 60 percent. I said, “Why are you so good at rehabilitation, but so weak at new projects?” Of course, the “new” involves building a canal; whether there’s a canal or not, it’s hard to hide. Rehabilitation involves dredging—I don’t need to explain it to you, Manong Pedro knows all about that. He taught us the tricks there.

This, let’s say, just one day, during their anniversary, I asked, “Why is it like this?” The next day, I received an answer. And the answer to me was very simple—why were they 40 percent behind? Because, they said, 40 percent of their projects were for Mindanao. And then Typhoon Pablo arrived, so everything was delayed. I said, “Is that so?” So I had Atty. Delantar, my assistant, check it. “Please check it. I know Pablo arrived in December.” It was true. Pablo arrived in the second week of December, so the 40 percent they were supposed to do was in the last two weeks of December. That’s where Christmas is, that’s where New Year’s is, that’s where they would do the 40 percent. He was even doing that in the Aguinaldo Room in Malacañang, trying to deceive me. So I said that afternoon, “He needs to be gone this afternoon.”

Let’s just look at the difference. Our problem in the Philippines, it’s very simple in the field of water. During the rainy season, twenty typhoons a year visit us. If we can collect that water, we can use it in the dry season, and we’ll be fine. The situation you mentioned earlier, Manong Willie, in Bulacan, during the rainy season it rushes, during the dry season, there’s none.

In Tarlac, I think, it's clearer. Go to Tarlac City, during the rainy season, it really rushes, destroying the dikes; during the dry season, you wouldn't think it's a river because it has no water, not even a drop. If we manage it, we can store it during the rainy season, distribute it during the dry season, then it would really be a big help to our Irrigators Associations. It will really improve their harvest, and that's really what we're discussing.

Now, I can't tell you at the start that I'm perfect, that we got right what we should have gotten right. Now, I have more confidence that we are doing what needs to be done. But that's what I keep repeating, we went through a system like that. We might revisit it if we make a mistake, then we'll cry even more. Now, the situation is different. It is in our hands to maintain this change to make it permanent.

Good afternoon. Thank you very much.
