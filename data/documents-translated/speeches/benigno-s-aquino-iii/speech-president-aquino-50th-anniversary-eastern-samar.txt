Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

On the 50th anniversary of the founding of Eastern Samar

[Declared at the Municipal Gymnasium, Arteche, Eastern Samar on October 30, 2015]

To my beloved Samarnons, thank you very much for continuing to walk the Straight Path. You are my strength. You are my Boss. You are still the ones making the change. [1] [Applause] Maybe when I return here, we will make that a song. [Laughter]

This morning, we gather to celebrate the 50th Founding Anniversary of Eastern Samar. This day is truly historic, not only today, but also your province itself. In the year 1521, on the island of Homonhon, the conqueror Ferdinand Magellan, or the one who attempted to conquer, Ferdinand Magellan, first set foot on Philippine soil. According to the account of Magellan’s companion in the voyage, Antonio Pigafetta, when he sighted the island of Homonhon, he said, “We found two fountains of very clear water; we called it the ‘Waters of Good Signs.’” He said this was because your province is a source of the beauty of marine resources and natural resources. And it is true: Until now, despite the trials faced, as we collectively traverse the Straight Path, we really see so many “good signs” here in Eastern Samar, and throughout Samar Island.

Just a while ago, we had an aerial inspection of some parts of the Samar Road Rehabilitation Project we are building, which we are targeting to complete by May 2016. The allocated fund for this is: P8.165 billion. It aims to expand and develop the 222.23 kilometer Wright-Taft-Borongan-Guiuan Road. This also includes the repair of 39 bridges and the construction of 22 new bridges, along with associated slope protection, drainage structures, and installation of road safety measures. You know, when we were reviewing the data, 222 kilometers—I just remembered, from Manila, from the Rizal Monument, to Baguio, it’s 250 kilometers. So that 222 additional kilometers is almost equal to when we built a road that long. Your officials are really diligent, especially Ben Evardone, and now even more so with Mel Sarmiento. [Applause] Of course, our provincial governors are also not lacking, so when you hear them, it’s like we’re listening to a chorus. When we are united, things get done faster.

That part of the road from Wright to Taft will be concreted in Samar and Eastern Samar. This will facilitate the travel of products and services between the two provinces. The road from Taft to Guiuan, which is currently the only road leading to your 13 coastal communities, will surely improve your local commerce and tourism. Likewise, travel between Tacloban and Borongan will be sped up from four hours to three hours.

We are also inspecting the Junction Taft-Oras-San Policarpo-Arteche-Lapinig Road, which has been allocated more than P756 million. According to the DPWH, 95 percent of the road concreting is complete and we are targeting to finish it by December. By June of next year, the asphalting of this road will be completed, and it will be fully usable.

Let's look at the data for the entire Samar Island. In terms of infrastructure programs, the total funds allocated by the previous administration to your island from 2006 to 2010 was P10.1 billion. Under us, in the Straight Path, from 2011 to the present, this has almost doubled to P19.2 billion. Of course, the 2016 budget is with Congress, and with the diligence of your representatives, I believe we will really surpass double what the previous administration did.

Bosses, as we connect our rural and urban areas, we not only boost local commerce and the economy—we also speed up the delivery of benefits to our Bosses, such as social services, and also, aid when we face disasters. This only proves that in pursuing the Straight Path, no one will be left behind.

Regarding health: Just a while ago, led by our Department of Health, we had a ceremonial turnover of several sets of health apparatus for the provinces of Samar Island. Included in this set are the blood pressure apparatus, glucometer sets, and weighing scales that you saw. Earlier, you saw me test the weighing scale because its brand is ‘Detecto.’ I remember, that’s a popular brand. We don’t purchase brands we’ve “never heard of” but at prices of a “very heard” brand. Now, to be sure, the weight of my right-hand man, Jun Delantar—Atty. Delantar who is missing—seems like he’s continuing his diet, but the scale seems correct because I weighed him last week in Casiguran, his t-shirt is now loose, unlike when we were in Casiguran. Let’s continue working hard to achieve the goal of Universal Health Care.

We also know that: Coconut farming is the main industry in your province, but the income here is not maximized. The problem, it seems, is that there are few processing plants for making products from coconuts, and this is apparently limited to coconut oil, or copra. I think you know, the coconut, even the husk that comes from the coco coir—there’s a factory here now for coco coir—there’s coco sugar. There are many different products.

Now, led by Dr. Justino Arboleda of Bicol State University, we funded a project to address the question “How can we expand income from coconut farming?” What’s good about this: After their proposal, once this factory is built, after three years of implementation, they say we can recover the government’s investment. If we prove this proposed project, it will be truly pleasing to our coconut farmers. The fund itself will generate more and more funds to be replicated and given to our farmers to help them maximize their income. I ask for your pardon for the unexpected delay. Dr. Arboleda thought that when this project was being built, it was already funded by DAP; and because programs under DAP were stopped, he also thought of stopping the implementation of the project. But when there was clarity, rest assured that we will expedite it so that as soon as possible, we can deliver benefits to our farmers. Let me repeat: The project, a new factory, will process all products from coconuts, not just oil, or aside from copra or oil, this factory will process them. And what’s good, if operated properly, the capital will be recovered in three years; that capital can be transferred to the next association of coconut farmers, more and more of these factories will be built.

Clearly: These projects and delivery of services that we are undertaking are not just to say that we have accomplished something. They were carefully studied to address the needs of our communities. Our main objective in the Straight Path: to empower and give our Bosses the opportunity to progress on the path to advancement.

Indeed, the people of Samar Island are very lucky to have public servants like DILG Secretary Mel Sarmiento, Congressman Ben Evardone, Guiuan Mayor Sheen Gonzales, and many others. Sec. Mel, as you know, has experience and success in Congress. Moreover, I admire his acceptance and facing the challenge of becoming our new head in the DILG, even if his term will only be brief. Think about it, I believe, Kuya Mel was sure to be re-elected, but he traded that certainty, and after serving in the DILG for a few months, he will be left out. Because Mel doesn't just think about himself, but about what can help the country. That is the kind of leadership we have in our party that sacrifices because others or one's fellowmen are more important than oneself. [Applause] We saw, just after Typhoon Lando, that he truly demonstrated strength and readiness in facing our country's challenges.

Congressman Ben Evardone, too, is truly tested in the field of public service. Despite the criticisms made, especially from us, he does not waver, but instead stands even taller to be a voice of reason and truth. Think about it, there was someone in Germany, in the past, named Goebbels. He was Hitler's propaganda minister. It was said that he started the belief that if you repeat a lie, it sounds like the truth. Now, if we allow only lies to be heard, someone might believe them. Ben ensures that what comes out is balanced so that the people can really see what or where the truth lies. For that, thank you very much, Ben. [Applause] Those who make a living out of criticism and lying, when someone opposes them, will turn their attention to the one who opposes. My point here is, we are lucky again in Samar; there are people who are truly willing to stand up for the welfare of all.

Mayor Sheen Gonzales of Guiuan, I remember, during the time when Typhoon Yolanda struck, my first acquaintance with him, he faced me with a bandaged hand because he really had an injury while helping his constituents evacuate from the storm surge. But he did not give in to the challenge, but instead led the evacuation of his constituents. Instead of complaining and just talking about the shortcomings, he chose to move his constituents away from the danger of the storm surge. And that's not all, he is very easy to work with. Because of his close coordination with the national government, we were able to quickly rebuild Guiuan.

Of course, to all our Bosses from Samar, who are truly models of resilience, and demonstrate the value of contribution and cooperation with each other, with the community, and with the government. Thank you very much to all of you. [Applause]

You can see it: We are not being lazy, just waiting for fruits to fall from the tree; instead, we are all working together to reap the rewards for everyone, especially those who need it most. To the youth who are given the opportunity to study and graduate, led by DepEd, CHED, and TESDA; to the ordinary Filipinos whose livelihoods are improving because of the roads we are fixing; to the families who are now seeing hope to rise from poverty and progress because of the benefits of Pantawid Pamilya; to every Boss whose doors of opportunity have been opened to improve themselves, their fellowmen, their family, or the country, and who are maximizing these opportunities—you made all of this happen; you are still making change happen.

Now, the question is: Where do we want to take our country next year? Will we continue to walk the Straight Path, honorable and progressive? Or will we deviate and return to a path that is dirty, complicated, and self-serving? Let's also add this: Will we steer the nation towards the path of “maybe”—maybe straight, maybe knowledgeable, maybe we’ll get something out of it?

Bosses, in the coming year, the fate of our country is once again in the hands of every Filipino. You yourselves are experiencing the positive changes that we started in the past five years. You will also ensure that all of this continues. The direction we are taking in the coming years is clear: Advance, defend, and accelerate the Straight Path.

I have already practiced a little here, but before I get into that, I just want to emphasize: Your road, which was mentioned earlier as having been started during my mother's time, these circumferential roads were opened, and it was said earlier that they seemed to have been neglected. We have now completed, or are close to completing, that circumferential road and major infrastructure. Of course, we had helpers and partners in this, such as the Millennium Challenge Corporation [applause], and from what I hear, their smooth operation pleased them, so we may receive new aid from the Millennium Challenge Corporation. [Applause] Now, if that doesn't go to Samar, it might go to another place. You might almost be finished with it.

Now, I also want to react to something: You know, my political opponents call me insensitive. They say I have no heart. Let me ask you: What is more important? When there is a disaster, do I run to the area, embrace people, and take tearful photos? Or do I arrive in the area, remain strong so that our countrymen who have been hit can lean on me? [Applause] And upon arrival, not to say, “That’s your problem.” But to say, “Here is a solution to the problems you are facing.” Who do you think cares more in that way?

Brothers and sisters, truly, during our tour earlier, you had so many coconuts. You also had many other trees. Many projects have been done here, such as the National Greening Program and the agriculture programs. I just noticed that the coconut trees are almost touching each other, and every place where coconuts can be planted has been planted. I noticed that there are no farm-to-market roads that would make it easier to harvest the coconuts and bring them to market. That might be the problem, but that’s where the diligence of the Samarnons was seen, who didn’t complain and complain. They made a way to improve what they found. Of course, the government should be there to help you make your lives easier and better. [Applause]

Earlier, you might have noticed that I laughed when Ben said that the national roads were gone. The provincial roads weren’t mentioned either. The municipal roads weren’t mentioned either. We ended up talking about barangay roads. You might ask, Mar and I laughed a little. Why? Our colleague Ed Nachura, who I know was a congressman here in Samar, was with us. When we were together during our first term, Christmas time came. Of course, we need to greet our barangay captains and councilors. I said, “This Christmas is really heavy,” during my first year as a congressman. 159 times 10 people we need to greet, just from the barangay council. Ed Nachura said to me, “Ah, is that your problem? Because I have 500 plus barangays.” Imagine, he is obligated to greet five thousand people, and of course, the greeting isn’t just a Christmas card, just read ten. That won’t do. So, what that means is, imagine making each barangay just one kilometer. Wouldn’t each barangay say that’s not that big? Five hundred kilometers in just one district, with Ed Nachura.

But as our economy continues to improve, why can't we find a way to expand our infrastructure further and further? Now, someone might ask, “How will we do that?” Next year, there will be an election. I, I can no longer continue this, so I cannot tell you that I will finish all of this. I only have eight months left. You will be the ones to say who will continue. When the election comes, my vote will also count as one. So all of us who will vote, we will make our own direction. And I believe, many will make promises. But I believe, in the more than five years that we have served you, we haven't broken any promises yet. [Applause]

And this is the last thing, please excuse me if we got a little carried away, I just noticed that we are really on schedule. [Laughter] Honestly, I just want to ask you if we can give the members of the cabinet a round of applause and let me tell you why. Most of the members of my cabinet are bringing crackers because, like this, our schedule should have been in Manila already, we should have returned. We will arrive in Manila around 1:30, they will have lunch around 3 o'clock, and after 3 o'clock, they need to be smiling. The point is, we are really serious. Our well-being, we are comfortable, no. The comfort and well-being of others come first. [Applause] Because the Secretary of Health reminds us, don't abuse your bodies too much, add something to your memo-rice, even just a little. Do you know what memo-rice is? It's just a memory of rice. That's why it's memo-rice. [Laughter]

This is the most important thing that Mel and I struggled with the other day: My beloved Samar people: I offer this to you to achieve our aspirations for a good life for our country. [Applause]

That's all from me. Good afternoon to all of you and thank you very much.

[1] To my dear Samareños, thank you very much for continuously walking the Straight Path. You are still my strength. You are still my Boss. You are still the ones making change happen.

[2] My dear Samareños: Let us unite towards the realization of our dream of a comfortable life in our country.
