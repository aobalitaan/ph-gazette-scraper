Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, at the gathering of partners in the Straight Path

Let me begin my speech by saying that I am truly delighted that so many of our colleagues, from the very beginning until now, are still here. There’s Tita Nonie Uy, in front of us, but her husband and my father were the first to get together, to support my mother, and to help us; Manong Mokring Barcenas here on the left, even during the Constitutional Convention, he was already fighting for what is right, and until now he is truly, truly zealous in continuing to fight for everything.

Earlier, we heard from Vice Mayor Rey Calooy of Libagon, Southern Leyte. He used to help us during the campaign, and now he is the one running. Next year, I will be retired, and there will be successors like Rey.

Often, they say to me, thank you for this, thank you for that. Earlier, the thank you for bottom-up budgeting was mentioned. I always say, don’t thank me. It’s like, I am the one who will be the target of the cannonball, I am the one who will be the dartboard for those who relentlessly criticize and think they are so clever. I take all of that, but I could not have done this without all of you. And seeing all of you still here really strengthens my resolve. [Applause]

This, as you all know, I have never lied to the people, especially to you, my allies. This gathering we have now, look, they say, “They are divided. Some are wearing yellow, some orange, some green, some red.” I think they are mistaken. Those wearing orange, I think, are mustard; dark yellow. The orange that is a little too much, that is also heading that way. [Laughter] Those wearing green, they chose green, apple green. It can be called yellow-green.

But perhaps what is important here are the cameras of our media, later our crowds will look at them. You, if you hear audio of someone clapping, you see the person clapping. I saw a gathering recently where someone was clapping, but the person was fanning themselves. I don’t know now if the venue for fanning and clapping was different. But let’s not talk about that. We don’t care about that.

Last Tuesday, we traveled to Naga for the third anniversary of the passing of Secretary Jesse Robredo. There, we commemorated his life, and together with our Cabinet and other allies, we had the opportunity to talk to our countrymen. During that meeting, a beneficiary of the Pantawid Pamilya program asked me, and his question was: How can I be sure that the program that brings him hope will continue, even – and this is the most important, or especially – when I step down from office?

To be honest, we were made to think by this question. On one hand, we were happy because he recognized and others of our countrymen also recognized the benefits of our programs, such as the Pantawid Pamilya. I am also pleased with the opportunity to lead the opening of the door of opportunity for our citizens, especially those who are most in need. On the other hand, we really want to lessen their anxieties about the future, and assure them that our service will continue.

As we approach the 2016 elections, this kind of question is becoming more common, not only from the beneficiaries of Pantawid Pamilya, but also from many others who are benefiting from the positive transformation brought about by the Straight Path. This expansion of opportunity and the decrease in unemployment; these all-time highs in direct investments, as well as the credit ratings upgrades where, for the first time in our history, we achieved investment grade status; the infrastructure being built throughout the country, including the Cebu Bus Rapid Transit here in your city; the modern equipment for our uniformed personnel; the suppression of corruption and the restoration of justice; the recovery of our national dignity. With all the changes we have sown because of the mandate given to us by our Bosses in 2010, we are seeing a growing sentiment: We must ensure that these are not wasted, but rather continued and further developed to benefit future generations.

For me, if the Straight Path has brought us transformation, it is only right that we stay on it. And the huge crossroads we are facing is the election next year, which stands as a referendum on all that we have worked for. The question is: Is the direction we are taking correct? Isn't it right to prosecute and hold accountable those who promised to serve, but only oppressed us and wronged us? Isn't it right to continue the economic upliftment and the services that expand capabilities and opportunities? For those of us who have a reasonable perspective, we should continue and further accelerate the reforms we have started. The only key and the one who holds the power to make this happen is none other than our Bosses: the people.

Now, we are already seeing the competition begin for those who will succeed us. Some have already declared, and others are about to declare their candidacy. We are sure that there are those who will continue what we have started because they have been with us; they have really seen who is sincere and not opportunistic.

There are also those we trust are aligned with us in many ways—in good intentions and trust. We have no doubt about his/her ability. I believe in his/her good intentions, which is why we are working to be with him/her to present to the public a united and strong tandem. What's even better is that if this person joins us, his/her knowledge and experience will be honed and deepened, and good intentions will truly be enriched with sufficient management skills. Our hope will be even stronger that even in 2022, the Straight Path will continue. [Applause]

But let us not forget, not everyone who aspires to take our place shares our path of righteousness. On the other hand, there are those who clearly say things that are contrary to the path of righteousness; as others say, they merely took advantage of being associated with the path of righteousness. They add that the five years they applauded him for, he now calls a failure. The question is: When was he truly genuine, during the five years of praise, or sometime in the future because until now, what he says doesn't seem genuine? [Applause]

If we are talking about the next election, of course, we cannot help but think: When it comes to complex problems, when it comes to challenges that require strong political will, when it comes to the breadth of knowledge and strategy, who will we turn to? I don't think any of you are thinking of returning us to the crooked path. To those who might fight for what is right? Will we side with those who might continue the path of righteousness? Surely, no one will side with those who will truly destroy everything that the Straight Path has achieved.

My belief is that too much is at stake to entrust to someone who “might continue the path of righteousness,” and even more so to someone who “will not continue the path of righteousness.” Let us go with someone who will surely continue everything we have started. And I think you all know exactly who I am referring to, and that is none other than Mar Roxas, who is with us now. [Applause]

I know many of us already know him; we know his true character, beyond what the cameras focus on, and beyond the attacks of people who try to bring him down. We know that Mar has integrity, is reliable, and has a deep understanding. [Applause] But we also know: Not everyone is aware of Mar’s true character. Like me, he doesn't have the habit of boasting about his accomplishments. In Tagalog, that means, for every little thing you do, you need to praise yourself ten times. From our perspective, Mar quietly fulfills his sworn duty to the people, and there is no need to advertise “you should thank me,” right? Our job now: Ensure that our fellow Filipinos see the truth that Mar is the deserving one and will surely continue the path of righteousness. [Applause]

I am also aware: There may be some who are still hesitant about Mar—not because of his ability or integrity, but because it will be complex to introduce him to our countrymen and raise his numbers.

What I can tell you: Doing what is right is not always easy, and if we compare it to what we have gone through in recent years, I know we can succeed. Isn't it true that everything important should be fought for and worked hard for? What we can assure you is that when we persevere, when we choose what is right, we will surely have something new to offer. [Applause] I promise you, I will be with you in working; I will contribute everything I can contribute; I will not abandon Mar. [Applause]

I also know: The Cebuanos, they truly know what it means to walk the Straight Path. Even during my mother's time, you showed solidarity with good governance, and you were a pillar of national support in fighting and eradicating the dictatorship. You Cebuanos were the ones who sheltered my mother at the beginning of EDSA. I was truly at peace then; with our countrymen here, I knew my mother was in good hands and safe from any danger. I also remember: One month after my father was assassinated, I spoke to the public for the first time, and it happened here in Cebu, at Fuente Osmeña. [Applause]

As I disembarked from the PAL plane at that time, the stairs were at the tail. At the bottom of the plane, waiting [were] AVSECOM. Those who were sentenced to kill my father were from AVSECOM. The PAL crew looked around, looked at me and said to me, “Good luck to you.”

But truthfully, the immense support and enthusiasm of the people I interacted with made me feel as if the country was already liberated from Martial Law, even back in 1983; and that happened here in Cebu. After the dictatorship, you continued to show your care. When I approached you when I ran for Senator and President, with Tita Margot going house-to-house, your unwavering trust and support were present. In the past five years of our administration, you showed that you were truly aligned with the agenda of change. When Yolanda struck, even though you were affected, especially in Bantayan Island, you voluntarily cooperated and showed solidarity to expand the delivery of aid to the many affected by the disaster, led by Governor Junjun Davide. [Applause]

Yolanda was no joke, you know that. And Junjun Davide was one of those who lessened my problems. My first question was, “Junjun, how is Cebu?” He replied, “We’re still okay here. Only Bantayan is where we need to go. It looks like that’s where it hit hardest.” But after that, he was already there. He said, “Our volunteers are ready to repack for the aid to be sent to areas ravaged by Yolanda, like Leyte.”

Just a reminder, 44 out of our 81 provinces were affected by Typhoon Yolanda, so the help of the Cebuanos in addressing their needs was truly immense. That’s why, in the upcoming referendum on the Straight Path, I am confident that the Cebuanos will continue to stand up for what is right. [Applause]

It’s very clear: We need to continue uniting towards broad development. My question is: Do you agree with those who say we should just stop the benefits received by more than 4.4 million households in the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program? This year, 333,673 were the first batch to graduate from high school with the help of our Pantawid Pamilya Program; [applause] they are part of the first batch of our beneficiaries in the expanded Pantawid Pamilya. What’s even better is that 13,469 of them received honors and various awards when they graduated from their respective high schools. Can anyone say that if we had stopped this program, they would have been able to continue their studies? They are the youth who were almost deprived of the opportunity to build a good future if they hadn’t been given a chance. Despite this, there are still those who want to criticize it; saying it should be investigated because this program has leakage. But the truth is, the data they are using is from 2009. Just a reminder to those who are turning a blind eye and deaf ear: I became President in 2010—the second half of 2010. I wasn’t responsible for that in 2009. It was a different President they should be holding accountable. And I remember, the one criticizing me was with someone who declared they were running for president. Maybe they should just ask them.

Here in Cebu, would you agree to simply stop our programs, such as those for infrastructure, where more than P22.8 billion has already been allocated from 2011 to the present? [Applause]

Let's look at that. From 2005 to 2010, the average annual budget of the DPWH here in Cebu was P1.6 billion. In our first five years in office, it reached an average of P4 billion from – let me repeat – P1.6 billion. [Applause] This year, the agency's budget has reached P7 billion, and next year, if approved by Congress, our proposed budget for the DPWH for Cebu is P9 billion. We can certainly go much further with this fund. Is there anyone saying we should go back to just P1 billion?

Let's also recall: In the past, the overpriced lamps purchased by the previous leadership for the lamps leading to your Convention Center were also controversial. According to the report, each lamp cost P224,000. Lamps are expensive, because now we are building socialized housing at that amount. Do we want this crooked practice to happen again? It is clear where we should stand.

I emphasize: We have achieved successes in the past five years because of sound governance, and without imposing new taxes except for the Sin Tax. Whatever we have now, we have achieved through contribution. Isn't it true: I didn't single-handedly fight corruption; our fight succeeds because you and every decent Filipino are behind me. Our initiatives to eradicate poverty succeed because of your support, and because of the proper implementation of directives at various levels of government. The economy succeeds because we are with the private sector and the labor sector, which show confidence in our reform agenda. Trust in fellow Filipinos, trust in the Straight Path: That is the key to reaching the great potential of our race.

You know, let me just emphasize the budget. In the past, we could only offer you the same opportunities that they called their “country.” The highest was probably P1.6 billion. We were able to lower the debt to GDP ratio, so we can’t say that the debt was further expanded. Your taxes were not raised either, but from P1.6 billion, it averaged P4 billion, now it’s P7 billion, P9 billion next year. I think there are two things we should know about that: A lot can be achieved with proper spending. But the more important thing is the second: That those who did this, you made the key to correct the system, to get what is due to you. [Applause]

You were given less before, and it ended up in various pockets. Aside from your benefit, who would say we’re back to the way things were?

I am confident: One day, we will return to this intersection, and we will say: I did not back down from the challenge; I did what was right; I contributed to the continuation of the Straight Path. I was there at the beginning, and I helped continue it, towards the realization of the collective dream of the Filipino people.

You know, earlier our representatives from the BPO, or the Business Processing Outsourcing centers were mentioned. The one who established that, perhaps we have forgotten, was Mar Roxas. [Applause] About 15 years ago, as Secretary of DTI, he introduced it. When it started, only a few hundred people worked there. In September of last year, 2014, I reached one million direct employees in the BPO sector. Now, there is another factor called the multiplier factor of 3.5 to 4. That means, for every direct employee of the BPO, there is someone driving a taxi, someone who built the condominiums for them, someone manning the convenience store. That’s the added jobs because of the BPO. The good news is, according to a large BPO, when we started, he said their target was to reach 30,000 employees before my term ended. During our last interview, and it was quite some time ago, he told me they had already hired 50,000 employees in that one company. But the best news he told me was that just in Manila, if they had a building on EDSA, and near a transport hub, they could easily fill the building no matter how big it was. That’s the confidence of this industry, so next year, 1.3 million employees are expected, $25 billion will be contributed to our economy. [Applause]

Tsuneishi, which has a shipyard here in Cebu, whenever I talk to them, they always tell me: Their orders for shipbuilding are fully booked. Another company, TESDA reported to us, named Hanjin, from Korea, is located in Subic. They said they need 2,000 welders in the next three months because the type of ship they are building there in Korea, ten, their parent company wants to make twenty per year here in Subic. And they are very happy with us.

During Labor Day, our brother with a disability was here earlier. We had many problems to bring, but when we saw that there was a training program to provide livelihood for our countrymen who are truly disabled, the program was designed, even the equipment was modified, and they were really getting into machine works as well. I really said, it seems like there are no more problems we can't overcome if we work together.

So, colleagues, before I finish, we are heading towards the elections. Many will take the lead, many will boast, many criticisms, much mudslinging will happen. The time will come again when everyone says, “The situation is very noisy again.” Perhaps just a reminder: Let's look at where the one who promises comes from so we know where his promises will take us. And there, by any measure, I decided a long time ago, Mar Roxas is really the one who should steer us.

Good afternoon. Thank you very much.
