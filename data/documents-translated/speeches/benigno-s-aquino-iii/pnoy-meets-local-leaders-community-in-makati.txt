Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

During his meeting with local leaders and communities of Makati

[Delivered at the Makati Coliseum, Makati City, on April 8, 2016]

Those two who spoke before me introduced themselves. So, please allow me to introduce myself to you as well: I am Noynoy Aquino, the favorite son of Ninoy and Cory Aquino. [Laughter and applause] I am not the youngest among us. I am in the middle of my siblings. I am only a few years older than Kris. You can see in our appearance, we don't look that different. It's only a few years. [Laughter] It can be counted on almost two hands.

We have been here for a while now, listening to everyone who spoke before us. Let me just count how many pages this speech is, and you might get angry at me if it's too long. We succeeded today: this speech is only four and a half pages long.

My fellow citizens, the election is fast approaching; the people will choose our replacement. Everyone knows that the job of the President is a heavy one. I have a speech, I already mentioned, the job of the President is like watching 200 TV channels at the same time. While you are watching, there is always someone asking what is happening on each of these channels, and you must know what is currently happening, what happened before the present, and what will happen after what you are watching. And if you make a mistake, you will not be forgiven for not seeing or thinking about what will happen next.

You know, when we came into office, about 10 million of our countrymen were abroad, and we needed to know what was happening even in events overseas. When something happens in another place, the first question immediately is: Are any Filipinos involved there? You know, when the conflict in Ukraine happened, I was actually surprised that even in Kiev, Ukraine, there were 180 Filipinos working and living there. If we were to ask: Can anyone raise their hand and point to where Ukraine is on the map in this gym, would anyone be able to point it out? There are 180 Filipinos there.

To be honest, everyone who wants to talk to me, we try to schedule everyone so they can talk. I can't meet with everyone at the same time; we won't accomplish anything if we all talk at once, right? I said, I wish it were the same when it comes to problems. If possible, the problems that come up that a President needs to face, if possible, could also be scheduled so they don't come one after another. I said, 'one after another' isn't the right way to describe it. The correct description of the problems a President needs to face is 'layered' or 'piled up'.

To help you understand, let me give you an example, just two days in my life: Last Friday, April 1, just last week, we had a long security meeting. We discussed the hijacking of a tugboat by Abu Sayyaf with ten Indonesian hostages who were kidnapped. While discussing this, we also learned that, allegedly, Abu Sayyaf had also kidnapped Malaysians. This attack was carried out in Malaysia.

We also discussed that day that the Indonesian Foreign Minister was arriving to find out what happened to their citizens. We want to maintain a very good relationship in the field between the Philippines and Indonesia. And to really accommodate the visit of their Foreign Minister, we went to Cavite, and we talked to the Indonesian Foreign Minister at Villamor Airbase to inform them of all the events regarding our desire to recover their citizens here.

After that meeting, we went to Cavite to inaugurate a building from PAGCOR and another one from the budget for Cavite, in the town of Carmona. We went to Kawit, for a meeting with our colleagues and a little campaigning.

On our way back to Manila, DILG Secretary Mel Sarmiento mentioned to us that the next day, he was going to Kidapawan. I asked, “What will you do in Kidapawan?” To be honest, that was the first time I heard that there were people blocking the highway in Kidapawan and that there had been a violent dispersal.

You know, my job is really 24/7, 365 days a year, and sometimes my body complains too. That night, I suddenly got a cold. I needed to rest—I was wearing a sweater, wrapped up, with no aircon or electric fan. We tried to sleep, and then there were a few more problems the next day, even with our stomachs. And the doctor really advised me that I needed to rest even a little. We rested on Saturday, if you think about it.

On that same Saturday, before we could rest, the problem at NAIA happened where the power went out for five hours. On Sunday, our strength somewhat recovered, we called the Executive Secretary (ES), and I said, “Jojo, please call all concerned parties—DILG, the police, and DSWD, and other agencies—regarding what happened in Kidapawan. I also want to know what happened at the airport.” It was on Sunday that I was calling for a meeting. ES said, “Let me find out if they are all ready to speak.” To be honest, I felt uneasy. Sunday was the only day of rest for our colleagues, but I was really eager to start resolving all these events. ES came back to me and said, “Boss, if possible, let’s have the meeting on Monday. Not all of our contacts are ready to brief you yet.” I said, “You know my attitude. I won’t be able to rest until we’ve provided solutions to all the problems that happened.” But if they aren’t ready, nothing will happen at the meeting, and gathering data will be delayed. So, we talked on Monday.

My fellow citizens, that was only two days. You gave me six years, you think about that. Every day that God creates, you have a pile of problems that need to be discussed, and then most of the time, what you hear are just criticisms of you. Is that right? [Applause]

My only point is: It’s really not easy to be President of the Republic of the Philippines, or President anywhere. Despite this, we exhaust every opportunity to serve the Filipino people. Anyone, I think, would be happy when their hard work bears fruit. As we walked the Straight and Narrow Path, this is the result of our initiatives—ours, you are included there; you initiated this—for Makati:

We acknowledge: Makati is a wealthy city, but we helped you with your roads and flood control projects. From 2011 to 2016, we allocated P3.19 billion for your infrastructure. [Applause] That’s a far cry from the P786.39 million allocated by our predecessor from 2005 to 2010.

We also focused on social services. The number of TWSP scholars—this is a TESDA program—in Makati that we helped is almost 30,000 Filipinos. We expanded Philhealth, so we now support 713,133 residents of Makati. [Applause]

Earlier, we listened to a beneficiary of 4Ps. In Pantawid Pamilya, the number of 4Ps members we found in Makati in 2010 was many, zero. Makati had no members in 2010. Now, we are helping 4,229 households. [Applause]

Because of our contributions, we recorded the lowest poverty incidence in the country since 2009, the lowest unemployment rate in a decade, and the lowest annual average hunger rate in 11 years.

Why are we doing all of this? Because our vow in the Straight Path is: No one will be left behind, and we will truly leave things much better than we found them.

Of course, we help those in the margins with their education, ensuring they gain the capacity to enter better jobs. We also made sure that there are increasingly more jobs available because if you study, you train, you have the ability, but there's nothing to apply for, then it's all useless.

If you have an illness, maybe you already have a job, the situation is improving, but a serious illness strikes and you're left to fend for yourself – “let Batman handle it” – and you're back to living below the poverty line. That's why the state also supports the sick. I remember a video I watched of a mother whose child had dengue fever, and to pay off the hospital debt—and this is a true story—she borrowed money to pay the hospital bill. She borrowed again to pay off the first loan. She borrowed a third time to pay off the second. It felt like she only saw debts coming from all sides. In the coming days, you will see this video. Now, if you are truly qualified and in need, we have what we call “No Balance Billing” for hospitals, where PhilHealth and the State cover your needs. [Applause]

These beneficiaries of the Straight Path program are genuine in their words. Often when I talk to them, they say to me: Thank you. And I reply: Why are you thanking me? You started the change. You united your strength to give us the opportunity to change the story of our country. [Applause]

I think I'm not the only one who sometimes prays: I hope all candidates will truly mean everything they say. For example, the former mayor here, [applause] called us “a failure and insensitive.” I ask, can a failure and an insensitive person turn the former Sick Man of Asia into Asia’s Rising Tiger? [Applause] Can a failure handle the modernization we are doing for our Armed Forces, police, and even firefighters? Is free medical care for the poorest 40 percent of our population, and our nearing reach of 4.6 million households through the Pantawid Pamilya program, also the work of a failure and an insensitive person? [Applause]

We focus on the needs of others. You are the witnesses, not just on birthdays. We don't show our love for our Bosses through cakes alone. Speaking of cakes, it's good that your mayor now is Kid Peña, [applause] because the birthday cakes here have improved—he has them put up for bidding and supplied by a well-known brand. Is that right? The good thing is, the brand is well-known, and the cake is even cheaper. Compare that to before when the supplier wasn't even known, yet the cake was more expensive. If you're wondering, don't ask me for the answer because I'm also wondering why the non-branded is more expensive than the branded. [Applause] Even with just cakes, you've progressed with Kid Peña. There are probably many other areas where you can progress as well.

This person who is strongly attacking our candidate, already had allegations of corruption back in 2014. Am I the only one waiting for a clear answer from this person, who is one of those aspiring to be President of the Philippines?

You know, 30 years have passed since EDSA. During that time, our friend Jojo Binay—who is a candidate now, and says a lot about me—I know he clearly understood what was right. Thirty years ago, we were together saying that the darkness of the dictatorship was wrong, it needed to be fought, and we risked our lives.

Here in Makati, on the first day of the EDSA Revolution, we were together; here we received and learned the news about the People Power Revolution. Here we thanked those who were oppressed and guarded the ballots during the snap elections. I remember the two of us: Someone brought out a radio, they called it a boombox back then, and we were listening to Fidel Ramos and Juan Ponce Enrile. Everyone with us at the gathering looked at each other and laughed. They really believed that Martial Law was over. The two of us, Jojo Binay and I, looked at each other because we knew “This is it. We're going to be arrested. We'll be imprisoned on the island near Corregidor and Batman will have to save us.”

At those moments, I approached him and said goodbye. I said, “Friend, my mother is in Cebu. I have siblings at home in Times. I need to put them in a good condition.” He said to me, “I will take you.” And he did take me. And we really grew close to him during those times because he prioritized our welfare before his own. Thirty years ago, it was very clear: Others first before self.

Truly: Back then, we were comrades, standing together; we opposed the dictatorship, and were willing to risk our lives for others. That’s where our relationship began to deepen. It was very clear during those times who Jejomar Binay was. Now, the question is: Where has the Jejomar Binay I knew gone? [Applause]

For example, you said: The 4Ps, you will make it 5Ps. [Laughter] He said that while making it 5Ps, he will lower taxes. When I heard that, I said, “You’re so clever.” All the wives in this hall, if your husbands tell you, “Next month, I will reduce your budget.” The wife will look. “And while I’m reducing your budget, you need to increase what you can buy.” Will that couple agree? That’s probably why one of your children said he will investigate the 4Ps, because it should be 5Ps. So when we think about everything he said, he shouldn’t be angry with me. He is much older than me. Older brother, what is it really?

Comrades, you know, someone told me: Just be practical. Don’t endorse anyone, don’t favor anyone, and you won’t gain more enemies. As for me, if I do that, will I be true to my word to you? As Father of the Nation, it is clear to me that it is my duty to guide you and the entire nation.

To be very clear, let me give you an example, like the example used. What are we fighting for right now? We went to Pampanga and spoke to a mother. This mother, she told us, has seven children, and her husband left her. She said that her livelihood is “selling things here and there.”

When we listen to “selling things here and there,” sometimes she sells, sometimes she can’t sell. That’s where she gets her livelihood. If she can’t sell, how about her seven children?

She said that because of 4Ps, she was able to get three of her seven children through school. And those three, currently have permanent jobs. [Applause]

If this housewife came to this stage, spoke to all of us, and told her story, “You, you may not know it, but you have already helped three of my seven children. They are now standing on their own two feet. They are not a burden to you. You weren’t asked to take a single peso from your pockets, and you were able to help. I still have four children hoping you will continue the current change that is happening, so that we can reach a point where we are no longer a burden to anyone. We can stand on our own two feet.” If we, are visited, stood up to, and asked, “Can you risk the good change that is happening to us? I hope you will support us, continue your support for us so that we can truly stand on our own two feet. We must continue this Straight Path.” [Applause]

My Bosses, it is indeed campaign season, and everyone is saying beautiful things. The question is: Will they be able to fulfill their sweet promises? I remember: In 1965, we read this, Mr. Marcos said, “I will make this country great again.” We know the result after 21 years in office. The one who replaced me also made many promises. You already know what happened. It seems the opposite of the words he uttered.

Truly: The upcoming election on May 9th, that election, is a referendum. What do you want: what they did, or what we did? You are the Boss, and you will be followed. As for me: We have already started something very good. Will we go back to the dark past? Will we just pray again and say “Hopefully, we’ll get lucky again and the situation will improve”? Then, we’ll fight again to restore everyone’s trust in the government. But we don’t need to gamble.

Clearly: We don’t need that now, because at present, we have Mar Roxas and Leni Robredo. [Applause] Is it a good idea to gamble on “maybe”—maybe they will continue what we have worked for; maybe these others won’t deceive us; maybe they will be true to their promises. Why should we rely on “maybe” when we already have something certain?

The vow of Mar and Leni: They will continue the Straight Path. Why should we believe them? It’s very clear: We were with them in building and traversing this path. We were with them in facing all criticisms, pain, and hardship. We share the same belief that this is the right thing to do, that we really must help those on the margins of society.

You can see it yourselves: In the latest survey, our numbers are even higher now—my personal ones—compared to when I was elected. This proves that you are still my strength; you are still the ones making the change; you started and will continue all of this. [Applause]

To be honest, we entered the campaign lacking in funds and equipment. You can see it, even the party’s clothes, there weren’t any that matched, the designs were all different. The only thing we had in common was that we were all wearing yellow. Why is that? We didn’t steal to buy a lot of t-shirts to give to you. [Applause] But this, this is new. Because I used this back in 2010. Luckily, someone who takes good care of me at home hid my 2010 one for me.

We contribute what we can, and those who support us also contribute. From the very beginning, the people have been behind us, because we remained true to the people. That is what we clearly fight for. [Applause]

Comrades, that is where we stand, clearly fighting for the Filipino people: Mar Roxas and Leni Robredo.

We have spoken to many people, and it's been repeated: You've done enough. You can now be quiet. Not just quiet in politics, quiet—you know—in other things. The point is very simple: I have an obligation to you. It's really hard to say goodbye, sometimes it's sad. But what's important to me is that when I say goodbye to you, when we've retired, you are in good condition.

From the very beginning, I never said, “I am the best. I am the most intelligent. I am the most perfect person.” No. From the very beginning, what we always said, even from the time of my father, my mother, was, “Our strength is you.” That’s why we talk about “People Power.” [Applause] My parents taught me again, “The Filipino is worth dying for.” My mother said, “The Filipino is worth living for.” I, for my part, added “fighting for.”

Here, what is happening to us now, we created it. We come from dark periods in our history. There is no reason to return to that darkness because the strength is truly in your hands. Let's use it correctly. Let's not get lost. Let's not be swayed by beautiful words. We know the truth. Let's fight for the truth. Let's fight, especially, for the next generation who are relying on all of us. [Applause]

You know, Jillian earlier, was a little rushed when she said her mother did her homework. That's not true. Her mother is helping her. Because you may not know, Jillian goes to a rather ordinary school, the Philippine Science High School. [Applause] So, in time, she will teach me higher Math.

Comrades, we might not be able to return before May 9th arrives. I was taught to rely on the people, as always, I rely on you because you are truly the source of the strength we draw from to fight for everything. [Applause]

Good afternoon. Thank you all very much.
