Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, at the Ceremonial Deactivation of Arms and Decommissioning of Combatants of the Moro Islamic Liberation Front (MILF)

As an introduction, allow me to share some things that have been a puzzle to me over the past years.

When I was young, when violence was emerging here, wouldn't it be fair to say that: The root of the conflict is the exploitation of a few people, often Christians, of our countrymen here in Mindanao. When the greedy saw those who were tilling the land, they titled the lands to themselves. This pushed our Moro brothers to fight for what is rightfully theirs. The question we ask is: If abuse of the law is the root of the problem, shouldn't a law recognizing the rights and culture of our Muslim brothers be the solution? It would have been better if the government's response to the call of our countrymen had been similar to the Indigenous Peoples Rights Act, which we have now.

We know that this is not what happened. The government sent some parts of our Philippine Constabulary. When the conflict intensified and the unrest could no longer be controlled, the Armed Forces were sent. What happened: Instead of achieving peace, the situation became even more complicated.

Furthermore: If you were in the position of our brothers and sisters who were dispossessed of their land during that time, wouldn't you lose hope if everyone you approached had the same tone and the response was “Nothing can be done about it”? Please also consider: Due to this conflict in agrarian issues, where no justice could be obtained, they took the law into their own hands and armed groups emerged.

My view: If you are being oppressed and forced to leave the system, or even to fight it, because you have lost trust, because the State’s care is lacking, wouldn't your response have been different? After decades of shooting, after the use of weapons, even our Armed Forces are saying: The solution here in Muslim Mindanao will not come from the AFP alone; a whole of government approach is needed. Legitimate grievances need a legitimate response; we need to help those of our brothers and sisters who have been marginalized so that they can have true justice.

When this became clear to me, new questions arose. If the wounds caused by the shooting and retaliation are already too deep, how can trust be sown? With apologies to Usec. Manny Bautista, I will use what happened to his family as an example. His father, General Teodulfo Bautista, was once invited to Sulu to engage in peace talks. But it turned out that his counterpart did not seek peace but violence. These kinds of violent incidents exacerbated the grief caused by the war for many Filipinos, so you cannot expect trust to grow between the two sides.

In this situation, how can we reach an agreement acceptable to all if the negotiators themselves do not trust each other? I thought that it might take generations before there is true dialogue. One suggestion was to have an exchange of students in our country. Young people from Mindanao would study in Luzon and the Visayas; and young people from Luzon and the Visayas would study in Mindanao. In this way, the new generations can build stronger relationships, and when they are the ones holding the reins of power, they can talk as friends, instead of as enemies with their own long lists of grievances. This is a long process, and I likely won't live to see it bear fruit, like someone who is no longer that young.

This is the reason why today is truly historic. If we go back a few years, seeing an armed group that has long fought the government lay down their arms was just a distant dream. We are not talking about just one, two, or a dozen pieces of weaponry here. These are some of the highest-caliber weapons. They are modern and not outdated; these weapons are capable of—and have indeed—caused immense suffering.

Bosses, I need to emphasize: Our Moro brothers and sisters have promised, and here is the proof of their sincerity before us. Our brothers and sisters voluntarily laid down their arms. They know that the threat of violence from private armed groups still exists, but with this act, they seem to be telling us: “Brothers and sisters, we no longer need these defenses. We entrust our safety to you with full confidence. Brothers and sisters, we can now lower these arms because we believe you are with us in our desire to change our society and our lives.” They are doing this even though the Bangsamoro Basic Law is still pending, and the Bangsamoro Transition Authority is yet to be established.

That is why I cannot say that we are gambling by talking to the MILF. Gambling is a matter of chance, and you have no proof of your fate. What we are doing now is very strong evidence of the MILF’s complete and sincere cooperation in our peace process, and their willingness to abandon the path of conflict. They are now extending their hand as an invitation towards greater trust and cooperation. It is sad that some of our legislators want to respond to this invitation by stopping the BBL. Instead of asking: “How can I improve the BBL to address the grievances of our countrymen?” some are even considering: “How can I stop or obstruct it?”

In this moment, I think we are like at a wedding or like at Heart Break Hill in the Boston Marathon. At a wedding, we are told that you are already in front of the altar, you know this is the one you will marry, but you suddenly start to wonder: Will he squeeze the toothpaste from the middle or the end? Will he lower the toilet seat? Of course: You love the person you are going to marry, and these concerns are insignificant, but you still think about them. Similarly, at Heart Break Hill, we are on our way to fulfilling our relationship towards peace; should we now have doubts about whether we can finish the marathon? Will we suddenly lose faith, especially when the finish line is within sight?

This is the truth: Our brothers and sisters in the Bangsamoro are not asking for too much; what they desire is a decent and peaceful life, just like any Filipino. We must also admit that we have fallen short with each other: There is nothing written in our religion and laws that requires us to continue the mistakes of the past. Here we are now, given the opportunity to correct the wrong, will we turn our backs on it?

What I can tell you is this: I am personally monitoring the BBL. There was one meeting, with members of Congress, where, in our desire to have a true “meeting of the minds” and answer any doubts, we stayed up until dawn scrutinizing and analyzing the draft BBL. What we really want is to improve this law further and further.

Our brothers and sisters have already shown an example that is now part of our history. What the response of the majority of Filipinos will be to their good deed will also be recorded in history. The question we ask is this: If there are ten steps to fully come closer, and they have already taken nine and a half steps, will you deny them the remaining half step? When you look in the mirror, will your conscience not haunt you? If, by blocking this very good solution, you reach a point where your own family is harmed, will you be able to face them and say, “Sorry, I hindered peace”?

To those of you who are drafting the law, and to those who will eventually vote in the plebiscite regarding the BBL: Remember this day. We who neglected them, we who proposed wrong solutions, we who were not zealous in protesting and preventing abuse against them, will we snatch from them the opportunity to live with dignity and peace? Will we cling to fears that have no basis? Do we really want to return to solutions that have been tried but did not work and only gave birth to more conflicts, and only deepened the wound of distrust?

I recall what a German pastor, Dietrich Bonhoeffer, said. He was a pastor who was killed during the Nazi era in Germany: “First they came for the communists, but I was not a communist so I did not speak out. Then they came for the socialists and the trade-unionists, but I was neither so I did not speak out. Then they came for the Jews, but I was not a Jew, so I did not speak out. And when they came for me, there was no one left to speak out for me.” It is true: If you allow the rights of others to be trampled upon, don't you also agree to repeat this cycle, and one day you yourself could become a victim?

My Bosses, let me emphasize: What we once thought would never happen, is happening. Our brothers and sisters in the MILF have already taken this huge step, and you might think: Will they still have difficulty with the other steps? I think the process will become easier and easier. My appeal is: Let us reciprocate the trust they have shown us. Let us strive to reach a point where we can say: We really gave them every opportunity to change their lives and achieve their dreams. But this is it: If your view is to make it difficult to pass the law, it's like saying we begrudged them what should have been theirs; we made sure they had no opportunity to improve; we guaranteed they would never come down from the mountains. That is what opposing the BBL equates to.

You can't say you are for peace, but you make the drafting of the BBL difficult. It's like you weren't content with the nine needles' eyes, you added a tenth and eleventh; it seems you have no desire other than to ensure there is no room for peace. Please help me: What should I answer when someone asks me, “Where is the conscience of the people who are delaying the process for us to have peace?”

Hundreds of thousands have been harmed and died, and the number of those who have lost their homes and communities has become countless, due to four decades of conflict. If you still don't side with peace, how many more will lose their loved ones? How many more communities will be caught in the crossfire and insecurity? How many who are suffering will suffer even more? How much longer before chaos knocks on your own door and your own family is affected?

We cannot deny it: We owe our brothers and sisters in the Bangsamoro a debt. We have an opportunity now to correct the narrative of the suffering we collectively faced. This is our time. This is what our fallen countrymen fought for. Let us show those who gave their lives, our countrymen, and the whole world: Even if we come from different backgrounds and beliefs, because we are bound by trust and care for one another, there is no challenge we cannot overcome as a nation.

Good day to all of you. Thank you very much.
