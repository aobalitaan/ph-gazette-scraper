Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, on the 113th anniversary of the police service.

Today, we celebrate the 113th anniversary of the police service in the country. For more than 11 decades, the police have contributed to promoting peace and order in the country and have been partners with Filipinos in facing various challenges.

Clearly: The entire nation relies on the skill and courage of our police and other members of the uniformed ranks; that in times of danger or disaster, you should be there—ready to act, ready to respond, not hesitating to sacrifice your lives, just to protect the peace, tranquility, and rights of our countrymen. But for a long time, the situation has been: you already have a heavy responsibility, you still have to find ways to get your equipment—that when it comes to your welfare, it seems what they say is true, “Let Batman handle it.”

For some of our colleagues here who have been in the service for a long time, you probably remember: Before, because of the lack of firearms in your ranks, new police officers had to buy the rights to firearms from those who were retiring. In your desire to serve, you went into debt just to get into the service, and your salary was still deducted when you were already employed. You were worried because during an encounter, you couldn't just rely on your pleasing personality with the bad elements. How did that practice become right?

Because of many decades of poor administration and neglect, perhaps some of you thought then: My service is useless if I myself am being ignored. This is exactly what we changed in the four years that we walked the straight path. It is true: The time when your welfare is set aside is over. Our administration continues to strive to push for meaningful reforms that will further strengthen your ranks.

Proof of this is the closure of the firearms shortage last year. Now we have a 1:1 police to pistol ratio. We also recognize the value of science and systematic study in suppressing crime in the country. That's why we are focused on upgrading our science and forensic gathering capability. An example of this is our new Integrated Ballistics Identification System (IBIS) for recording information on all firearms in the country, and the new Automated Fingerprint Identification System (AFIS) used to quickly compare and identify fingerprints in a crime. We also implemented the principle of “work smarter” in operations—because the more comprehensive data we collect, the more strategic we are in determining where to deploy more police. With the implementation of the Crime Incident Recording System since 2013, our police are now more organized in recording various crimes. This has addressed the previously fragmented data in the PNP's record system.

With the transformation we are experiencing, the government's ability to take care of your welfare and that of your families is increasing. For example, in the housing project, 98 percent of the target of 31,200 homes for our Phase II of our AFP/PNP Housing Program has been completed; equivalent to 30,558 homes built. 14,040 units of this are allocated for our police, and now the final list of awardees in this program is being submitted to the NHA.

We are also preparing to hire an initial 7,439 from our target of 30,000 non-uniformed personnel for administrative work. This way, the police officers they replace can patrol the communities and truly focus on safeguarding the safety of our Bosses.

It is clear to us that the number of police officers should be increased because the population of our country is growing. But before we can do this, we must first address the problem of providing pensions to our uniformed personnel that we inherited. What we found: There is no pension system for you, so year after year, funds from our national budget must be allocated for pensions. In addition, the pension of retirees is indexed to the salary of active soldiers and police officers. We were told that more than four trillion pesos would be needed to fix the pension system—that’s the seed capital. This means: even if we give the entire proposed 2.6 trillion budget for 2015, it still wouldn’t be enough funds for just one program—that pension system. That’s how big the problem is that was left to us. But we are determined; we are already making ways to address this.

In addition to finding ways to increase your numbers, we also plan to purchase the necessary equipment such as radios and patrol vehicles. Don't worry that our plan for “shoot, scoot, and communicate” will not be fulfilled, due to the decision on the DAP. We continue to take steps to ensure that your modernization continues.

We are really maximizing our opportunity to provide benefits to our police force. For example, in our proposed budget for next year, we allocated two billion pesos for your Capability Enhancement Program; and 100 million pesos for the construction of 18 police stations across the country.

This is what good governance means: the needs of our uniformed personnel are being addressed. Law enforcers are being cared for and reciprocated with the care of the government and the state. With the continued strengthening of your ranks, we are confident that you are in good hands; we thank Sec. Mar Roxas and Police Chief Alan Purisima for their truly reliable leadership of the PNP. We salute and thank you also for your exceptional service.

There are examples, such as the Zamboanga City Public Safety Company, the first group to deploy to Zamboanga City to defend the city after it was attacked by lawless elements last September. We are also with Police Superintendent Lambert Suerte for the Benguet Provincial Public Safety Company. Because of their courage and skill, we seized the largest NPA camp in the Cordillera, and arrested some of its leaders in 2013. Also here is the Criminal Investigation and Detection Group led by Police Director Benjamin Magalong, which arrested 3,453 fugitives from the law—including 22 of the most wanted in the country—last year. SPO1 Dominador Canlas also distinguished himself by rescuing three children during an operation against the NPA last year in Tarlac.

They are just some of our brave police officers that we recognize today. They reflect the concern and professionalism of our other police officers who have truly excelled in service. Just this SONA, we paid tribute to four rookie policewomen who did not hesitate to fulfill their duty when the Martilyo Gang attacked the SM Mall of Asia. There is also SPO3 Erlinda Gagaoin of Tubao in La Union; when their police mobile patrol could not pass through the field to bring home an elderly woman, she chose to carry the grandmother and take her home. There is no doubt: Filipinos no longer need to watch movies to see a police officer to idolize. Through the good examples of our awardees and other exemplary police officers, you demonstrate what is expected of those who wear the uniform and carry the badge.

It is true: We have come a long way since we embarked on the straight and narrow path. But despite all our achievements, we are aware that challenges to our security remain. As law enforcers, your responsibility is clear: to defend our citizens and to make criminals feel that they cannot escape justice; that once we gather sufficient evidence, once we catch them in the act, they will feel the strength of the state.

As servants of the Filipino people, when we return home, when we look in the mirror, we must ask ourselves: Did I do what I should have done today? If our answer is yes, let us challenge ourselves further, and say: Tomorrow, I will surpass what I have done. In this way, we can ensure the arrival of a time when children can play safely in the streets; those returning home from the office have peace of mind; and law enforcers are confident in their ability to defend not only themselves and their loved ones, but all Filipinos who trust them.

Thank you very much and happy anniversary to all of you.
