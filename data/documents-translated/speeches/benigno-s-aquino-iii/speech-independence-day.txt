Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, during the celebration of Independence Day

[Delivered in Sta. Barbara, Iloilo, on June 12, 2015]

In our history, Independence Day is often celebrated in Manila. This is because many events related to our achievement of freedom took place in Manila and its surrounding areas. As President, we go around the country, because we know that the freedom we enjoy today is the result of the actions and struggles of many Filipinos, wherever they may be in the Philippines. In 2011, we went to Kawit, Cavite; in 2012, to the Church of Barasoain in Malolos, Bulacan; in 2013, to Liwasang Bonifacio in Manila, and last year, we went to Naga, Camarines Sur. Now, we gather here in Sta. Barbara, Iloilo to recognize the contribution of the Visayas in the fight for freedom. Next year, we plan to go to a province in Mindanao, to look up at the flag and remember the heroism of our ancestors.

Let us remember: As an archipelago, the islands of the Philippines are separated. Back then, transportation was limited, making it difficult to travel even to neighboring towns; there was also no technology to speed up communication. This is said to be one of the reasons why many uprisings did not happen simultaneously in the early stages of our revolution. However, in 1898, despite these limitations, Filipinos united and revolted simultaneously throughout the Philippines. This proves that even then, Filipinos clearly knew how to distinguish right from wrong; and their response to this was to stand on the side of justice.

For more than 300 years of Spanish colonization, a system existed that caused great suffering to our people. There was, for example, forced labor or *polo y servicio*; the prohibition of Filipino priests from leading their own parishes; the brutality of the civil guards, arbitrary arrests; and the torture of prisoners, as well as widespread discrimination against Filipinos.

These issues are what the propagandists like Jose Rizal, Marcelo H. del Pilar, and your fellow Ilonggo, Graciano Lopez Jaena, fought to reform. But the government still did not listen to them. Perhaps our ancestors asked themselves: Is it excessive to dream and ask for justice and a life with dignity? If they did not fight, how many more generations would suffer? After all, who else could the Filipino expect, but his fellow Filipino?

As a result, the final stage of our revolution erupted. In 1896, Andres Bonifacio led in Manila, and Emilio Aguinaldo in Cavite. In the Visayas, Generals Francisco del Castillo of Aklan, Esteban Contreras of Capiz, Leon Kilat of Cebu, and Martin Delgado and Teresa Magbanua of Iloilo led the fight in 1897 and 1898. In truth, right here in Santa Barbara, the Philippine flag was first raised outside of Luzon on November 17, 1898. Embroidered on that flag were three stars: symbols of Luzon, Mindanao, and your island of Panay representing the entire Visayas.

When the First Republic was established, representatives from the Visayas readily joined. Perhaps, this was their recognition of the principle laid down by Apolinario Mabini in upholding our republic, and as we remember, he said, “We must face foreigners as a united nation, because this is the strongest shield against the abuse of the powerful.”

One hundred and seventeen years have passed since independence was declared; we are now free from the chains of foreign conquerors. Despite this, there is a new challenge to fight corruption and poverty in the country. It is clear: Through unity, our heroes prevailed in achieving freedom, so through unity we will also overcome the problems we face today.

We began to traverse the Straight Path to restore a government that truly serves and represents the aspirations of the people. Our vow: No one will be left behind in development, whether in Luzon, Visayas or Mindanao; every Filipino will benefit from the progress we are enjoying. It is not possible for only those at the top to rise, while those at the bottom are left behind.

How do we fulfill this? It's simple: We expand opportunities, and we empower the majority of our countrymen to maximize the benefits it brings. For example, the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program. A cash grant is given to our countrymen, in exchange for conditions that will ensure that their hardship is not inherited by the next generation. These conditions include ensuring that their children attend school. Nationwide, there are now more than 4.4 million households benefiting from Pantawid Pamilya; 904,725 of them are in the Visayas, and 94,190 are here in Iloilo.

Another example: We all know how much help a proper irrigation system is to farmers. Here with you, the Jalaur River Multi-purpose Project is underway. This is a large project that was first conceived the year I was born. It was promised for a very long time, but we, on the Straight and Narrow Path, are now implementing it. We cannot help but think: If this had been addressed before, how many thousands of farmers should have already progressed?

You have probably also heard about the initiative we launched in your neighboring provinces of Negros Oriental and Negros Occidental: Recently, we signed an Executive Order creating the Negros Island Region, which unites the aforementioned provinces. Think about it: those two provinces on one island, in different regions. It's like, one island, the same problems, the same opportunities arriving, yet it will be divided? With the creation of the Negros Island Region, the government will be more focused on the allocation of funds in one region. They will also benefit more directly and quickly from these projects: People from Negros will no longer need to cross the sea to avail of the services allocated for them. What's even better is that the neighboring regions, which have long shared provinces far from them, will also receive benefits from the government more quickly, because services will be more focused on their citizens.

All stakeholders agreed that this initiative would benefit more people. We listened to the voice of our Bosses, our countrymen in those provinces—expressed by their leaders from the barangay chiefs up to their governors, representatives—and from the business communities. So when Secretary Mar Roxas approached me with this proposal, and the necessary consultations were conducted, we immediately signed it. As you know, Secretary Mar, a true son of Panay Island, led the promotion of this. As I mentioned earlier, we know that when we task a Mar Roxas to lead a project or program, you can expect it to succeed. [Applause]

I am confident: As long as we continue to walk the Straight Path, we can continue the change we are currently enjoying. With the rise of modern technology and in the age of social media, we have greater capacity, and a deeper responsibility to unite in seeking solutions to our problems. Instead of being a burden to our fellow citizens, may we all do our part, contribute what we can to the best of our ability, so that together we can uplift our beloved country.

When we visited Japan this June, many expressed their admiration for the reforms we have implemented in the past five years. But they repeatedly asked the same question. The question was, “How can we be sure this will continue?” My response: It is up to the people; the people will continue the change that they themselves have made. They know what is right, and what is wrong. I am confident that they will once again choose the right leaders, especially since they see the results of our good governance.

We have proven this in EDSA 1986, and we have done it again in 2010 when the people united to end the corrupt practices. As our term nears its end, I recall the question of many during Martial Law to those who feared fighting the dictatorship: “If not now, when? If not you, who?”

I am fully confident: Our Bosses will continue what we have started so well. We know that if you give the Filipino the right opportunity, he will show excellence in all fields. The call to us now is: Remain united to promote the welfare of others, especially those most in need. May we use the past as our guide, live out the lessons we have learned in the present, so that we can reach the destination we aspire to.

Good day to you all. Thank you very much.
