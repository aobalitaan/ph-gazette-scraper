Statement of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, following his attendance at the Seventh Bali Democracy Forum

Good evening to all of you.

We have returned from a short but meaningful stay in Indonesia. We thank our friend, President Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono, and the organizers of the Bali Democracy Forum for their warm welcome to our delegation.

As co-chairs of the discussion, we took the opportunity to share our experiences in advancing our collective goals. We explained how fragile the power of a regime is when its foundation is violence and intimidation, rather than trust and the support of the people. We emphasized: the process of consensus building is complex and lengthy, but it is what will bring about genuine and widespread transformation. Our position is clear: instead of creating only short-term solutions, we ensure that our problems will not be inherited by future generations.

They likely prepared criticisms even before we left yesterday, asking or having asked, “Why does the President need to travel so much when there are many things to attend to domestically?” Our answer to them is: Filipinos do not live on another planet. We are surrounded by countries with close ties to our past, present, and future. In the international community, one’s problem is also the problem of others; one’s success is the success of all. Part of fulfilling our aspirations is sowing the seeds of trust and cooperation with our neighboring countries.

A strong example of this is our long-standing friendship with Indonesia. In times of disaster, we support each other. When Mt. Gunung Agung in Bali erupted in 1963, the Philippines was quick to send seven C-47 aircraft, carrying paramedics and aid for those affected. When we were struck by Typhoon Yolanda, President Yudhoyono immediately expressed his condolences, along with the provision of nearly two million dollars in total assistance for our people. Indonesia is also with us in promoting peace in Mindanao, in asserting a just resolution to the territorial issue, and even in upholding security against terrorism and for the welfare of our citizens in the face of global climate change.

Therefore, this type of relationship truly needs to be nurtured. I would just like to emphasize: This meaningful and close relationship between countries does not sprout like mushrooms. Understanding and mutual support must be cultivated, and the seeds of trust between the two must be carefully nurtured.

A mix of sadness and joy is what the end of President Yudhoyono’s term brings us. On one hand, we are saddened because a leader who stood with us through the challenges we faced is stepping down. On the other hand, we are happy because we were given the opportunity to know a president and big brother who serves as an inspiration, not only to the people of Indonesia but also to the member countries of ASEAN. The honor he bestowed upon us, the “Bintang Republik Indonesia Adipurna,” is a symbol of the strong relationship between the Philippines and Indonesia, and of the interconnected efforts of our people in reaching our dreams.

Our attendance at the Bali Democracy Forum is proof of the Filipino people’s readiness to cooperate to achieve a more stable and prosperous region and community of nations. We will continue to nurture and care for the good relationships we have sown so that the next generation may reap a Philippines that is a partner in progress not only in ASEAN but in various parts of the world; a zealous advocate of peace; and a model of a strong democracy.

Good evening. Thank you very much to all of you.
