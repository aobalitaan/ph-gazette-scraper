Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, upon his return from a visit to the United States and Canada.

We are pleased to be back in the Philippines, from our successful visit to the United States and Canada. As you know, these two countries are very close to us. That is why we made the most of every moment of our trip, to further strengthen relations and cooperation with them, and to deliver more opportunities for our Filipino compatriots.

We are grateful, because in every meeting we attended in the United States and Canada, we received a very warm welcome. Upon arriving in Canada, we met with Governor-General David Johnston and Prime Minister Stephen Harper. We personally thanked them for the immediate assistance they sent after Typhoon Yolanda, and for their support in promoting our peace process for Mindanao, and for designating us as a Priority Emerging Market and Country of Focus of their country. Because of these designations, we expect more investors from Canada to come to the Philippines. I would just like to emphasize: Canada is one of the largest providers of official development assistance to the country. In 2014, their aid reached 111.95 [million] U.S. dollars. There are still 47 ongoing grants from their country, and 28 of these are allocated for typhoon-related assistance. In our discussion with the Canadian government, they further affirmed their position to continue supporting the Philippines through ODA.

Together with Prime Minister Harper, we discussed steps to further strengthen the small and medium enterprises (SMEs) of our two countries. There is an offer from the Canadian Trade and Development Facility, to more effectively allow our small and medium enterprises SME’s to enter the thriving Canadian market. As the host economy of APEC, this will help our priority of promoting more vibrant SME relations in the Asia-Pacific region.

In addition to growing commerce and the economy, Canada is also our partner in strengthening security and combating crime and terrorism. Prime Minister Harper said: His government is ready to contribute to training in the field of security, particularly in our port security.

We also signed various agreements in Canada. First of all, the Philippines-Canada Mutual Accountability Framework, which promotes transparent and honest relations and cooperation between our countries. Their indicative budget for the initiatives covered by this agreement is 82 million Canadian dollars. We also signed a Memorandum of Understanding for Occupational Safety and Health, and another memorandum of understanding for human resource deployment and development in the province of British Columbia. The idea behind these agreements is simple: to protect the welfare and interests of our compatriots who are working and interested in working in Canada.

We are also looking forward to the start of exploratory discussions on the possibility of a free trade agreement with Canada. Once such an agreement is implemented, it is certain: our trade will develop further, and it will create jobs and opportunities for Filipinos.

In business meetings, we almost ran out of time with the praise and recognition we received from all sides regarding our improving economy. We, in turn, enthusiastically presented the various opportunities in the country to the businessmen, particularly in our Public-Private Partnerships sector and financial services. In Chicago, we met with businessmen who are members of the U.S. Chamber of Commerce, U.S.-ASEAN Business Council, and National Center for APEC; and some of them expressed a desire to expand operations in our country. In Toronto, we interviewed their top banking and insurance companies, who are members of the Asia-Pacific Foundation of Canada. These include Sun Life Financial and Manulife Financial Corporation, both of which have long been investing in the country. In our meeting with them, along with the desire to expand these companies in our country, the possibility of establishing catastrophe and agricultural insurance was mentioned. We will study this, because of the great benefit of this type of service during disasters. But this is just proof: We are really convincing well-known companies to invest further in our market.

There's a saying: Save the best for last. During the last business meeting in Vancouver, we spoke with the head of a large business. We won't name the company yet, for fear of jinxing their plans. The important thing is: Currently, the company has a $20 million or 1.3 billion peso investment in the country; this is for their waste-to-energy facility, which is expected to be completed this year. Once completed, it is estimated that the government will collect a total revenue of 8.5 billion pesos from this facility over 30 years.

The good news is: According to their leader and officials, they are also interested in entering the energy, tourism, and agriculture sectors. Even I was surprised: they are considering not just corn, but also planting blueberries and cranberries in the country. Let me clarify: This is not wishful thinking; they are really planning to do this within a year and a half. I even asked them if we are already planting strawberries in provinces like Bukidnon, Quezon, and Baguio, would it also be possible to plant these blueberries and cranberries there? It would be good to study this further, but they said the requirements would be the same.

The government of British Columbia also offered to share their knowledge of sea planes. Of course, we are open to this, especially since we have 7,107 islands, and it would be easier for our tourists to travel if they could be landed by plane on the beach.

Truly, the world's perception is changing, not only of the Philippines, but also of our compatriots living abroad. I remember what leaders like Mayor Rahm Emmanuel of Chicago, and Premier Christy Clark of British Columbia told us. They seem to run out of praise for Filipinos. And in various communities, Filipinos are reportedly shining; their presence and strength are truly felt due to their contribution to the community and local economy.

That's why we are so grateful to our compatriots abroad, who in various fields are truly raising our flag. This is one of the reasons for our visit and check-in with Filipino communities in Chicago, Toronto, and Vancouver. If you were there at each venue, you would feel the vibrancy and energy of our compatriots. Even far from the country, they are aware of the changes we are all enjoying together. At the same time, they expressed their support and willingness to contribute further to our reform agenda.

Despite this, we also told our Bosses: We are not yet content with what we have achieved. Your government continues to work hard. We continue to expand opportunities for progress in our country, as well as prepare for the future, so that leaving the country will no longer be a necessity for anyone.

Even while abroad, we are closely monitoring events in the Philippines. For example, the entry of Typhoon Dodong into the Philippine Area of Responsibility, and even the eruption of Mt. Bulusan. Although we know that our designated agencies are taking care of our compatriots, we will not be complacent. Before we can rest today, we are requesting direct updates, and monitoring the situation.

We consider this trip an investment in the future of the Filipino. Along with bringing home the positive results we have reaped today, we have also sown seeds that the people will surely harvest in the coming times.

We can now say: During our five days away from the country, we spent more than 37 hours in the air and an estimated 83 hours on land, no matter how many time zones we had to cross—the fatigue, lack of sleep, and bumpy ride associated with this trip outside the country were truly worth it.

Even though we landed in our homeland in the early hours of the morning, here is your government, wide awake and very much alive, saying: Boss, wherever you are in the world, we and the entire world truly see: The Filipinos are here, with heads held high, and ready to seize every opportunity to achieve the Filipino dream: A just, progressive, and admired Philippines.

Thank you very much. Good day to all of you.
