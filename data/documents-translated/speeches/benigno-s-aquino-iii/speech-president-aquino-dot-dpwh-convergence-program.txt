Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, at the briefing of the DOT-DPWH Convergence Program.

Because of you, I was able to get closer to the beach. [Laughter] The last time I was here in Samal, I didn't see a beach. Now I've seen it and, at least, about twenty steps more and I'll be on the beach. But, I probably won't be able to now, I'll come back next year, around July 5th. [Applause]

When I started as a congressman in 1998, that was right after the height of what was called the “Asian Financial Crisis.” As a congressman, we wanted to bring projects to our district. When I visited the house of one of our barangay captains, there was a listing there for a “road.” That “road,” when I got to the barangay captain’s house, was a piece of cement, exactly the size of the barangay’s vehicle. It turned out the “road” was a parking lot for the vehicle. And it wasn’t two… Isn’t it five meters… This is only half, really five meters and it really only fits one vehicle.

Now, why did I tell that story? Because look at this, in tourism projects and DPWH, they both have what is called TRIP, the Tourism Road Infrastructure Program. Notice the numbers, 463 tourism roads, 15 regions—I suppose the National Capital Region doesn’t need tourism roads, it already has many roads, Metro Manila is different—75 provinces. That made me think: there are 81 provinces in the Philippines. I thought maybe Tarlac is one of the six lagging behind. [Laughter] That’s why I need to come back here to Davao del Norte. I’m sure they benefited here. They are included in the 75. But Secretary Jimenez said that it wasn’t supposed to be provinces: 75 clusters. So groups. So we probably have one in Tarlac, so I’ll go back next week, I’ll look for it if it’s there. If not, I still have time to beg them. [Laughter]

In Filipino, the translation or meaning of the word “convergence” is “pagtatagpo” (coming together). And it is true: Our Department of Tourism and Department of Public Works and Highways Program (DOT-DPWH) Convergence Program brings together a single objective—to further boost tourism in our country, and to create more jobs to ensure the progress of more Filipinos.

I often mention: The infrastructure and tourism sectors are called “low-lying fruits.” Like fruits, they are easy to pick because they are low-lying and can be immediately utilized—not tomorrow or in the distant future, but now. How did we say this? In infrastructure alone, many are already employed in construction. Of course, with the construction of roads and bridges in selected and strategic locations, it will be easier for tourists to reach our tourism sites. Please also note that as part of my job [is] sales manager and salesman with the number of sites we will visit. I haven't met the whale shark yet. It's coming soon.

If we can make the travel of vacationers more comfortable, they will naturally be happy, more encouraged to visit and return to our destinations. And most importantly, when you talk to our countrymen, “Come to the Philippines,” they will be further encouraged, right? This is a big contribution to the growth of our local and national economy. It's not just that: For every tourist who arrives in the Philippines, it is estimated that one job is created for our Filipinos. There is also what is called a multiplier effect. When a tourist vacations, they stay in a hotel, eat in a restaurant, ride a taxi, hire a tour guide, right? The result: Increased income and livelihood for our countrymen.

Now, because of the benefits of tourism and infrastructure, not only in economic growth, but also in the progress of more Filipinos, we are really focusing on this, and allocating sufficient funds. Let's look at the data. From 2006 to 2010, the total funds allocated by our predecessor for the Tourism Road Infrastructure of the country was P13.79 billion. Under us, from 2011 to 2015—we are still waiting for congressional approval for 2016, that's just a part of it—it has reached P60.48 billion. [Applause] In total, 463 tourism roads have been built, are being built, and are queued for construction throughout the country. The good news is: We have proposed a P24 billion budget for the DPWH for 2016 to complete other tourism roads that we are currently building. That P24 billion is not the total budget of the DPW. It is only for tourism-related projects. [Applause] The calm sea is really distracting. [Laughter]

You know, as President, we are invited to so many places with very beautiful scenery, right? You’re given ten seconds, “This is famous in our area!” You look this way, then that way. Sometimes my neck even gets strained from turning because that’s what those ten seconds are for: the vehicle, airplane, helicopter, car is moving. But when I return here, I won’t be doing that anymore because I’m already settled. [Applause] I remember, the other day, I was talking to someone. “When was the last time you went on vacation?” I said, “Before, during Christmas, Holy Week, right? Like most people. I was in the private sector, with a two-week guarantee.” When was the last time we went on vacation? Just last year, during Holy Week, I remember we were battling the MERS-Corona Virus. But we can do this, just 10 months more. [Laughter]

Let's examine its effect on the tourism sector. We are 5 years ahead of schedule on our initial target of 35.5 million domestic travelers under our term. That means there will be 35.5 million tourists. The painful thing—painful in a good way, painful for Mon Jimenez, good for us—the target of six years, we reached 37.5 million travelers as early as 2011. It’s as if we surpassed his target in just one year. Of course, we don’t want Mon Jimenez to do nothing, so we changed the target and increased it a little. From 30, we will make it 51.7 million travelers. [Applause] Not in 2016, it needs to be in 2015. [Laughter] And there are still a few months left in 2015. I heard that we have already surpassed it and reached 54.6 million back in 2014. [Applause] So, 30 became 50; should 50 then become 80? [Laughter] Not necessarily, just 10 months left. Maybe 70 is achievable. It also depends on you, on your help in attracting tourists, making them happy and not afraid.

Regarding international tourist arrivals, we also raised the average annual rate of growth to 8.3% from 2010 to 2014. This is higher than the recorded 6.7% from 2001 to 2009.

Let's look at the data here in Davao del Norte. From 2011 to 2015, we have allocated more than P9 billion in funds for your roads and bridges, farm-to-market roads, ports, irrigation projects, and flood control. And you know, [Manong Dolpo], a colleague of my mother, I might get reported to my mother if he mentions anything. [Laughter] We will prioritize studying that, if it can be done. As for [Anton], he almost went house-to-house during my senatorial campaign. So when he seconds a motion, we really listen more attentively, and especially to all of you.

I am certain that you, the people of Davao, are seeing and experiencing the progress in your province. Just a while ago, we had an inspection of some parts of the Igacos Circumferential Road Project. You know, it’s not that… well, I hope you won’t be offended, but it’s close to “igapos” in Tagalog [Igacos]. [Laughter] Maybe we should change it a bit. In government, one thing I always worry about is the acronym. Every first letter is grouped together to create a word. Like… PIA has so many, Philippine Information Agency. There’s also another PIA. PSF, President’s Social Fund, which we call Price Stabilization Fund, there are so many. And when we talk and it’s all acronyms, I say, “Wait a moment. We’re simplifying with acronyms, but you’re making it harder, maybe we’re talking about a different topic here.” Everyone has their own acronyms, but that’s different. Sorry about that. I feel too much at home here. [Applause]

Honestly, while looking at this sea, I’m thinking if I brought my swimming trunks. [Laughter] That distracts us from what we’re saying. I need to hurry a little. I will be talking to the children and then [the young] next. I have a school to visit, and then the young once. Once, O-N-C-E. [Laughter] Let’s not make the young wait too long. The young once can probably wait a little longer.

We passed through your Island Garden City of Samal Circumferential Road, where we widened and cemented almost 27 kilometers of it. They say over 80% of this project is complete; the target completion is January–2016, Babes, to be clear–January, 2016. [Applause] Maybe that means December 2015. If it’s December, Babes, or November, we won’t be offended if you don’t fulfill the agreement for January. It’s okay with us if it’s earlier. When this opens, travel will be easier for those who want to go to Pearl Farm Beach Resort for water sports, snorkeling, or scuba diving–I can only do scuba diving. They can also go to the Tridacna Sanctuary to see your extraordinary Giant Clams; and of course, to the Samal Botanical Garden, where you can witness the stunning beauty of nature. You know, regarding nature, on the other side of Samal, they say 90 kilometers away from the circumferential road, there’s a group that wants to preserve nature as is, including the road. “Don’t build a road here, it might pose a threat to nature, which they call sustainable tourism.” The bottom line is, you are the boss, you will decide. If you say do it, or don’t do it, we just listen and are your humble servants. Surely, when these roads are fully utilized, many more will be convinced that “It’s more fun in the Philippines,” especially here in Davao. [Applause]

Clearly: Our DOT-DPWH Convergence Program is concrete proof of the transformation brought about by the Straight Path. We focus on and develop the sectors that we know are beneficial to the people. Connected to this, when our Bosses are given honest service, they themselves will volunteer to help keep our destinations neat and beautiful. This is a virtuous cycle: The fellow citizens we have uplifted from the margins of society are also contributing to the growth of our national economy, and to the progress of society as a whole.

My Bosses, in just over five years, we have accomplished so much, including the construction and ongoing construction of infrastructure left and right. And to be honest, as we go around the country, we often hear from our countrymen: “Mr. President, thank you very much; we have been waiting for these roads and bridges for a long time.” Thank you for the Dalican-Batad Road in Banaue, Ifugao; thank you for the Taytay-El Nido Road in Palawan; thank you for the Daanbantayan Road in Cebu; thank you also for the Panglao Island Circumferential Road in Bohol. You know, to be honest, we could take credit for all of this; I could just boast about my own accomplishments. But that’s not our way; we know that every success on the Straight Path is made by you, my Bosses, the Filipino people. [Applause] I was even thinking to myself: Isn’t this really what the government should be doing? Isn’t the main purpose of the government really to improve the lives of Filipinos?

My Bosses, since we embarked on the Straight Path, we have proven the power of unity and cooperation. We know that when the collective aspirations of Filipinos come together and are moved in one direction, we can certainly succeed in promoting positive transformation. My Bosses, we have started well, [applause] let us remember the positive transformation we have experienced; let us continue and advance the Straight Path. [Laughter and applause]

It’s like your road, only 80 percent complete. [Laughter] But to be honest, let’s look at the overall strategy. We won’t build a road that turns out to be a parking lot for Barangay Captain, then we rush over for a photo opportunity, then I’ll put up a sign saying, “You see, I love you.” No. It’s important here to complete it. You, what is your potential? Tourism, it’s big. How do we increase the number of tourists? Of course, we make their travel easier. And when they get here, it’s up to you to take care of them. Of course, if they like you, they’ll keep coming back. If they don’t like you, of course we all fail. Now, we need the skills that are being called for to participate in the growth of our economy. Here, are you left to study to gain those skills? No. The DepEd budget has doubled since we started. We have allocated a large amount to TESDA, as well as to CHED. In 4P’s last year, we helped over 300,000 fourth year high school students. As I told you in my SONA, more than 13,000 of the 300,000 [were] honor students. They, I think, are now in various colleges being helped by our CHED funds.

In other words, earlier, it was repeated, someone told me: This project, we’ve been waiting for it for ten years; in other places, waiting for twenty years, thirty years, fifty years. One project we visited in Iloilo, the Jalaur Multi-Purpose, they thought of it the year I was born. Well, I’m not that old yet. [Laughter] But I’m not that young either. The bridge there in Isabela, Lullutan Bridge, was damaged in 1995, we completed it in 2015. Every time we hear about decades of needs that are not being met, what’s the difference between us and why we can do what others should have done? Because, of course, decades of hardship, suffering, waiting, could have been alleviated. Now, next year we have another election, and my contract is up… To be clear, June 30 before 12 noon, the new President needs to take an oath. That means I’m retired before then.

Now, my only point here is: Something happened, nothing happened. Referendum next year; where are we headed? Will we move forward or will we return to the past? Or a version of the past? You made this, you will continue this. That's why I always rely on the people, just like my parents. I believe that the decision you will make is correct so that what we leave to those who come after us will be far better than what we received. It is in your hands.

Good afternoon. Thank you very much to all of you.
