Comrades, many of you, I think, have already watched the interviews being conducted with us in recent weeks. They call it an “exit interview.” And in those interviews, we look back on what we have done, or failed to do, in the past six years.

I will give you two questions that briefly crossed my mind. There is a survey firm. Their question in their survey was: “Are you satisfied with what the government is doing, in relation to the increase in your salary?”

I asked the surveyor. I said, “Can we go out there to Mendiola?” I was talking to someone from Malacañang. “Let's find ten people—you choose those ten people—who look like they are working; not students, because of course students don't have salaries.” I said to them: “You choose ten, and I bet you that out of the ten you ask, eleven will say they are not satisfied with their salary. So, if we can't find anyone satisfied with their salary, how can the government have hope that it is doing enough to increase my salary?” This is the problem, as President. The doctor nodded—he has a doctorate in statistics: “It seems right, doesn't it? Our question seems vague.”

Let me give you a question. This one comes from the media. They keep asking this: “When you ran, you said ‘If there is no corruption, there will be no poverty.’ Why, as your term is ending, are there still poor people?” I asked them immediately; I immediately asked them, “Can you tell me the formula, where today you are poor, tomorrow you wake up rich?” Of course, we exclude becoming a thief. We exclude becoming a drug lord, because we can't suggest that “Tomorrow, become a drug lord to get rich.” That's not possible, right? I said it's unlikely that you also need to increase your bet on the lotto, to be one of the millions hoping to win. So, I said to them, let's think about what we have done in the past six years.

And this is what I said to them: Think about it, if you are a person with no skills, the job you will take is very ordinary. Maybe a helper, maybe a street cleaner. Many of you with no skills are chasing after those few jobs, of course the salary is low. That low salary, you can't support your family. You live hand to mouth, they say. If you get sick, your situation will only worsen. So, I said, what have we done?

The government cannot simply decree that today you are poor, tomorrow you are rich. But the government can help you to maximize all the opportunities that are already there for our countrymen to take advantage of. And that is what we tried to do.

You know, even though the election is over, many people are saying they care and will help solve poverty. It's easy to say that. But let's just ask: What have they done or what do they plan to do?

We believe that everything important requires hard work. It needs effort. As I have mentioned many times: the people are the country's primary wealth. It is the government's job to maximize all opportunities for our Bosses. And you can see the efforts of our administration to improve the situation of our citizens.

So, let me go back to the question: What have we done?

During our term, the government fulfilled its true mandate: to serve and empower the people. We focused not only on a single sector or a single problem, but on various needs and those who need them. We addressed the inherited backlogs and reformed our basic education. We expanded the benefits and coverage of PhilHealth. Now, if you are sick, the state is there to help. Through Pantawid Pamilya, we provided cash grants to monitor the health and education of your children. We engaged with industry and found out what they need in the market. What we want is for the qualifications of our students to be relevant upon graduation, and for them to get real jobs, instead of just temporary ones.

Let's move on to TESDA. It is clear: it is one of the most striking examples of bringing about positive change, not only in their agency, but also in the lives of many Filipinos.

With us now is one of those we often use as an example, Christina Reyes, who looks very different now. She used to be an OFW. She was forced to return to the Philippines due to abuse by her employer. Instead of feeling joy upon returning, she faced great anxiety and worry. Due to job failure, she experienced depression and hopelessness; she didn't know how to address her problems, especially in the financial aspect. She even reached a point where she thought about ending it all. But she didn't give up completely. When she heard about TESDA's program, she enrolled, trained, and took a wellness massage course. After graduating, she was employed by a spa; she was made operations manager due to her skills; and then, she established her own spa. Now, four years after training, she has five branches of her spa, with at least 32 employees. And in addition to that, as Joel mentioned earlier, she has three franchisees of her spa in our area in Tarlac—in Gerona, Santa Ignacia, and Camiling. Christina is now earning a lot. More than me, Joel says, every month. But that's just life. I won't say how much. But Joel often tells us, “Boss, after this, come to us at TESDA, and your life will improve.” [Laughter and applause]

Regarding the Sari-sari Store Training and Access to Resources Program or STAR program of TESDA and Coke, beneficiaries are trained in bookkeeping, inventory management, accounting, and other knowledge to professionalize their operation of their sari-sari stores. Even how to ensure that their additional income is not wasted is taught in this program. Currently, beneficiaries of the program, previously reported to me as 34,000, has now reversed: 43,000 sari-sari store owners have completed the training. There is one, whose previous income was 800 pesos per day; with the help of the STAR program, his income is now 4,000 pesos per day. And Joel will say again: “Boss, you're almost catching up there.” That's just life, brothers and sisters.

Aside from them, we have also shared the story of a person with disability, Mark Escora. He used to be a barker, now he is an escalation supervisor in a BPO. As an escalation supervisor, he monitors difficult calls in the call center, and oversees the agents. This July, Mark completed his training in preparation for promotion as an escalation manager.

In the Special Training for Employment Program, in addition to providing free training and allowance, TESDA also provided starter toolkits. I remember Joel once told us, he spoke with Art Yap, a member of the government we replaced. He asked Joel, how does TESDA manage to provide quality tools? Joel's simple answer: “We're the ones running it.”

These are just some of the successes achieved by TESDA in the past six years. Currently, TESDA's Technical Vocational Education and Training (TVET) is able to graduate more than 10 million course graduates. The good news is: The placement rate in the Training for Work Scholarship Program is now at 72 percent. I just noticed earlier, 72 percent was in 2014. We are now in 2016; I know you are doing your impact assessment: I hope to read a higher than 72 percent in 2015. This is a long way from the previous placement rate of TESDA scholars, which was only 28.5 percent.

This is what we mean by maximizing the opportunities opening up in our country. The good thing is, we have the capacity to sustain and continue these programs. For example, with our TESDA scholars who have been employed, because they now have a salary, they are able to pay taxes. And if you add it all up, the state's investment in them may be recovered in the first year of their livelihood, and later on, it will even be more than enough. All the taxes paid by our scholar until they retire will be the ones to open opportunities for more of our countrymen. This is the cycle of long-term benefits.

Proof of this is TESDA and our beneficiaries: The Filipino, when given an opportunity, will truly make the most of it, not only to improve themselves and their families, but also the nation.

One of our partners, and who has believed in this capability of our Bosses even before, is none other than Senator-elect Joel Villanueva. This Joel, was not content with “Bahala na,” or just saying that something has been done. He diligently maximizes every opportunity and scrutinizes the details to serve better. It is not surprising that during his term, he reformed TESDA, and saved it from the image of corruption and sluggish service. In fact, last year, TESDA was awarded the Nationwide ISO 9001:2008 Certification, which symbolizes the agency's quality and reliable work. We are confident that in Joel's new position, he will work overtime in the Senate to create more jobs and opportunities for many Filipinos.

It is clear: All the successes we have achieved are just part of our extensive effort to fulfill our promise to leave our country in a better situation than we found it.

On the Straight Path, we do what is right and reasonable, maximize the opportunity to serve, and focus service on the welfare of the majority. Along with economic growth, we are improving infrastructure, education, health, and other social services, to uplift our Bosses.

My appeal: Now that we are enjoying change, let us continue to monitor and contribute to make it permanent. Let us further strengthen our mandate to deliver more immediate benefits to our people.

Until the end, all I can say is: It is a great honor for me to lead with you, and to serve the Filipino people with all my heart and integrity.

And in conclusion, I will take back Joel’s joke. I remembered a story he told me one day.

I always boast about Filipinos, they are truly courageous. An example of this is their study of other languages. We all know we have Japanese friends. The Japanese, if it’s not perfect, they won’t do it, right? When they speak, and their English isn’t perfect, they’ll just switch to Japanese; they don’t want to be embarrassed. I said, it’s the opposite in the Philippines. A Filipino, knowing only a little, feels sometimes that they can already teach English.

So, coincidentally, this courage was demonstrated. There’s a Filipino who will have a job in America. The problem is, he doesn’t know how to speak English. So he approached his friend who has been in America for a long time. He said: “Friend, help me.”

He said, “What’s your problem?”

“But how will I even eat there, I don’t know how to speak English?”

“Ah, that’s easy. You go to a small restaurant, look for these letters: D-I-N-E-R. Diner. Every place in America has that, and the food is cheap there.”

“Oh, is that how it is? Okay.”

“Now, if you get served quickly, sit at the counter; it’s like a bar. Sit there. Then when someone approaches you and asks you what you want; you’ll say: ‘What can I get you?’ That’s easy. You just answer with ‘Coffee and donuts please.’”

“But what is that?”

“Coffee; coffee. Donut, right?”

“Ah yes, that’s delicious,” he said.

“And after that, friend, what? It’s your turn next. After a month, let’s meet, I’ll help you if you still have problems.”

“Okay, thank you!”

So, our friend who knows a little English went first. When he arrived there, for his first breakfast, he went to that diner. He found one. He sat at the counter. He ordered “Coffee and donuts please!” It was given to him, he was happy. But the problem is, for a month he’s been eating breakfast, lunch, and dinner: “Coffee and donuts, please.”

Now his friend arrived and they met again. He said, “Friend, it’s good you finally arrived.” “Oh, what’s the problem?” “I’m already purging from coffee and donuts; teach me something else!”

“That’s all? We can definitely do that. Tomorrow, go back to that diner, okay, and say, ‘Ham and eggs, please.’” He said, “Ham and eggs? What is that?”

“Ham: ham! Eggs: eggs!” “Wow okay,” he said like that. When he slept, truthfully, he was already chewing. He dreamed of ham and eggs. So, he woke up early. He was the first customer at the diner he always went to. The waitress said to him, “Coffee and donuts?”

“No, no, no. Ham and eggs, please.”

“Okay. How do you like your eggs?”

He/She said: “Pardon?”

The waitress looked: “You know, scrambled, over-easy, fried, poached, boiled, eggs Benedict?” The list of how the egg can be prepared is long.

“Ah, ok. Coffee and donuts please.” [Laughter and applause]

The moral of the story is: Put the Filipino in, increase their capabilities, and there is nothing they cannot handle.

Good morning. Thank you very much.
