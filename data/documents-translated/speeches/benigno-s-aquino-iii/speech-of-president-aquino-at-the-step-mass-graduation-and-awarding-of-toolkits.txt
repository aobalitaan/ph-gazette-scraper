Let’s look at an example of your colleagues: Ms. Jennifer Noble of Batangas City, who was among the first to graduate from this TESDA initiative. As a single parent, she faces challenges every day in raising her children alone. Before undergoing TESDA training, she had no permanent source of income; she was a cold drink vendor at Batangas Port, and was lucky if she earned P300 pesos a day. Of course, when the weather is bad, no one travels to Batangas port. Then the income stops. In the face of hardship, she could have just complained about her situation every day. But instead of giving up, she strived and took advantage of the opportunity to improve her quality of life. When she learned about the free training offered by TESDA, she immediately enrolled in the Beauty Care and Wellness Massage course. With the help of the TESDA certification and the toolkit provided to her, she now has a permanent livelihood and regular clients. She is also a trainer at a technical-vocational school. And in total, from P300 when she was lucky, she now earns at least P2,000 a day. [Applause] Jennifer is an example of what the STEP graduates this year want to achieve—to take a step closer to the dream of progress.

This program is just one part of TESDA’s broader strategy to empower our countrymen to become partners in progress. There is also the Training for Work Scholarship Program, TWSP. From July 2010 to April 2015, the number of graduates reached 808,781. What’s even better: If previously, only 28.5 percent of TESDA scholars found employment, now, almost 70 percent of TWSP graduates have found immediate employment within six months. Think about it: Since we took office, we have graduated more than 7.8 million trainees in various technical and vocational education and training courses of TESDA.

When I was a congressman, we had a barangay in Tarlac, and someone approached me, someone who exports Christmas decorations, making Santa Claus. They were looking for someone to stuff the fabric kits with cotton. Sorry, I’m not technical in dress-making. They would simply assemble the Santa Claus figure and it had a good effect on the barangay for about four years. The captain told me, “We no longer have fights every night here. There’s less drinking, and the gambling has decreased.” I asked, “How did that happen?” “Well, everyone has a quota.” So when invited, “Friend, let’s drink.” “I’m sorry, I’m still far from my quota.” The quota is that the one ordering would bring the materials, return after the agreed time, and upon return, the payment is included.
