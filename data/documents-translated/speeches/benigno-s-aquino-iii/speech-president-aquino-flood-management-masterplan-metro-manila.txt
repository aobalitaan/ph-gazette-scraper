Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, regarding the Flood Management Masterplan for Metro Manila and the inauguration of the new RTU building.

When I was listening to the speech of our Honorable President, he said that being ridiculed, insulted, and maligned is already part of the job. I said, I'm not alone. [Applause] He was right: It seems easier to insult than to work. Let's see if insults are edible.

You know, I was just looking at this 11-story building, I clearly remember the groundbreaking, in 2010. Because in 2010, the budget left to me was less than ten percent of the National Budget. I took office, noon on June 30, 2010. That means, either six or eight percent of the National Budget was left to us, to spend for the entire six months of 2010. Boyet came in, we needed about P600 million here. I said, “Boyet, with the P100 billion, I'm not even sure if the salaries of government employees have been paid for these six months. Let's study it.” But because of Boyet's diligence, you now have a new building; [looking at] all the equipment; he told me, even the generator that can power the entire building is installed; the classrooms are even air-conditioned [applause]–that's why Speaker [Belmonte] is looking at me angrily. Speaker, the school I came from, Ateneo, still doesn't have air conditioning until now. [Laughter]

I agree that we might need to think about the name, “RTU.” Because in the military and police, “RTU” means “return to unit.” [Laughter] Usually, someone has committed a wrongdoing. But maybe “RizalTech” would be good, like “CalTech.” You all seem to agree. Of course, that's your decision.

Before we go to Mandaluyong, we came from inspecting in-city housing for our countrymen in Manggahan, Pasig. This housing is part of our comprehensive strategy—led by the DILG and the NHA—to move those living in danger zones like the Manggahan Floodway away from the risk of flooding, and to build back better their community.

The place we visited, if you go to the Manggahan Spillway, the homes of our countrymen are not above the Manggahan Spillway. Those being evacuated are inside the Manggahan Spillway. So they will live in the path of the water, if the water rushes in, right? Truly, inspired by the inspiration given to us by Jesse Robredo, we really found a solution for our countrymen to be placed in proper housing.

You know, earlier our good Secretary Babes Singson mentioned—you know, next year, on July 1, 2016, I expect that when I talk to a babes, it won't be Babes Singson anymore. [Laughter and applause] But I've been hoping for a long time, so don't be too impatient. [Laughter]

Anyway, let's go back to the problem. Earlier we were talking about flooding. According to the DPWH, we don't have enough drainage capacity for the entire National Capital Region and neighboring cities. Let me emphasize: Our canals and esteros cannot handle the large volume of water. That's why, when it rains heavily, and water flows down from the Sierra Madre, rivers overflow; parts of Metro Manila are submerged, causing illness and severe traffic. Laguna Lake, which could have absorbed the water, is also silted, so the water level in the lake rises quickly and floods the houses around it. Manila Bay, which is another outlet, also has many low-lying coastal areas that are easily flooded, especially if combined with high tide. Now, our solution: A comprehensive, complete, and clear Flood Management Master Plan that Secretary Babes Singson presented earlier. This includes the ongoing Main Drainage Project for Mandaluyong.

Clearly: There needs to be a path and destination for the water when it floods. But the reality—and those who have flown over Metro Manila know this—is there still vacant land where we can build infrastructure in the National Capital Region? We can build cisterns like the one in Fort Bonifacio in Burgos Circle, but this involves digging, which will further narrow the traffic.

Since we're talking about traffic, here's a report from the DTI: Of course, as always, there's good news and bad news. Let's start with the good news: More than 22,400 new vehicles or cars are sold every month. Of course, if you have a new car, you want to use it. I'll add, almost 100,000 new motorcycles every month this year 2014. The sum of that: 1.2 million new motorcycles last year. The result: Of course, it's even more crowded on the roads. And naturally, because of the traffic, the car and motorcycle you worked hard for will wear out faster because it's just stuck in traffic.

The ideal situation: Add more bridges and widen the streets; but even this will take years to implement and will involve a lot of discussion, especially regarding the right of way. Let's take the case of the Guadalupe bridge. It should accommodate 200,000 vehicles per day; but what passes through it all day is about 250,000 vehicles. It really exceeds the capacity of our bridge. That bridge also needs to be retrofitted because it could collapse if an earthquake occurs. We need to build another bridge across the Pasig River, from Global City to Shaw, but there is opposition to this because they say they will be inconvenienced. Let me also share: A variety of plans to alleviate the congestion of vehicles will soon be presented to me. Mine: The most radical is to divide the number of vehicles traveling—alternate odd and even plate numbers on our roads each week. Traffic will definitely ease because half of the vehicles will be gone, but surely many will protest because they won't be able to use their cars.

That's what we're facing. In the fight against flooding, from laying infrastructure, and even in organizing traffic, the prevailing mindset is this: “We're okay with the solution, we're okay with the construction, just don't let us be inconvenienced during implementation.”

How can we do everything if everyone says that what we need should not be done by them. Next you will fly again to the National Capital Region, look at the vacant space. You will probably see the Rizal Monument. You will probably also see some cemeteries. And those cemeteries are also getting crowded.

So let me repeat: In flooding, it's the simplest thing we understand—coming from the Sierra Madre, different rivers, different tributaries, different creeks will pour in. During Ondoy, the volume that flowed was three times more than what the passage could handle. The water needs a destination. Now, our drainage here, when we look at it, there's a lot of drainage, [it's good]. There are many buildings, and various other infrastructure with drainage, but the key to that: Dispose of it in the nearest body of water, without anyone caring about the capacity of that body of water. In the entire NCR, only Manila and part of Makati have a sewerage and septic system. Of course, a complete plan has been made that they need to implement. There is logic in putting these systems in place. It's a long process because the entire National Capital Region and the neighboring provinces that we still need to take care of for a complete solution. But all those solutions, including finding funds for them, we can do everything; when the time comes, there will be lawsuits left and right to get the right of way, because our cooperation ends when we are not inconvenienced. It's hard to have no one inconvenienced.

I admit: We want to address all problems—and if possible, we should have done this yesterday. But there are limitations to what we can do, and the implementation of a step cannot be immediate. We are striving to address everything that can be addressed, and my appeal: Unite and increase understanding and patience. With your contribution, we can ensure that we progress together.

Bosses, on the Straight Path, our aim is: to correct what needs to be corrected; to fix what can be fixed and what should be fixed. Along with this is the investment in a better and more progressive future for every Filipino. This is the essence of our inauguration of the Sen. Neptali A. Gonzales Academic Hall of Rizal Technological University today. [Applause] We were the ones who broke ground on this, and you can see the result of our cooperation.

This will serve as the new academic building for those studying education, as well as Information Technology. We are grateful, first of all, to your good Representative Boyet Gonzales, who really focused on this project, and the inauguration is perfectly timed with his birthday this Saturday. [Applause] We also thank Speaker Sonny Belmonte for his support, and for prioritizing Mandaluyong before Quezon City [Laughter and applause]. I guess that means Quezon City needs double the support. [Laughter] The most important thing: This building is the result of the contributions of the people of Mandaluyong. So, thank you very much. [Applause]

It is admirable that we were able to build such a large building, especially since it is crowded in the city. Another admirable thing about this project: We were told that the I.T. facilities of RizalTech will be on par with those of other well-known universities. But for me, the greatest success of this facility is all those who will benefit from it, because they will share what they learn with the next generation in time.

This new center of education in your city will be visited by experts. Because the equipment is more modern, they will be able to impart knowledge to students more efficiently. Those who are taught, upon returning to their communities, will be able to share what they have learned. Because of this, the transmission of knowledge to more of our countrymen will be accelerated. This is what we call the multiplier effect, where the benefits received by a few are passed on and inherited by many.

I remember what my father said: “Once you grasp knowledge, it belongs to you for life.” So, to the youth and students who are the primary beneficiaries of this new facility: You now have the tools to maximize your learning. My challenge to you: Use what you have learned to deliver more benefits and services to your fellow human beings. In your help, rest assured that those you empower will rise to a higher level; and in that way, you will ensure that they reach higher dreams for our country.

As President, my goal remains unchanged: to bequeath a Philippines that is better than the one we inherited; to see the ordinary Filipino within reach of his aspirations, because he is supported by the State and possesses the ability to fulfill them. The housing this morning, our Flood Management Master Plan, this very building—and even other successes in the field of economics and infrastructure, the developing Armed Forces and strengthened PNP, the millions of Filipinos covered by CCT and able to receive free treatment because of PhilHealth—all of these and many more serve as concrete proof of what we can achieve if we strengthen our unity on the Straight Path.

The point here is: If the implementation of good plans is stopped because of corruption or unreliable people placed in power, who can say that the needs of the Filipino will really be met when we step down from office? But if we side with those who are honest and proven capable, we will surely reach our aspirations faster.

In my last SONA, we said: We have come so far, but this is just the beginning. In the remaining time in my term, trust that we will double our zeal and further strengthen our commitment to the nation, while encouraging our countrymen to remain on the Straight Path to expand its benefits. I am confident: With the guidance of the Lord, and with our unwavering support for good governance, we will surely reach the great potential of our race; surely we will pass on a better Philippines to those who come after us.

In closing, let me just reiterate what our good President said. When Boyet asked me for this building, the enrollment at Rizal Technological University was 19,000. Now it is 30,000. That seems to be proof of the data saying that we have also reduced the number of out-of-school youth.

Now I am also thinking. It seems that our good President and Boyet Gonzales are talking. Maybe next week, they will say: Our population has doubled, so double our building too. [Laughter and applause] Let me prioritize Quezon City first. [Laughter] You who have been holding umbrellas for a while now, we are all sweating. I don't want to ask if there is no auditorium here, or it might be added to the buildings I need to build. [Applause]

Let me emphasize: In our system, all you have to do is show the need, and we will find a way. And since 2010, when this was first mentioned, no one has left me with anything but debt. And I was asked for funding. I thought: Where will we get all the funding for this? But you are all witnesses: We were able to do this because we worked together. We were able to address unemployment and so much has changed in our society. But the best thing I want you to think about: All of this is just the beginning. But it is up to you if we will continue it.

Good day. Thank you all very much.
