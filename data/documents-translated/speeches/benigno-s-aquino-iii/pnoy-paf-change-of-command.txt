Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

at the turnover ceremony of the Philippine Air Force

[Delivered in Lipa City, Batangas, on March 9, 2016]

During the 66th anniversary of the Philippine Air Force in 2013, I faced you, and my pledge was: Before I step down from office, our modern aircraft will be guarding our skies. A scene is solid proof that we fulfilled our word.

During our recent official visit to America, upon returning to our airspace, we were met by two brand new FA-50 jets and accompanied us until landing. You all know, it was no small feat for the Air Force to successfully escort us. It required proper coordination between our ground forces and our pilots. I remember during my mother's time: She was returning from a state visit to Brunei, we were with her. The plan was for F5s to meet her plane in the air to escort it upon landing. That didn't happen, because the Philippine planes did not meet in the air.

So, as I looked out the window during the moments we were greeted by the FA-50s, and saw these two flying in formation, we were naturally impressed once again by our Filipino soldiers, and of course, by the unity of the people in the Straight and Narrow Path, because of how far our Armed Forces have come. This is solid proof that we can achieve a lot if we are united.

Let's take a moment to look back: Didn't they say before that our Air Force was “all air and no force”? Being a member of the Air Force was said to be one of the most difficult services back then. Many of our pilots who we trained were forced to leave, because they were pilots with nothing to fly. We can't blame them. When we started, we only had one C-130. This means we only had one heavy lift aircraft capable of delivering tons of equipment to soldiers stationed in remote areas, or providing aid to those affected by storms. Even from this alone, we can see the style of the previous leadership. They asked your uniformed ranks to protect the security of the country and guard our territory. But when it came to equipment, capabilities, and your welfare, it was as if they were saying: Let Batman handle it; just endure.

For a long time, you soldiers strived to make the most of what we had. For example, our helicopters carried M60s, which were belt-fed; they often jammed back then, so they always needed to be repaired during combat. To make it work properly, our soldiers, as I was told, would put a roller made from a sardine can. Apparently, that's the shiny attachment I used to see. When foreigners saw this, they copied it and made it formal. Perhaps they used a different brand of sardines. [Laughter]

I think, given the difficult situation we faced then, no one dared to dream that one day, we would have these modern vehicles. But times have changed: In the Straight and Narrow Path, we dared to dream, we are fulfilling our dreams, and we are making them a reality.

Our commitment: No one will be left behind in progress. For us, reform reaches the skies. Because of the lost decade, we are working doubly hard to strengthen our Armed Forces. We worked together; you identified and laid out your primary needs in a logical and reasonable manner, and we are addressing them together.

From July 2010 to February 2016, we have already allocated a total of P58.43 billion for our AFP Modernization and Capability Upgrade Program. This is far from the P31.75 billion allocated by the three previous administrations for the uniformed ranks. Under the same program, we have completed 68 projects, which is also more than the 45 combined completed by the three administrations before us.

Now, our former single C-130 has become three; we also have three C-295 medium-lift aircraft, and eighteen SF-260TP aircraft. In addition to this are eight Bell-412 combat utility helicopters, and eight AW-109E attack helicopters. Aside from the two new lead-in fighter jets I mentioned, the arrival of two more C-130s is already scheduled for this year; as well as our rental from Japan of five TC-90 training aircraft, which will also help our Navy patrol our territory, particularly in the West Philippine Sea. Next year, six Close Air Support Aircraft, and two Long Range Patrol Aircraft will arrive. In the coming ten FA-50s, two will land in our country in December of the current year, and the other eight, we expect to follow in 2017. All of these additional equipment are just part of our PAF Flight Plan 2028, which aims to develop the Air Force's ability to guard our territory.

More and more of our equipment is arriving. You all know that the value of these is no joke. Those who will fly these are undoubtedly younger than me and Lt. Gen. Jeff Delgado. But I expect you to be as mature as we are in flying our modern vehicles. These aren't like watermelon seeds in number. Let's take care of these together; use them at the right time and for the precise purpose. Live up to being responsible and professional soldiers. Let's work together to increase our modern equipment, and thus further strengthen our Armed Forces.

To be truthful, your government isn't just focusing on equipment; we are also addressing the other needs of our uniformed personnel. For our AFP/PNP Housing Program, from May 2011 to December 2015, we have already built 61,378 housing units. 25 percent of the AFP allocation there was allocated for our Air Force.

During our term, we increased your Subsistence Allowance and Monthly Hazard Pay, and we supported the provision of Provisional Allowance for our soldiers, police, and other members of the uniformed service. There is also a Monthly Combat Pay for our soldiers who are involved in operations. Aside from this, we also promoted livelihood programs for active or retired soldiers.

It is clear: We were able to strengthen the Air Force without overlooking other needs, especially those of our fellow citizens who are in the margins of society. When we respond, we don't just address sectors one by one, but we strive to do it simultaneously. One example of this is the pouring of funds into our Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program. Our target this year: to finally reach 4.6 million households as beneficiaries. Now, with the help of the program, 1.5 million households or the equivalent of 7.7 million Filipinos have already crossed what we call the “poverty line.”

We were able to do all of this without adding to the burden of our Bosses; we did not add any new taxes, except for the Sin Tax, which aims to improve our health services.

Truly: Our Armed Forces now has an administration that not only dares to dream, but truly fulfills our highest aspirations. At the end of all this: We were able to deliver positive change to your ranks, because of your dedication and professionalism. With your unwavering sacrifice and concern for our country, the state can only reciprocate with care for you and your families.

Today, we recognize the exemplary leadership of Lt. Gen. Jeffrey Delgado in your army. Even during my mother’s time, Lt. Gen. Delgado already demonstrated excellence as a member of the Presidential Security Group. When we appointed him as your Commanding General in 2014, and until this moment, he carries with him a sharp mind, unquestionable ability, and his inherent skill as a soldier, and as your chief.

As his colleagues say: This Lt. Gen. Delgado is not just a good leader, but also a “friend of the people”: caring for people, and if you have a problem, whether it’s about service or even personal life, he is very approachable. That’s why to Lt. Gen. Jeffrey Delgado: On behalf of every soldier of the Air Force, I salute you, and I thank you for your almost two years of strong leadership in this unit. [Applause]

Now, our Air Force has a new Commanding General. As before, extensive knowledge and experience in the service were the basis for our selection of Lt. Gen. Edgar Fallorina. In the service, Lt. Gen. Fallorina is known for his “attention to detail.” They say that with the number of papers that pass through his desk every day as AFP Deputy Chief of Staff, even a small word, a wrong punctuation mark or spacing, does not escape his notice without being corrected. His principle: To instill in the hearts and minds of every personnel, soldier or officer, the value of doing what is right at every opportunity and at every level of the institution.

This discipline reflects his principle of always being “one step ahead.” Whether in a simple meeting or in actual operations, he is always prepared and has data at hand, allowing him to accomplish any objective or mission quickly, accurately, and efficiently. This characteristic will undoubtedly be beneficial in our pursuit of advancing our Air Force.

The trust we place in your new leadership requires you to reciprocate with commensurate service. So, to Lt. Gen. Edgar Fallorina: Before you now stand the brave soldiers of the Philippine Air Force. Your marching orders: Guide our Air Force to even greater heights, while maintaining a culture of concern, honor, and professionalism throughout the force.

In May, the Filipino people will once again face a crossroads. With your leadership, I expect: The entire force will fulfill its duty, based on the expectations of the people, and as servants of democracy; I am confident that we can conduct a honest, peaceful, and orderly election this year.

Truly: Since we embarked on the Straight and Narrow Path, we have come a long way. From supporting our Armed Forces, to guarding our territory, to focusing daily on the security of Filipinos against malevolent elements and any calamity—you have been there, wholeheartedly facing every challenge, and proving that what were once considered “mission impossible,” can be accomplished through unity and teamwork.

I also trust that, in the final stages of our term, and as we continue our initiatives to strengthen the entire Armed Forces, you will be behind me and will further demonstrate unwavering service to our country and to our Bosses, the Filipino people.

We only have a few months left in our term and we will be moving on to a new chapter in our lives. Perhaps, Jeff Delgado will advise us on how to best use our retirement time. When that time comes, if we meet again, General Fallorina, you are not authorized to call me “tatang.” [Laughter] I am a father to the nation, but I don't have any children yet. But if there is anything I can help you with, any advice, just one call is all it takes.

Colleagues, perhaps if I have anything left to aspire to in my life, you might think that after June, and I hope you do: Once there was someone who dared to dream, and that dream became a reality because of your cooperation, your collaboration, and your unity on the Straight and Narrow Path.

Good day to you all. Thank you very much.
