Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

In the 2015 National Assembly of the League of Barangays in the Philippines

[declared at the Marriott Hotel, Pasay City on September 28, 2015]

As I understand it, the policy to receive a full pension after working in the government is: you need 20 years of service. When I calculated one day, by 2016 I had 18 and a half years. I was short by one and a half years. Our good DBM Secretary Butch Abad, he entered government before me—you can see that, my hair is thicker than his—when Butch Abad retires, he served in our government for 19 and a half years; meaning, he is still short half a year to receive a full pension. We even joked that we should apply in our respective barangays, maybe there's a position we can be appointed to so we can complete the required 20 years and complete our pension. [Applause]

You saw Leila earlier. She said, “This is probably the largest group I’ve visited and spoken to.” Honestly, we forced her to speak. You’ll be surprised, our fiery and feisty Secretary of Justice, when facing a criminal, she doesn’t back down. But when she’s talking to you because you haven’t been accused, she’s shy. I said, it’s better to be shy than to be thick-skinned. Is that right? [Applause]

I believe life is difficult if you are a governor; it becomes even more difficult if you are a mayor, like Mayor Amante, because everyone in his jurisdiction goes to him. But I think the life of barangay captains and councilors is far more difficult, because you are literally on-call 24/7, right? [Applause]

When I was a congressman, I could go home two or three days a week. Tarlac is close. But usually, people have to seek us out if I didn't reach them when I got home. But a captain, people don't have to wait for him to come home because he lives there.

As the fathers and mothers of your communities, you are the first people people run to with their problems; and often, you didn't create the problem, but you are the first people they go to. You are the first point of contact for government assistance. You are the frontliners in public service. Whether it's a simple need, or a disaster, illness, and even disputes, you are expected to address the complaints.

That's why today, we truly pay tribute to our captains and barangay officials, particularly those who make up our Lupong Tagapamayapa, whom we recognize for their immeasurable contribution in the field of community peace and order. [Applause] You might think that just because I am President, I don't seek justice.

Truly, our twelve awardees today are excellent. The criteria for this award, or the basis for this award, are no joke. One of the standards is that the board must have resolved at least 90 percent of the cases; and our awardees, truthfully, the average is not 90 percent. Their average for 2013 and 2014 was low: only 98 percent. [Applause] When I saw 98 percent, I said, maybe they got lazy because they only had two percent left to go. I hope we become even more diligent so that we all become hall of famers.

Because I am with our frontliners in public service, I cannot help but say that I truly share with you the challenges faced by our Bosses. I am sure you feel that out of ten requests, if you granted nine, people will remember the one you couldn't accommodate. Is that right? [Applause] As my father used to say, he wasn't alone. Those of you who were included in the complaints because of one request not granted, you are not alone.

My point is: If we look back at the various problems left to us in 2010; if we think about a time when lies, cheating, and theft prevailed in our government, where our countrymen had lost hope; and if we compare that to where we are now and where we are going, I believe all the sacrifices, insults, and criticisms have been worth it.

What I want to say is: We advanced transformation because we implemented positive politics, instead of traditional politics, in our government. And now, isn't it wonderful to think that your children will no longer have to go through all the hardships we faced, because we did what was right and we sided with what is just? For me, this is the ultimate measure of any service. I was reminded of what some of our countrymen said when we visited Davao and Apayao in recent weeks. It would be best to let our Bosses speak about the transformation. Please look at our screens:

[Testimonial videos from Davao at Apayao]

[Applause]

It's not only those from Davao and Apayao who are saying that. You know that. In every place, we can find testimonies like this: What our countrymen once thought was impossible, we have made possible and continue to make possible.

In Apayao, I first heard that: We have reached a point where we can no longer add infrastructure because it is physically impossible given the remaining time. A different effect from the disheartening situation we found in 2010. Let me add: Now, because of the simultaneous construction in various parts of the country, I was told that we are running short of workers, contractors, materials, and even what are called aggregates—and I asked myself: Aggregates? Isn't that stone and sand? I was told, “Yes.” How can we run out of stone and sand? But that's apparently what's happening because everything is being done simultaneously, and there are indeed points where the necessary materials are becoming scarce.

My point here is: Isn't this problem better than the lack of infrastructure or insufficient services because public funds are misallocated? Let me repeat, when it comes to Apayao, we actually have funds, but it's no longer feasible to release them because they can no longer be used within the remaining time. The road will be opened. Once it's opened, as you know, it will settle. To settle, two rainy seasons need to pass.

Let us remember: During the time of our predecessors, we were all neglected for almost a decade. As a friend of mine said, “I cannot forgive those who came before you, because almost ten years of my life that could have been prosperous were lost under his term.” And it is clear that what was lost, we can no longer recover. Those ten years are gone.

You, those in the barangays, should not become “partisan politicians.” But it is also clear that as leaders of your communities, you hold the power to promote the well-being of your constituents. My appeal is: You should help in the discussion; encourage your colleagues to engage in discourse and debate and ask: Who is truly worthy to lead the country when 2016 arrives? I don't need to tell you which candidate I will support. My vote goes to the one who will surely continue the Straight Path.

Comrades, like any important matter, I think we cannot simply abandon all that we have worked for. If we still want to reach higher aspirations, we need to advance the Straight Path. Let me emphasize: In the next election, not just six years are at stake, but the very life and future of every Filipino. If we deviate from the straight and tested path in 2016, no one can guarantee that we can recover by 2022.

My question to you now is: With all the good things you have seen in the past five years of good governance, would anyone say that we should abandon the Straight Path? If you are truly a public servant, can you say face to face to your constituents: Let us stop the Straight Path? Or can you do the opposite, with full conviction: Let us continue the positive reforms; let us continue to support the Straight Path.

We are only at the beginning of the great story of our race, and I believe that every Filipino has the ability to do what is right and reasonable so that we can achieve our most cherished aspirations as a nation as soon as possible. With the guidance of the Lord and our continued unity on the Straight Path, let us together shape a Philippines that we and future generations can truly be proud of.

Before I finish, perhaps you know that there are only about eight months left in our service. It might be good if I start saying goodbye now.

My fellow constituents, or the barangay officials were my first supporters when I ran for Congressman. When I took office, they were also the ones who really proved, or helped us to make our service genuine and for our constituents to attain complete service. In all that I have gone through, whether in the administration or often in the opposition, my 159 captains and I have never left each other. They brought me from Congress, and I think they started me on the path to Malacañang. So, on behalf of my entire family, my other colleagues, I truly believe that if our barangay or our barangays are well-managed because our barangay officials are well-organized, we will truly shape a strong and truly progressive community that will contribute to achieving our aspirations, which have remained a dream for a long time.

You have been a strong partner in our term of office. I hope we surpass what we have accomplished in the past years.

Good afternoon to all of you and thank you very much.
