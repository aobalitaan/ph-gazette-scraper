Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

At the 37th commencement ceremony for the PNPA Masundayaw Class of 2016

[Delivered at Camp General Mariano Castañeda, Silang, Cavite, on March 10, 2016]

This is now the last time I will attend a graduation ceremony at the Philippine National Police Academy as your President. As I face you now, we see your stepping into a new stage of your lives as public servants. To the new members of our uniformed service, the Masundayaw Class of 2016, to your parents and loved ones: Congratulations to all of you. [Applause]

In the almost six years that we have traveled the Straight Path, we have come a long way; we have achieved many things. We also know that there are still things that need to be addressed, but what is important is that we are focusing on them so that they are no longer passed on to those who come after us.

You all know where we came from. You entered as recruits in 2012, when the horror stories about the police were still fresh, those that happened during the time of our predecessors. There was the purchase of helicopters at brand new prices, but it turned out they were just secondhand. There were new police officers who, just to get a position, had to buy the rights from those who were retiring. It was also told to us that there were large expenses just to enter the police force. The solution for those who wanted to serve: to borrow money. Consequently, even before becoming a police officer and receiving a salary, the thought was: How to recover from being deeply in debt?

Even when we were still legislators, we learned about the difficult situation of our firefighters. When there is a fire, of course there is thick smoke; it is difficult to breathe. But because there were no breathing apparatus, our firefighters would just spray water on the smoke to drive it away and to be able to respond.

These are just some of the problems we found in your ranks. We wondered: In this situation, how can those like you who want to enter the service be motivated? You pour out dedication to care for the State, but the support given to you is insufficient. How can you see a bright future for your family, if it is blurred by the negligence of a corrupt system?

That time is over. On the Straight Path, we implemented reforms; we emphasized that those who defend and care for the people should also be protected and cared for by the State. In our pledge of “No one will be left behind in progress,” you in the uniformed service are included.

During our term, we developed “Shoot, Scoot, and Communicate” for your ranks. If before, you were in service without assigned firearms, under the Straight Path, we achieved a 1:1 police-to-pistol ratio. In 2013, we were able to provide 74,879 units of Glock 17 9mm pistols to our police force. Along with this was the purchase of other equipment such as 1,490 patrol jeeps, 247 4x4 personnel carriers and 69 light transport vehicles, 1,095 motorcycles, and 12,949 handheld radios.

Aside from providing equipment to more effectively suppress crime, we also improved systems and projects focused on smarter and more studied strategies in promoting peace and order. You now have a modern Integrated Ballistics Identification System, as well as an Automated Fingerprint Identification System. I would also like to mention the Closed-Circuit Television Project of our PNP, to monitor what are considered crime-prone areas in our National Capital Region.

Another thing we are striving to address, and PNP Chief Ricardo Marquez can attest to my persistence, is the provision of raincoats to our police officers. Perhaps this won't be used much now during El Niño, but what about when the rainy season hits again? We cannot ignore the health and well-being of those in service, especially when they are involved in our operations. And even though this is the third, fourth time I've instructed Gen. Marquez about this, I'm sure that before I step down, Gen. Marquez will have completed a significant portion of this.

As I mentioned earlier, we also need to review our PNP Law. In this way, we can identify the provisions that hinder the immediate imposition of penalties on leaders who are lacking in the performance of their duties. Likewise, this will strengthen discipline in following our policies, and protect the rights of all.

In addition to what I mentioned to the police, we also fully support strengthening the capabilities of our BJMP personnel. During our term, the funds we allocated to the agency reached P393 million. This includes the purchase and provision to you of caliber firearms, portable radio systems, and even prisoner vans. For the BFP, a total of P4.46 billion has been allocated under our term. Included in what we purchased for you are new fire trucks, new uniforms and protective gear for firefighters, as well as self-contained breathing apparatus.

For our entire uniformed personnel, we signed an Executive Order that will increase your allowances: The monthly hazard pay, from P240, has increased to P390 this year; this EO also covers the increase in your provisional allowance depending on rank, where some may receive up to P14,214, and the officers’ allowance for Senior Inspectors and above. [Applause] We are also really focusing on housing for our soldiers and police. More than 61,300 units have been completed under our housing program for our uniformed personnel, and members of the PNP, BJMP, and BFP are included as beneficiaries.

Let me emphasize: All the benefits and support you enjoy are the result of the intense reforms we are promoting under the Straight Path. The late Secretary Jesse Robredo started the positive transformation in our DILG; this was continued and truly expanded by Secretary Mar Roxas, who led your department for almost three years. Now, that legacy of honest and reliable service is being continued by our current DILG Secretary Mel Sarmiento.

With all these investments to develop the capabilities of you, members of the uniformed service, we are seeing positive results. You have proven: When the State takes care of you, you become even more motivated to fulfill your mandate. Just look at our crime statistics: Before, crime reporting was handwritten in logbooks. Now, we use technology for more efficient and faster crime reporting. And because the data is more accurate, we can formulate more effective strategies to solve crimes. Our Crime Solution Efficiency, from 18.64 percent when we took office in 2010, has increased to 51.36 percent in 2015. Under Oplan Lambat-Sibat, we have arrested 746 Most Wanted Persons in NCR, 499 in Region 3, and 580 in Region 4-A. As our former chief at the DILG said, because of the arrest of the brains and leaders of the syndicate, its tentacles are also being suppressed, so the number of crimes is falling. Along with the pursuit of criminals, we have confiscated or impounded thousands of firearms and unlicensed vehicles.

To our BJMP, from July 2010 to December 2015, you apprehended 7,237 inmates and visitors carrying contraband; you also seized 48,699 deadly weapons, more than 1,100 grams of shabu and marijuana, and almost 4,900 drug paraphernalia. For all of this, a huge congratulations.

But along with this, one can ask: How did drugs and contraband get into the prisons? I believe we still need to strengthen our monitoring and processes to further improve our service.

It is clear: We have managed to strengthen your ranks without forgetting other needs. We are not focusing on one thing at a time, but we are also addressing other sectors in need. One example of this is the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program. Our target this year: To reach 4.6 million households as beneficiaries. It’s as if only 200,000 remain as the remaining target. Now, with the help of the program, which was mentioned earlier, 7.7 million Filipinos have already surpassed what we call the poverty line. Our goal: To ensure they do not fall back into what we call the poverty line.

We have done all of this without adding to the burden of our Bosses; we did not add any new taxes, except for the Sin Tax, which aims to improve our health services.

Now, as new police officers and members of our uniformed service, the responsibility of continuing these successes rests on your shoulders. As we often say: This is just the beginning, so I hope you don't waste the hard work of those who came before you.

We all know that in May, there will be elections again. For the uniformed ranks, this is a time of various temptations. I repeat my challenge to every batch of graduates like you: If you are offered an envelope in exchange for your honor, will you sell it? If someone promises you a promotion in exchange for your silence in the face of corruption or abuse, will you accept it? If you are forced to choose: country or self, which will you prioritize?

I am confident that the PNPA has thoroughly trained you to always stay on the Straight Path. In the coming months, your marching orders are: Ensure that our elections are safe, peaceful, and truly reflect the will of our Bosses. Follow your duty, and obey the orders of your officers, leaders, and the entire leadership. Always remember: You are not alone. You have many partners in service, and we can do our job better and more effectively if your coordination is proper. We can further advance your welfare if you show trust in your fellow public servants, especially your brothers and sisters in the uniformed service.

You know, as President, every time I visit our military camps, part of their tradition is to render honors. But I noticed, especially at the beginning, that when it comes to our police force, it seemed like you didn't know what to do. To be honest, I don't lack attention. Sometimes, if we don't break tradition, we just have to waive these recognitions. But through this simple act, we can also reflect the entirety, how enthusiastic and diligent you are in fulfilling your service.

Eventually, as time passed and I got to know you better, your enthusiasm in giving attention became noticeable. Now, your bearing is more dignified and even your salute is more elegant. This is proof of your confidence and higher self-esteem, brought about by the changes we have made and are continuing to make in your ranks and in our society.

Indeed, many of you have shown excellence in service, and have already done so. Among them is Senior Inspector Charity Galvez, who successfully led a team to repel 250 rebel groups that attacked their precinct in Agusan del Sur in 2011. There are also four policewomen, namely Juliet Macabadbad, Delia Langpawen, Mercelina Bantiyag, and Maricel Rueco. Even though they were new to the service, they wholeheartedly responded and fought to pursue the Martilyo Gang that was robbing a mall in Pasay. For their good example, and for all others in our ranks who risk their lives for peace, we offer our sincere gratitude.

Let us remember: Facing crossroads is part of our daily life and service. As members of the uniformed service, will you care for our countrymen, or will you simply prolong their suffering? As members of the PNP, BJMP, and BFP, more than any other government personnel, you will serve as the face of the government in our communities. You play a very large role in preserving and improving our society. You are the ones people turn to, the ones they confide in, the frontliners in times of need.

As I have said, when you try to scoop water with your hands—and this was taught to me by my teachers—you need to be careful. There should be no space at all between your hands. Because if we open them, and there is even a little space, it is said that our principles are like water that leaks. It is not acceptable to let go of just a piece of our principles; everything is affected when we give that kind of leeway.

I am confident that on all occasions, you will serve as faithful public servants; you will always side with what is right and just; you will always serve our Bosses, the Filipino people. With the help of the Masundayaw Class of 2016, of our Bosses, and of the Almighty, we will leave our country better, more developed, and more peaceful than we found it.

In closing, I just want to remind you: What we did right, everyone benefited from; what we did wrong, everyone also suffered from. I hope it will not be difficult to choose right over wrong.

And, just one more thing, perhaps this is what our graduates are waiting for: In observance of the time-honored tradition of the Philippine National Police Academy, on the occasion of the graduation of PNPA Masundayaw Class of 2016, I, Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Republic of the Philippines, hereby pardon all outstanding punishment tours and confinement to quarters of BSPS Classes 2017, 2018, and 2019 of the Cadet Corps, Philippine National Police Academy, as recommended by the director of the PNPA, effective today. Granted this tenth day of March, the year of Lord, 2016, at PNPA Camp General Mariano Castañeda, Silang, Cavite. [Applause]

Good afternoon once again. Thank you all very much.
