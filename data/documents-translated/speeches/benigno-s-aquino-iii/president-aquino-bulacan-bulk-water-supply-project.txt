Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, at the signing of contracts for the Bulacan Bulk Water Supply Project.

I noticed earlier, [two of you here are my classmates.] They might say it's all classmates, relatives of mine… Chito Cruz and Andy Ibarra are my classmates. Gerry Esquivel, we were in the same batch, because he was in the honor section, [laughter] we were just semi-honors. I’ve been looking at our LWUA administrator earlier, [who we can’t say is happy]. I’ve been asking him earlier, maybe you noticed, I was talking to him earlier. Why are you sad, I asked? Many water districts will have their problems fixed with this project but you’re sad. Maybe his daughter is getting married, that’s why. [Laughter] Because, you see, there’s really no reason.

But before I begin with our formal speech, I just want to say to Manong Willy that I am truly glad to be here today. You know, when Manong Willy and I were together in Congress, it so happened that he was the president of what was called the Central Luzon Caucus, congressmen who are part of Central Luzon or Gitnang Luzon, and I was the chairman. The time came when I was in the opposition and I told Manong Willy, “Manong Willie, we might have a hard time working with Malacañang if I’m still the chairman of the group, so I have no problem stepping down from this position.” And he told me, even though there might be a penalty for that, he said, “No, stay there.” That was the agreement of the whole group. So there, we saw from the very beginning that Manong Willy is truly easy to talk to. You know, whenever we meet, the topic is always about water: flooding, too much water, lack of water, the Angat Dam is dangerous if there’s an earthquake. About 95% of what we talked about was about water. And this project, this will address many of the issues he faced. So before I leave office, at least I can say, “Manong Willy, if your hair falls out like mine, I think it won’t be because of water anymore.” So, congratulations to you. [Applause]

Now, it’s like a tag team with Manong Willy and Joel Villanueva. When I’m here, Manong Willy takes care of things. When he’s in Manila, Joel reminds everyone that there’s a shortage in Bulacan.

Now, we move on to the formal part of my speech.

We are all aware of how important water is. It is a natural resource, the source of life. That is why, from the very start of our term, we immediately focused on its proper management. Sadly, when we came into office, this sector seemed to have dried up due to neglect in the past. The system we found: fragmented, everyone for themselves, haphazard. Imagine: there is only one water sector, but 30 agencies are managing it, dividing its administration. This number is excessive, yet their services are insufficient, so the supply of clean water remains lacking throughout the country. Consequently, instead of the benefits flowing to the people, they are being held back, and only a few are benefiting.

We immediately addressed this. We designated Secretary Babes Singson as our water czar. He formed an Inter-Agency Committee on the Water Sector, which implemented what we call the Integrated Water Resources Management Policy. Its objective: To determine the total water needs, so that our use of it becomes accurate and efficient. And because we are uniting the previously separate agencies, the rhythm and direction of our efforts to develop this sector have become unified.

Today, we witnessed another proof of our initiative to maximize the benefits for our Bosses: The signing of contracts for the Metropolitan Waterworks and Sewerage System for the Bulacan Bulk Water Supply Project.

This is the twelfth Public-Private Partnership project awarded under our administration, and the first awarded PPP project in our water sector. This P24.4 billion project aims to deliver clean water to 22 water districts in 21 municipalities and three cities here in Bulacan. Perhaps I should say, “Here in Bulacan” where the Cojuangcos originated from in Malolos. It will supply an estimated 388 million liters of water per day from Angat Dam. The number of beneficiaries is estimated at 3 million residents of Bulacan.

Aside from addressing the water needs of your province, this project will also help the health of our Bulakenyo constituents. Because the coverage of MWSS services is expanding, we can reduce the use of water from deep wells and groundwater aquifers, which, according to a study by the National Water Resources Board, are reportedly contaminated with seawater.

This Bulacan Bulk Water Supply Project is just part of what we call the Water Security Legacy Program of MWSS. The aim of this program is to address the problems of water infrastructure and water distribution. If you recall, in 2012, we completed the Angat Water Utilization and Aqueduct Improvement Project Phase 2. This past July 2015, we inspected the rehabilitation to be undertaken at Angat Dam and Dike. Currently, the bidding for the Angat Water Transmission Improvement Project is ongoing, and we will soon begin the bidding for the Kaliwa Dam.

It is very important that we ensure a stable supply of water in the country, especially as we prepare for and address the threat of El Niño. You can rest assured that your government is doing everything to ensure the continued provision of water services for our citizens.

Of course, we are confident that we can implement our good programs because of our partners in the agenda of broad reforms. We are truly blessed to have allies like MWSS Administrator Gerry Esquivel and his dedicated colleagues. You can see, whatever mud is thrown at him, he wholeheartedly faces it, because he knows he is on the side of what is right and just. As a public servant, he clearly understands the obligations he needs to fulfill, and the service he needs to deliver to our Bosses. So to Administrator Gerry Esquivel: Thank you very much for your enthusiasm and professionalism. [Applause]

Likewise, we sincerely thank each agency and organization from the private and public sectors, who are joining hands to achieve success for this project. You truly prove that: On the Straight Path, the contribution of each one is not just drop by drop—it’s a flood of help and support from everyone to achieve our collective aspirations for the Filipino people. Again, thank you very much to all of you. [Applause]

At this point, I just want to highlight what our good friend Ramon Ang is doing. As you know, there were questions, for example, about the electricity in Mindanao. I will no longer go into the details of why it is not that easy to resolve, but we can leave it that the situation in Mindanao is far better than what we found.

Now, [when] Filipinos have a need, [the] companies led by Mr. Ang helped us. They are changing the model for our electricity, which previously required all produced electricity to be sold before the plant was even built. He is building electrical power plants that will join the competitive bidding to sell their product. In short, if there is a shortage, he is already addressing it, including our future needs. That bidding for the Cavite-Laguna expressway, their bid was almost double. The bid, the premium they call it, of course went to the national treasury. Although he lost in the second round of bidding, he did not hold a grudge against us, but the price was corrected for the Filipino people. [Applause]

Regarding this water as well. The transmission, if my memory serves me right, the newest one in the tunnels is from 1968? The oldest in the transmission tunnels is from 1932, the year my father was born. Then, '68 is the newest. We were almost born with our classmates during '68. Now, we don't necessarily need to understand that, let the one who replaces me worry about it. I might say, “I’ve inherited so many problems. Maybe the next one will have a boring life if they don’t have any problems. I’ll pass them on to them.” But the point is: Let's look for our equipment, if it's from 1968, it's almost fifty years old and still functioning properly. Of course, there's what they call wear-and-tear. That's dangerous, the condition is different now.

The point of this is, everything we do now should ensure that the one who replaces us has fewer problems. We believe that problems not addressed today are passed on to the next, continuing the hardship for our fellow Filipinos, and that is not right. That is not what a government given power by the people should do.

Let me emphasize this: As a father of the nation, it is my duty to promote what is good for the majority. Our focus is not on self-promotion, not on maintaining ourselves in power, but on responding to the true needs of our countrymen, not just now, but also for future generations. Whatever the reaction, no matter how intense the criticisms we receive for difficult decisions, we wholeheartedly fulfill our sworn duty to serve our Bosses, the Filipino people, faithfully.

We are approaching the crossroads where the nation will once again face the choice of new leaders. The question is: Will we continue on the Straight Path, or will we turn back to the complicated situation we found ourselves in? We have seen the results of a crooked, self-serving, and individualistic practice. Likewise, we have witnessed the positive results of a system that is upright, helpful, and compassionate. I have full confidence: Filipinos know the right direction our country should take. It is still the Filipinos who will bring about the realization of our collective dream for a more developed and comfortable Philippines.

Good day. Thank you very much to all of you.
