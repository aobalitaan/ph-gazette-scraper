Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

On the 30th anniversary of the establishment of the Presidential Security Group

[Delivered at the PSG Grandstand, Malacañang Park, City of Manila, on March 1, 2016]

Indeed, in the final year of my term, several important commemorations have fallen. Last week, we celebrated the 30th anniversary of the EDSA People Power Revolution. Now, we rejoice for the 30th anniversary of our Presidential Security Group.

It would be good for me to emphasize the situation of our Armed Forces under the dictatorship. The day my father was assassinated, the head of the Aviation Security Command, Gen. Luther Custodio, was immediately summoned to Malacañang. As we understand it, various operational plans had already been prepared for my father's return, and they were waiting for which plan would be implemented upon his arrival. You would really wonder: If the plans were already ready, why wasn't the order simply radioed, and why was a personal meeting in Malacañang necessary? During those times, it seemed that other plans had indeed been prepared for my father's return.

What I want to emphasize here is that the AFP and the police at that time, especially the Presidential Security Command, seemed to have become the private army or Praetorian Guard of the incumbent. Imagine: Their total number was between 15 and 20 thousand people, or almost 10 percent of the combined forces of the uniformed services at that time. Aside from seeming to be outside the scope of any law at that time, it seemed that their only focus was to implement the desires of the dictator.

You can see the big difference between President Marcos' Presidential Security Command and the Presidential Security Group established by my mother and led by Col. Voltaire Gazmin after EDSA. Under Volts, although the number of the PSG decreased, to the point of being less than 10 percent of the former size of the Presidential Security Command, your entire unit truly became professional and courageous.

I remember back then: My mother's own security detail carried clutch bags. Inside: Bottles wrapped in towels to look like guns, and at least make people hesitate to attack them. Isn't it true that, even after the dictatorship, everything was taken from this camp? Even the jalousies and doorknobs were not spared. The Ministry of National Defense, seized the armored assets. Even their fun run, they were still using armor. Luckily, your Group was truly reliable, because when the height of the coup in 1987 arrived, the PSG also had armored assets.

During those times, it was not only the PSG's ability that was tested, but your very principles. In the face of the coup, the question that seemed to be asked by every soldier at that time was: Will I just side with my comrades in the PMA and in the barracks, or with the people I should protect? We already know where our brave soldiers sided: with no one else but the people. During those times of intense tension, you proved the words our mother said at your first anniversary: “The Armed Forces as a whole are the shield and sword of the Republic, but the Presidential Security Group has been the breastplate protecting the Heart of the Republic.”

Over the past almost six years, there has been no one more witness to the positive transformation of the PSG, of your dedication and professionalism, than myself. Earlier, our group commander mentioned what happened in Zamboanga. That is just one example, during the Zamboanga Siege in 2013. As I recall, Raul Ubando had only been Group Commander for about two weeks, coming from the exemplary leadership of Chito Dizon. Despite this, our command and monitoring and our actions in the areas of combat were successful. You know, I don't usually ask for jobs that can't be done. But the Group said at the time: “Sir, we're going to the stadium. We didn't survey the stadium. We don't know who's there, and the stadium is also within the enemy's mortar range.” I remember being picked up in a bulletproof vehicle. It was worse than what we use daily because this vehicle still had a deadbolt inside. I said, “The situation here in Zamboanga is really different.” My response to our very new Group Commander at the time was, “We need to ‘lead from the front.’ Our countrymen need us. They need to see that they are not alone. Do what you can do. Leave everything to God and what we cannot do.” Our good Group Commander's only answer was, “Yes, sir.” There was no long debate, no hesitation, the right thing was done. And as you saw: Our Group Commander and the entire PSG really showed their stuff, which is why our operation in Zamboanga was peaceful and successful.

All the risks we've faced, you've always been there, and guarding my safety. From the aforementioned crisis in Zamboanga, the earthquake in Cebu and Bohol, Typhoon Yolanda, the visit of His Holiness the Pope and President Obama, ensuring the security of those who attended the APEC, to my travels within and outside the country—repeatedly, we have a professional PSG that gives our President stability and peace of mind, so that he can do what needs to be done, no matter how intense the challenge.

Let me add: You could have taken advantage of your proximity to power, like our predecessors thought. But not once did I feel that you were acting only for any favor; rather, I know that you stand up to truly contribute to the reforms we are laying down. That's why, unlike others, we don't just say we'll reduce the problem; with your help, we are truly reducing the problems of our countrymen. Just one on our long list is the 7.7 million countrymen we have lifted out of poverty.

Therefore, to all of you: Never look at your job as just a job. I think, sometimes, you yourselves forget or do not fully realize the extent and depth of the scope of your service. Because of you, I am at peace with my security, so I can fully serve our Bosses. This is all I ask of you: The next time we go to different parts of the country where we are already delivering concrete change—from the roads and bridges that our Bosses have been waiting for for decades, to the sitios that have long been shrouded in darkness and we are now giving light, to the classrooms that we are now building for the youth—I want you to stand up, look at them, and wholeheartedly say to yourself: “I am part of this change. This is my contribution to the Philippines, to the Filipino now, and to future generations.”

To Secretary Volts Gazmin, to former PSG Commander Chito Dizon, to our current “Group” leader Rear Admiral Raul Ubando, to those we are honoring today, and to each and every one of you who comprise our Presidential Security Group: I have no words to reciprocate your unwavering service other than “Thank you very much.”

Thank you, for ensuring my family’s and my safety every day. Thank you, for sharing in the hardships and joys of the job, even knowing that more often than not, it is hardship that we share. Thank you, for treating me not only as your Commander-in-Chief, but also as a friend and family member. Thank you, because with you there, I am able to more effectively live up to the mandate of our Bosses to fight for widespread change in society. Thank you, because of you, we have reached this point in our history, and we are reaching the Philippines we have long dreamed of.

We have certainly been through a lot. Many have come and gone. Included in our remembrance today is every member of the PSG, from the past until now. Among them is one of our colleagues who said goodbye to us just last year, and who was my partner for over a decade, Bong Fuyonan. Bong, you truly reflect the simple, yet honest and reliable service of a true member of the PSG. Wherever you are now, I am sure you are still watching over all of us. And thank you very much as well.

Comrades, there are only 121 days left in my term. I have full confidence: In the remaining time we have together, and for years to come, you will continue to live by the principles that guide the PSG in service. Your motto of “Integrity, Service, and Excellence” is no longer just words posted in your office, or written on paper—it is forever etched in the hearts and minds of Filipinos as they look up to you as the shield of the president, and soldiers of the people.

Now on your 30th year, and the last anniversary I will face with you as President of the Philippines, allow me to emphasize: Since I assumed office, in every moment of service, until this very moment, not once did I feel my commitment to being your shield weaken or falter; rather, it has grown stronger and more steadfast. I truly salute all of you.

As the former head of the Presidential Escorts, Bodette Honrado, said as my mother’s term was ending, “Whatever happens, we will leave here with our heads held high.” Now, facing you, what I can say to you is: One day, when you leave this service, I will be confident, and you too will be able to say with your heads held high, “I have fulfilled my duty, and even exceeded expectations.”

To you and the entire Presidential Security Group: It is a great honor to be your leader and Commander-in-Chief.

Happy anniversary to all of you. Thank you very much.
