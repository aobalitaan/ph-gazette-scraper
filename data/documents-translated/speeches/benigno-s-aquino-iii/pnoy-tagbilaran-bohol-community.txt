Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

In a meeting with local leaders and communities of Tagbilaran, Bohol

[Declared in the City of Tagbilaran, Bohol, on March 2, 2016]

I remember someone once told me, of course, during a campaign, especially during Martial Law, convincing people. So in a neighboring area of yours in Cebu, there was a Senator before Martial Law, he arrived at a gathering—you might have heard this story—when he arrived at the gathering, he found only three people in the plaza. When he arrived, he said, “Should I even speak here? There are only three people left to talk to.” The emcee said to him, “It seems like they are waiting for you. If you speak, we might get those three, we’ll be at 100 percent here. Please speak.” [Laughter] They were very eager, you know how it was during Martial Law, right? It was illegal to gather more than three people: illegal assembly.

It was an opportunity, a bit of a campaign to be able to speak. So he spoke on the stage, like this. He used the PA system to talk to the three—because if I only found three people, I would just approach them one by one. Why would I speak loudly through the speaker? I might deafen them, and I wouldn’t convince them even more. But he was really a veteran politician, he spoke, he told stories. Midway through the speech, one of the three who remained stood up and left. When he left, he said to the emcee, looking back, “Sir, there are only two left. We are at 100 percent, continue.” The person believed him and continued his speech. As he was about to finish his speech, the other one stood up again. Only one was left in the plaza. He said, looking back again, “Sir, everyone before you failed to convince him. You are the one he was waiting for. Continue, just one left. We’re good.” But the speech ended and the one person didn’t leave. He approached him because he wanted to say that his campaign here was worth it. When he went there, he approached the person and said, “Friend, you must be convinced now.” The reply was, “You know, before you even arrived, I was already convinced. In fact, the sound system you are using, I lent it to you. [Laughter] After you’re done, I’ll take my sound system back.”

So that was my problem earlier: There was a prepared speech, the teleprompter was there, my speechwriter stayed up all night working on it, but I think, I believe you are already convinced. [Applause] I might sound like a broken record if I repeat everything. But if you’ll allow me, let me just say—look, it’s just one page—I call these “bullet points.”

Earlier, we were at the new Panglao Airport that you are having built. Sec. Jun said it will be finished next year, 2017. Now, the first good news I received, Ed and Rene—we were with them—they told me that even now, while the airport is being built, PAL is already requesting it. They want to have international flights here in Bohol. They will use Tagbilaran, with four flights per week expected. Imagine, the airport is still being built but it will definitely be able to accommodate all these foreign tourists to add to your numbers.

Because it is estimated that right now you have around 500,000 tourists per year. Once the airport is finished—that’s next year—we will reach 1.7 million tourists per year. [Applause] And that’s next year, I will no longer be President, expect that I will be among those adding to the number of tourists because I will be a tourist there myself. [Laughter] Hopefully we’ll get lucky, next year—I told Rene the other day—maybe if I’m fortunate enough to find someone, of course there will be a honeymoon. [Laughter] We can go to the Loboc River Cruise with the fireflies. Of course, we’ll pass through Panglao.

Now, this is my earnest request: When I go on my honeymoon, you know we’re all together, but on the honeymoon, please don’t join me. [Laughter] If possible, let’s have a competition first. [Laughter]

Earlier, I had a photo taken at the airport, because I think you've probably seen it if you've been there, especially from the air, it's nice to look at. The area has been leveled and is gradually really looking like an airport. Honestly, with the diligence of all of you officials here—special mention to Ed and Rene, and also Bob Cajes—they're like a tag team in wrestling. If one texts you, give them two minutes, the second will text you. After another minute, the third will text. If you're forgetful, you won't have a chance to forget what they need. They really are a tag team, you can see it—I'll mention later what we've accomplished together.

But the point is, of course, I'm happy that the airport is here. Your tourists will triple; for every additional tourist, it is estimated that there will be an additional job for our fellow Filipinos. 500 will become 1.7, with 1.2 million new jobs. Where will that come from? There will be tourist guides, drivers, those in resorts, restaurants, farmers selling, and fishermen selling at these latitude tourist facilities. And what's even better is that you don't need to be highly educated to participate in this industry.

Again, with your diligence, with the mandate you have given us, next year, your airport will be new, and tourists will really triple. So congratulations to all of you. [Applause]

At the beginning, we promised all of you that no one would be left behind. And I'll just repeat, all our colleagues here are really diligent. What has Bohol gained from following the Straight Path? Just a little, because the commitment was: No province will receive less than P2 billion per annum in resources. Here in Bohol, I'm going on six years, Bro. Armin told me, 2 times 6 equals 12 billion. What went to Bohol was almost P19 billion; we exceeded that national average. [Applause] Earlier, we were also reminded, like—let's just repeat—Rene reminded us earlier, he said that my mother, when she was President, was the first to commission a feasibility study here for the Panglao airport. So it's good, I think, when I see my mother again, if we can say that she started it, I finished it, I think she'll probably say again that I'm her favorite son. [Laughter]

Now, remember the 2013 earthquake? Honestly, one night, we really made an effort to empathize: We looked at all the situations; we slept with you. Actually, not here in Tagbilaran, [in] Loon. We were in Loon, on the lawn, because it was grassy, in a tent. Inside the tent, the army gave us cots, folding beds. When I sat on the folding bed, it turned out the middle had a metal support. So all night, your back was on the metal support because the [mattress] was thin. [Laughter] But it was okay, it was just one night. And all night, we were entertained because there were aftershocks. [Laughter] When I woke up in the morning: I washed my face, brushed my teeth, I couldn't bathe with just a bottle of water. But it was enough for washing my face, brushing my teeth, a little for washing my hands, and that was good. With all my companions then—Dinky from DSWD was there, there were many of us—we experienced what you were going through during that time when we were really going around here.

To be honest, I'll admit, that's over, we have a lot of… Our early recovery cluster is there, there are pictures here behind us. We've restored a lot that's better than what we found. Isn't that right? [Applause] But during our rounds at that time, I'll really admit to you, how can we restore all of this? Just with electricity, Icot Petilla, our Secretary then, really struggled to restore it within the time he promised.

There are just a few highlights of walking the Straight Path. Previously, the beneficiaries of the 4Ps—the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program—here in Bohol were—to be exact—the total number of households was 531. That's how many the 4Ps helped when we started. Currently, we are helping 59,222 of our countrymen. [Applause] And you know the result of Pantawid, in the first re-validation of our data, there are already 7.7 million households that have surpassed what is called the “poverty line.” We have lifted them out of poverty. Some of our opponents say we have no compassion.

In Philhealth, 1.2 million Boholanos are now members of PhilHealth, included in the 93 million Filipinos who are members of PhilHealth and are being cared for. [Applause]

Regarding electricity, we just had a ceremonial lighting of our sitios here in Central Visayas. Here in Bohol, we reportedly no longer have any debt for sitio electrification, but it’s only 97% in Central Visayas. I promise that before this month ends, all debts in Central Visayas will be settled, that’s over 32,000 sitios.

Perhaps this is what I really want to emphasize: Everything we accomplished under the Straight Path, anyone could have done it. Isn't that right? And I remembered what a friend told me when we were starting. He said he could never forgive those we replaced for the ten years that were lost. Imagine: If the Panglao airport had been completed ten years ago. Or even eight years ago. How many jobs would have been created? How many sick people would have been helped if PhilHealth wasn’t only 51 percent? How many children would have continued their studies if the 560 here were 59,000 of those in need? How many would have graduated? How many would be in college, or maybe even have already graduated from college? Like the first batch who were helped in high school in 2014. Imagine, around 13 to 14,000 out of the 300 plus thousand helped by Pantawid Pamilya were honor students. The two who spoke for all of them are now in UP College of Engineering, a quota course. They were able to get in. If everything we did had been started when we replaced those in power, going in the same direction, where would the Philippines be now?

We can’t blame my friend for saying, “I can’t forgive them for the ten years that were lost.” Ten years were lost, time has passed, we can’t get it back. Isn't that right?

Earlier, many people told me, and in other places I’ve visited: Thank you for this, thank you for that. Inside, I wondered, why are you thanking me? I was the one who volunteered when you called on me. I said, help me, let’s change society. And look at us now. Before, when we said airport, that was the last stop for me to leave the Philippines. Now what we’re talking about is: How can we expand tourism to create jobs for us in the Philippines?

When we all think that all our problems have no solution, now we can point to 7.7 million people who were not promised, but were lifted out of poverty, but have crossed what is called the “poverty line.” And where did we start? You trusted me, you trusted all of us, didn't you? Because of you, you gave the mandate, the decision was right, here we are now.

Now, I don't want to be a broken record. You see on television, sometimes they insert the good things we are doing. Whatever is happening, not just in Bohol, but throughout the Philippines. You are also aware of the so-called “accolades” and “praise” that other countries are saying to us. And we have really come a long way.

Now, for me, for the upcoming election on May 9th, it's very simple. It's like a referendum. Was what happened on the Straight Path right or wrong? Now, maybe you saw the commercial, we are experts at wooing, but maybe not at buying helmets. But the point is, sometimes I don't know if I should just shake my head at what our opponents are saying. Earlier, I saw: He will increase the benefits. And while he increases the benefits of those in 4Ps, he will reduce taxes. I said, that's great. Where will the funds for that come from? And there's a lot more. You decide. Just look at what they are saying. Read it in the newspapers, look at the ads.

For me, it's very simple. It might be too simple, let's just look at the poster. Is the photo of you on your poster the same as the photo of you in real life? If it's already blurry on the poster, let's think a little. Of course, in wooing, we all went through wooing, everyone will promise everything. Let's see if the promises have meaning or not. And perhaps the most important question to ask them: Where were you when we were making [the] solutions to this problem?

Don't forget, when we started, the amount allocated to my budget, June 30, 2010, was barely seven percent. Almost the entire budget was already allocated to various places. The amount left for me to decide on was seven percent. And on top of that, I was reminded that we needed a new airport in Bohol. Where would I get that 7 billion pesos?

Everything we've been doing, even just now, 6,200 kilometers of national road, 1,700 tourism roads, 3,005 farm-to-market roads, that's just from him [Sec. Singson]. When we talked to Bro. Armin, he left us with a backlog of schools [classrooms], 66,800; the budget can only afford 8,000 per year. Eight times six, that's already 48. I still have a debt to pay when I leave here. But truthfully, this year, we will complete over 180,000 classrooms, including replacing the 30 to 40 year old [classrooms], including those for K to 12. I will no longer pass on all these needs to my successor to fix. We have already fixed them.

I just want to emphasize: Perhaps the easiest… We have one opponent because when we talked, he told me, “Friend, I accept that our politics are different.” So if I say that the people are my “Boss,” who is his “Boss”? If I believe we should help everyone, especially those on the margins of society, the question is, who does he plan to help? If he believes—that our politics are different—mine is to help the 4.6 million households this year that are being helped by Pantawid Pamilya. Not just to bring relief goods when there's a storm, go for picture-taking, a photo opportunity, and that's okay. No way! It's not help to give someone a piece of food to eat now when you could teach them… As they say, “Do you give a man a fish or teach him to fish?” I choose to teach him to fish.

It should also be clear to him that I am defending the country against other countries, especially in the West Philippine Sea. They say they will talk to our rivals. I said, I really don't talk to them. My only problem when I talk to them is that they immediately start with, “All of this is ours.” So what kind of negotiation can we have if that's their perspective: All of this is ours, so don't come in here. But this has been ours for a long time. So we fought for it: We are in arbitration, we are in ASEAN. Let me repeat: This is campaign season—compete with good ads, good jingles, good slogans. But perhaps the best thing here is to compete with principles.

And I would just like to emphasize, we have two people now—let's focus on Leni first: Leni, to be honest, I first met her the day after Jesse disappeared. Jesse, he was like a father to me, he insisted on not involving us in politics. He said we should have a private life so we could grow up normally, not disturbed by the complicated world of politics. Jesse was like that too. We met once when Leni picked up Jesse. And when Jesse introduced us, we waved at each other, “Good evening.” That was our dialogue.

The time came when Jesse disappeared. Then I asked everyone in the Cabinet, who knows Leni? And it turned out that almost all of us barely knew her. And I will tell you—no matter what kind of bible you put in front of me, I can put my hand on it—I was very impressed by her strength of spirit. I went there and said it had been twenty-four hours and we hadn't found any evidence, the news might not be good. The sadness, the resentment, all of that could have taken over. She was truly strong, she strengthened herself especially for her three children.

When her constituents in Camarines called on her, she could have said, “Couldn’t you leave us alone for a while? Jesse and we have already contributed so much.” But she answered the call. She fought a strong opponent in that district and won. And now, she is heading for re-election. She could have said, “Can’t we just leave it at that, I’m doing well here, I’ll just run for re-election.” Or she could have said, “I think I’ve prepared the next one, maybe I can just take care of my children.” But not only did we ask her to run again, we even asked her to run as Vice President of the Philippines without any planning.

And the Vice President is very important. We cannot say what fate holds. Whatever we are looking for in the presidency, we need to look for in the Vice Presidency, because that is really the system. If we just give away the Vice Presidency, we might wake up one day and feel like a nightmare has happened to us.

I will repeat to you: I have no doubt, not even a little, that in time you gave a mandate to Leni Robredo, and when her term ends, you will say, “Our trust in you wasn’t just worth it. Our investment in you was more than enough.” That, expect it from Leni Robredo. [Applause]

Let's go to Mar: You know, Mar sacrificed in 2010. He was our party’s candidate, he made way. But perhaps more important was what happened after that. How many times has he been the face of the government, especially during times when the situation was really, really bad? Zamboanga crisis, Yolanda, many storms where he was always the first to respond. Mar, perhaps not everyone knows, because it wasn’t Mar’s intention that when he went to a disaster-stricken area, he’d ask, “What’s the best photo opportunity here?” [Others] would arrive in the morning, distribute five relief goods, have a photo opportunity, be in the newspaper the next day, and then leave. Mar was the opposite.

Mar would go to a place, like during Typhoon Yolanda. You might not know, because of the strength of the wind where they were, they worked together, along with his security, and Sec. Voltz—I sent him ahead to Yolanda before it arrived to ensure we were prepared. The door was being blown away in the building where they were hiding. Our two Secretaries almost disappeared. And truly, when a vehicle couldn’t be used, they walked to inspect and find out where the greatest needs were. That wasn’t publicized much. Instead, what was publicized was that he was lacking in some areas.

He went to Northern Samar, the vehicle couldn’t pass, so he rode a motorcycle. Why did he ride a motorcycle? It was his job as Secretary of the Interior and Local Government: Where is the problem? What are the needs? How do we deliver aid? What did we see? Didn’t his rounds reveal the problem? No. What was shown was that he fell off the motorcycle he was riding.

Let me reiterate [the saying]: To see where one is going, look at where one came from. And I will just compare it to one of his opponents during Martial Law, a human rights lawyer. Now, this person running for president is getting the son of the dictator during Martial Law. It makes you think. His next “claim to fame,” he defended democracy against those who staged coups during my mother’s time. And now, his running mate, the one who planned a coup back then. Isn’t it legitimate to ask—he’s older than me, so he says: Brother, where will you take us? Isn’t that a legitimate question?

Comrades, let me reiterate, May 9, what happened to us in these six years, it’s not impossible. Although we started with so many problems, what will we do? We’ve come a long way, as I told you, I want to be able to tell you when I step down from office, I will leave something far better than what I inherited. I think, we can say that we have fulfilled that promise. [Applause]

But I earnestly plead: Before, some were suggesting that we revisit the Constitution, to give it a second chance. I said, hold on a moment. What’s important is not the person in office. What’s important is the platform, the principles that anyone, if this is the right path, should continue. Continue it.

And you know, I spoke with President Obama and New Zealand Prime Minister John Key. I first met [Key], his daughter was about to start studying in Paris. And it seemed like any father separated from his daughter, it makes you think. President Obama was the last one who told me about that. Their eldest child is also about to become independent, to study in college. There’s worry again that my child, I take care of, I watch over, I monitor, I’m not there every day to check on him.

I myself still aspire to reach that point, but you have already called me the “Father of the Nation.” And frankly, can I abandon you? Absolutely not. I think my conscience is truly at peace because we have Mar Roxas to continue, with the help of Leni Robredo, along with our entire slate for the Senate. [Applause]

Just the final part: We always say when we accomplish something, “Don’t thank me, I didn’t do it.” I always reiterate: We did this. We made the Philippines what it is today. We will also continue our upward and improving lives here in our Motherland. And where will that strength come from? I have nothing to boast about but you. As long as you are behind me, until the last day of our term, I can say “Let’s face any problem. As long as time is on our side, we can overcome everything.”

My earnest plea: Should we stop now when we’ve already reached a higher starting point compared to when we began in 2010? Everything that has happened to us, in the context of a feast, they call it just an “appetizer” because our progress will only accelerate. As long as we are united, let’s choose wisely. So this May 9th, it’s a referendum. The question is simple: Has our life improved on the Straight Path? Is our path on the Straight Path correct? And if the answer to that is “Yes,” don’t forget, someone needs to continue: Mar Roxas and Leni Robredo, along with our candidates for the Senate. [Applause]

Good afternoon. Thank you all very much.
