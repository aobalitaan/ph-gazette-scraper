Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

In his meeting with local leaders and communities of Muntinlupa

[Declared at the Muntinlupa Sports Center, City of Muntinlupa, on April 28, 2016]

As I was approaching here, I remembered, I think I remembered this place we are going to. And what I remember, this was made a shelter, right? Because of the floods, when we pass through Muntinlupa, it doesn't seem so clear to those not from here that there is a flood in Muntinlupa because the Laguna de Bay lake seems so far away. But when we went around with Ruffy in 2007 and 2010, the lake wasn't actually that far from Muntinlupa, right? And maybe I'll start there.

When we were just starting our term, every day as we went to the office and went home, we were bombarded with problems inherited from the previous administration. Let me give you a problem related to Muntinlupa. There was a company, a Belgian company, given a contract to dredge the Pasig River. Now, we had it studied, someone complained that the dredging to deepen the Pasig River did not actually happen.

We had it studied and we have a Filipina scientist who is very capable of studying it: Was dredging done or not? And according to her study, because when you disturb it, right, the riverbed, it's like wood, it has layers. And the shells that are left, it can now be determined when the land was last disturbed by looking at the layers. In short, P5 billion was spent to dredge the Pasig River. According to her study, they were lucky that 40 percent of the P5 billion was used for the project. You figure out where the 60 percent of the P5 billion went.

What is the connection to Muntinlupa? The same company, it seems, was given or will be given a contract by my predecessor to dredge here in Laguna de Bay. Because they were supposedly successful in the Pasig River, the contract here is bigger, P18.7 billion; billion with a B, you see. So I was briefed about the project. I said, “What’s the purpose of that?” “We need to deepen it so that the lake can hold more water.” “Oh, is that so, how will you do it?” “We will dredge.” Well, since I was a congressman, I’ve been somewhat apprehensive about dredging. Right, when you dredge, and it deepens, when the rain comes, it’s the same—look, “What happened with the dredging?” New soil has arrived there. Before, it was difficult to prove whether something was done or not, but now it’s not. We now have a scientifically qualified person to examine it.

So when I asked, “What’s the plan for the mud that you’ll take from there?” “Well, we’ll take it from this part of the lake, and we’ll bring it to the other part of the lake.” I said, “How again?” “We’ll get mud from here, and we’ll deposit it on the other side of the lake.” Well, I’m not really a scientist, I’m a social scientist. But I remembered from our studies, whether it’s physics or geometry, isn’t it one of the laws, they say: matter occupies space. So you take the matter from here, and you put it in the same container, the container can still hold the same amount, right?

In short, we’re going to play with mud that belongs to us, but we’ll be charged P18.7 billion pesos. I said, “Oh, is that so? That’s not possible.” We stopped the contract, there’s arbitration now, you know. It’s still ongoing now, to protect the interests of our countrymen.

I will revisit our plan regarding the lake. For almost six years, we have been here, walking the Straight Path. Just a little over two months remain in the term you gave me, and first of all, thank you very much. [Applause]

I am taking advantage of this tour of the Philippines to express my gratitude and also to say goodbye. Now, to help me remember everything, I just need this cheat sheet. [Laughter] Because if I only accomplished one thing in six years, it would be easy to memorize, right? If only two things, I can still manage. But with so much done in every place, it’s getting a little difficult to memorize, isn’t it? [Applause]

From 2011 to 2016, the budget for Muntinlupa alone was: P2.05 billion. Almost triple. [Applause] How did we do that? We didn’t raise taxes except for the Sin Tax—and that Sin Tax, was for health. But first and foremost, of course, most importantly: Let’s not steal the people’s funds. [Applause] Number two: the taxes. There’s already a law, let’s collect them correctly, no exemptions, we all have a part in this. And then when the funds arrive, spend them properly. Where are we directing these funds? There are those for infrastructure, including transport projects, we are now using it to get here, the MCX which they say will reduce traffic on Commerce Avenue. We already have funding from Japan, they call it a “Concessional Loan.” The interest is very, very small. And they agreed because we have a good relationship with them. They agreed to a one-time release of funds, not an installment plan; so this will speed up the construction. Now, I will no longer inaugurate that train. There are so many issues: land, right of way, detailed engineering design, NEDA approval. The point is, it’s all set, from design to funding. It’s reading-ready and whoever replaces me will be the one to inaugurate it.

One of the results that I am really happy about, in 2008, we had around 2.8 million out-of-school youth. The number shown to me in 2013, if I'm not mistaken, was only 1.2 million out-of-school youth. [Applause] Before, they wouldn't even graduate; now, they are honor students.

For us, 72 percent now get a job before the six months are up after graduation. [Applause] That's 9 million course graduates.

Now, what's the use of that? They graduated, their lives improved. The next thing is, for example, if you enter TESDA, train for BPO—the call centers. In their first year of work, they pay taxes, they've paid back what the government spent to educate them. The next 30, 35 years that they work, it's like a bonus, and we can give the same opportunity to the next generation.

One thing we pursued was talking to businessmen. Do you know the tragedy when we were starting out? Some people entered courses like Physical Occupational Therapy. So that those who studied, who worked hard to pass, to get all their titles, have somewhere to go.' That's the result now. Now, they told me that there used to be a lot of nurses, about 250,000, that was just one year when I first took office. Now, it seems like we're short on nurses.
