Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

In his meeting with local leaders and communities of Tarlac

[Declared at Maria Cristina Freedom Park, Tarlac City, on April 11, 2016]

Do you know, when we ran in 2010, the first day of the campaign was here in Tarlac. And it was reminded to us, the last day of the campaign, was also here in Tarlac. Now, I am no longer campaigning for myself. It's like, this is my farewell to you. There are only 80 days left in the term you gave me. Perhaps it would be good to look back.

But before that, let me tell you a story: Because in other places when I go, I sometimes introduce myself. I say, “I am my parents’ favorite son.” [Laughter] Now, I say, I am from Tarlac. And I was told in Tarlac that once, there was a retiree who needed to go to Manila because he had papers to fix for his pension.

This old retiree happened to want to raise a water buffalo. And he treated this water buffalo like a dog. From a young age, he trained it to follow him everywhere. Wherever he went, the water buffalo followed him.

The day came when he needed to go to Manila to fix the papers for his benefits. He rode a Rabbit [bus liner]. When he boarded the Rabbit, the conductor said to him, “Boss, just a moment. This bus is for people. Your water buffalo can’t just come along.” The man said now, “Just let my water buffalo be. It knows what it’s doing.”

As the bus left, it was already on the highway, and the water buffalo followed because that was its habit—to follow its master. The driver looked back, “This water buffalo might get into an accident, or cause an accident. Should I speed up so I can leave it behind?” He sped up. At thirty, forty, he looked back, “Look, the water buffalo is still following. That’s not fast enough.” He drove at 70 kph. “You’ll be left behind here.”

When he looked back, he saw the water buffalo. Its tongue was hanging out. He said to the man, “Boss, your water buffalo has its tongue hanging out. It looks tired.” The owner said, “Please check where its tongue is pointing.” The driver looked in the rearview mirror, “To the left, sir.” “Ah, be careful. It’s going to overtake [you].” [Laughter]

When I introduce myself, I say, “That’s how we are in Tarlac. Even our water buffalo are unique.” [Applause]

You know, the life of the President—I think you’ll understand—is not easy. Even during the campaign, there came a time when Leni’s late husband, Jesse, and I had sorties in Bicol. Within 24 hours, he gave me 21 engagements. Imagine that, 21. Of course, each place you go to, you’re already weak after thirty minutes. Sometimes, an hour, plus travel time.

So sometimes, it would really be early morning, we’d be side-by-side on the bus, and I’d say to my companion, “Rico, please tell Jesse that the water buffalo also know how to cry.” That’s why I told you about the water buffalo. But let’s leave the water buffalo now. Let’s look at the past.

You know, when I became a congressman—with your permission, with your concern—we were able to work together on many things. But secondly, you taught me a lot about what it really means to be a true public servant. Let me give you some examples of what we worked on together.

Isn't it true that in the past, whenever it rained in San Roque Junction, there would be flooding? Then I asked, 'Why is there still flooding there even though the sun has been shining brightly for three weeks?' You agreed that our PDAF should go to barangay projects, but not directly to the barangays. We fixed Masalasak Creek so that the water would flow continuously and drain quickly. But even after those three weeks, the water was still there.

Around here near the Capitol, I remember during my first run, there was a small street that always filled with water and had decaying things in it. People walked through it on old tires. Of course, the tires didn't have inner tubes, so when you stepped on them, they would sink. If you stepped wrong, you'd slip and end up 'swimming' in the decaying water. Those who remember that time called it 'The Rubber Bridge.' Of course, that's gone now too. Even MacArthur Highway was in terrible condition. It would flood every rainy season. That's why we fixed Masalasak Creek, and we've been fighting for it every year.

Perhaps the most important thing is, I visited the Tagumbao Bridge earlier. What is the Tagumbao Bridge? It connects Sembrano, in the town of Gerona, and Tagumbao, on opposite banks of the river. The problem is, during the rainy season, the river current is very strong. People couldn't cross the river because the water was rushing. People from Gerona, from the [Westen], the western portion of Gerona, would go to Sta. Ignacia, then come here to Tarlac City, just to get to the town center of Gerona. During the rainy season, Gerona felt like two separate towns.

When I first ran for office, that was a strong request. And to be honest, I saw the need. We appealed as a congressman, we fought for it in the budget, and I challenged the DPWH back then. I said, 'Give me your entire PDAF for my three-year term. Let's build this bridge.' The DPWH told me at that time that they couldn't start a project unless it was fully funded. They said they couldn't do an installment plan. The problem was, every year, the water rushed. The rushing water, of course, would widen the riverbanks. What couldn't be done then would be even harder to do as time went on because prices would keep going up. And earlier, I really asked if we could visit the Tagumbao Bridge. And the bridge is really beautiful, the work is polished, and most importantly: The estimate was almost P380 million, with P108 million [in savings] in the contract. The bridge is finished, and we still have savings. That's what the Straight Path is all about. [Applause]

I always say, you are my bosses. I'm not bragging, but I want you to know what we've worked on together, where it went.

Let's go through it one by one: From 2005 to 2010, the funds given to us by the national government for infrastructure amounted to P2.06 billion. That's already a significant amount. But when we took office, and not just in Tarlac, but in all areas of the Philippines, this happened: The previous P2.06 million that went to Tarlac, we, from 2011 to 2016—for infrastructure alone, not including Balog-Balog—P13.61 billion was allocated. [Applause]

Earlier, we had a groundbreaking ceremony at Clark Green City. Honestly, we think of Clark—Clark Airport, where is it? It's in Pampanga. Clark Green City will be built in Capas. What is this project? P607 billion. It will have 1.1 million residents, 800,000 will be employed. If you compare, the new community will be approximately 10,000 hectares. If we went to Bonifacio Global City, imagine BGC getting bigger and bigger, because it's only about 200 hectares. This, when finished, will be almost 10,000 hectares, a new community that is truly resilient to the effects of global climate change; it has a lot of green technology and renewable energy; it's far from flooding; there are many jobs, schools, and hospitals. Earlier, the groundbreaking was held. The first equivalent of Bonifacio Global City is now being constructed.

Let's go back to the other projects—I already told you about the Tagumbao Bridge: Our highways, the Tarlac-Pangasinan-La Union Expressway, two phases have been completed; the Paniqui-Moncada section of MacArthur or Manila North Road; the Sapang Burgos bridge in Paniqui. Our Central Luzon Link Expressway will also begin, a thirty-kilometer road that will connect Tarlac City to Cabanatuan City.

All of these roads are just part of the 18,000 kilometers of national roads that we built during our term, including 107,000 lineal meters of bridges.

For flood control: Just this 2016, we allocated P1.02 billion. We are using this for major rivers such as the Camiling River, Tarlac River, and Rio Chico River.

To support our farmers, from 2011 to 2016, we allocated P727.44 million for the construction of farm-to-market road projects, and P10.59 billion for irrigation systems. This includes the Balog-Balog Multipurpose Project Phase 2 which our mother already started. There is also the Mapalad Small Water Impounding Project and the Rehabilitation and Repair of the Tarlac-San Miguel-O’Donnel River Integrated Irrigation System.

From 2011 to 2015, we also allocated P245.48 million for production and postharvest machineries and equipment. There are also 20 Shared Service Facilities from 2013 to 2015, with a total value of P7.02 million.

In classrooms, we turned over the latest six classrooms for Corazon C. Aquino High School, along with the 22 previously built in that area; 28 in total, which are part of the 1,855 classrooms to be completed in Tarlac overall; this is part of the total 185,000 classrooms nationwide. [Applause]

I would like to emphasize regarding education: When I was your congressman, you were witnesses. We were able to get eight to ten classrooms for the second district of Tarlac, which was already a big thing. Because you gave me the permission to lead, it means the Philippines will improve even further. [Applause]

Before, the government budget was for eight thousand classrooms, to be divided throughout the Philippines. When we came in, we conducted an inventory: How many classrooms were lacking in the Philippines? What we found was 66,800. Eight thousand classrooms per year, you gave me six years, we would finish 48,000. But the debt was 66,800. I will leave with a remaining debt.

But because Bro. Armin Luistro of our DepEd is good, the DPWH did a great job, many helped, there's even a PPP Project there. Let me repeat to you: Before, we could manage eight to ten classrooms per district. Now, the number of classrooms to be completed before we step down is 185,000. [Applause] And not only the debt we inherited, but also the additional classrooms needed for K to 12, and the thirty to forty-year-old buildings that need to be replaced, all of these will now be addressed.

Of course, you all know that we have addressed the shortage of chairs, books, and teachers. Regarding teachers, we have approximately 145,000 new teachers under the Straight Path program.

Sitio Electrification Program: We were told before that all barangays in the Philippines have been electrified. What wasn't said, but was whispered, was that once electricity was connected to the barangay hall, they would list the entire barangay as having electricity. It wasn't clarified that the barangay hall had electricity, but the sitios did not. In 2011, we took an inventory. We were told that 32,441 sitios throughout the Philippines had no electricity. And this past March, we completed the electrification of those 32,441 sitios. They all have electricity now. [Applause] I hope it's not just teleseryes and karaoke singing that they benefit from.

To be honest, we spoke with the candidate for Vice Mayor in Gerona, Boy Agudo, and he told me, “That’s not right. We still lack [electricity] in Gerona.” We just came from Gerona, and he said that they were able to get electricity in February, and also in March.

Regarding TESDA: 16,351 have graduated here in Tarlac from 2010 to 2015 in the TWSP led by our esteemed Tesdaman. [Applause] What’s good about this is that they are part of the 9 million graduates of technical vocational education in the country. I’m not saying that 9 million are TESDA scholars. Some take two or three courses. The good news is, when we came in, the employment rate six months after training was only 28.5 percent. Under the Straight Path, 72 percent of those who completed this training got a job within six months. [Applause]

In truth, there is a program with TESDA and Coca-Cola. They even teach sari-sari store owners how to increase their income. One person I spoke with, his sari-sari store used to earn P800 a day, now it earns P5,000 a day. What does that mean? P5,000 for 30 days in a month is P150,000. The salary of the President of the Republic of the Philippines is P120,000. In other words, after this, maybe I should apply to TESDA so my salary can increase. [Applause]

In the Pantawid Pamilya Pilipino Program, when we came in, it didn't reach 800,000, but only over 780,000 households were supported. Now, we are about to complete the last 200,000 households. Last year, we were helping 4.4 million families; now, 4.6 million households are covered by the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program. [Applause] Of course, Tarlac will not be left out here. You know, Batangas, Cavite—I forgot the other place, just those I've been visiting lately—before, not a single household was helped by Pantawid Pamilya. Now they are there, we are all the same, with 49,000, with 59,000. Here in Tarlac, let me repeat, 39,400 are being helped by the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program. [Applause]

Do you want me to present other reports? The report is a bit long.

In the Health Facilities Enhancement Program, in Tarlac alone, we used P414.57 million to improve our barangay health stations, rural health units, urban health centers, and hospitals in municipalities and cities. This includes the Tarlac Provincial Hospital, where we are constructing a Four-storey Outpatient Department and Ward Building. [Applause] Of course, aside from the hospital is membership in PhilHealth. PhilHealth now covers 93 percent of Filipinos. There are many others, but if we continue, we might not finish the report until tomorrow.

What I just want to emphasize: Who is Noynoy Aquino? Before, he was just his parents’ favorite son, but because you recognized him, you gave him the opportunity, we led the Straight Path here and all the changes that happened here started in Tarlac. You gave the trust, you gave the opportunity, so I think the whole Philippines is looking at the really good example that Tarlac made.

Now, when I was asked [in] 2009, “Can you run?” My natural first answer, I said, “Why would you make me run? I oppose all the foolishness done by the current administration. And then you’ll pass on to me the fixing of all the problems he’ll leave?” With the weight of those problems, I was lucky not to be driven away from Malacañang because the problems left to us were really heavy. You know that.

If we recall an example: For instance, they entered into a contract where they would dredge Laguna Lake because they said they would increase the amount of water it contains. They would extract mud, the silt of the lake. I asked, “Where will you take what you dredge?” They answered me, “We will transfer it.” “Transfer it where?” “To the other side of the lake.” So you take it from here, you bring it to the same lake, it’s still there in the lake. As they used to say when we were young, “What happened? It’s still a lake.” Nothing.

To play with mud—our mud, it’s in the Philippines—what we will spend—it’s supposedly cheap: P18.7 billion. That money could have built four housing units for the uniform services. But it will be taken there. What benefit do we get? Will the water capacity increase? No. The mud is still there. Will it become beautiful? That’s really the kind of project that was left to us.

There's one more thing, and this was during the first four months, when I had just assumed office and not many people knew about it: They called it the Investment Competitiveness Fund. Four companies with five locations in the Philippines were promised: Stay in the Philippines, and we guarantee your electricity for 10, 15, even up to 20 years. What's the problem with that? Well, 42 percent of the energy we need to have electricity, we import. That means we won't be setting the price. Whatever the price in the market, that's what we have to pay. The price of what we use to generate electricity fluctuates, and then you're going to sell it, but you can't raise the price. It's fixed.

They said the payment would be relatively low. The initial estimate there was: We would pay P41 billion to those four companies. It was already painful then, there were other companies, in the same business, and our Constitution has an equal protection clause, they could also ask for the same. And if we pay everything to them, if our negotiations weren't successful, we really wouldn't have accomplished anything. We would have done nothing but pay off all the debts inherited by us.

My brothers and sisters, Bosses, my purpose with you today is simple: Everything we've done—the many testimonies about what happened to us because of Pantawid, what happened to us because PhilHealth coverage was expanded, not just to members, but also to illnesses. What benefits did we get from the new farm-to-market roads? And many other things. Everything we've accomplished, could have been done by those who came before us, right? We all had the same opportunity. The question here is: Did you do it? And if you didn't do it, we'll ask: Why didn't you do it? Why were your priorities different?

As someone close to me said, “What you replaced, I can't forgive, almost ten years of our lives, [he was in office for nine and a half years] a large-scale PhilHealth program could have been started during that time. A large-scale Pantawid Pamilya program could have been started to help those truly on the margins of society. Many of their children would probably have graduated, finished high school, been helped by TESDA, and have good jobs, but it didn't happen.” We, and I repeat, we, you gave me the opportunity. Of course, you were also part of “we who made the change that we are enjoying in our society today.” [Applause]

Please take note of our t-shirts, each one is different. What does that mean? It’s not like we stole a huge amount of money that allows us to distribute t-shirts. What we have is diligence. What we have is that we are truly focused on your well-being. What we have is that we truly love you—not just in words, but more importantly, in deeds. [Applause]

Now, what if one day—and perhaps I’ll use the story of a housewife I heard. And how many of us are in the same situation, because some stories are even more painful. But this one, a beneficiary of Pantawid Pamilya, said to me: She said she is a housewife, with seven children. Now, her husband left them. She is alone, taking care of their seven children. Then she said, her livelihood is selling things.

In Tagalog, “patinda-tinda” is somewhat important. If you are selling, “What is your job? You are selling.” But when you say “patinda-tinda,” sometimes you have something to sell, sometimes you don’t. Sometimes you earn enough to support your family, sometimes you don’t. She said in her testimony, because of Pantawid Pamilya, three of her seven children have already graduated high school, have permanent jobs, and are helping her raise the other four. [Applause]

Now, if that woman, or other women—I think here in Tarlac, there are also similar stories—were to come up on this stage, speak to all of us and tell us, “You know, you may not know, you have helped our family so much. We are now standing on our own two feet. We didn’t come to you and ask you to take money out of your pockets to contribute to our rice purchase. Here, you really helped us give our children an education, get a job afterwards, which is now their livelihood.” If we were asked, “You helped us, and you didn’t feel burdened. You may not know, you are helping us.”

Now, the administration will change. Perspectives might change again. We might return to a time where no one cares anymore. I repeat, in the past, 780,000 households, now 4.6 million households are being helped by us. [Applause] Who among us, when approached by this mother or other members of all those who benefited from the Straight Path, would be told, “Will you not help me? Will you risk the change that is happening to us? Can you risk the future of my remaining four children? When I have finished their studies as well, you will no longer be burdened with us.” How many times have we been able to help without even knowing it?

As Sec. Mel Sarmiento said, in their place, whenever there is a Parent-Teacher Association meeting, the child runs to his father and tells his father, “Oh no, Dad, we have to contribute again.” Because that’s how it is with PTAs.

Let me repeat to you: All the events happening at present, these happened, we are fulfilling our promise to you. That we will leave the Philippines, undeniably better than what we found it to be.

Those currently being educated through the Pantawid Pamilya program, of course, will not graduate within six years—we still have K-12. Those who have been helped by TESDA, 72 percent, other industries that really cooperated with us extensively, have a placement rate of 95 to 98 percent. The number of investors coming in here…

And I might forget, there's a company in Concepcion, there's a company, Luen Thai, that makes Coach bags. I spoke with the owner recently and he wants to increase their thousands of employees here in Tarlac. I might forget, let Vic handle this Luen Thai, which really provides a lot of help.

This Clark Green City, we already have a partnership with Filinvest who are really building the first phase of it. The question here, in this organization, is that promises are not broken. We promised, and you are witnesses that it is coming true.

All of Mar Roxas' and Leni Robredo's opponents, the best we can say is, “maybe.” Maybe they will surpass them, we hope so, if they can manage it. Maybe they are experienced, maybe they won't be dictators, maybe what they say will be true. You know, one opponent of Mar said, “I will make that 4Ps into 5Ps.” [Laughter] You probably know who that is. But that's only half of it. “And while I'm making it 5Ps, I will reduce taxes.” [Laughter]

Here in Tarlac, that kind of line doesn't really work, right? Because the wives in front of me, when your husband comes home and says, “Next month, I will reduce your budget.” You'll start to get angry now. “And after I reduce your budget, you'll increase what you buy.” Can you do that? Why did he say he'll make the 4Ps into 5Ps and also reduce taxes? You're really clever. I know here in Tarlac, we don't get fooled like that. [Applause]

Leni Robredo has an opponent, she supposedly visited us recently, and she promised here in Tarlac that she will defeat the candidates I endorsed. Will that happen, my brothers and sisters? I think those of you who are older here, you remember the panel truck back then. [Laughter] She said, “I am ready to apologize if I knew what I should apologize for.” What does that mean? Everything they did was right. So if it’s right, we’ll repeat it. And if we repeat it, everything will be fine with us.

I’ll go back to you, my brothers and sisters: Mar and Leni, you might not know them as well. Why? What did Mar do? He worked and worked. It’s not in our culture in this organization, when there’s a fire or another disaster, “Run there, get ahead!” “I’ll be here, I’ll give relief goods. Once I’ve given three relief goods, I’m leaving.” Mar, when there’s a disaster, he’s there, like during Yolanda. Before it arrived, he prepared what was needed to help our countrymen when the storm hit. He stayed until the water was restored, the electricity, the police who were missing were fixed—twenty police officers out of 290 were left there. After that, all we read are criticisms of him. We are being defeated by those who delivered a few pieces of relief goods.

Leni, on the other hand—I really remember my father and mother. The two of them said, “They wouldn’t have reached where they are if they hadn’t had each other as partners.” You know, I clearly remember when I was young, my grandfather, my mother’s father, was worried because my father had been sentenced to death: death by musketry. My grandfather, Lolo Pepe, offered, “Can I be the one you imprison? Release Ninoy, let him leave the Philippines. While I’m imprisoned, I will be the guarantee that Ninoy will behave.” My grandfather, when he died, was also in his mid-70s, he was already old. That’s how much he loved my father, “Imprison me instead. Release Ninoy.” Then what happened was, my father was sentenced to death, he was here in Luisita, walking in the garden, a vein burst—it’s called a cerebral hemorrhage—he died. I believe he was worried about how he could help his son-in-law who felt so sorry for him.

Leni and Jesse, they both really supported each other, helped each other, that’s why, everything Jesse showed, if his wife didn’t say anything but “Stop that. Don’t,” nothing would have happened. But Jesse really showed us what is called “true public service.” Not taking advantage, but really contributing, helping, sympathizing with all the problems.

I didn't really know Leni back then, but Jesse chose to keep his family's life separate from politics. It was a good intention to give them a normal life. So when the accident happened that took Jesse's life, that was the first time I spoke to Leni. And when we met, it had been more than 24 hours since Jesse was gone, how do you tell someone you don't know very well that the news isn't good? It's natural, of course, for her to cry a lot. It's natural for her to be angry. There are so many things that could be done. But that's when I really saw how strong Leni is, that instead of us comforting her during her time of loss, we could say that we were given strength because Leni was there. Instead of us supporting her, she supported us. [Applause] We've also seen this in her stances on various laws that she has also passed.

So, I'll go back to, why Mar? Mar doesn't need to promise, he's already with us on the Straight Path, even before the 2010 campaign. When we needed to be in the opposition, he was with us in the opposition. When we needed to run this country, to put it on the right track, Mar Roxas was with us too. All the hardships, I could say, he followed my lead. In Zamboanga, he went ahead. In Yolanda, he went ahead. There were many times he went ahead as Secretary of the Interior and Local Government before I arrived. What I did, Mar did too. But when we read it in the newspaper, of course, what's mentioned is, “Mar went around Samar, a storm arrived. The road was closed, he rode a motorcycle.” Instead of saying, “The Secretary of the Interior and Local Government went around to find out the condition of our countrymen, which is his job.” What was highlighted was, “Mar Roxas' motorcycle fell.” That's the same.

I'll repeat it to you: It's courtship season. Those who are smiling in front of me, do you remember when you were being courted? Many courted you, they said beautiful things, but you only married one. You should only marry one. But the point is: Who was the most genuine? Who did you choose from all those who approached you? It's the same with this. All the beautiful words, all the hair fixing—thankfully, I don't fix mine much—everything being done to make you say “Choose me” is happening now. There are posters out there, pictures of them when they graduated from high school are attached, even if it was 50 years ago.

What are we saying? Continue the Straight Path. What happened to us, wasn't it, we stumbled, we stood up, we learned to walk. Walking now, of course, running comes next. All that we planted in these six years, we are harvesting now. But there will be a bigger harvest next time, unless we replace those who are leading us. They'll say, “Instead of going straight, let's make a left turn, a right turn, a U-turn.” That's fine with us.

To be sure, let us focus on what is real, on what we can achieve, not just promises, but actual accomplishments. The next president, with your permission, is none other than Mar Roxas.
