[MOCK TRANSLATION] Talumpati ng Kagalang-galang Benigno S. Aquino III Pangulo ng Pilipinas Sa pagtatapos ng Philippine Military Academy Pudang Kalis Class ng 2013

Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III President of the Philippines At the graduation of the Philippine Military Academy Pudang Kalis Class of 2013

[Declared at Fort del Pilar, Baguio City, on March 17, 2013]

I couldn't help but ask our Secretary of National Defense earlier if you were imitating his disposition, because in all the graduations I've attended, you were the only one who graduated looking like you had a “tiger look.” Did you graduate with resentment? I suppose you were just being professional.

It has become a yearly tradition for Presidents to address the cadets here at Fort Del Pilar, to welcome and honor the graduating class of the Philippine Military Academy. This is the third time I have been given this honor, and I will never tire of being with you—not only as your Commander-in-Chief, but also to personally witness a success collectively achieved by the Filipino people.

I say this because wasn't your march onto the stage earlier a symbol of the contribution of a larger society towards achieving your aspirations? It is true that you went through the examinations, you woke up at dawn for physical training, and you carried a great deal of sacrifice on your shoulders to reach this point; my admiration for what you have been through is immeasurable. But also consider this: Before you could finish high school and be accepted as a plebe here at PMA, your families also went through sacrifices to allow you to study, and their support continues until this point. Your teachers and counselors also stayed up late to ensure they had something meaningful to teach you. The entire nation shouldered the responsibility of building and operating the institution of the Philippine Military Academy, and will continue to contribute throughout the length of your careers as members of the Armed Forces of the Philippines.

These are just examples of the hardships faced by your valedictorian, Jestoni Lanaja. His upbringing was not luxurious. One of his brothers was unable to attend college, and one of his sisters could not have her disability checked at a hospital. But through gathering tuba, his father was able to support Jestoni until he became a cadet here at PMA; and now, he holds the Presidential Saber, a symbol of the honor and responsibility that comes with being class valedictorian.

It’s also a success story, for example, that of Prolen Bonacua, who even before being assigned as a soldier, already fought a battle and overcame Hodgkin’s lymphoma. Now, he will graduate as third in your class. I am certain that the honor and joy he feels now is equal to, if not greater than, that of his father, a machinist, and his mother, a factory worker—who both toiled to ensure Prolen would not face cancer alone, and that opportunities would open for their child, thanks to a good education.

The Pudang Kalis Class of 2013 is not lacking in stories of hardship and triumph: from Maryam Balais, your salutatorian who even suffered a hand injury during training, to Cadets Aragona, Gonzales, Landicho, and your other friends who were once crew members at Jollibee before entering the PMA—all of you are beneficiaries not only of your own efforts, but also of the contributions of your fellowmen. Therefore, the challenge to you now is this: May the responsibility and contribution shown by those who helped you give birth to a deep sense of responsibility in your hearts and minds, so that you, in turn, will dedicate effort and time to your fellowmen. The nation collectively achieved this success of yours; I hope that the focus of each of your actions will also be collective—because isn’t it true that all problems stem from thinking only of self-interest?

Let us just look, for example, at the situation in Sabah. Did those who initiated this ever think about how many would be affected by causing trouble? There are an estimated hundreds of thousands of Filipinos residing and earning a living peacefully in Sabah; what if our neighbor suddenly sends them home after we have nurtured a relationship with them for so long to restore trust? We all know how complex this issue is: Would a Prime Minister of Malaysia simply agree to relinquish a place that has been under their laws for so long? Would a President of the Philippines simply let go of the claim they are pursuing? Put yourselves in my position: whether the grievances of those who went there are legitimate or not, how will this be weighed against the lives and livelihoods that will be put in danger if conflict begins? We all know that every action has a reaction, and there are problems that cannot be resolved quickly— problems that will only give birth to other problems if force or recklessness is used. What is needed: careful and honest analysis and discussion, so that the right solution can mature.

Did the promoters of this venture ever consider this while they were inciting and fueling it—while detailing the huge amount of money needed to hire a boat, buy fuel, provisions, guns and ammunition? You studied this in your Logistics classes; you know the preparations needed to transport two hundred people across the sea, and to arm some of them. What exactly was going through the minds of those behind this incident?

The truth is clear: whoever masterminded this only thought of their own self-interest, while trampling on the welfare of their fellow citizens. And even if we believe that those who went there simply wanted to take a stand—wouldn't it be more productive if that stand was pursued through reasonable discussion, and if it followed the law and clear policies?

Let us look at the situation in the West Philippine Sea as an example. Isn't it true that, like in Sabah, we are advocating for a stance based on a rules-based approach, and focused on peaceful resolution, in Bajo de Masinloc? If we resort to brute force and recklessness, the problem will only escalate, and will likely be passed on to the next generation. We have also seen the positive results of talking with our fellow Filipinos who want to advance the Bangsamoro: Sincere dialogue is more effective, more productive, and more beneficial for everyone than pointing guns at each other. Calmness and reason are not equivalent to cowardice—rather, they are a measure of true courage and conviction, because in this way, we consider the welfare not only of those in the present, but especially of those who will come in the future.

That is precisely the crossroads you will face as soldiers. Will you succumb to anger, or will you face challenges calmly? Will you take shortcuts in your work, or will you consider the impact of your actions and decisions on those who come after you? Will you simply pass on the problems that come before you to the next desk, or will you pour out all your strength, all your effort, all your “Heart and Honor of the Soldiers of a United Race” to solve them, and not pass them on to the next generation of Filipinos?

As the next leaders of our uniformed ranks, a great responsibility will be placed on your shoulders. And as someone slightly older than you, perhaps this is the lesson I will leave you with: Whenever you face a crossroads, simply put yourself in the place of the most aggrieved, the most oppressed, the poorest, those most in need of compassion—and surely, it will become clear what is right and what is wrong. Those on the fringes of society, our Bosses, the people, will direct our course; as long as their welfare is in our minds, we will surely not lose our way.

You know what, I will be frank with you, being President of the Philippines is not easy. Some say I need to take a vacation, because they notice the depth of my eye bags and especially how much thinner my hair has become. That’s why I’m happy to come to PMA because I have longer hair than you. There is a part of me that agrees with them—but a larger part says that I only have six years in office, six years to lay the foundations for the changes we have long desired. We are almost halfway through our term, and in the face of the successes we are gradually reaping, I am even more encouraged. So, like your forced marches: No matter how heavy my burden, as long as I can push my right foot forward, and follow it with my left—especially if I know you are behind me and the entire Filipino nation is ready to contribute to the betterment of our society—we will continue to pursue our dreams.

As I have said to those before you: The training is over, the theory is over, your four-year vacation here at Fort Del Pilar is over; it is time for the real adventure. Go forth and show that our united nation did not make a mistake in choosing you as members of the Pudang Kalis Class of 2013 of the Philippine Military Academy.

Thank you very much, and good day to all of you.
