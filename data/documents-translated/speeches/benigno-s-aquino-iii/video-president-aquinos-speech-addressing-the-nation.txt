Statement of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, Regarding the incident in Mamasapano, Maguindanao

[Delivered at the Malacañan Palace on January 28, 2015]

My fellow citizens, good evening to all of you.

I am addressing you today to report what we know about what happened in Mamasapano, Maguindanao, last Saturday and Sunday. We are doing this not to preempt the board of inquiry designated to uncover the whole truth, but because you have the right to know what we know at this point.

Last Saturday night, January 24th, a group of members of the Special Action Force of our Philippine National Police went to Barangay Tukanalipao, Mamasapano, Maguindanao. Their mission was to serve outstanding warrants of arrest on two notorious terrorists long sought by authorities: Abdul Basit Usman and Zulkipli Bin Hir, alias Abu Marwan. In the performance of their duty, 44 of our police officers were killed, while 16 were wounded, including 3 civilians, according to the latest report from our NDRRMC.

As President, as Father of the Nation, it is deeply saddening to think that our police officers had to sacrifice their lives in this mission. And if there is a definition of a hero, it is them: Those who faced danger to prevent a threat to our safety; those who were wounded; those who offered their lives in the name of peace. As a sign of respect for those who died, we will declare a National Day of Mourning as a symbol of the grief and sympathy of our entire nation.

Marwan and Usman were not ordinary criminals. There is a long list of warrants against them; Usman alone has at least eight outstanding warrants, while Marwan has at least two. 2002 was one of the earliest, so that means, even when I was still a Congressman, they were already being hunted. Let me clarify: When there is a warrant against a person, every law enforcement officer is duty-bound to enforce it. That is why, even before, many operations to arrest or attempt to arrest Marwan and Usman, as well as other terrorists, were conducted by various branches of the security sector, including the AFP, PNP, and NBI.

They did not always ask for my permission, because it would be impractical to wait for clearance from me. My duty: to ensure that they fulfill their responsibility. There were times when they would escalate the situation to us to give advice, look at the situation from a broader perspective, or explain the possible implications.

For example, this includes the response to rogue MNLF elements in Zamboanga, the arrest of the top individuals on our Most Wanted Persons list, or the raid on our peacekeepers in the Golan Heights. In the case of the Golan Heights, the battalion commander there alone could not decide on their withdrawal. As having primary responsibility for our foreign relations, we needed to be informed to ensure that our obligations to the United Nations are also being addressed.

Marwan was part of the Central Committee of Jemaah Islamiya, which carried out the Bali bombing in Indonesia. Here, two successive explosions occurred, so first responders and others who did not leave were hit. 202 people died here, and Marwan is a suspect in this. In Cagayan de Oro in 2012, Marwan attempted to imitate this modus operandi; a second bomb was discovered so it did not explode, but two people still died in this incident. There are allegations that in 2006, together with Umbra Kato, Marwan led the planting of a bomb to attempt the life of the then-governor of Maguindanao, Andal Ampatuan. Because Marwan was a member of international terrorist networks, with connections to other terrorist groups, he was able to acquire knowledge, equipment, funds, in addition to creating safe havens for his fellow terrorists. This is why he was considered the primary target of the operation. Usman, meanwhile, is linked to nine bombing incidents in Mindanao. He is the main accused in the bombing in General Santos City in 2002, where 15 were killed and 60 were injured.

Together with another terrorist named Mawiyah, Marwan and Usman carried out terrorism in various parts of Mindanao. The two are also known trainers in bomb-making. There are reports that they have a workshop for improvised explosive devices, which they sell to their fellow terrorists. They have already hurt and killed many people, and as long as they are free, the threat to the safety of our citizens continues.

Let me emphasize: In Article II, Section 4 of the Constitution, it states that “The primary duty of government is to serve and protect the people.” That is why, when our police learned of the precise location of Marwan and Usman, they decided to act to enforce the warrants against them. Actionable intelligence was gathered by our authorities: They identified not only the region, province, or municipality, but the very houses where the two were hiding. If this information was not acted upon, Marwan and Usman could escape, and we would have to start the long process of searching for them again.

Past 4 a.m. on Sunday, the Special Action Force reached the stronghold where Marwan and Usman were hiding. In the ensuing encounter, the main target, Marwan, was reportedly killed. When the shooting was heard, Usman and his companions fought back. There were non-combatants seen in the residences of Marwan and Usman; our forces really needed to get close to them to avoid harming the innocent. The house of Marwan was attacked first; Usman responded and the element of surprise was lost, which would make it more difficult to fulfill their mission. That is why the SAF decided to withdraw and rendezvous with their comrades who were guarding their route out of the encounter area. It appears that the bloodiest part of the clash happened during this withdrawal.

Like many others, I also have questions about the incident, so we expect that the board of inquiry formed will find the truth about this incident. In the briefings given to me by the PNP regarding the continuing operation against Marwan and Usman, I repeatedly stressed the need for proper, sufficient, and timely coordination. The terrain in this operation is said to be complex: the land itself is muddy, with swampland, marshes, and rivers that need to be crossed to reach the destination of our SAF. There are many forces scattered in this area: the BIFF, MILF, and even a Private Armed Group. Even if the MILF and BIFF are already separate, many of them are related by blood or marriage. Strangers cannot simply enter. Our troops need to enter quietly and slowly; otherwise, their targets may be alerted.

Especially since the SAF force is not large compared to those around who may interfere, it is important that the Armed Forces be on standby to position troops, equipment, and supplies such as cannons if our police need support. They need enough time to deploy their forces where they can be most helpful. And in this kind of firefight, if assistance from the Armed Forces is needed, it will not arrive immediately, especially since the members of the 6th Infantry Division, who are closest to the action, have various duties.

In my repeated reminders about the need for coordination, the SAF director replied, “Yes Sir.” He only said that operational security is also needed, or ensuring that only those who need to know about the operation are informed. However, I stressed that other branches or their heads still need to be alerted; the notice needs to be given at the right time, and the information needs to be complete, so they can prepare properly.

My question: Why and how did it happen that the AFP battalion near the operation was only informed when they were already close to or at the jump-off point? The problem here is that the soldiers belonging to this battalion were scattered in various locations guarding the main supply route in this area. In other words, the notice came very close to the time of the encounter, and it is difficult to say whether there was enough time to prepare assistance if needed. If there was compliance with my order to ensure sufficient coordination, it was a very minimum compliance. I was surprised to learn that the commander of the Western Mindanao Command, or even the 6th Infantry Division, was only informed after the first encounter with Marwan and Usman; the SAF force was already leaving, and problems were already arising at this point.

On the side of the MILF: We have made great strides because we trust each other. We have proven that we can work together: In 2014, a Japanese national was rescued in Maguindanao; in the same year, a bombing in Maguindanao was prevented. I also read Chairman Al Haj Murad's statement about the incident in Mamasapano; their formation of a Special Investigative Commission to determine the details and truth of the incident is a good first step. I hope that soon, the MILF will show more concrete proof of solidarity in pursuing peace, towards seeking the truth, and holding those responsible accountable.

Let us also avoid spreading speculation about the events. As it is written in the Bible: The truth shall set us free. A board of inquiry has been designated to gather the whole truth. Let us just wait for its results.

We have come a long way towards the peace we have long desired for Mindanao. Everyone has shown great trust to reach this point. In the incident in Mamasapano, there are those who are taking advantage of the tragedy to reduce trust; they want the peace process to fail. There are also those suggesting that the passage of the Bangsamoro Basic Law in the House and Senate be stopped.

This should not happen. The entire peace process is at stake in this law. If the passage of the law fails soon, the peace process will fail, and the status quo will remain. If that is the case, what else can we expect but the same result: People losing hope and fleeing to the mountains; those who have been denied justice choosing to commit violence against others. We would have, in effect, helped Marwan and Usman achieve their goals. Do we want to return to the point where communities are always ready to run to evacuation centers, because there is always a threat of gunfire? If that happens, who will benefit? If the peace process fails, how many more graves will we need to dig? How many more children will idolize people like Marwan; how many will want to become Usman; how many engineers will choose to make bombs, rather than build buildings?

Let us also consider this: The members of the Special Action Force perished while fulfilling their duty to maintain peace and order. If the peace process does not succeed; if we return to the status quo, or if the violence worsens, wouldn't this be the very opposite of what they sacrificed their lives for?

Isn't it true that: In the face of the challenges ahead to advance peace, we should even more unite, and we should even more pursue the next steps such as the passage of the Bangsamoro Basic Law; the establishment of the Bangsamoro Transition Authority; expanding opportunities for all; and correcting the political system where a few benefit from the misfortune of so many of our countrymen.

To the families of the fallen members of the Special Action Force: I deeply feel the grief you are experiencing. I also know that this may be accompanied by anxiety for your future, especially if your relative who sacrificed for this operation was the breadwinner. I assure you: The state will provide everything it can provide according to laws and regulations. On this occasion, I directly appeal to the public that if possible, let us also provide aid, and maximize help to the families of the fallen, as recognition of the greatness of the heroes who offered their lives for the peace we aspire to.

In the face of our mourning, in the face of the desires of some to retaliate and seek revenge, in the face of the threat to change the trust we have forged over a long period of time, now, our ability to show greater compassion for our fellow human beings is being tested. That is why, to all my fellow peace-seekers, from legislators, to members of the uniformed ranks, to the leaders and members of the MILF, to our countrymen in Bangsamoro, to every decent Filipino: Let us show what a nation bound by a common aspiration can achieve. Let us ensure that the sacrifice of the fallen members of the Special Action Force will not be in vain. We will achieve justice, hopefully, through the proper process, and without letting go of our dreams of achieving broad and lasting peace.

Thank you very much, and good evening to all of you.
