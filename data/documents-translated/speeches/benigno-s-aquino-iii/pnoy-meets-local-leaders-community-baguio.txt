Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

In a meeting with local leaders and communities of Baguio

[Declared in the City of Baguio, Benguet, on March 13, 2016]

They told me, I will go to Baguio and I will convince people. But many have already been convinced here. I remember, maybe you remember this too, but for those who haven't heard: Sometimes, when I go to a place where many are already convinced, it becomes a little difficult. How do you convince someone who is already convinced?

This happened on this day: It was still Martial Law when my father died. In a place in the Visayas, there was a former Senator. He was already a Senator before Martial Law. Then he arrived at a plaza because there was a rally there. When he arrived—he was a little late—the people he found were only three. He said to the emcee, “Is there still a need for me here, when I only have three people to talk to?” The emcee said, “Sir, those three haven't been convinced yet. Maybe if you speak, we can convince them. It would be a waste.” When I go to a place and there are only three people, of course, I'll just borrow three chairs and I'll just stand there. Just approach them, have a conversation. But this politician was a bit traditional, when he saw the stage and the mic, he went up the stage and used the mic to talk to the three people left in the plaza.

Midway through his speech, one person stood up and left. He looked at the emcee, “Sir, it seems you’re the only one they’re waiting for. You’ve convinced them. Please continue. You just need to convince two more, and we’ll be at 100 percent here.” He continued his speech, and as he was about to finish, another person stood up and left again. Only one remained. He looked at the emcee again, “You’re really great, sir. You convinced two of the unconvinced. Continue, just one more and we’re all set.” The speech ended and he was no longer left by the very last person. He approached him now. He said, “Brad, I’m sure you’re convinced now.” The reply was, “Sir, to be honest, even before you arrived, I was already convinced. In fact, the sound system you’re using is mine. I was waiting for you to finish so I could take it home.” [Laughter]

It’s really difficult when you go to a place where everyone is already convinced. But maybe there are still some we haven’t convinced outside, I just want to talk to them, please help me.

You know, often when I go to Baguio, I tell stories about my youth. My father was a very busy man. Honestly, he didn’t want to bother our family, so when someone visited the house, he would talk to them, he would insist on finishing at the house so we would have some privacy, he would take them to his office, and talk to everyone who wanted to talk to him there. With the many places he went, when night came, we would already be asleep, because we were children and studying in grade school, he didn’t want to bother us. So sometimes, he would come home very early, meaning at nine o’clock in the evening, he would be carrying siopao and siomai, he would wake us up because we were just falling asleep. My younger sisters, while listening to him, would be like that. I, on the other hand, was eager to join him with the siopao and siomai, the two of us understood each other very well.

In Baguio, we often went on vacation, before Martial Law. And when we arrived in Baguio, we were like a complete normal family. We always went to church together. All meals, breakfast, lunch, dinner, we were together. He would take me horseback riding, or maybe boating in Burnham park. [Ronnie] experienced it, he showed me back then, there were cars there, bicycles there, and others. It felt like when we were there, he really played the role of a father to us. Complete during the vacation with him, not working.

We reached a point where we were getting older, in college. I still vividly remember Baguio; our parents treated us as adults within our group of friends. That meant they allowed us to go on vacation together, just us friends. We were all eighteen years old and above. And it felt like you had accomplished so much when you were driving alone. Cooking, budgeting, everything at that time, it felt like we were becoming adults.

Let me tell you a story: Once, during our first time going there, to be honest, there was a bit of cheating. We cooked food at our house to bring with us, but it was only half-cooked. So when we arrived here, all we had to do was reheat it. But my father, during Martial Law, he gave us the opportunity to visit three times a week. I would go home earlier than my friends because I would visit my father. When I left my friends—because of course, we were acting like we could already take care of ourselves, we were old enough—they cooked food. They brought canned goods. They got a pan, filled the pan with water, placed the can in the pan of water, and turned on the stove. They said, “It will heat up.” And it did heat up, but they didn't open the can. The can exploded, and the ceiling was covered. Of course, we didn't tell our parents about it, because we weren't that skilled yet.

The point of this is: Whenever I think of Baguio, there are so many memories. Of course, including picking strawberries yourself. I think for many people, a rite of passage as a child was going to Baguio, going to Good Shepherd, and eating peanut brittle. For lunch, a snack, fresh strawberries. And when you were leaving, strawberry jam. When you got older, strawberry wine. They, I haven't really drunk much of it. I just taste it. That's all I can handle.

Let me add—and this, I should say before I forget: You know, when I ran for Senator, I came to you, I knocked on your doors, Baguio and Benguet, you did not abandon us. [Applause] When I ran for President, Baguio and Benguet again, you did not abandon us. [Applause] In 2013, I asked for your help again, and truly, our senatorial candidates won 9-3 here in Baguio and Benguet. And that was the final result. In all of this, thank you very much to all of you. [Applause]

Here, I will use a few notes. What did Baguio and Benguet gain when you believed in me? Let's first look at the infrastructure: bridges, roads, and the like. Just for the DPWH for now, we also have what we call Private-Public Partnerships. The one we replaced, they reportedly gave you P5.69 billion from 2005 to 2010. That's about five years. We, from 2011 to 2016, turned their P5.69 billion into P12.08 billion for infrastructure. [Applause] Where did that go? The DPWH reported to us: to concreting, upgrading the Gurel-Bokod-Kabayan-Buguias-Abatan Road, Baguio Circumferential Road-Eastern Link, Ballay-Tabio-Tonoman-Bulalacao-Tawangan-Lusod Road, Ambangeg Junction National Road, and many others.

Let me emphasize: We don't just consistently help one sector. It's not just infrastructure. But I will add this: In farm-to-market roads, the DPWH told us that we have completed 200 of them here in Benguet. The completed irrigation projects are at 242 from 2011 to the present. We have also brought in three agricultural tramlines in Benguet. With the tramline, before you would carry your product, cross the mountain, and when it reaches the road, the vegetables would be bruised, not fit for a salad. This tramline brings it closer to the road, you can take it to the market, it looks more perfect, of course, the price is better. Three of them have been built. The flagship project here that seems to have become controversial in agriculture is the Benguet Agri-Pinoy Trading Center being built in La Trinidad. We are aiming to help an estimated 20,538 farmers. It was partially opened in October, and is expected to be fully operational in the 2nd quarter of this year. We also have 37 Shared Service Facilities from the DTI. For example: In the loom weaving industry, a Shared Service Facility was built for the Sitio Upper Goldfield Weavers Association in 2013. Their income per year was P100,000. Because of this facility, they were able to increase their income to P141,000, and that's not even the full year of 2014. I'm sure that in 2014, it contributed much more.

Let's look at the IT-BPM industry—call centers and others. Mar Roxas, perhaps not everyone knows, he started this industry in the Philippines when he was Secretary of the Department of Trade and Industry. The beginning of this industry with direct employment was 2,400 people. The total revenue at that time was $20 million in 2000. In 2014, direct employment in this sector reached 1.07 million, and the total added to our economy was $18.9 billion. The DTI told us that this industry continues to grow, and is expected to reach 1.3 million direct employees this year, and add $25 billion to our economy. And one of the main areas that is a center of the IT-BPM industry is Baguio. [Applause]

Someone might say, especially our opponents, “Okay, Mar Roxas started that. But what help did he give?” That's where Kuya Joel and TESDA come in. [Applause] They have training for IT-BPM… When you hear Joel's jingle, it was during the 1970s. It feels different, so that's proof of who is older among us. He's just trying to win by having more hair. The Training for Work Scholarship Program or TWSP of TESDA has helped 25,314 people from 2010 to 2015. Seven thousand of these went to what we call the IT-BPM industry. It's good here, according to a TESDA study, in 2014, we reached 71.9 percent placement rates for TESDA graduates. You know, when we came in, it was only 28.5 percent, almost one-fourth of TESDA trainees were able to find a job within six months. During Joel's time, almost 72 percent have a job within six months after training. [Applause] He always highlights this in the accomplishment report… Let me give you an example: The slaughterhouse that is now very famous in Cavite and other places, the starting monthly salary was P160,000, going to Australia and Canada. There is a lot of demand. He said a mechanic went to Australia, it was P250,000. We have an OFW who returned to the Philippines, studied massage therapy with him, after two years, if I'm not mistaken, he has built four spas and has 35 employees. Those in the Coke Star Program—helping sari-sari store owners, teaching them budgeting on which products to stock, how to display them, how to do bookkeeping, how to do accounting, basically all the skills taught to grow their income. They were helped, and most of their stories are like this. Before the program came, this woman earned P800 a day. When she finished the program, she earned P5,000 a day. At P5,000, multiply that by 30 days, it will probably be P150,000. Everything I told you, their salaries are higher than the salary of the President of the Republic of the Philippines. So that's okay. That's why I really admire Kuya Joel, and his colleagues at TESDA, including what Mark Lapid is doing for tourism, because for every foreign tourist who comes to the Philippines, it is estimated that one job is provided to Filipinos. From three million, we are heading towards six million this year. Imagine how many jobs have been created.

Now, regarding the IT-BPO—I always say that it’s direct employment—what does that mean? Those who work in IT-BPM, of course, need places to go to like 7-11, and others, or convenience stores. They need transportation, they need housing. There are so many additional jobs that support the IT-BPM, so the factor is 3 ½ to 4. For every one IT-BPM employee, there are three to four additional jobs for our overall economy.

Let's move on to the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program. What we saw before, it seemed like the 4Ps were being used for politics. You know, when it started, there was something called the National Household Targeting Survey, or in Tagalog, it’s called Listahanan. We were looking for who were the most in need in our society. And the result that came out: 4.6 million households could be said to be “living below the poverty line.” Those who replaced it, they only helped 780,000 households. We, this year, will be able to fill the 4.6 million households. I suppose you all know what the 4Ps are? Those who know the 4Ps can raise their hands? We have a few. Let me just explain: Our belief is that we need to maximize opportunities for every Filipino. Those who can’t afford to send their children to school, we provide them with support. The condition: Keep your child in school; that is one of the main conditions.

What is the result? With the help we are doing, in the initial study, there is now Listahanan 2, the second survey, validation of the first. There are now 7.7 million Filipinos who have risen above what is called the “poverty line” and it won’t be long before we have a new program for those we call the “near poor,” to further distance them from poverty. What does “near poor” mean? You’ve already risen, you have funds, but suddenly a serious illness occurs. When a serious illness occurs, all your savings are used up, or maybe an earthquake happens, or a typhoon, all your savings are used up, and you fall back into poverty. Those are the next programs. That is what it means to continue the Straight Path: Let’s pursue and distance them further and further from the so-called “poverty line.”

In Benguet, look, when I took office… Like this, 780,000—if you’ll allow me, I’ll round it off, let’s make it 800,000. The provinces of the Philippines, 81—to make the computation easier as well, let’s make it 80. Let’s divide 800,000 by 80 provinces, the result, to be equal, is 10,000 each. Others will say, the population can’t be equal. But on average, hopefully it’s close to ten thousand.

Upon assuming office, the number of beneficiaries here in Benguet was 2,151. Now, in 2015, we are covering 14,713 households here in Benguet. [Applause]

In Cebu—which I believe has a larger population than Benguet—when we arrived, only over 5,600 households were covered. To be honest, Benguet is actually fortunate because in Bohol, with their total population, only 531 households were being assisted when we came. And in Cavite, I was surprised the other day to discover that we started with 4Ps, with none, zero—there were apparently no households covered by 4Ps in Cavite when we arrived.

Recently, a survey came out and it said that four out of every five Filipinos would vote for someone who will continue the Pantawid Pamilya program. And it’s not surprising, candidates claiming credit for the success of this program have emerged, even though they have always been against the Straight Path. Some say they will make the Conditional Cash Transfer “unconditional.” That’s okay. The first “C” will be removed, so it will just become “Cash Transfer.” Hopefully, that cash transfer will reach those who need it. Of course, we will ask: Isn’t it “conditional” cash transfer? Everyone who is eligible to be a beneficiary is treated equally. If you remove the conditions, what will happen? Will they just dictate, point to who will be included? Let’s also include—with no conditions—include everyone, even those who don’t need it. Someone even said, “I will increase your benefits while decreasing your taxes.” Is that even possible? The housewives here, can you increase what you can buy while decreasing your budget? If there are any, raise your hand. I will make you Budget Secretary tomorrow.

Another one will give sacks of rice. Sometimes I wonder: It’s amazing how they all make these promises, but there’s no proof that they can actually deliver. There’s one who made an even bolder statement, “I will expand the benefits of 4Ps.” But his own son shouted—we heard this around 2013—“That program needs to be investigated!” This, in the face of numerous allegations of corruption against him that he refuses to answer or clarify. Would you entrust the large funds of Pantawid Pamilya, which is already helping many of our countrymen, to these candidates and their promises?

You know, with the Straight Path, we are beginning to reach the potential not only of your province, but of the entire country, including Baguio. Of course, we wouldn't have reached this point without your support, my Bosses, and that of your local leaders. If you hadn't given me a mandate, I wouldn't have been able to lead you here. If you hadn't trusted me that we could achieve what we aspire to, we wouldn't have even tried, and the things that push us to leave this country would have continued.

With the leadership of our good Vice Governor Nelson Dangwa, along with Congressman Nick Aliping Jr. and Congressman Ronnie Cosalan, nothing can stop the progress of your province and your city. [Applause]

In the coming elections, the people will once again face a crossroads. It is indeed a referendum: Will we continue what we have started so well? Or will we return to the old ways? As far as I'm concerned, we are already with someone who is sure to continue the Straight Path. Among all the candidates, in my opinion, he is the only one who truly has “K”: conscience, capability, compassion for others, and of course, Korina. No one else but Mar Roxas. [Applause]

Perhaps, if Mar had been my partner from the beginning, and he was our running mate, I am sure we would have gone much further. Think about it: If something happens to the President, the Vice President will take over the position? If they have different stances, everything that has been started will be derailed and stopped. That's how important it is to vote for a tandem.

Now, Aika is here, you've already heard her, as the representative of the champion of the Straight Path for Vice President, who is none other than Leni Robredo. Who wouldn't admire Leni? You know, in our family, the month of August is a bit tense for us when August comes. Why? Perhaps in the past, our father almost got caught in the Plaza Miranda bombing, that was August 21st. My father was killed on August 21st. My mother also passed away on August 1st. I was ambushed on August 28th. So many things happen in August. And on August 18th, Jesse passed away as well.

Now, Jesse, like my father, wanted to give his family a private life as well. He didn't want to involve them in politics. And when the time came that Jesse was missing, I, as Jesse's colleague, as we can say his chief, had the obligation to inform his family about the results of our search for Jesse. And it turned out that almost no one really got to be with Leni because Jesse insisted on not involving them in political life. I can place any bible on top of my hand and tell you: Of course, I was worried, how would I tell Leni and her children what happened, or what hasn't been confirmed yet, but looks like bad news? How would I ease their burden as the father of the nation? Honestly, I was surprised by Leni's resilience. I didn't see a single tear fall. And to me, I felt that it was heavy for her, but she still understands others. She doesn't want to [worsen] the situation of her fellow citizens.

With the great admiration, recognition, and gratitude for Jesse, many wanted to help his widow and his children. Let me tell you: Not once did we notice Leni wanting or even trying to abuse or take advantage of anyone. So many were rejected, those who voluntarily offered to help them, and she didn't want to tarnish Jesse's reputation by going around everywhere.

She didn't have any plans to run for office—Aika mentioned that earlier—she was persuaded, and ran in their district in Camarines Sur. She had a tough opponent, and she won. She could have pursued re-election—re-election is easier than looking for another position. But when the people called, their whole family had a hard time deciding. Aika explained why. There came a point where a decision was made: If the people suggest it, if the people need her, she is ready to enter a higher position, which we can say is a more bloody battle, politics is dirtier. Because that is really her character, that is what we can expect from Leni: strong, and truly caring for others. That's why I am really appealing to you, we really need a Mar Roxas here, we still have a Leni Robredo. The Straight Path is surely continued. [Applause]

Comrades, earlier, I spoke with the new graduates of the PMA. They asked me, “What do you think is your legacy?” I said, the most vivid thing for me is that we have changed the mindset of our countrymen. Here, not far from you, I visited Apayao, and someone recounted a story. They said their community was quite remote. When someone gets sick, they would pass through the forest, trek for two days to bring the sick person to the nearest hospital. Our forest, it became like a cemetery because they no longer reached the hospital. They said now, they have a road so it only takes minutes to get to the hospital. Our good Governor Butchie Bulot even joked. He said to me, “Back then here with us, it was about having a beautiful ambulance.” I asked, why does the ambulance need to be beautiful? “That was the only healthcare, being able to rush you to the hospital.” That was then. Now, he says, “We are now the referral hospital for neighboring provinces.”

It’s easy to see the before and the after, the past and the present. What I said about those we helped in Benguet, it wasn’t just in Benguet, it wasn’t just in Baguio City. Every province, people no longer need to bow their heads and say, “I’m sorry. I fell short.” Because we can say, “How far the national government has helped you, and how many results there are because we came together, we dared to dream, and we really did everything to achieve the dreams we once had.” The people of Apayao said, they never thought they would stop dreaming of having a road. Now, it’s a reality.

The question is, on May 9th, we will be asked: Where are we going? We suggest, we have really come a long way on the Straight Path. I truly stand by my promise to you, I will leave this country much better than I found it. The question is: Who wants to gamble what we have achieved? It’s like we just learned to walk now. We are running now. Then suddenly we will be told, “Stop. Left turn or right turn?” But the most painful thing, “U-turn.” Do you want to go back to that situation?

Let me give you an example of a “U-turn”: One of the projects I stopped was the dredging of Laguna Lake. What did they say there? They said, we need to increase the amount of water in this lake. Sounds good. How will we do that? We’ll dredge. Okay. Where will we put the materials we dredge up? We’ll take it from one part of the lake, move it to the other part of the lake. I said, how will the water increase there if you don’t remove it from the lake? You just left it, moved it to another place. And you know, for the privilege of playing with our own mud—Laguna Lake is part of the Philippines—it’s like we have the right to play with our mud, we’ll just be charged a little: P18.7 billion. More than what we delivered to Benguet to play with mud. That’s the “U-turn.”

I will revisit: Campaign season, courting season, a competition of commercials, a competition of slogans, but I believe, only a few will be fooled by the deceivers.

Let us listen to all the candidates and you will see what I am saying, the truth will come out and come out. So I believe, on May 9th, there won't be anyone with a sound mind saying “Come on. Turn left, turn right, and U-turn.” I believe, what will be shouted is “Let us continue the Straight Path.” [Applause]

And with your permission, let me introduce to you the one who will continue to walk the Straight Path, in my opinion, the next President of the Republic of the Philippines: None other than Mar Roxas.
