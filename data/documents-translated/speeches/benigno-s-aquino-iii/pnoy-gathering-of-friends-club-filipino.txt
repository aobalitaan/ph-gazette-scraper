Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

In his meeting with supporters of the Straight Path

[Delivered at the Club Filipino, San Juan City, on April 14, 2016]

They say Gary Valenciano is here. I told him, “I’m ready, Gary. I’m ready to introduce you, while Mar Roxas will duet with you.” [Laughter] I won’t use the teleprompter and I will move around. I hope no one gets dizzy while we move around.

I was thinking about what we should talk about today. Perhaps the Tagalog saying is good, they say, “To know where you are going, look at where you came from.” [Applause] Earlier, Leni said you already know her story. Maybe it’s time for me to tell my story. [Laughter]

I think, among all of us politicians here, is there anyone who entered politics thinking, “I want to be a congressman, I want to be a mayor because I want to be interviewed by the media”? I don’t think so. All of us, especially when we were starting out, we always think, “I have something to contribute. I can help my fellow countrymen here. There are things wrong with the society I live in. I can change that.” Is that right? And like me, I’m sure many of you also experienced frustrations along the way.

I recently came from Tarlac. We have a town in Tarlac in the second district, named Gerona. Gerona has the Tarlac River running through it. There’s a western portion and then the center of Gerona. During the summer, the Tarlac River is completely dry. You really think of the river as a memory, it has no water. People cross the riverbed. But during the rainy season, the water rushes through. When the water rushes, Gerona is divided. Those from Gerona on the western side have to go around through the town of Sta. Ignacia—which is a different district—and go to Tarlac City just to get to the center.

When I first took office, our ambition was that we would have a bridge that we could cross even during the rainy season. And as a representative, we fought for that.

The President said in 1998—during my first run for congressman, right after the Asian Financial Crisis—there were no funds. Every department you go to, there are no funds, no funds, no funds. During that time, PDAF already existed. I asked the DPWH, “Can I commit my entire PDAF to you for my term?” Three years worth of PDAF to build the bridge. The DPWH told me there was no system for an installment plan. It needed to be complete.

What is the problem? Every year, during the rainy season, the river swells and destroys the embankment. Every year, the area needing a bridge expands. It's as if whenever you find funding, the finish line is moved further away. In short: It's clear they need—they probably have the right to remain a town—but it seems there's no way to do it.

Why did I tell this story? The bridge is currently named Tagumbao Bridge. It was inaugurated, completed in 2014, and is 600 lineal meters long. [Applause] And if you see the situation in the western barangay—honestly, Gerona seemed to have already made progress when we arrived. The western area was really left behind. Now, not only is Gerona fully connected, it also serves as an alternative route when traveling from Tarlac to Pangasinan. We've increased our potential routes.

Let me give you another example: In Batasan, someone approached me. They said, “We don't consider you our congressman. We can't find our congressman, but maybe we can consult you about our problem.” I said, “What is the problem?” “We don't have fare to get home.” You know, in Batasan, right, when you're on the 5th floor or 6th floor, sometimes you see seminars for professionals with problems.

I was asked on Monday, and the next day after the session, we met again. They told me—I had given them something the previous day. I said, “How much did it cost and where did you end up?” I won't say what province they came from. I wouldn't want to offend our colleagues. The next day, we met again. They said to me, “It's embarrassing, could we approach you again?” I said, “What is the problem?” “We don't have fare.” Can a politician frown? No. Just smile. “Okay,” I said, “here.” Reach for your wallet again.

That Wednesday arrived, the last day of the session—I'm not joking, this really happened—there they were again. [Laughter] I saw them at the opposite end of the plenary, and then I said, “Here they come again.” Then I looked around, “Where is their congressman here?” Of course, we were also frustrated, wanting to help, but it felt like—we are humans too—we were being taken advantage of too much by this.

When I gave him his third fare, I said, “Brad, if you’ll allow me, I’ll just give you some advice.” “What is it?” “Tomorrow, if you have nothing to do, just don’t leave the house. [Laughter] So you won’t have to worry about getting home.” [Applause] Thank God, we understood each other, and he didn’t bother me after that.

I also had a regular customer at Batasan. I can still see his face now. [Laughter] To be honest, he became my regular at Batasan, he wasn’t even my constituent. When I became a senator, he followed me to the Senate. I said, “Ma’am, you came here.” “When you leave, we have no one to approach anymore.”

It didn’t end there; eventually, at Times [Theater]. On weekends, they would go to me at Times. And they wouldn’t even say what their problem was, they would just smile. “Okay,” I would say. But it wasn’t bad. If you can help, why not. I hope you can help permanently.

But how many of us have been approached by our constituents? Perhaps they said they needed an operation, we gave them a few hundred pesos, but we know that’s far from what they need. Perhaps we gave them a thousand, but it’s still not enough for their needs. Perhaps they need to buy medicine, we can only help with a small portion of the medicine they require.

We all thought: Is there no way for them to handle the problem on their own, to resolve their own problem? Is there nothing we can do to complete the help for them? Didn't we all aspire to that?

Let's go back to my district in the past: I was lucky to have 14 kilometers that could be paved every year. 14 kilometers divided by 159 barangays, I couldn't even promise one kilometer, and I fought for that. Schools: Those who were with me from the 11th, 12th, 13th Congress—admit it—when we had eight or ten classrooms in the district, once you reached a dozen, you were great and you produced them year after year. That was a big thing. And sometimes you think: You brought twelve new classrooms, how many more need to be replaced, aside from those that are lacking?

We all have ambitions. Sometimes, some people really get tired. It's like when there's a problem, you give a complete solution, you move on to another problem, you give it a solution, you get a little motivated. But when the solution is always lacking, instead of reducing the problems you encountered, it just stays the same or sometimes you even get overwhelmed with more problems. It's like being trampled on by problems that never end. How many of us say, “I’m getting tired”? “I won’t be able to complete this job”? “I don’t think I can fulfill my ambition to really bring change to our community”? “Maybe someone else is more capable”? And that’s what we’ve been going through for a long time.

But now, what is our situation? Let me just go back to Tarlac: Before, there were eight, ten, a dozen in the district. Before, the national budget could only support 8,000 classrooms. Now, with all of your help, with the help of our countrymen, we have provided 185,000 classrooms. [Applause]

The 699-meter Tagumbao Bridge, which was once considered impossible, is just a part of the 107,000 lineal meters of bridges that we have already built throughout the country. [Applause] Those who we couldn’t help with medical treatment, 93 million are now members of PhilHealth. [Applause] The Pantawid Pamilya program, which many criticized and which everyone now claims ownership of, [applause] we started with only 800,000 households. It’s still unclear how they were included there. There were still provinces like Cavite and Batangas that had zero—no one was helped. We are now helping 4.6 million households. [Applause]

I think you all saw the video earlier, I just forgot the woman’s name. Her husband had to leave their house to work. What he left his wife was P20 for four weeks. The video showed her gathering shells, so they would have something to eat. It didn’t mention the rice—how many kilos can you buy with twenty pesos? It leaves you hanging. Divided by four weeks. Now, they’ve been in the program for eight weeks. Their child has already graduated from college, they have another child studying in college, they supposedly have their own buy and sell business for appliances, and they’ve really gotten back on their feet because we gave them a livelihood program. [Applause]

You, in all of your districts, in all of your towns, you will find people who can testify to the changes that have happened to them. And I repeat: How many of us have thought that we could help millions? That we could change the face of our country?

Let me add: In the past, in Lanao del Sur, in the municipalities of Wao and Bumbaran, when visiting the governor, the trip would take eight hours, because he had to pass through Cagayan de Oro, Iligan, Bukidnon, to reach these distant towns. It is said that the necessary roads have been opened, and next they will be cemented. The eight-hour trip now takes an hour and a half. [Applause]

Let me reiterate: 18,000 kilometers of national roads have already been built. Many tourism roads were not yet included in the 18,000. These tourism roads make it easy for tourists to go to places, such as for whale watching. It is now easy to get there, tourists are flocking, our countrymen there have livelihoods, and the environment is also being taken care of. That—everything I am mentioning to you—is the Straight Path.

Let me add: When we were starting, when we went to other countries, when I met with leaders of other countries, it felt like during my first meetings with other leaders, they were only tolerating talking to us. It felt like they were merely obliged to speak with us. Now, there came a point—I won’t mention which country here in ASEAN, one of the wealthiest countries in ASEAN—there was a public forum. They said to me, “President Aquino, your GDP grew by… Ours grew by…” Ours at that time was around 6 percent plus, theirs 1 percent plus. “What is your secret?” Honestly, I thought to myself, “Why would I share that with you? We are just now starting to recover.” But I was diplomatic. I said, “We copied your example.” Honestly, we are now truly considered a partner by many countries. [Applause]

You will remember the story of Mel Sarmiento. He went as mayor [to another country] to represent the Philippines. The delegation from Japan arrived, “From Japan! Please, welcome!” The delegation from Singapore arrived, “From Singapore! Welcome!” They saw Mel’s passport, “From the Philippines… Purpose of visit?” [Laughter] Then they apparently asked him, “Proof that you are here to attend an international conference.” Luckily, he had a letter with him.

He even brought calling cards, a hundred pieces. When he returned, he had about 101 calling cards. [Laughter] He said that once they knew it was the Philippines, other mayors from other countries didn't want to approach them. They were afraid they would be asked for ambulances and fire trucks. That was then. Now, Mel is giving away fire trucks, patrol vehicles. [Applause]

Cesar Purisima also attended a conference. The groups were clustered together, with tables and chairs. Apparently, Cesar Purisima was alone there. Finally, in the afternoon, as the breakout session was ending, someone approached him. Cesar said, “Finally, someone will talk to me.” The other person said, “Can I borrow this chair?” [Laughter] That was then. Now, for how many years was Cesar Purisima the best Finance Minister? That's where the former Sick Man of Asia came from, now, Asia’s Rising Tiger.

Comrades, I am not the candidate. It's unlikely I'll be the one speaking at length here. Now, if Gary Valenciano invites me later, I will call Mar.

This, I think, is the simple thing I want to leave with all of you: Let's look at where we came from. For years, for decades, we thought, “That is the fate of the Filipino. It will never change.” And now, we have shown, “How much change can happen with good governance.” [Applause]

Why were so many infrastructures built? The answer is simple: We didn't steal anything. [Applause]

Look at the Sitio Electrification Program, we were able to provide electricity to over 32,600 sitios. The target was 32,400—it was increased by more than 200. I asked our NEA Administrator now, “Why was it increased?” “There were those who didn't believe. They didn't register in 2011. Now that all their surroundings have been connected, they said, ‘Wait, us too!’” It went through the process. Even those that were very difficult to connect to the grid because they were very remote areas, very expensive wiring and posts, to connect them, they said they will go to renewable. Solar energy is the next being done currently and being bid out. [Applause]

Earlier, Zeny Maranan told us that there should be no deception here, no lies. Some of you, I'm meeting for the first time now, but truly, we have no province or town that we neglected or abandoned. It's simple: Does that community have a need? If the answer is “yes,” let's do it. And so we did. What we did wasn't for show, wasn't for making ourselves look good. As I said to you, it's okay with me to be handsome to my mother. But the people need it. The project is proper, the state's investment is beneficial.

Where are we now? We have some unfinished projects that are really big. For instance, the one in Laguna Lake: the problem is flooding. Both sides—western and eastern side. We thought of the Laguna Lake ring road dike. Benefit: it will control the flood, it will open up areas that are difficult to reach now, there will be opportunities.

Where did we end up? We reached the bidding stage, but we already closed that bidding. We can understand the businessmen, this is a PPP Project. The infrastructure we need, the private sector will pay for it, they will even pay us.

Why did it end up with the bidding not pushing through? It's simple: Our payment involves reclaiming land. The reclaimed land, the businessmen are waiting for assurance that they will indeed be paid. So, a proclamation is needed stating “Alienable and Disposable”. But I can't issue the proclamation because the land is only to be reclaimed. There is no land yet that can be disposed of. So we will be transitioning—I only have 77 days left. When that's over, they might forget that they are owed payment. So they said, “We’ll just wait for the next administration for clarity.”

Another project: We will transfer the National Bilibid to your area. Why will we transfer it there? Muntinlupa is extremely congested. All the solutions made were just band-aid solutions. There's a lack of facilities— “There’s a scapegoat here, let’s add more there.” I think, if we look for the masterplan of Bilibid, and compare it to the actual situation, even an engineer as skilled as Babes Singson would get dizzy.

Those of you who are my age, do you remember the movie “Escape from New York”? What was the concept there? All the criminals in New York, were walled off, left there, fend for yourselves. To a degree—not to the same degree—Bilibid is like that. Upon arrival at the prison grounds, it’s like their own community. There’s a lack of space, a lack of cells, a lack of modern methods.

The plan here is really a new facility that will meet the ratio of living space to the number of inmates. The jail will be proper, controllable, observable, monitored, all the inmates. The community of Nueva Ecija has agreed. The entire process will be completed soon, but the most painful part is that once the documentation is finished, everything that needs to be done will reach June 29th. If I sign it, it's a midnight deal. If I don't sign it, we'll have to pray. I hope the next person in office implements this solution for Bilibid.

What is the problem? Drugs are still being smuggled in. It seems there's still a syndicate outside, entering inside, continuing their syndicate activities. We're having a hard time controlling it. BuCor officials have raided it 29 times, and yet they still find things. That's just how it is. It's like the problem was just allowed to grow and grow. We have a solution. By June 29th, the election will be over, and if Mar Roxas wins, I'm sure he will continue it, so I might not need to sign it. [Applause]

The main point—and this is perhaps my most earnest plea to everyone—we've come so far because of following the Straight Path. We can't be sure, if we deviate from Mar and Leni, that their replacements will share the same vision. We're not sure about their capabilities, their experience, and where they come from, what their focus is. Will we return to self-interest, or will we continue to focus on others? [Applause]

What do the surveys say? For me, who wants to return to a situation where those on the margins of society will just say, “That’s just life. No one understands us. No one helps us.” We are all helping them, the entire Philippines is helping them. We may not feel burdened by helping them progress, we are doing what is right. Not just helping them so we can feed them once, but helping them so they can feed and heal themselves every day. [Applause]

But let me go back to the survey. What the survey tells us is that people are somewhat confused. To varying degrees, all our opposition say they will continue [the current programs]. We all know, “Wait a minute, when we were doing this, were you with us? Or were you making things difficult for us?” What is the truth? Were you making things difficult for us, or will you help us? Or will you continue [the programs]? Let’s go back: We need to clarify this to all our countrymen. We cannot gamble with this. We don’t need to gamble on going back to the way things were, that we might get lucky down the line, somewhere in the future, that we can repeat what happened. We can still expand and broaden the changes happening in our society, but that won’t happen if we’re lazy, if we let pure talk prevail. We need to unite all Filipinos to show—to demonstrate that only one team will truly continue what we started. Of course, that’s no one else but Mar and Leni. [Applause]

77 days left, let me take this opportunity to say goodbye to all of you. If the time comes that we made a mistake and we need to stand up again to fight for what is right, I think, if I am still here, why wouldn’t I join you? Why wouldn’t you put me in front of the cannonballs? That’s okay with me. But the point is: Why do we need to gamble that we’ll have that opportunity? Why give our doubts about our opponents a chance to become true? Our biggest fears. All that’s needed here is for us to work harder.

Let me repeat: Everything we do is for the next generation, for the future generation. I have my two nephews, Joshua and Bimby, with me at home now. Bimby is really a very young child. Sometimes when I get tired, I just look at them. Do I want them to go through everything I’ve been through? I think, all the hardships we, the older generation, have gone through, none of us would probably say, “I hope you go through it too.” Especially if we love them. And they might not even go through it. But going back to that: How diligent are we in fighting for what is right? How enthusiastically will we do what we need to do?

All of you here, leaders, politicians, sectors. You are looked up to by your countrymen; they look to you for advice. Then we suddenly fall silent, “it’s up to you, it’s okay with me.” Well, that’s not what leaders do. We might be the first to be swept away by the wind. That would be embarrassing.

There is a danger—there is a danger that we will be swayed by beautiful ads, by the use of social media to confuse our countrymen, so that they will have space for others to enter. Our job, on the other hand, is to tell the truth, so that our countrymen can make the right decision, and to ensure that what is happening now is just a real beginning, and that what we say, “You ain’t seen nothing yet,” will come true. [Applause] I like your applause, we might not finish here.

Comrades, you know, I was advised—the traditional politicians will tell you, “Just be practical, be quiet, be a friend of everybody.” But if I become a “friend of everybody,” I feel like a “friend of nobody.” Especially to my Bosses, the Filipino people, whom I promised to lead to a better time. [Applause]

Just one last thing: Everything we have done, no one aspired to be able to do it when we started. But we did it. What we have done now, of course, increases our confidence that we can achieve even higher dreams, that we can do even more. But—this is the heavy part—we need a leader who will continue what we are doing, one who will not turn us around. [Applause]

With your permission, I would like to introduce the next President of the Republic of the Philippines, none other than Mar Roxas.
