Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, In tribute and mourning for the fallen PNP-Special Action Force troopers.

One of the most difficult duties as president is to express condolences to those who have lost loved ones. As I stand before you today, I admit, I cannot help but ask myself: What can I say, so that, in some way, I can ease your hearts?

The life of anyone is very important. Although we all know that we will all disappear from this world, there is always the question of “Did someone have to die in this instance?” This is despite our constant search for ways, to the best of our ability, to avoid any unnecessary loss or death.

I want to tell you that I deeply feel what you are feeling. I understand every family of our police officers who died in the encounter last Sunday. I too have lost a loved one suddenly. I remember when my late father and I were talking. He decided then that the time had come for him to return to our Motherland from America.

He himself said: we are getting closer and closer to a bloody revolution. He returned alone. His companions were only the media, and he was clearly defenseless. He said: perhaps, at least, Mr. Marcos would become curious and give me the opportunity to dialogue with him, and reach him with the true situation of the country. In this way, the peaceful restoration of our democracy could begin.

I was alone watching television at our house in Boston, waiting for news about what happened to my father. I heard the announcer on television say: “Opposition leader Benigno Aquino was seen lying in a pool of blood; shots were fired.” At that moment, time seemed to stop for me, it stopped and I hardly noticed anything around me; my world turned upside down. I cannot explain the shock I felt then. Not once did we think there was even a possibility that he would be killed. Perhaps the heaviest thing was to return him to prison. So we were really shocked by what happened. Upon his return, my father was no longer able to set foot on his homeland. As he was descending from the plane, he was ruthlessly assassinated.

When the incident was confirmed, I did not allow myself to cry. I held back my tears, and transferred everything to anger.

Our father was the head of the family; he was our protector, our guide and inspiration, and the source of strength that stabilized our family during the Martial Law years.

I feel that the dialogue my father and his colleagues are seeking has lost its space for proper conversation, and they have become like barking dogs who can no longer be spoken to reasonably. I said, I will give my father the proper respect; and then, I will do to them what they did to our family.

In this saddest and most painful moment, several questions swirl in my mind: Now that our father is gone, how can we reach his aspirations for the country? Does that mean the fight is over? If my father died for the country, and we haven't achieved any change, isn't his sacrifice, and that of thousands of Filipinos, a waste?

During those times, I couldn't help but think that the final stage would lead to a bloody uprising. But my perspective began to change when I saw how many people stood in solidarity and expressed their condolences outside our house in Times, Quezon City. It was as if they were no longer afraid of Martial Law. It was then that I saw the possibility of continuing the fight even without our leader.

To the families of our fallen police officers, I know that your world has also changed with the passing of your loved ones. You have already laid out plans; and dreams you wanted to achieve. And in an unexpected event, your future suddenly became blurred and darkened. So many questions begin with the word “Why”: Why did this happen? Why do people have to die? Why do families have to suffer?

To be honest, I cannot answer all of these. It was then that I remembered the advice I received when my father died. My confessor priest said: As Christians, our faith simply says: God created us in His image, a sign of His love. And because He loves us, He has a good plan for each of us. He said that when we go through times when we cannot see this plan, perhaps we are struggling because we are only looking at a fragment of His whole plan. But if we could only see the entirety of this plan, He said, the true beauty would be revealed to us.

I would also like to revisit a passage from the Book of John, Chapter 15, Verse 13. It says: “Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends.”

To the families of our fallen police officers, I know you also understand the reason for their joining the force. Those 44 police officers, at a young age of 26 to 39 years old, pushed themselves and maximized their capabilities to contribute their greatest contribution, not just for themselves or their families, but for our Motherland. They offered their lives for lasting peace and order.

As you know, they had two targets in the operation: Abdul Basit Usman, and Zulkipli Bin Hir or Abu Marwan. We have already mentioned how big of terrorists they were. They kill without restraint and specialize in how to spread terrorism further. Marwan was even a suspect in the Bali, Indonesia bombing. There, two successive explosions occurred. After the first explosion, they waited for the rescuers to arrive, and then detonated the second bomb. 202 people died there. In this SAF operation, it is said that the main target, Marwan, was killed.

You can rest assured that the second target we failed to capture before will also be captured. And you know me, when I make a promise, I deliver. Number 1 on the list of what we need to do is the arrest of Basit Usman. We have already ordered those concerned to plan a more effective operation to capture this person. Your government is already acting, not only at this time, but even before I faced you, to carry this out. We will prove the rule of law throughout the Philippines. I assure you, we will catch Usman.

Let us remember that we have formed a board of inquiry whose job is to determine what really happened and who did it. What we want is: to prevent an incident like this from happening again. As President, I want to be angry, but I cannot allow myself to be swayed by emotion. I cannot go through this with hasty decision-making. Because if I give in to anger, I might worsen the problem instead of resolving it. We will strive to achieve justice for all those who were killed and their loved ones left behind.

You can also count on this: We will strive to reduce the uncertainty about your future. We will address your needs in accordance with all laws and regulations. We will do everything, to the best of our ability, to help every family of our heroic police officers. Our colleagues in the government have already interviewed you, and I have also spoken to some of you and will speak to all of you.

Your government has already allocated sufficient financial assistance to the beneficiaries of each police officer who died or was injured. We will maximize all aid that can come from the government. From supporting the education of the beneficiaries, to helping them find jobs and livelihoods—these are just some examples of the assistance that your government will provide.

As I mentioned on Wednesday night, I am also directly appealing to the private sector and our citizens: Join the government to ensure that we can show our recognition of the heroism demonstrated by the fallen members of the PNP-Special Action Force.

It is clear: The husband, brother, or child you are honoring today is a hero. The nation owes them a great debt of gratitude. They sacrificed their lives to provide order and peace to the lives of many.

That is why our flag is flying at half-mast in all camps of our police and military, and in all offices of our government. Now that we have brought their remains home, the flag also covers each of them. This is a symbol of the state's recognition of their courage, dedication, and heroism. This is a symbol of mourning, not only of their families and friends, but of the entire nation. This is a symbol that they lived and died, not for themselves, but for the country.

On behalf of the Filipino people, we recognize the heroism of our fallen police officers. We thank them for the greatness of their sacrifice. We express our condolences to every family of our police officers who have lost a loved one.

At the very beginning, I mentioned: If nothing changes following my father's death, wouldn't his sacrifice have been in vain? Therefore, we must not allow the sacrifice of our heroic police officers to be for nothing. If their deaths lead to the achievement of the peace we aspire to, then we can say that their sacrifice was not wasted. This true and lasting change will be their legacy to the Filipino people.

Good afternoon. Thank you very much.
