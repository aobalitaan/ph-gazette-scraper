Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

in the inauguration of the Baybay Diversion Road

[Declared in the City of Baybay, Leyte, on April 18, 2016]

You know, my countrymen, my Bosses, my dream as your President is very simple: To leave a Philippines that is undeniably better than the one we inherited.

Now, we chose to come here to Baybay City, because we know, you are proof of the change happening in the country. What have we done here? You know, we only have a few days left in office. I think it's time to show a report card.

Just now, we inspected your Baybay Diversion Road Project. Its budget is: P374.63 million, for the five-kilometer two-lane road, including the 151.50 lineal meter bridge. Some may ask, “That seems like a large expense.” But as you can see, we had to cut through a mountain, there’s slope protection needed, and the most expensive part of it all is the construction of the bridge.

For road construction, February 2013; we opened this road we mentioned in January of this current year. The slope protection works will be completed by August 2016.

This Diversion Road will start at the junction of the Ormoc-Baybay-Southern Leyte Road in Brgy. Candadam, and will reach the junction of the Ormoc-Baybay-Southern Leyte Road in Brgy. Hipusngo. It is an important part of the Baybay City road network, and also serves as an alternative route to and from Baybay City.

Benefits: It alleviates traffic and vehicle congestion in Baybay City proper; motorists heading to cities like Tacloban or Ormoc, and also going to Baybay City Port, no longer need to pass through the city, where government agencies and other private buildings are located. It reduces traffic and speeds up travel, as testified to earlier, reducing demurrage, delays at the pier, and their expenses.

Travel from Maasin City to the cities of Tacloban and Ormoc has also been made easier. The Maasin-Tacloban trip, which used to take three and a half hours, now takes only three hours. The Maasin-Ormoc trip, two hours will now be just one and a half hours. Those who will benefit from this, they say, are at least 600 motorists per day at present, but as your economy grows and grows, more and more people will use this road. Maybe in just two or three years, Dominic will say, “This road is no longer enough.” Boying will second that. Just mention it to Mar Roxas, he will take care of it. [Applause] This is just one in the long list of infrastructure projects we focused on in the province of Leyte. There’s also the Tabango-Catmon-La Fortuna-Manlawaan-Gimarco Road, and the Junction Abuyog-Silago Road.

As Sec. Babes said: We have greatly improved all national roads and bridges here in your province. Aside from widening, there’s what’s called shoulder leveling, to make it safer for those traveling along the sides of the roads. Before, it was as if the shoulder and the road itself were leveled. Because people walking on the road were low, they were close to accidents. When it was leveled, they were on the shoulder, away from accidents.

All of this is just part of our broad strategy to connect provinces and cities, ease transportation throughout the country, and maximize the progress of the Filipino. Throughout the country, we have built and repaired more than 18,000 kilometers of national roads, and 107,000 linear meters of bridges. On top of that are 2,500 tourism roads and 3,000 plus farm-to-market roads.

Budget in 2010: P165 billion; now in 2016: it’s at P759.58 billion, a 360 percent increase in the budget for infrastructure without raising the taxes you pay. Kim Henares just corrected tax collection—still, many are angry with her.

Infrastructure in Leyte, public works and transportation projects, 2005 to 2010, P7 billion. Under the Straight Path, from 2011 to 2016, what we allocated was not P7 billion, but P27 billion. This is a 284 percent increase in the budget for Leyte for infrastructure alone. [Applause] They are mentioning the Tabanggo kat Mon la Fortuna Lawaan Guimarco Road Junction of Buyog Silangan Road, the ports of Tacloban, Ormoc, Hilongos, your Baybay Port, Javier Port, Carigara Port, and Babatngon Port.

Regarding Tacloban Airport, since the transfer of the airport will still take some time, we have completed the repairs to the runway, apron, taxiway, shore protection, and the damaged buildings such as the passenger terminal and administration building.

Before we leave this topic, I just want to show you a map. If you see this color, purple, and all of this is purple, purple also here, purple everywhere. Here is the airport—of course this is already a docket. You can't read this but it's here in the legend, it can rise by one to four meters when there is a storm surge, like Yolanda. The problem is, this study is from 2008. We weren't yet president then. The four meters is just about right. If we look at this wall, up to here, that's how high the wave is.

What's important: The airport, a strong disaster struck. What are we, an archipelago, the Philippines is an archipelago. The fastest way to bring aid, of course, is by plane. If the airport is flooded, full of what are called debris, the plane cannot land, it will take a long time to deliver aid. Let me go back, what will absorb the water, of course this is the thickest. Then up here, still within the scope of Tacloban where the San Juanico Bridge is located, the land narrows. So the large waves that came, went down here, went there. When it reached there at the top, it was narrow, the wave couldn't continue, the wave went back. It hit you twice here in Leyte.

Earlier, Secretary Singson mentioned that we will also construct a dike from Tanauan to Tacloban that will protect the road, and all the residences beyond the dike, so that it can catch high waves. If any water exceeds it, it will only be a small flood, and this dike will somewhat block it.

Now, we will start this year the portion in Palo; it is already in the budget because the process is complete. It has been studied where the exact location is, and where the people who will be affected will be relocated. It is necessary that there is a dike, and the houses are behind it. If the houses are in front of the dike, in front of the sea, the dike is useless. We have a complete plan for relocating those in front in the Palo portion. For Tacloban, they are requesting a delay in the project because the resettlement is not yet finalized, they say.

Let us remember: It seems we cannot use this airport 365, 24/7—365 days, 24 hours, 7 days a week—when there is heavy rain. We need to put it further inland—far from the sea, perhaps around here. Now, the study is not yet complete: Where is the best location? Ormoc was also considered; the situation in Ormoc is the same, it is also close to the water, and it will also be affected. It really needs to be protected.

Farm-to-market roads, let's go back to that: We have allocated from 2011 to 2016 a total of P881.55 million. Of the 218 projects funded from 2011 to 2015, 199 have been completed and are being utilized by at least 37,000 farmer households; the remaining 14 farm-to-market roads are currently under construction. The junction of the Ormoc-Merida-Isabel-Palompon National Road, in Brgy. Curva Domonar, and in Brgy. Bairan-Sitio Busay Guinciaman.

Irrigation: From 2011 to 2016, we have already allocated P2.73 billion for irrigation projects in your province. We completed 166 projects; only 14 are ongoing. Included in that are the Javier Communal Irrigation Project, Tabunok Communal Irrigation System, and the rehabilitation of the Tagbawto Communal Irrigation System. Those in front are laughing—it doesn't seem like this is Tagbawto. [Applause]

Production, post-harvest machineries and equipment in the province: From the same years 2011 to 2015, the amount was P75.52 million. Intercropping—let me explain it to you. Intercropping, during the time of Governor Icot Petilla, we sometimes flew in a helicopter and I saw—even when he was already a secretary—I said, “There are so many coconut trees.” It seemed like everything that could be planted was planted with coconuts.

In intercropping, what is being said? If we rely on copra and estimate an income of P20,000 per hectare per year, intercropping is said to allow us to plant cacao between the trees. If we plant cacao, the income will be P89,000; if bananas, it is said to reach an additional income of P102,325; if coffee, that one hectare in one year will earn P172,400 from just P20,000. It seems very good to continue this intercropping program where the livelihood of coconut farmers will really improve.

For P18.11 million, ten intercropping sites under the Prosperity for Coconuts and Development of the Nation Enterprise Development Project, or KAANIB, are now benefiting 702 farmers. This is still a pilot testing phase. Just regarding coffee, we spoke with the company Nestle. They told us that they import 80 percent of what they need and buy only 20 percent from the Philippines. Now, it's already 70-30. 70 percent is imported, 30 percent is from here. They want to reverse that. If possible, 80 percent of their needs should be from the Philippines, and only 20 percent should be imported. Let me go back: We have land, between the coconut trees, we can plant corn, bananas, cacao, coffee. Then the income, from P20,000, can reach almost P200,000. Who wouldn't like that?

P98.75 million pesos was allocated for coconut seedling planting and replanting for 26,556.54 hectares of land, including salt fertilizer worth P14.65 million for 12,095 hectares of land.

We salute the effort and creativity of Dr. Justino Arboleda of Bicol State University. He promoted the analysis of coconuts and the use of coco coir fiber or “coconet” as an effective tool to prevent landslides. Now, we are already using it. And according to the DPWH, as of March, the total area of coconet used nationwide has reached 5.2 million square meters. Because of this, we have saved funds for slope protection. The cost of P6.2 billion for concrete has been reduced to P1.59 billion due to the coconet. Our savings: P4.6 billion pesos.

That former husk, it was thrown away, used as fuel, litter, now fibers are extracted from it. Those fibers are made into coconet, the coconet is placed on the sides of mountains when a road is opened. A coconet is placed, grass will grow, plants will grow, the soil will become stable, eventually even trees and animals will increase in those slopes.

The old style was concrete. That meant stones, chicken wire, a little cement, and that's what held the land in place. The problem is, the soil heats up and cools down, the concrete doesn't follow the expansion or contraction of the soil, it cracks. When it cracks, in three or four years, we have to redo it. This coco coir, first, is cheaper; second, it lasts longer; and third, we benefit from a product that used to be just waste.

Our problem with coco coir is very simple: there’s a shortage of coco coir. It is exported to countries like China, and we are the ones experiencing the shortage. Simply put, we are sustaining the coconut industry. Dr. Arboleda is building a factory in Bicol. That is the proof of concept, where all coconut products—except copra—coconut water, coco peat, coco coir, coco sugar, and many other products—will be made in this factory. They estimate that after only three years of operation, the investment will be paid off. We can then transfer it to the next farmers group. It was only slightly delayed, but the concept seems close to being proven. And because a decision has been made regarding the coco levy fund, we have funds to use to stabilize these factories. I believe you already have one here, which will add to the income of our coconut farmers.

Pantawid Pamilya: Earlier, someone came forward and testified. In 2010, only 5,000 households benefiting from the program were covered here. Now, for Leyte, 125,455 households are already covered by the 4Ps program. Nationwide, 4.6 million households will be reached this year. That is a very long way from the 800,000 we found in 2010.

Classrooms: 4,057 will be completed this year, including your 1,500 plus deficiency. Regarding out-of-school youth: 2.9 million children across the Philippines were out-of-school youth in 2008. In 2013, it was down to 1.7 million after five years. The number of out-of-school youth decreased, truly proving that they returned to school.

Health Facilities Enhancement Program: P243.38 million to improve your barangay health stations, rural health units, urban health centers, and hospitals. I would be surprised if your health sector is neglected because, as far as I know, Secretary Janet Garin was born here in Baybay, and she will remember to help where she also came from.

These programs are just some of what we are doing in Leyte. In the past almost six years, we have seen not only the recovery, but the progress of your province.

Of course, all our successes would not be possible without the cooperation of our agencies. Special mention to the DPWH, DILG, and DOH. Also, with your support and trust, my Bosses, to all of you: Thank you very much. [Applause]

Now, if we didn't have dedicated public servants at the local level like Tita Carmen, Boying, Dominic, we would really struggle. Here in Leyte, I had an instruction. Right? When there's a disaster, you expect the local chief executive to do everything they can to fix their area, right? They promised left and right, repeatedly, “We will cooperate with whatever is needed.” Until now, I am still waiting for that cooperation. If I made a mistake during Yolanda, I should have suspended someone; I just endured it instead of suspending them. So I apologize for the delayed aid, especially to those under his jurisdiction because I was wrong to expect him to fulfill his promise.

Perhaps some are asking, 5,000 families before, now 125,000 households are part of the Pantawid Pamilya program. And the others we have achieved through the Straight Path, how did we do it? It's simple: We restored a government that truly cares for and nurtures its people. Every opportunity, we focused on your needs and addressed them. “Not tomorrow, but now” is what we tell our colleagues. It’s good, we delivered our benefits without adding burden to everyone, except for the Sin Tax which has a different purpose.

I just want to emphasize, especially during elections, many make promises to you but the promises that matter, you’ve seen that we’ve already delivered on them. Let us also not forget all that we have worked hard for. It could all just vanish, especially if the next leaders have a crooked administration.

On May 9th, it is in your hands whether what we have started and expanded will continue. As Father of the Nation, it is my duty to guide you on the right path. My only request is: Let us carefully examine the candidates for the country. As for me, looking them in the eye, I can tell you who has integrity, who has ability, who truly puts the country before self. And it is no other than the Righteous Team: Mar Roxas and Leni Robredo. [Applause]

You are witnesses to the change that is happening. You can see that our projects are interconnected, and centered on a single goal: To raise the standard of living of our Bosses. Now that we have gained momentum, and are starting to move forward, the opportunity to accelerate further is before us. As we move in the same direction, continuing the contribution and compassion on the Straight Path, I am confident that nothing can stop us from collectively achieving our highest aspirations.

You know, let me also take this opportunity, as your President, this might be my last trip to Baybay. I am already saying goodbye. During my first visit to Leyte, truthfully, I was with Tita Carmen and Tita Maten in Congress. That’s where we met. When I landed in Tacloban during that time, I kept thinking, this must be Imelda’s town. Maybe no one will talk to me here. Truthfully, those two made a way for us to get closer. I ran for senator, you helped me, thank you very much. [Applause]

I returned to you when we were challenged to run for President. I knocked on your hearts and you gave me another chance. So earlier, when the witness was thanking us, and you as well, I really couldn't get out of my mind that it was reversed: I should be the one thanking you. You gave me the opportunity to lead a great nation that truly became a bridge for the changes we are enjoying now. If I have achieved anything, it is clear that if you hadn't trusted us, if you hadn't given me the chance, we wouldn't have been able to do it.

Comrades, I will just keep telling you: May 9th is not too far away. There are many suitors these days. I don't watch much TV anymore, I just get my news from the newspaper. I happened to be watching TV and I saw a commercial for one of Mar's opponents. One of his promises is millions of jobs every year. There's a promise that every town should have its own hospital and many other promises.

I was just thinking. Is it that easy? Perhaps my only advice to him is to look at my picture from 2010, you'll see how much thicker my hair was then compared to now. [Laughter] So please: When you make a promise—and perhaps that's the only thing I'll be proud of, my father taught me to have my word. I will always tell you the truth. Whether it's ugly or beautiful, it's my obligation to tell you the truth so you can make the right decision. I don't have the habit of flattering people, and I think you wouldn't waste your time listening to flattery.

You are the witnesses to what happened or didn't happen in the six years you gave me. If you think we've come a long way, it would be a shame to risk it by maybe turning left, turning right, but most importantly, making a U-turn, going back to the old days of nothing happening. I don't think anyone would want that.

I was taught to rely on the people. You have been our strength from the beginning and until now. Command me, you are my Boss.

Good afternoon. Thank you all very much.
