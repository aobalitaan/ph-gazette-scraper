This is my last time to address you as your Commander in Chief. Of course, we cannot avoid looking back on what we have been through and shared. If you remember, when we started, we said: We can dream again. And in the past years, together we changed the image of the AFP: From a force long neglected and a haven for corruption, we strengthened your ranks to become more modern and more capable of serving the Filipino people, our Bosses.

We have indeed come a long way in our journey along the Straight Path. I remember, when I was still in Congress, I attended a hearing discussing the purchase of a C130. The sad truth then: We couldn't even afford a secondhand one. Now, as you can see, we have four, and another one is on its way. Last month, the BRP Tarlac, our largest ship, arrived. For the first time, we have a naval vessel that can serve as a hospital and command center of the government during disasters. In the past six years, for your modernization, we have allocated 65.89 billion pesos and completed 70 projects. This is far from the 45 total number of projects implemented in the past three administrations, amounting to 31.75 billion pesos.

In short, we doubled the funds: in one-third, or just one-third of the time it took, the time we spent, if you compare it to the eighteen years of previous administrations.

How did we do this? In every step we took, our constant question was: What benefit will this bring to the people? If the answer was none, or if we saw that the benefits to our countrymen were not maximized, we stopped it; we replaced it with programs and initiatives where every peso spent will raise their standard of living. Our mantra: Good governance is good economics.

During the campaign, we saw its effectiveness. We passed by a newly constructed road from Talisay to Bacolod. This was to connect their airport to the community. Before, the area of Talisay was inaccessible and unnoticed. Now, because of the road, various establishments have sprung up there such as residences, stores, factories, offices and other buildings. The result: Because we delivered the necessary services, the standard of living of our countrymen there has improved, and this is just the beginning.

Now, we have done the same spirit in our uniformed ranks. With proper management, and proper allocation of funds, your modernization has become historic. Some of the things we once only dreamed of, we now hold. What's even better: You are not only receiving blessings from the state; you are reciprocating it with commensurate service. In the end, it becomes a virtuous cycle; everyone works together, and in the end, the Filipino wins.

Because of each of your contributions, the uniformed ranks are now etched in the minds of the Filipino people as truly dependable and reliable, especially in times of need. Every day, you demonstrate the spirit and true meaning of the word “dedication.” No matter how difficult the task, whatever equipment you hold, I have never heard any excuses or reasons from you. Instead, you always face it with your head held high, and push your capabilities to the limit to accomplish the job. That is why, on behalf of every countryman you have saved, helped, or comforted: Thank you very much, thank you very much.

Despite our achievements, I am confident that you will not become complacent. There is a thought, and it says: “If you do not grow, you stagnate; and after that, you deteriorate.” For example, in a confrontation. If a unit loses, it is the leader's responsibility to study what happened. Where did we go wrong? This is what we need to correct. Where were we lacking? This is what we need to fill. A good leader looks at the finished battle, but is already prepared and focused for the battles to come.

Let's revisit the mission at Ayungin Shoal. There was an attempt to block the delivery of necessary supplies to our sailors stationed at the BRP Sierra Madre. As we all know, our rival country in the West Philippine Sea has a significant advantage in terms of equipment. Our Navy then thought: How can we accomplish the mission? They used what is called seamanship. They routed our fishing vessels through the shallow parts of the sea, preventing the rival's modern naval vessels from getting close. The result: Mission accomplished—we successfully delivered supplies to the BRP Sierra Madre.

This kind of courage and steadfastness, strategy and innovation, should not be lost in our thinking. We cannot be stuck in a box, lacking imagination, and short on inspiration in fulfilling our duties. If we become complacent, we may win today, we may succeed tomorrow; but the opponent we defeat is guaranteed to keep looking for ways to win.

The call to us is: Every mission you face, may you study it thoroughly, regardless of your field or rank, to open up new insights and understanding. Remember that whatever the state of our systems, they can always be improved further.

It is also necessary to maintain and develop a culture of free and honest communication within our ranks. It cannot be that only what those at the top want to hear is what those at the bottom say. It is important to always be truthful. Along with the strict implementation of the command hierarchy and chain of command, everyone must be encouraged to contribute new ideas. In this way, we can further improve our service.

This is also a time for gratitude. I would like to acknowledge the leadership of our Armed Forces, from the very beginning until this point. Of course, to Secretary Voltz Gazmin, who spearheaded the promotion of a culture of efficient, thorough, and strong relations between the officers and soldiers of our armed forces and our government. You know, when I appointed him, he said to me, “Can I do just one year or two?” I’m sorry, Secretary Gazmin, I forgot about that conversation; three days to go now, make the most of it.

To Lt. Gen. Glorioso Miranda, and to our former Chiefs of Staff; and to all our service commanders. Thank you very much for being exemplary leaders.

We also honor today the 32nd Infantry Battalion. When the Zamboanga Siege erupted, they were supposed to be undergoing retraining there. Suddenly, they were designated to block the evil plans of the rogue MNLF forces, and they stabilized the situation while allowing other units to come in. Currently, even in the ongoing encounter against the Abu Sayyaf Group, they are once again at the forefront. Even if they are said to be an ordinary Infantry Battalion, we all see that they are diligently pursuing the enemies of society.

I will also take this opportunity to thank our PSG, led by Rear Admiral Raul Ubando. You already know this: In his first week as the new head of the PSG, he was immediately thrown into the Zamboanga Siege. Then, the earthquake in Cebu and Bohol followed the next month; after that, perhaps he didn’t have enough action, it was also accompanied by Typhoon Yolanda. Add to that the visit of His Holiness the Pope and President Obama, as well as the large gatherings here, such as the APEC and the World Economic Forum.

My memory of the Zamboanga crisis is very vivid. During that time, we needed to lift the morale of our countrymen. I told him, we will proceed to the stadium where the evacuees were. But he said, as Group Commander of the PSG: “Sir, that wasn’t cleared. We don’t know who is there, and that area is also within the mortar range of our enemy.” My response was: “We need to ‘lead from the front.’ As president, we need to reach any part of the country. Our people need us. Do what you can do. Let God take care of us and what we cannot do.” The only answer I received from Group was: “Yes, sir.” And as you saw: Our Group Commander and the entire PSG really showed their skills, which is why our operation in Zamboanga was peaceful and successful.

Therefore, to those who comprise the PSG: With every step on the Straight Path, I felt secure, because you were there and always demonstrated professionalism, and proper coordination with our security forces. To all of you: Thank you very much.

We also recognize today some of my aide-de-camps. Perhaps, others think that life is good being an aide to the President. What many do not know is that they are the loyal ones and the first to absorb the stress, and even sometimes, our anger. When something happened in the middle of the night, such as during the Arab Spring, we had a lot of things we needed to entrust to them. And who was there? Our aide-de-camp. What happened? They were awakened at two o'clock, given a task that needed to be completed by half-past seven in the morning. All our questions were listed, carefully understood so they could be passed on to the secretaries we needed to talk to in order to resolve the problems of our countrymen in the Middle East. In those moments, my assistant and aides were my partners and those I relied on.

You know, aside from explaining to me how to read our maps, especially the grid coordinates, the study of the symbols of our forces, especially in the current operation in Sulu, they really shared what they had trained for. They also give us a perspective, not from the highest ranks, but they also pursue it even in the lowest ranks so we can know the state of the puzzle and the mindset of our entire force.

You know, they even have to take care of everything, even the arrangement of my barong, and if my remaining hair is messy. When I have a lot on my mind, they are also my companions. I hope that after this, when they are resting with their wives and loved ones, they might miss those piled-up problems, watching TV, watching the same channels together, with no one understanding what they are watching.

For me, it is true that a system with an aide for the President was established, to help the person in office with their work. But I also believe: they are also trained on how to make decisions by those above them and how to lay out solutions, so that when the time comes, they will truly be well-shaped for service. Therefore, to all my aides: I congratulate you, and once again, thank you very much.

Comrades, before we take the Straight Path, your dedication and idealism were what you used to fill in your lack of equipment. Now that only a few days remain in my term, the distance we have reached makes me smile when I think that you can reach even further after I retire. As I always say: this is just the beginning.

If there is anything I will leave you with as a testament, it is this: do not forget the question we always ask whenever we find ourselves at a crossroads: How does this help the Filipino people? When you answer yes to that, most likely, you are on the right track.

Comrades, as you all know: During the Martial Law era, the dictator used the Armed Forces in his abuse of our countrymen. For almost a decade, your ranks were separated from the ordinary citizens. I apologize, it wasn't almost a decade: it was more than a decade that happened. It was like a reunion during the EDSA People Power Revolution. And now, it can no longer be denied that you are truly partners and defenders of our Bosses, the Filipino people.

May the Armed Forces remain united with the people. I am confident: With the guidance of God, our continued dedication to our Bosses, and our firm commitment to our flag, we can leave the next generation with a safer, stronger, and more developed Philippines.

In conclusion: here we feel how inadequate it is to simply say thank you very much to all of you. I believe I truly represent the entire Filipino people who deeply feel all your sacrifices, everything you went through for their benefit.

And that awareness of our countrymen, remember: that will also continue how they will cherish those who truly cared for them.

Long live our Armed Forces and thank you very much. Good afternoon to all of you.
