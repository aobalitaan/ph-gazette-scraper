Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, on the 116th anniversary of the proclamation of Philippine independence.

[ Delivered in Naga City, Camarines Sur, on June 12, 2014 ]

[Greetings]

My beloved Bicolanos, good morning to all of you. [Applause]

Leni and John helped me learn Bikolano. I hope I’m right. If I’m wrong, the fault is solely mine. [Laughter] It’s really good to be back in Naga, but you know we still have a few places to go today. If it’s still very cool here, I might think about not showing up there. That’s a big issue because we invited the diplomatic community to Malacañang at noon.

So, please excuse me if our trip is a bit rushed. I hope this speech we prepared for you is correct.

One hundred and sixteen years have passed since the National Anthem was formally played and the National Flag was raised in Kawit, Cavite, as symbols of a free and united Philippines. On that day, the independence of the Philippines was also declared: A nation freed from the chains of foreigners, composed of citizens who hold their own destiny.

This is what we commemorate and celebrate today—we consider June 12 as the culmination of all the sacrifices, adventures, and triumphs of our ancestors, in order to achieve independence from Spain. Everyone is aware: The fulfillment of our heroes’ aspirations did not happen in one fell swoop. It is the result of rising from failure and trials, and the sacrifice and contribution of so many people, united by a single purpose: To live with dignity and freedom from oppression. There was the Propaganda Movement that planted change in the minds of Filipinos; the Katipunan that was born, spread, and became a haven for heroes; the many clashes between Filipino guerrillas and the Spanish army; the publication of two novels by Gat Jose Rizal, and his execution or killing in Bagumbayan on December 30, 1896.

And we also know: The courage, the greatness, and the revolution and independence that resulted from it are not unique to a single region. Filipino blood was shed in various parts of the Philippines, to nourish the aspiration of a united nation. In fact, here, we commemorate every January 4th the Fifteen Martyrs of Bikol. After being arrested, tortured, and sentenced after only one day of trial, eleven of the fifteen so-called sons of Bicol were hanged in Bagumbayan five days after Gat Jose Rizal was shot. And of the four remaining, two died in prison, and two were deported to prison on the island of Fernando Po in Africa and eventually died there as well.

It is clear to us now: The events concerning your Fifteen Martyrs served as the spark of the revolution here in Bikol. Before this, the desire for freedom among the Bikolanos was not yet overflowing; the unrest beginning to brew in various parts of the country was still far away. But after the torture of the fifteen Bikolanos simply because of the fear and speculation of a few Spaniards at that time, the people could no longer restrain themselves. Perhaps the question in the hearts of the Bikolanos then was, “Where is due process?” Isn’t it true that anyone who is arrested and severely tortured will eventually be forced to say whatever the torturer wants? If this happened to them—priests, teachers, writers, lawyers, businessmen, and public servants—who can say that ordinary people cannot be arrested, tortured, deported, imprisoned, or hanged just like that? Whether they were members of the Katipunan or not, whether the allegations were true or not, can what was done to trample on the process and the rights of the Fifteen Martyrs truly be called justice?

It is timely to revisit these events, especially now that we are treading the path of reform and true justice. We all know one of the hottest issues today: Several prominent personalities have been charged by the Ombudsman regarding the issue of the pork barrel scam. They say they are being politically persecuted. Let me just remind you: It was during the election season in 2013 when the news first surfaced about the illegal detention of Benhur Luy, in March, as well as about the fake NGOs and the pocketing of public funds. We instructed the good Secretary Leila de Lima, do not make accusations without sufficient evidence. We followed due process: We conducted an investigation, gathered and are still gathering evidence, and now a case has been filed. This could have been done quickly and without a solid basis to damage the reputation of the candidates involved in the controversy, but, and you are all witnesses, we went through the proper system to reveal the truth. And now, we are the ones being accused of politicking? You decide who you will side with in this matter.

I often recall what my father told me. He said, “In a true democracy, it is the obligation of everyone to fight not only for the rights of their friends, but also for their enemies. If you trample on anyone’s rights, or if anyone’s rights are trampled on, the time will come when your own rights will be disregarded.” He himself experienced injustice. A year before Martial Law was declared, my father told Mr. Marcos, as the Chief Executive who accused him, that it was his obligation to bring my father to court. But my father was only tried when Mr. Marcos placed the country under Martial Law. My father was brought before a court martial, where the military would try a civilian. In a court composed of judges, lawyers, and witnesses appointed by Mr. Marcos himself, the dictatorship twisted justice. In short, Mr. Marcos was the accuser, he was also the judge, and he still had the power to decide on them. This clearly shows how the dictator twisted the justice system to get what he wanted.

The experience of our family taught me the importance of fighting for equality in order to end the cycle of injustice. Now that I am in a position to live by these lessons, I am doing, and I am doing everything I can to give justice not only to a few, but to the entire nation.

And this is the lesson of history: Events in one part of our archipelago affect the fate of the entire country; the fight against oppression, corruption, or poverty in one province contributes to the overall change we enjoy as a united people. This is also the idea that is being put into practice by the projects and initiatives of your government. There is the Bicol International Airport we are building in Daraga, which will revitalize the tourism sector, and improve our connections within and outside the country. The proposed one-stop Migrants Resource Center here in Naga is part of our agenda to enhance the capabilities of OFWs around the world; while the extension of the PNR charter passed by Congress will create opportunities, not only in the provinces that the rails will pass through, but for every Filipino who can benefit from the opportunities that will open up due to a more vibrant economy.

Eradicating corruption and abuse of the system anywhere in the Philippines—at the local and national levels—will result in stronger institutions that can serve every Filipino. Each of us has something to contribute so that the sacrifices of our ancestors are not in vain. Two years from now, we will need to choose new leaders for our country again. The challenge to us: Choose candidates who can fight for the interests of every citizen, in the face of any challenge. We do not need someone who is good at reciting a script, good at dancing, or good at singing. It is our responsibility to leave a more just and more developed Philippines than the one we inherited.

As history progresses, we defend and continue to achieve the aspirations of our ancestors: After the First Republic established in Malolos fell, we fought against other foreigners who occupied the country. We rose from the damage caused by the war. We toppled the dictatorship through a peaceful revolution in 1986. Heroes continue to emerge who will uphold various forms of freedom in many parts of the country: The sailors stationed in Ayungin; Jesse Robredo here in Naga; the millions of Filipinos who gathered in EDSA. This is what the teachers who sweat to guide our students live by; this is what the soldiers and police who protect our territory and security promote; this is what our professionals and public servants uphold in their daily performance of their duties.

As heirs of the freedom fought for by those who came before us, it is the duty of each of us not to allow our country to return to its former state; to not trigger new sacrifices for many Filipinos. Isn’t it true that true success in achieving change is measured when it no longer requires heavy sacrifices?

This Independence Day, let us all pay tribute to the heroes who fought for what is right. Let us use them as inspiration in our continued journey on the straight and narrow path. Let us internalize the lessons they left us: Caring for our fellow human beings will lead us to what we aspire to as a race. Only in this way can we say that we are truly worthy of their sacrifices; only in this way can we establish a Philippines that is truly just and free.

Again, a good morning to all, and thank you very much.
