Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III President of the Philippines Inspection and briefing for major infrastructure projects in Bulacan

Just a while ago, we inspected the additional portion being constructed of the Plaridel Bypass Road project, which will connect the NLEX and Maharlika Highway. Once completed, the travel time between Burol, Balagtas, and Maasim, San Rafael, which used to take more than an hour, will be reduced to almost half an hour. Approximately 15,000 motorists per day will benefit from this. If we talk about commerce, the products coming from here, and even those from other places that need to pass through Bulacan, will be delivered to the market faster. The return on this: Of course, the fruits and vegetables, because they are delivered fresher to the central market, can be sold at more reasonable prices.

We also had a briefing earlier regarding the flood control projects here in your province. We know that when there is heavy rain, Bulacan is one of the first to be affected. These constructions in your area are part of our comprehensive Flood Management Master Plan, which will address the flooding here in Bulacan, in Metro Manila, and in some neighboring towns. We prioritized the flood wall in Meycauayan, Obando, as well as in Marilao. Those at the bottom, the two in the middle of the photos, are what we refer to as protection. We also heard earlier a report about the various infrastructure projects being undertaken here with you.

Let me highlight some of our big-ticket items for the province of Bulacan. Let's start with Angat Dam, which supplies water to the National Capital Region and neighboring towns. Angat, according to MWSS Administrator Gerry Esquivel, its rehabilitation is long overdue. Since July 2015, we have been undertaking what is called “retrofitting” to strengthen the dam and dike, especially in the event of an earthquake that we hope will not happen. We have also installed flood forecasting and warning systems, including flood control protection. Let me emphasize: Angat is vulnerable to earthquakes, so we are taking these steps. This includes the construction of a new access tunnel for water from Angat to Ipo Dam; after this, we will repair the three old access tunnels that are weak and often leak. If my memory serves me right, some of these tunnels were built during the time my father was born. So they are no longer young.

Let me also mention the recently signed Bulacan Bulk Water Supply Project, which I heard has been promised to you for a long time. What I know is, I wasn't the one who made that promise. I'm just the one who will make it happen. Construction will begin this July; the groundbreaking will be this April. And once it's finished, it will deliver 200 million liters of water per day to 13 water districts in your province. [Applause] Manong Willie, will no longer complain that the water flowing to you cannot be used for drinking or irrigation. Now, what's the point of all this? Water is the natural source of life. Without it, our Bosses will face severe hardship. All I can say is: You see, we are continuously acting to ensure that you are never put in a difficult situation, and these projects that will deliver service to you will continue even after I step down from office after 128 days. But I'm not counting that closely. [Laughter]

I just want to emphasize: All the problems we are solving, every opportunity we are improving the situation of the ordinary Filipino, all the successes we have achieved and will achieve on the Straight Path—you are making these changes happen. It would not be possible to fulfill our pledge to leave a Philippines that is far better than the one we inherited, without your full trust and support. So, to our Bosses, especially to our beloved Bulakenyos: Thank you very much to all of you. [Applause]

Because of your contribution to the Straight Path, a new school building for Ramona S. Trillana High School has been concretized. It was mentioned to us: Before this school building existed, teachers and students suffered in classrooms that were termite-infested, old, and flooded during rain or storms. Back then, space was a problem, and with the number of students enrolling, they held double shifts in classes; there were even some who were forced to use a tent just to have classes. Now, the situation here is very different.

This new building is part of our comprehensive initiative to further improve the quality of education in the country. I would also like to share: This new school building of yours is the result of a well-thought-out strategy of good governance that we call “convergence,” where government agencies work together to address our various needs. Here, DepEd and PAGCOR jointly assessed and identified what should be allocated; the DPWH, in partnership with DepEd, implemented the project.

We thank Chairman Bong Naguiat and PAGCOR, not only for the support they have extended to this project, but for all their efforts to change the image of their agency: from being a coffee shop for the corrupt, to a GOCC that truly cares for the people. [Applause] We also thank Secretary Babes Singson and Bro. Armin Luistro, who continue to steer the DPWH and DepEd to deliver more meaningful service to our Bosses, who gave us the mandate.

This school building, these new roads, flood control projects, and other services that you are enjoying, are solid proof that your needs have not been set aside on the Straight Path. If you were to sum up the funds we poured into infrastructure alone in Bulacan from 2011 to 2016, it would reach P19.04 billion—that is almost four times the P5.01 billion allocated by our predecessor for you in his last six years in office. If you look at the budget we allocated this year for national infrastructure: it amounts to approximately P766 billion. That is equivalent to 5 percent of our current Gross Domestic Product. Far from the amount inherited from those we replaced in 2010, which was only P165 billion.

Since we took office, we have increased the allocation for infrastructure year after year. That is why we have already repaired more than 6,200 kilometers of national roads. There is another thousand coming. We have also completed 1,550 kilometers of tourism roads and 3,700 local roads for our communities. I believe it is clear that there is a difference when the focus of governance is only the interest of the people: The State has a true ability to improve the quality of life of our citizens. On the Straight Path, the focus is: to alleviate poverty, not tomorrow, but, as the youth say, “Now na.”

You are all witnesses to all the positive changes. Of course, as I repeatedly emphasize: this is just the beginning of all this and the reform could become permanent. You, my Bosses, will continue the transformation and we can maximize the opportunity to further develop our country.

For me, of course, as I mentioned earlier, I only have 128 days left: We have a tandem that will surely continue all that we have achieved—those with one word and many accomplishments, those who will truly prioritize the country before themselves; the only partnership of the Straight Path: none other than Mar Roxas and Leni Robredo. [Applause]

My Bosses, we are now at a higher starting point; we can still reach a far better level for our country. We can do that, if we remain on the Straight Path. But if we choose the opposite, who can say for sure that others will continue what we have started? What will happen to the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program, Philhealth, infrastructure, the jobs created, the modernization of our police and military? Worse, if we make the mistake of electing the corrupt, abusive, or those who make excessive promises, what guarantee do we have that we can recover by 2022?

I am confident that: My Bosses will choose those who truly have integrity, a clear plan and vision, those who have already proven themselves and are truly loyal to the country; and in the end, you will surely lead our country to the fulfillment of many more dreams.

Earlier, while I was listening to our students, I said, “You are good at declaiming.” I hope you’re not offended, young lady. [Laughter]

When we were in Los Angeles, we interviewed a young person, fourteen years old, who asked me about K-12. So I asked him in response, “Is there a day when you study your lessons and you tell yourself ‘I have too much time. I wish I had something else to do with this time’?” He looked at me and said, “No, sir. It’s always lacking.” That’s the point: If before it was ten years, forcing everything to be taught to you as if in a funnel, then you need to fill it in ten years and compete in the job market with people who studied for twelve years. Wouldn’t we be at a disadvantage there? I said, “Our country is one of only three with a ten-year Basic Education Program.”

During my time with Manong Willie, but he was a little ahead of me, [laughter], computers were just being thought of, and if you talked to them, you probably used punch cards. Now, if you really don’t know how to use a computer, you’ll really be left behind. So you need to learn more skills, you can’t reduce the time to study.

Earlier, when you were describing the problem here and the first speakers, I said, “We’re not different. We’re the same in Tarlac. You, in Central Luzon, our problem is water. During the rainy season, it rushes; during the dry season, there is none. With you, it’s been added: during high tide, there’s flooding.” [Laughter] I remember the high tide, every day. In other words, every day there is a potential problem. Earlier, it was mentioned about studying. It’s like wading to class, if there is a classroom.

Why am I mentioning this? It’s very simple: I have a friend, at the start of my term, who said to me, “You know what I can’t forgive your predecessor for, the almost ten years that were lost to us.” This building in front of us could have been built then, right? They say about P16 million was spent there, but if it were four storeys, the cost per unit would be lower. PAGCOR spent one billion pesos on coffee. You do the math, divide one billion by sixteen million, that’s how many classrooms. The bottom line is, when it was left to me, 66,800 classrooms were lacking, and we were even told it wasn’t lacking because of double shifting here. In Quezon City, I remember, if you were unlucky, it was quadruple shifting. The child would come home late. A grade school child, arriving home at nine o’clock at night.

He was in office for almost ten years, the one I replaced. We had P1 billion that we could have spent on coffee, but we didn’t build a classroom worth P16 million. That was the “Straight Path.” It could have been done. The painful part is, once time runs, it runs. There’s no going back, no rewind, no “Can we start again?” What was lost is lost, we can no longer recover it. If the management is right, this is what we can do: We can build roads, we can address flooding, we can do everything, and we can do it properly and thoroughly.

Some of these are really big projects. That 4,000 square kilometers mentioned earlier, just imagine that for flood control. We can’t do that overnight. Of course, we need detailed engineering and design. Where will it be placed? What will it look like? What will its form be? It needs to go through experts. There’s soil analysis, weather pattern analysis, all sorts of analysis. Two years is probably too short for doing detailed engineering and design.

It’s like the plan is almost finished, there’s a groundbreaking, then we go back to square one. That’s why that’s important. On May 9, we will vote. Our future really depends on that. We can choose someone who promises the moon and the stars, then when you have a flood, they’ll pass by with relief goods one day, then leave you to fend for yourselves. Or we can create a permanent solution. Maybe we can avoid wasting time like the six years we worked on together.

I am just your servant. You are my boss, you will decide for all of us.

Good afternoon. Thank you all very much.
