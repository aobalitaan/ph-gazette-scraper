Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, at the Inauguration of the Iloilo River Plains Subdivision Phase 1

Senate President Frank Drilon; Secretary Mar Roxas; Secretary Babes Singson; NHA General Manager Chito Cruz;

You know, Chito was my classmate. Sometimes they ask if he was my professor—no. He was my classmate. We asked him earlier—what did we promise here: daycare, basketball court, all the community facilities, school. Church, we are not involved in that because it is prohibited to come from the government—the church came from Mrs. Drilon, they say. Now, of course, Chito and I have a long history, he is not used to his word being broken. So I asked him, “When will this be finished?” He said, next year. That probably means sooner because I know Chito idolizes Babes Singson—when he promises six months, it means four months. Jed Mabilog, our good mayor, is still here to remind us. Maybe you already have an allowance for that next year. [Laughter and applause]

Our good governor, Art Defensor, will also remind us that the people of Iloilo will not be upset if he does it earlier, instead of next year, we are okay with it being done this year.

Boboy will also help, our good vice governor—Boboy Tupas. And if Chito is not here in Iloilo, our good congressmen will take care of it: Richard Garin, Manong Cadio Goricetta, Toto Defensor, and of course Jun Tupas. I would also like to greet Mr. Luis Oquiñena of Gawad Kalinga, other local government officials present, fellow workers in government, honored guests, my beloved fellow Ilonggos:

Good afternoon to all.

The food that Senate President Franklin Drilon fed me was delicious. I said, “Don’t make it too delicious because I slow down when it’s delicious.”

You know, I should also greet Mrs. Mila Drilon. If Tita Mila wasn’t here, I think our good Senate President wouldn’t be as agile. [Laughter and applause]

You know, when Senator Frank Drilon invited me to visit you here in Iloilo, I didn't hesitate to say “yes.” We all know Senator Frank; there's a reason why he's a “Big Man in the Senate.” Here we are, and as we toured Iloilo, we visited and witnessed big-time projects as well, which surely have big-time benefits for the province and our countrymen here in Iloilo. They toured [tour] us around a very long road connecting four bridges. That's why it was so expensive. In Luzon, we call something like that an expressway. Now, I'm worried, next week, I might be able to go home to Tarlac and my constituents will be looking at me, saying “When will we have half of what's in Iloilo?” I might be delayed in returning home. [Laughter and applause] So, like you, I am also excited for the bigger successes coming to Iloilo in the years to come.

Truly, this day is packed and worthwhile, and it is even more complete now that I am facing all of you, our beloved Ilonggos. It hasn't changed: There is nothing like the heart, enthusiasm, and unity of the Ilonggos. Thank you very much for your continued support for the right path towards the progress of our nation. It's getting long, isn't it? So when I return to Manila, I'll say, “Who is this Ilongga he's courting who speaks Ilonggo so well?” But that's not our purpose here. Let's prioritize the nation first.

From the very beginning of our term, we have called on all Filipinos: Let us walk together on the straight path; the path that will lead us to the fulfillment of our collective aspirations. The success we have today proves: cooperation and contribution bear positive results; genuine concern for others and the nation leads to something worthwhile.

This afternoon, we are gathered to inaugurate this more than five-hectare Iloilo River Plains Subdivision Phase 1. There are two sources of the more than 169 million peso funding for this project. One hundred million pesos came from our 2011 Disbursement Acceleration Program, or DAP. The remaining more than 69 million pesos came from the 2011 General Appropriations Act or GAA. This is concrete proof of the effectiveness of our funding system. It's clear: When funds from taxes paid by the people are used properly, it brings benefits to Filipinos, and doesn't just end up in the pockets of a few greedy individuals.

Let me repeat: I think Chito will never forget this. He was still a child then. But, Chito, we have houses here, our community activity centers, basketball courts, schools, day care centers, health centers—are we also providing barangay outposts? Okay. So, to put it simply, half has been delivered. There's still half coming. And if we earnestly ask, and add prayer, I think my classmate will produce all of these sooner rather than later.

Now, I know why you didn't make me your godfather. You find it difficult when we talk. You have more burdens to carry.

This housing project offers one thousand new homes that will serve as shelter for one thousand poor families here in Barangay Camalig and Barangay Lanit in the district of Jaro. What changes will these housing units bring to our Ilonggo countrymen?

These new homes bring peace of mind. Before, families lived around the Iloilo River. Because of this precarious and dangerous location, those who lived here always felt on the brink of danger and uncertainty. A little rain and thunder would make your heart pound because of the possibility of heavy rain or a storm, of the river rising and overflowing into the community. Now, your location is safer and your homes are more durable. You no longer need to worry. Whether it rains or shines, the whole family is more at peace. And the truth is: not only the residents will benefit from this. Because the Iloilo River is now further away from the houses, the river will also be spared from the danger caused by garbage and pollution that are killing the river.

Therefore, this project is just a part of a grand plan to achieve the full potential of Iloilo. Along with the successive construction of infrastructure that will ease the flow of transportation in the province, there are also buildings that will surely enrich your local tourism and economy.

Just this morning, we came from Barangay Ungka in the Jaro district, for the inauguration of four bridges along the Iloilo Circumferential Road or C-1. After that, we went straight to Iloilo Business Park in the Mandurriao district for the inspection and briefing of your Iloilo Convention Center, which is now being proposed by Senate President to be the site of the APEC next year. I said, “The infrastructure is ahead of you, I haven’t delivered anything to Tarlac yet.” In the same district, we then inaugurated the expanded two-kilometer portion of Senator Benigno S. Aquino Jr. Avenue. I said, it’s good that we are expanding these roads right away. Because of the progress of Iloilo and the entire Panay Island, commerce will surely flourish—the roads will be filled with vehicles and people. And of course, as you progress, the pressure or demand on our infrastructure also increases. That’s how fast and diligent Secretary Babes Singson is: Before anyone complains about the roads being congested and traffic jams, DPWH has already laid out the solution. The entire province is now all set to move forward even further.

There in the Bangsamoro, because as you know, maybe you’ve heard, I said, right now we are dealing with conflict, killings, and unrest. I hope that peace can be achieved quickly and that in time—I think as long as people are alive, there will always be problems. I hope, maybe as early as five years from now, when we go to the Autonomous Region of Muslim Mindanao, the people there will tell us, well, I will be retired by then, but they will tell us, “This progress is amazing—it’s congested, polluted, and the criminals here are white collar!” But that’s probably better than all the shootings, kidnappings, and bombings.

The transformation of Iloilo that we are seeing is truly remarkable. And it is clear that this did not happen in an instant. It comes from careful planning, collective effort, prudent spending of public funds, and the determination that this will be realized. The key to this: You are aware of your strengths. You know the blessings that come with the strategic location of your province, your natural resources, and the well-known diligence and talent of the Ilonggos—and it is very evident that you are maximizing the potential of these positive qualities.

This success comes from the cooperation of the local and national government—led and guided by our good Senate President Frank Drilon, along with the private sector and partner organizations such as Gawad Kalinga. But above all, it comes from our Ilonggo compatriots, who, in addition to trust and strong support, also contribute to the realization of our initiatives. You are the ones who strive and continue to strive for change. Iloilo’s future belongs to you. I think I have about 75 percent of that.

From restoring integrity to our institutions, to raising the national dignity in the eyes of the world; from wider coverage of health services, to a more secure future due to a better education system—aside from the remnants of the previous administration and some exploiters, is there anyone who would oppose the positive changes that the country is now enjoying? There are definitely more Filipinos who are betting and stepping onto the straight and narrow path. There are more of us on the side of righteousness and accountability. There is no doubt: You are still the ones who make, and continue to make, change. And you will also continue the initiatives that we have started. The challenge for our Bosses in the coming times: use the opportunity to choose honest and worthy leaders again. This will dictate whether we will regress back to the old system, or move forward towards a more developed and comfortable country.

As the greater Iloilo advances in development, we ensure that no Ilonggo is left behind. These homes are just the beginning of a new dawn that the families who will benefit from this project will face. Our goal: to empower our fellow countrymen who are most in need. To deliver enough opportunities to use their abilities and skills, to improve themselves, their families, and even the entire country.

In the end, we are still one family, under one roof, as Filipinos. Through cooperation, let us move the Philippines towards permanent and widespread development.

Before I take my leave, that is a strong request. When we first met, we all had long hair. Perhaps when I leave in 2016, we can return to the way things were. We will repeat the horror to return to the light. No, please. Let us go straight to the light, right?

Good day. Thank you all very much.
