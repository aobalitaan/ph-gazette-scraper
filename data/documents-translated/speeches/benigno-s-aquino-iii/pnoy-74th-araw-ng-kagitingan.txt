Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

on the 74th anniversary of the Day of Valor 2016

[Delivered at the Mount Samat Memorial Shrine, Bataan, on April 9, 2016]

This is now the sixth and final Day of Valor that I will lead as your President. Every year, we ensured our participation in the commemoration, to personally express gratitude and acknowledge the sacrifice of our veterans.

If we speak of recognizing your sacrifice, I am certain you are aware of the fruits of the Straight Path. Primarily, among the reforms we implemented was the cleansing of the list of those receiving aid, through the Pensioner’s Validation Program. It simply isn’t right that funds allocated for you should be divided or taken by those who are not qualified.

Now that the list is being cleansed, we can be certain: The aid will reach you directly. For your health and that of your dependents, there is the Veterans Hospitalization Program. In the past almost six years, 10,600 veterans or your relatives have received free treatment—not only at the Veterans Memorial Medical Center, but also at 183 accredited public and private hospitals throughout the country. The amount of P91 million has already been returned to the people in recognition of your sacrifice under this program.

Your daily hospitalization subsidy has also increased: The previous P1,200 is now P1,500. Aside from this, we have greatly improved the VMMC: It now has an eye center; the Operating Room Complex, Medical Intensive Care Unit, and Outpatient Department Complex have been renovated; and the Renal Dialysis Unit, Medical Intensive Therapy Unit, and Post-Anesthesia Care unit have been expanded.

Alongside the care for your health, we are also focused on nurturing the future. In education: Your direct descendants who are veterans may receive and are receiving up to P36,000 per year as financial assistance for college studies. We have already allocated P370 million within our term.

Aside from personally informing you about the benefits we extend to veterans, I also annually lead our commemoration, because as a student of history, I see this as an opportunity to contribute to enriching our national understanding of the past.

If we recall, even before the outbreak of World War II, we were not yet a fully independent nation. But despite our limited capabilities, as Filipinos, we did what was right and reasonable for our fellow human beings. Under the leadership of President Manuel L. Quezon at that time, the Philippines opened its doors to Jewish refugees from Europe due to the violence against them.

The positive results of our generosity could have been even wider, if only the Philippines had not been caught up in the advent of World War II. Although we were not yet making decisions for ourselves at that time, we were not spared from the suffering caused by the decisions of others.

The damage caused by World War II was immense, which happened less than a generation after World War I. With the destroyed livelihoods and infrastructure, especially the many lives it claimed, it truly brought down the affected countries.

Therefore, we are led to ask: What is the most important lesson that World War II has shared with us? It is clear: In times of conflict or turmoil, a free nation, even those not involved in the clash, cannot remain indifferent or silent. If we do not address the root of the conflict, it is certain that a time will come when this problem will worsen, to the point where everyone will inevitably be affected.

Let us look at the situation now in Europe, where they are affected by the turmoil in Syria. Primarily, this is their problem with refugees, and the spread of terrorism that is destroying many lives. Linked to the instability in Syria is the potential spread of disease due to the disruption of health services. The result of this, if we allow this situation to continue, could be an outbreak and we will all be affected.

It is true: Countries live together in one world, so the problem of one is the problem of all. If there is a problem in another part of the world, no matter how far away, we must participate and contribute to the solution. Because if we just let it grow, in the end we will be affected by its negative consequences. And if this happens, we will only think: That when it was still small, we should have done something. Here we see the importance of urging our leaders to make correct and reasonable decisions on how to address conflicts and turmoil as soon as possible.

It is also true: In a war, once the fighting is over, it is clear who is considered the loser or winner among the leaders and combatants. But here is another truth. Wherever conflict strikes, when it comes to civilians, when it comes to ordinary people: everyone is a victim; everyone loses.

In the case of the war you, as veterans, went through, and of the civilians who were affected: How many of those who perished could have become outstanding teachers, lawyers, or leaders? How many could have invented new technologies, or discovered a cure for cancer and other serious illnesses? How many of them could have contributed to reaching the potential of humanity?

Perhaps these questions can no longer be answered. What we can do is focus on the future, and as we reach for our dreams, ensure that the bitter lessons of the past are not forgotten. As you know, our country is facing another critical juncture in the coming elections. My question to you is: Will we choose leaders who prioritize their own interests, while guaranteeing the suffering of those below? Will we choose leaders who want to take shortcuts in everything, without considering those who are trampled upon? Will we choose very sweet promises, but seem to have insufficient understanding of the processes that need to be gone through to achieve them?

As you know, my position on this matter: I stand with what is right, with what is honest, with someone who has extensive knowledge in governance. We stand with those who will continue the Straight Path. You know who I am referring to, and I am confident that our dreams for future generations are aligned.

The Philippines has indeed gone through so much. You, the veterans who have gone through many upheavals—you who have witnessed the repeated falls and rises of our country—you are perhaps the ones who can give the most profound testimony to how far we have come in the past almost six years. You, more than anyone else, can say how deep the positive transformation we are experiencing has been. I am confident that you will be among those who will advance what we are fighting for: Let us continue the change; let us continue to reach for our dreams; let us further advance the Straight Path.

Before I conclude, when I entered, a woman greeted me and said, “Thank you for the six years.” My proper response to that should have been, “Thank you for the mandate you have given me to make change. This opportunity is truly unparalleled.” This is it then, I bid farewell to you all in Bataan.

Good day and thank you very much.
