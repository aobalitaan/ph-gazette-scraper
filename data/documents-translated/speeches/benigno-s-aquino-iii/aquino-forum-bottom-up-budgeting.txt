Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, at the 2016 National Assembly of the League of Barangays in the Philippines and forum on Bottom-Up Budgeting

I think many of you have heard me say that the job in politics I would never want is being a barangay captain. Because that is true: 24/7, 365.25 days a year you are on-call. And if you can’t fulfill even half of the ten requests, if you only gave nine and a half, that one half you didn’t give, that’s all that will be remembered. Am I right? [Applause]

The most difficult election is yours. In our place in Tarlac, it’s a competition of relatives, godfathers, godmothers, and godchildren. Sometimes—I think this also happens in your areas—the difference in votes is: one, two. How difficult is it to reconcile after the election, right?

Let me tell you a story. This came from Mel Sarmiento, so I will continue his speech. [Laughter] Why is it difficult to run for office among you? There was someone who wanted to serve as a barangay captain. And in his home, of course he has a wife, he has three children, all of them are voters. There are five voters in his house. The day of the election came, the votes were counted. He received two votes. In the entire barangay. [Laughter] So the next meal they had after the counting, the father who ran for captain looked at his family. He said, “There are three Judases at this table.” [Laughter]

I also put myself in his situation. I wouldn’t say anything about his competence or lack thereof, but it was really very difficult, wasn’t it? At your table, at your dining room, your children, your wife, with you, and not everyone even voted for you. [Laughter] How would you lift your own dignity?

Earlier, Mel was also sharing her difficulties, saying she went here and there, asking for assistance, almost seeking help for the problems approaching her, and she didn't get anything. She is not alone.

When I was a congressman—three terms in Tarlac—that was the usual response to us. For example, a flood. How could I fix a flood if you're just a congressman? So we approached them. There were two offices we went to, one in Pampanga, one in Pangasinan. When we went, they told us, “You are covered by this project in Pangasinan.” I asked the one in Pangasinan, “Oh, that’s wrong. You are covered by the one in Pampanga,” because Tarlac is in the middle of these two projects. So we went to Pampanga, and the response we got was, “No, the one in Pangasinan was mistaken. They are the ones who cover you.” [Laughter] In other words, we kept going back and forth between the two and we didn’t get anything done. So, Mel, you’re really not alone. [Laughter]

In the end, to be honest, of course the congressman’s PDAF (Priority Development Assistance Fund) would be brought to the barangay. That was the style we adopted. By God’s grace, instead of me getting funds from the National Government, I was asked for funds by the National Government. To be honest, the road traversing MacArthur Highway in Tarlac City, which frequently floods because the Tarlac River is higher than the road, we helped, we used the PDAF to fund the DPWH (Department of Public Works and Highways) to fix Masalasak Creek in our area so the flooding would stop. I said, “This is new. Instead of us asking, we are the ones being asked.” And to be honest, when we were a congressman, we only talked to barangay officials. I would ask and consult them, what their problems were and we would agree on a solution. That’s why when a national government agency asked us and I told the barangays under my jurisdiction if we could postpone the project because the flooding in our area was really severe, our barangay councils agreed. I really felt that we were helping each other and we really benefited from it.

And this is my weak… in English, “Poor attempt at trying to follow after Mel Sarmiento.” [Laughter] As I said yesterday, Butch Abad should have just spoken for me now, so he would be the one to worry. [Just kidding.]

It would be good if I started my speech by looking back at the situation we found, which I know you all know as the fathers and mothers of your barangays.

You may have noticed that I often promote the Pantawid Pamilya. When we came to this program, the number of beneficiaries reached was small: approximately 780,000 households. Far from the current 4.4 million households that we are helping today. What is sad about what we found: Aside from the limited scope of the CCT or 4Ps during that time, the selection of beneficiaries was also questioned. It was no longer determined who were the most in need, it seemed those close to those in power were favored. That was also the situation we found in Philhealth. Before elections, the number of program beneficiaries would suddenly increase.

It is true: The previous state of Pantawid Pamilya, Philhealth, and other programs served as a sign of the mindset that prevailed in the old system: Governance was focused on politics. Every action of those in power seemed focused on the question: How can I stay in office? The citizens, meanwhile, were left lacking in service and attention from the government.

We, in the path of righteousness, have always emphasized one thing from the beginning: The people are the boss; your mandate comes from you; you give us strength. My stance has always been: I am here as your servant, and it is clear to me that whatever the bosses want, that is what I will focus on.

As leaders, dictating is not our style; instead, we believe in building consensus that will lead us to the most appropriate response to the problem. One way we put this philosophy into practice is through Bottom-Up Budgeting, where the government and communities have one spirit and direction.

Isn't it true that: Those who are immersed in the problem are likely to know the most appropriate solution? They are the most studied on the problem; they are also the most eager to fix it. As Chief Executive, I believe: We must listen to those with real experience and reasonable suggestions to correct what is wrong.

Just imagine what will happen if the State chooses not to listen to those it serves. This reminds me of the current water crisis in the city of Flint, Michigan in the United States, where even after many residents complained that their water contained lead and was unsafe to drink, their concerns were not immediately addressed. It even reached a point where there were reports of illnesses among their youth. We can say that ignoring the initial appeals of ordinary citizens contributed to the worsening of the situation.

Listen to the people: This is the principle behind our initiatives. The KALAHI-CIDSS program, which aims to deliver services and benefits to the most remote and isolated communities in the country, is proof of this. The number of households reached under this program: 3.9 million.

From our experience with KALAHI-CIDSS, we further expanded the strategy of direct empowerment. In Bottom-Up Budgeting, communities and civil society organizations meet, and from this, appropriate suggestions arising from their own experiences are formed. These are then brought to the local government, which serves as a bridge to the national government that will assist and guide the implementation of the agreed-upon plan.

Because the solution comes from consensus, which we can say is the most heartfelt by the community, we can expect their full support for it, everyone will contribute, and the project is more likely to succeed.

Let's take as an example the people of Brgy. Anahawan in Calauag, Quezon. Imagine, for almost six decades, they lived without a proper water supply. Through BUB and the shared responsibility of their LGU, citizens, and local Civil Society Organization, they collectively pushed for the installation of an irrigation system. Now, they no longer need to walk 3 kilometers just to fetch water from the nearest water source, because there are 15 communal faucets in their community.

Now, the report from our good Secretary Butch Abad of the Department of Budget and Management: Since the implementation of the BUB in 2013, P74.06 billion has been allocated for more than 54,000 approved BUB projects. 13,712 have already been completed; while others are ongoing and in the pre-implementation stage such as planning, designing, and bidding. It is expected that the bulk of the projects will be completed by next year.

We asked Butch: Why is the number of completed projects still relatively small? His explanation to us: There is a process that the implementation of the BUB needs to go through; the implementing entities have varying expertise and levels of readiness; that is why the implementation of the projects is not simultaneous. We are now at the municipal level, and we plan to expand the BUB to the barangay level. [Applause]

Let me emphasize: This will use the people’s money, so the implementation cannot be haphazard. That is why we are increasing the capacity of everyone through training. In this way, we can also expedite the realization of the plans.

That is how we are on the Straight Path. It is clear where we are headed: The state of each community is rising, and they are directly empowered to further promote the positive transformation that will bring benefits to all. If before, there was a government that lorded over others and indulged in dispensing services only to allies; now, with us, on the Straight Path, all levels of society and branches of government are united, and together we identify the right steps for a better future.

I think all of you here will agree that empowering our LGUs and barangays should be made permanent. You are witnesses to the fruits of good governance. You are also the ones who can say how far we have come. All our achievements are just the beginning of transformation, and you will tell us if we will continue. For this to happen, we need to elect a real team that will continue the Straight Path. [Applause] And for me, there is no one else but Mar Roxas and Leni Robredo.

My Bosses, as always, you will determine the direction we will take. I trust my Bosses: They will choose what is right and reasonable, they will side with those who have principles and have proven themselves, and they will truly lead our country to where it should be.

Actually, I started by drawing a parallel to the life of Mel Sarmiento. I was really happy with his story about the “calling card.” I can’t say that anyone asked for my calling card, because honestly, I don’t have one. But honestly, when we interviewed some heads of government, I really felt at the beginning that they were forcing themselves to deal with me. It felt like they were obligated to talk to me, how to rush and finish the conversation? There was even one who seemed to be lecturing me. I said, “That’s quite a presence.”

That was then. Now, one of them who was really having a hard time talking to me, we had a meeting, and he said to me, “Our economy is growing at 1.4 percent. The Philippine economy is growing at 6.6 percent. What is the Philippines’ secret?” I said, “We’re just catching up, and you’re going to copy us.” [Laughter] I told him, “Well, we just followed your example.” So I complimented him, and he didn’t ask me any more details. Our GDP is still higher than theirs now.

The other one, it was really like that, we were reprimanded. I had just sat down then. Perhaps I had only been serving for two or three months when I received a scolding from someone who wasn't a Filipino like me. That was our first gathering. The second, they visited the Philippines and asked us to visit them. Really, if I only had business cards, Mel, we could have distributed many. [Applause]

Let me conclude with this [riddle]: I will repeat, tomorrow-the-day-after-tomorrow, who will shape it? Us. When will we shape it? Now. Will we say, “Let's continue what is happening here” or will you say, “Come on, let's take a risk, maybe something better will happen”? There is a saying: Keep pushing and you might ruin it. We are all politicians here, we know promises are often broken. Where will we go? To what is certain or to leaving it to fate? I think if we think carefully, we should go with what is certain. [Applause] So it's up to you.

Good day to everyone. Thank you very much.
