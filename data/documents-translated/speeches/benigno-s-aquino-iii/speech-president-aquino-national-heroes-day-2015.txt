Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, on the celebration of National Heroes Day

Today, as we stand in the Libingan ng mga Bayani (Heroes' Cemetery), I recall: When my mother passed away, those very people who opposed her struggles and continue to oppose them immediately suggested building a monument in her honor. Because of this, a saying from my childhood came to mind: “A little stone, a little cement, a monument.” It is true: It is easy to erect a statue; but it becomes worthless and loses its meaning if we do not live by the principles and fight for the causes of the individuals we honor.

This is the essence of our annual observance of National Heroes Day. We solemnly commemorate and look up to the monuments here, as a tribute to the Filipino heroes who stood firm, sacrificed, and offered their lives for their fellowmen and for the Motherland. We are deeply grateful to our countrymen who wholeheartedly faced danger to fight for what is right and just; those who tested their responsibility and weighed their convictions, and did not fall short; those who faced a crossroads, and chose to prioritize the interests of the majority over their own. Whether there is a monument or not, whether their names are written in the pages of history or not— we proudly recognize the contribution of every hero who rose up and strengthened the foundation of the peace and progress we enjoy today.

Today, we also received a copy of the book entitled “To Die Because of You” from the National Historical Commission of the Philippines. It is dedicated to the martyrs and Filipino heroes who stood against the dictatorship of Martial Law from 1972 to 1986. They are also among the Filipinos we thank and recognize today. As the philosopher George Santayana said: “Those who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it.” This book is truly a good legacy for the current generation, as well as for future generations, to avoid the mistakes of the past, and to serve as a guide for the steps towards the future.

In this gathering, I also recalled at this moment the statement of writer Cynthia Ozick. It is about heroism, she said:

“Three ‘participant’ categories of the Holocaust are commonly named: murderers, victims, bystanders. Imagination demands a choosing. Which, of this entangled trio, are we? Which are we most likely to become?

…When a whole population takes on the status of bystander, the victims are without allies; the criminals, unchecked, are strengthened; and only then do we need to speak of heroes. When a field is filled from end to end with sheep, a stag stands out. When a continent is filled end to end with the compliant, we learn what heroism is. And alas for the society that requires heroes.”

It is true: When violence happens—there is someone who commits violence and someone who is a victim of violence; and if you are neither the perpetrator, nor the one oppressed, what did you do? It is said that heroes are needed in these times. It is here that the greatness of certain individuals emerges and shines.

If we think about it: This is still very true today. When there is something wrong and unjust in society—if you just watch from the sidelines, if you just follow the dictates of the status quo, or complain without offering a solution, doesn't that just add to the problem, and prolong the suffering of your fellow citizens? On the other hand, if even one person stands up and confronts them to say, “What you are doing is wrong. I will not allow you to exploit the Filipino,” wouldn't that surely break the cycle of oppression and injustice?

Today, we value and honor the sacrifices made by Filipino heroes. Along with our glorification of them, we are called upon to contribute and continue the fight they started—in small or large ways—for the good of our nation.

There is a reason why the word “bayan” (nation/country) is embedded in the word “bayani” (hero). Each of us has the ability to transcend ourselves for the sake of others and our flag. Whether ordinary citizens or those in high positions, we all have a duty and the ability to participate in improving society—from simply following laws and regulations; to being generous to others, especially those in greater need; to being willing to fight for what is right and just at any time and opportunity.

In the history of our race, countless Filipinos have proven—from the revolution of 1896, our freedom from Spain in 1898, our achievement of independence from the United States in 1946, the EDSA People Power Revolution in 1986, to the unity of the people in 2010—that we can make meaningful change. We can uplift and develop our country. All we need is to unite, act, and work together, so that the heavier burdens of the country will no longer need to be borne by a few. The burden of each one will be lightened, and we will no longer be able to pass on any current problems to future generations.

As my father said: “We should not depend on one man. We should depend on all of us.” In the name of every Filipino hero: Let us join hands as one nation. I am confident that now that we have made a good start, we will continue towards the destination we aspire to; the Filipino will reach the beautiful future we dream of for our beloved country.

Good day. Thank you very much to all of you.
