Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

on the 45th anniversary of the Presidential Management Staff

[delivered at the PICC, Pasay City on August 20, 2015]

I was late getting here. I was looking at all of your faces, it seemed like you were either very serious, very angry, or very depressed. So I asked Julia [Abad], “Julia, has that PEI been released yet?” Butch was still talking about SSL4. It’s your anniversary here, everything really had a very somber feel. I even asked SND [Voltaire Gazmin], “Tol, are they copying you with your always expensive smile?” You can see, I just realized now that a singer is possible, even an Italian singer. There are also lights here on the side. It looks bright. It looks like we’ll be dancing and singing later. I heard your band isn’t a native talent. Imported. They didn’t tell me if it was imported or not. Anyway.

Even during my mother’s time, the Presidential Management Staff was already well-known. When you say PMS, you can expect fast, effective, and efficient work. Whenever my mother needed information, the PMS was ready to immediately provide accurate and up-to-date data. When there was a need to follow up on directives to government agencies, she also relied on the PMS. The PMS acted as my mother’s eyes and ears to learn about problems and focus on the implementation of solutions. I really saw the extent of the support this agency gave to her leadership.

As I entered the presidency, this is the kind of service we expected from the PMS. At the same time, I also saw how, due to nine and a half years of mismanagement and neglect by those who came before us, your capabilities were affected. I will take this opportunity to ask for your understanding if my frustration sometimes came out during those early days. After all, I consider you my direct action arm.

I admit it: That’s just how I pushed you and your esteemed Secretary, because I was really pushing myself to improve my work as President. Every year, in my opinion, the quality of your work really improves. That’s why I didn’t hesitate to approve your additional milestone anniversary bonus. [Applause] So that’s why you were serious earlier. [Laughter] Now you still had doubts that we would sign it. Honestly, it wouldn’t be right for those of you who work doubly hard in public service to be overlooked by those you support.

Likewise, I need to emphasize: Everything we do is focused on our Bosses, the people. Isn’t there a saying that “The truth shall set us free?” Part of what we do here in government is ensuring that we not only provide any answer, but must always provide the correct answer. If the response is insufficient or inappropriate, we are not far from what computer enthusiasts call “Garbage In, Garbage Out.” As President, every day, I need to make the right decisions, I need to go through various issues, I need to oversee the entire Executive Branch. To do this, I will continue to rely on accurate and complete information from the PMS.

Of course, along with my challenge to you is my recognition of all your contributions in the name of good governance.

First of all, thank you, Julia; even when we were still in the Senate, I already saw your dedication. Whether it was staying up all night, dealing with stress, or facing all sorts of pressure—you never ran out of energy and were always a fighter. So, tonight, cheers to you; [applause]

To your deputy, Third Bagro, and chief of staff Nancy Quimpo-Fajardo, and even to Kat, whom I saw earlier, they are still here. To Kat Manahan, your former head executive assistant, and to all the units under them: thank you for ensuring that my briefers are complete and everything is ready for any meeting or situation;

To the leaders and members of our policy and monitoring units, whose enthusiasm in coordinating with agencies is undeniable; a special mention here to Cedrik Forbes and Raf Ignacio. [Applause] Cedrik, your seeming lack of growth isn't a sign of your stress with me. And to Raf, who is now in America: I hope your dedication to work isn't a coping mechanism;

To the Office for Regional Concerns, who are always the first one in and last one out, and ensure that our official trips in various parts of the country are well-organized; I would particularly like to mention Jig, Jhai, and Shiela; [applause] our sing-alongs are my way of helping you release stress. I thank you three because even when you are tired, as soon as you see me, you can still manage to smile; Jhai, you were frowning earlier though.

To those overseeing appointments and personnel; to those handling requests, invitations, and speaking engagements; and to those serving in the secretariats of various meetings and activities;

Let me also mention everyone who helps ensure that spending from our Social Fund is correct, reasonable, and meaningful. As you know, in the new cottage industry of my critics, when I immediately show up in a place hit by disaster or tragedy, they say I’m an opportunist. I would also like to emphasize: those same critics say what “immediately” means. If I don’t show up immediately, they say my response is insufficient. We are not like some politicians who will only hand out a few bags of relief goods for a photo opportunity, and then are never seen again. My interest, I think the interest of all of us: to build back better, prevent that kind of tragedy from happening again, and reach our countrymen who truly need the care of the State. Thank you, and we are really able to do this.

I cannot mention all the offices and branches of the PMS, and even everyone who served in your agency during our administration; but it is clear that you play a very large and important role in ensuring that a President can fulfill all his responsibilities. I mentioned this before: In my job, it’s like watching 200 channels on TV; I need to follow every event simultaneously; and if a problem arises, I need to have answered it yesterday. In making my decisions, you have truly been my partners. Therefore, on your anniversary, please accept my personal gratitude for the professionalism and strong support that marks the Presidential Management Staff. Thank you also for the services I expect to improve from today and in the days to come.

I am confident that the PMS we will leave to the next administration will be a model of diligence, competence, and integrity. My call to each of you: Demonstrate these good principles, not only within your agency, but also in your dealings with others. Encourage others to join our agenda of effective, open, and honest service. As you return to your respective homes, after a long day of preparing briefs, chasing data from agencies, and planning activities; as you face the mirror, challenge yourselves: “I will surpass what I did today; I will still do what is right.”

Please, have faith: In the remaining 315 days of our term, even as we are nearing its end, I will further push you to improve your outputs; expect that I will also double my enthusiasm to further propel our country forward; and we will expand and strengthen our commitment to promote good governance in every aspect of society.

Comrades, next year, during your anniversary celebration, I might just be a visitor—assuming you invite me—because of the looks on your faces now, I don't know if you're angry with me. [Laughter] As—I need to emphasize this—a bachelor father of the nation, perhaps I can leave you with a riddle, and everyone listening to me:

Just remember what we said, especially during the 2010 campaign: “If there is no corruption, there is no poverty.” I was reminded of the story Senator Bam told us just the other day: He said he went to a place in Bukidnon, and the road they took was half paved and the other half was a really rough road. He said the SUV they were using even had a tire burst because of the roughness of the road. When they reached the place they intended to go, it was full of crops, which I was told were cabbage. He said the place in Bukidnon felt like Baguio in terms of climate. There was a sign there, promising that the unpaved road would be finished by March. You know, when the people were interviewed there, they said their rough road had been there for 50 years. And now they see that this road will really become a paved road. And they almost feel what the benefits will be to their lives and their livelihood. Of course, because transporting their crops will be more efficient, they will be able to sell them at a better price, because their cabbage won't be bouncing around like basketballs on the road anymore.

In my SONA, I mentioned projects like these that have been long awaited. My point here is: You were involved in identifying who needs help, what the needs are, and how they can truly be addressed. Looking back, you can say: The battle we entered was difficult, especially since we started with a citizenry that had become numb to lies, fraud, and theft in government. I believe that corruption in government is not seen in just one person; there is always collusion to succeed. The opposite of that is: if only one person refuses, the cycle of corruption will be broken. That's what is needed in every fight: someone to say, “There is a right way. ‘Good enough’ is not acceptable.”

If we compare our beginnings, it's like we were pushing a stalled truck; of course, that was very difficult. But didn't we manage to start the truck because we worked together, and to keep it running, it seems to be getting easier and easier? Isn't our journey becoming easier and faster, because this stalled vehicle is really running now? Haven't we come so far? With our continued cooperation, I believe we can achieve even greater dreams for the country.

That's all from me. You might even ask me to sing a song in Spanish or French. [Laughter] Asec. Delantar can handle that. So again, a happy anniversary to all of you and good evening.
