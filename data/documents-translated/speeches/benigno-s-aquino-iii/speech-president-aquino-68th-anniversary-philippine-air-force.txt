Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, on the 68th anniversary of the Philippine Air Force.

This will be the last anniversary of our Air Force that I will attend as your Commander-in-Chief. In one year, I will return to private life. So, as I face you, it is fitting that we revisit the promise I made: To leave behind a Philippines that is undeniably better than the one I inherited. The successful reforms we have implemented in the Air Force and throughout the Armed Forces prove that we are true to our word. I remember what a new graduate of the Philippine Military Academy said this year: Thank you, because our Air Force is no longer just “air” but truly has a “force.”

If we look back at history, it is sad to think that our Air Force, one of the first to reach the “jet age” in our region and known for operations such as the Bali and Congo Missions, was left “grounded” due to a system of corruption, transactionalism, and poor management that prevailed in our country for a long time.

You will be even more disheartened by the state we found our Air Force in when you consider how skilled Filipino pilots are. There is the courage shown by Captain Jesus Villamor and his squadron of Boeing P-26A, or the so-called “Peashooters,” who faced the stronger forces of Japan during World War II. The P-26, we can say, was an outdated plane: The cockpit was open and the landing gear was not retractable; therefore, it had a severe “drag” during its operation. Let me add: The P-26 had no oxygen system, making it difficult and dangerous to fly the plane at higher altitudes. It was slower, more difficult to maneuver, and had a lower operational ceiling compared to the Japanese zero fighter, which was considered the most modern fighter at that point in the world. The point here is: Villamor used the P-26, against much more modern planes and more skilled fighter pilots of Japan, who had experience because they had already been involved in Manchuria. But due to Villamor’s skill, even though they were outnumbered, lacked new equipment, and even experience, their group still managed to shoot down one bomber and three zero fighters of the enemy.

It’s not just in the Air Force that there are stories of the skill of our soldiers. There is also someone like Commodore Ramon Alcaraz. During World War II, he encountered nine Japanese zero fighters. Aboard a single Q-boat made of wood, he shot down three of the nine planes.

In 1989, there was the story of a Major Danilo Atienza of the 15th Strike Wing. What happened was: During my mother’s time, there were rebel members of the Air Force who joined the coup against her administration. The rebel pilots and soldiers had even trained in Samar and gathered all their assets in Sangley, including what we call Tora-Tora planes for their planned attack. After they rocketed Camp Aguinaldo and Malacañang, they were going to return to Sangley to prepare the 500-pound bombs they were going to drop. Major Atienza and his companions were ordered to retake the base from the rebel soldiers. Aboard his F-5 Freedom Fighter, he fought the rebels and inflicted severe damage on their ranks. In this operation, Major Atienza gave his life defending our democracy. If we think about it: If Major Atienza hadn’t decided to face the rebels, and if the bombing had gone through, we wouldn’t be here today and everything we are working on for your ranks wouldn’t exist. What I want to emphasize in all of this: There were corrupt individuals in the Air Force, but it was also people from the Air Force who corrected and rectified them.

Our Air Force’s history is full of such scenes of courage. Sadly: Instead of reciprocating your efforts with state care, our predecessors returned the status quo of neglect. What they left us: A citizenry yearning for progress and opportunity; an Armed Forces ready to do what is right and just but lacking in support and equipment; an Air Force with limited ability to reach lofty aspirations for the country.

It was this cycle of hopelessness and neglect of your welfare that we broke on the Straight Path. Through good governance, we have improved the capabilities and equipment of the soldiers of the nation. Our goal: To empower you to more effectively carry out your mandate. That is why we continue to push for modernization in the AFP. Imagine, in just five years, the number of projects we have completed in our modernization program: 55. We have surpassed the 45 projects completed by the previous three administrations.

We all know that the price of your equipment is no joke; we also know that reaching the “modern age” where our Armed Forces has missiles isn’t easy. Funding for modernization is truly needed, and our budget can’t just be focused on the Armed Forces. If I may share: When I was starting as a Congressman, it was just after the so-called Asian Financial Crisis, and we brought all the needs of our district to various offices. The response we always received was: Sorry, we have no funds. If we didn’t have money for a barangay back then, how could we buy our helicopters, medium-lift transports, and lead-in fighters? That’s the difference with the Straight Path: Governance is correct, spending the people’s money is correct, so you can allocate resources to all the priorities of society.

In your case, our single C-130 has now become three, and we are in the process of adding two more units to further strengthen your heavy-lift capability. You have also received the first of three CASA C-295 medium lift transports and two new Bell 412s that we purchased. Two more units of the C-295, two C-212 light lift transports, and six more Bell 412 combat utility helicopters are also scheduled to arrive. In addition to this, we also have six close-air support aircraft in the pipeline, which will further enhance your ability to carry out various operations in our country.

In December, you will receive the first two units of our FA-50 lead-in fighter trainer jets that we purchased from South Korea. Just this June, the maiden flight of the first FA-50 unit we bought was successfully carried out. These fighter jets are about to take off from our own airbase, which is expected to further develop our ability to protect our territory.

We are not only focusing on buying and upgrading equipment for your missions; your government is also addressing the other needs of our uniformed personnel. Under Phase 1 and Phase 2 of our housing program for our uniformed personnel, we have built 52,641 housing units. Six thousand, four hundred units of this are allocated for our Air Force. There is also the increased Subsistence Allowance for the AFP, and even the purchase of appropriate uniforms for members of the Air Force.

We also have the PAF Flight Plan 2028 underway, to make the transformation of your organization more extensive. I just want to emphasize: This initiative covers three administrations, and there are only 364 days left in our term. This means that if we want to see this plan succeed, we need to continuously support the Straight Path; if we deviate from it, the full realization of the positive changes we have started will surely be hindered.

It is clear: The State cares for you, and you reciprocate it. This investment of society in you is truly yielding dividends. For example: After Yolanda hit the Visayas, the Department of Science and Technology issued an advisory that the prevailing winds were still strong; our Air Force responded: “We are already at the marginal; we can handle those winds.” You know the needs of our fellow citizens affected by the super typhoon—from hunger, sickness, homelessness, and death; and you really pushed yourselves to fulfill your mission. On Day 1, after the typhoon, 24 hours after it struck, you were already flying helicopters and working with the Army to clear the runway in Leyte, which was key to continuously delivering people, equipment, and aid with our C-130s and other aircraft. At no time, in all the trials—from the unrest in Zamboanga, the earthquakes in Cebu and Bohol, and even other calamities, did we hear “Sir, we are tired; we don’t want to anymore; we have done our part.” Instead, you always strived and found ways to contribute and add more help.

I would also like to mention: The planes, helicopters, and other equipment are truly well-cared for by you, because you are very familiar with every asset we have. After each flight, it is immediately subjected to tune-up and preventive maintenance to prepare it for the next mission tomorrow. That is why, even with a high operational tempo, the equipment is maximized and the lifespan of our assets is extended.

This enthusiasm is not only seen in the Air Force; it is demonstrated by the entire Armed Forces and even our police, who are ready to help during disasters and in the face of danger. I remember when a storm hit Isabela, even as it was still raging, our police were already clearing the roads. And in this situation, how can the people not admire and love our soldiers, especially since they see that instead of taking advantage, you show empathy?

Truly: The courage and dedication of our Air Force and our entire uniformed personnel are beyond question. No matter how complex the situation, no matter how daunting the challenges before you, you are ready to uphold the interests and safety of the people. Even if your important operations are not reported every day, even if your heroism is not always proclaimed, you continue to excel for the sake of your fellow citizens and our flag. On behalf of the people, thank you very much for your service.

We would not have achieved the meaningful transformation that the AFP is now enjoying without a partner in reform like Secretary Voltaire Gazmin, who steers our military. Also present are the AFP Chiefs of Staff who support Sec. Volts in ensuring that we achieve a higher level of service to the people: We thank Generals Ricardo David, Eduardo Oban Jr., Jessie Dellosa, Emmanuel Bautista, and your current Chief of Staff, General Gregorio Pio Catapang. Of course, here in the Air Force, change has been possible because you have had leaders like Generals Oscar Rabena and Larry dela Cruz, and Commanding General Jeff Delgado who are truly focused on your welfare.

Clearly: There is now a government that truly cares for our soldiers who protect our citizens. In the remaining time we have in office, you can count on our unwavering support for your ranks. We will double our efforts to accelerate the development of an Air Force that our Bosses today, and future generations, can truly be proud of. My order and challenge to you: At every crossroads, continue to walk the path of what is right and just, and continue to be the defenders of our society.

Along with my greetings on your 68th anniversary, I would like to convey to you: It is a true honor for me to serve as President and Commander-in-Chief of an Armed Forces that serves as an example of excellence, discipline, and professionalism.

Thank you very much. Happy anniversary to all of you. And good afternoon.
