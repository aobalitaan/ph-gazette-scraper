Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, at the oath-taking of the new National Executive Board of the Union of Local Authorities of the Philippines

[Delivered at the Malacañan Palace, on October 2, 2014]

Thank you very much. Please, everyone, take your seats.

Secretary Mar Roxas; Governor Alfonso Umali Jr.; Governor Arthur Defensor Sr.; National Executive Board Officers of the Union of Local Authorities of the Philippines Inc.; fellow workers in government; honored guests; my dear countrymen:

I just want to clarify, I was the best man at Boy Umali’s renewal of vows, you see. [Laughter] Not when he first got married. [Laughter] Someone might doubt my age.

When was that, Boy, about two years ago, I think?

You know, during our trip, many, including the media who were with us, got sick because the schedule was reportedly quite heavy. And I, by God's grace, wasn't affected by anything. Now, I'm worried, I feel uneasy, maybe tomorrow I'll get the flu. [Laughter]

Many of you here today have been in politics for a long time. Perhaps many of you were in politics before me. Most likely, you are also familiar with the ins and outs of the political world, and the secret ingredients to win. It’s automatic for us to be very enthusiastic to showcase our abilities and our good qualities. It’s like we’ve already found the right formula for a marketing campaign to stay in power.

Let me ask you: Is there anyone here who said when they first ran that they would maintain the system they were used to? Did anyone promise you that if we were voted for, nothing would change? I don't think any of us said that when we ran. Instead, you laid out how to improve the situation of our citizens.

I know it's not easy what you went through before you reached where you are now. This wasn't just easily achieved, it was worked hard for. We have to endure insults, criticisms, and mudslinging every campaign season, sometimes even beyond the campaign, and sometimes even before the campaign. There are many sacrifices, fatigue, and self-restraint that need to be built up. Back when I was campaigning for congressman of Tarlac, I went through every road, climbed the dikes, did house-to-house visits in the morning and afternoon, and had meetings left and right in the evening. I tirelessly went around despite the intense heat to get closer to our countrymen.

There's a joke, perhaps you've already heard it: The formula for a campaign, they say, is what we can call KBL—and it's not the New Society Movement: but Weddings, Baptisms, and Funerals. It's like from birth to death, everyone runs to you for help. Honestly, it's really hard to refuse these requests, especially since we also want to help. It might even cause resentment, or they might think we're unapproachable.

Even when you're already in office, these kinds of requests for favors continue. I remember when I was newly elected Congressman in Tarlac. A councilor approached me and asked for help renting an orchestra for our town fiesta. I asked how much it would cost, and he replied that each orchestra would cost 40,000 pesos, and that was already the cheapest. Because he wanted us to get two orchestras so there would be relief for the musicians and they wouldn't get tired. Therefore, 80,000 pesos would be needed. My salary then, if I'm not mistaken, was 35,000 pesos; that didn't even include tax deductions. So I told him, even if we doubled my salary, it still wouldn't be enough for what he planned to spend on the rental. And now I ask you: How will this be funded? Isn't it true that when we fund these requests from our constituents, we are tempted to do all sorts of tricks?

During my term as Representative of Tarlac, I made it a point to approach and check on our constituents whenever I visited the different barangays. At first, when a project was proposed to me, it often came in no less than six folders per barangay, or resolutions from each barangay, and we have 159 barangays under our jurisdiction, the contents were almost all the same. Most of them just wanted to beautify their barangay hall. The question I would ask them in return: When we beautify your barangay hall, how many people will be fed there? How many will be helped in the community? How many lives will improve when we build monuments like your barangay hall? My suggestion was: Perhaps it would be better, I pleaded, if we used the funds we have for irrigation, road construction, or building schools. In my three terms as Congressman, I can proudly say that with the good cooperation of our constituents, the barangay captains and the barangay councils were there, at least three meaningful projects were implemented in each barangay. I'm not saying that all the problems in my district were solved after my term. But anyone with an open mind can see that we really made a difference during our term.

It's also probably unavoidable that even when you're in office, there are those who just oppose everything you lay out in terms of reforms. They are the ones who only offer criticism without offering any solutions. If someone asks you for ten things, and you manage to fulfill, let's say, 9.9 of them, the opposition will stubbornly dig up the 0.1 and proclaim that you didn't do it.

Now that we are President, we will not waste time on them. We are more focused on continuing to sow reforms and reap their fruits. There are over four million households who are beneficiaries of the Pantawid Pamilya Program, the 20 positive credit ratings actions we have achieved during our term, 2.5 million Filipinos who have risen above the poverty line, and 1.65 million added to the number of Filipinos employed from April 2013 to April 2014.

This is just some proof that the situation of the Philippines now is truly very different from what we found. Even other countries have very high confidence in us. When we visited Europe recently, a high-ranking official of a prominent bank around the world said: They said we should perhaps globalize the peso. This means the peso would become a world currency traded in other countries. Honestly, we were surprised by this suggestion; who among us thought someone would suggest this? When we heard this, I even asked Secretary Greg Domingo of the Department of Trade and Industry, who was with us at the meeting, if what I heard was correct. A more in-depth study is needed for such large-scale steps, but the message of the one who suggested it was clear: He is confident in the stability of our economy; he believes it is worth investing in the Philippines.

All of us here have been given a mandate by the people to serve them. We, as leaders, have promised to bring about change. But if your only goal is to remain in power, get stuck in the cycle of KBL (Kapit, Balik, Laban - Hold on, Return, Fight), and maintain the situation you found, isn't that just like exchanging seats in musical chairs?

How can we change if the extent of public service is limited to providing for weddings, baptisms, and funerals? My belief here is: We need to address not only the present, but more importantly, long-term problems. As the elders say, let us teach our countrymen how to fish, rather than let them keep coming back to ask for fish. Complete and comprehensive solutions are the key to the change we aspire to.

Let us take as an example what our late colleague, Secretary Jesse Robredo, did in Naga. The congestion of urban poor settlers in Naga was a long-standing problem, and Jesse inherited it when he became mayor. The situation was complex: It is not easy to simply relocate informal settlers if they have no source of livelihood in their new location. If there is no livelihood elsewhere, you can only expect people to cluster in the center of commerce. That is why, in addition to decent and permanent housing, the local government of Naga laid out livelihood programs and proper facilities in the new community. Thus: the former poor, once considered obstacles to development, became partners in promoting progress in Naga. The success of this program led by Jesse is what we are using as a basis to implement the relocation of informal settlers in Metro Manila.

If Jesse was able to think and lay out a long-term solution to the problem he faced, we are all called, as leaders, to respond in the same way. I know: If we can provide it, and if it is in accordance with the law, we will not withhold it from those under our jurisdiction, right? But as we have proven in Tarlac, I know we can get closer to the hearts of our countrymen if we give them not only for today, but especially for tomorrow, through genuine service, and by focusing on their welfare. In this way, they themselves will uplift their own lives.

The ultimate measure of excellence in public service is not found in dancing skills, karaoke, or making people laugh. It is measured by the improvement in the living conditions of our countrymen; and by giving them the strength to hold their own destiny. All the hardships in service will only be meaningful if they bring about significant change in our countrymen, especially those most in need. If a farmer who earns just enough for himself remains hand-to-mouth during your term, can you say that you have fulfilled your oath to bring about change? What future awaits them if they have a family, if they have no hope of reform from your leadership? The challenge to us public servants: make the lives of our countrymen brighter, so that their children no longer have to experience the hardships we have gone through.

Through the cooperation of ULAP, the local and national government, and the Filipino people, we will continue to focus our time on projects that truly deliver benefits to Filipinos.

To my fellow leaders, let us always ask ourselves: How far have we come since we started in our positions? Have we moved forward or backward? Did we remain true or deviate from the mandate given by the people? Just imagine the time when we have already retired, and look back at our jurisdiction. Wouldn't it be a wonderful feeling to say, “This town has changed so much, and I was part of those who contributed and participated to make this happen.”

As a father of the nation, my path is clear: to do what is right, and to always side with what is good for my fellow citizens. And as more of us work in the same direction, the faster we can accelerate the change we are enjoying now. When the time comes, when the Lord says “finished or not finished, pass your papers,” I truly hope that we will bequeath a more developed and brighter Philippines to the next generation of Filipinos.

Good day to all. Thank you very much to all of you.
