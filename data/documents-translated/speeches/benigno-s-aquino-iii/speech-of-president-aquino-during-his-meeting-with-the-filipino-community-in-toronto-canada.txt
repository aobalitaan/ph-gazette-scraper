Speech of Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, at his meeting with Filipinos in Toronto, Canada.

Good evening to all of you.

Allow me to begin by expressing our deep appreciation for the efforts of the Right Honorable Stephen Harper, who is with us today. Mr. Prime Minister, I thank you for being such an accommodating host, and for being a true friend of the Philippines. My mother said that the friendship between our two nations is one that is old and tested. I believe that under your watch, that partnership has truly deepened. Now it serves as a stronger cornerstone for the future for Filipinos and Canadians alike. Whether in helping ensure the welfare of Filipinos here in Canada, or in standing in solidarity with us in times of disaster, and in contributing to nurturing a more peaceful and more progressive Philippines, as demonstrated by Canada’s firm commitment to our peace process and the significant economic and developmental support from your nation. You and your government have indeed changed the lives of so many people. In our language, “Salamat” means thank you; and today, I say to you: Maraming salamat for all that you have done for our people.

This event, as in all other endeavors done with Canada’s help, only strengthens our meaningful partnership. Mr. Prime Minister, with your indulgence, as this is a rare occasion, and a rare reunion with my countrymen, I hope you won’t mind if I address them in Filipino.

I know that many of our countrymen leave the Philippines because it seems like there is no more light of hope in our country. As they say: People were voting with their feet. And we cannot blame them: For almost a decade, it seemed like darkness enveloped the government. Some politicians used their positions for their own interests. That’s why most promises were really broken. The people’s money, instead of going to services, was pocketed by so many people. Thus, it was easy to be convinced that “Yes, nothing will change” in our country.

You know, I remember a story during the height of Martial Law, and people were waking up a senator, arriving at a plaza. Upon arriving at the plaza, he found three people. He said to the emcee, “Should I still speak here, I’ll only be talking to three people?” [Laughter] The emcee said, “There are only three of them, but they don’t seem convinced yet. You need to convince them.” [Laughter and applause]

This politician—who seems eager to speak in front of the public and believes in his voice—gave a speech. Halfway through his speech, one of the three left, leaving only two. [Laughter] The emcee said, “You’re great, you convinced him. The one before you couldn’t convince him. Continue, there are two more.” [Laughter] So, he continued, and the speech was about to end. Another one of the two remaining stood up and left again. He looked at the emcee again. “I am really impressed with you; all those before him couldn’t convince them. You’re the only one needed. We can convince everyone. Please continue.” He finished the speech and went down the stage. He approached the person. “Friend, are you convinced now?”

The person replied to him, “You know, even before you arrived, I was already convinced. And in fact, the sound system you are using is mine. [Laughter and applause] I was waiting for you to finish so I could take it home.” [Laughter]

So, let’s be careful with politicians who are too fond of their voices, unlike our politicians here today.

But you know, in 2010, the voices of Filipinos united, and said that we should cast aside corruption and poverty; let us begin to tread the Straight Path towards broad development. With your help, and that of the entire Filipino people, we were given the opportunity to change the story of our Motherland.

As I told our Bosses: We may not be able to completely eliminate the country’s problems; within six years, we will begin to solve them. Before I step down from office, we will leave the Philippines greatly different from what we found. [Applause] And no one can deny that, even now, we are really seeing the change.

How did we say this? Let me give you some examples. When the Department of Public Works and Highways or DPWH is mentioned, doesn’t everyone immediately think of: kickbacks, slow pace, and inconvenience? For a long time, this agency has really been a nest of corruption. Now, because of good governance—under the management of Secretary Singson—when DPWH is mentioned, this is what you will answer: honest, fast, and true service. [Applause] The key to this change can be summarized in what we call the 5Rs: right projects at the right cost, at the right quality, and right on time, implemented by the right people. The result: As of March 2015, the DPWH’s savings reached P39 billion. [Applause]

This additional fund, or additional allocation of funds that were set aside or saved, we are using for additional projects, such as new roads, bridges, and disaster-related infrastructure.

Let’s look at an example: We put up an underpass at the Araneta Avenue and Quezon Avenue intersection; we finished it in 2012. Our predecessor said that the budget needed for this, the contract they allocated: P694 million. But because of proper management and honest spending, we lowered the total cost to P430 million [applause] without sacrificing the quality of the said underpass. What’s good about this is that Secretary Singson wasn’t content, the price went down, and the use of this road or underpass was opened 100 days ahead of schedule. Its use was expedited. [Applause]

Recently, I visited the province of Isabela; we opened a bridge whose name, and this is true, is Lullutan Bridge. [Laughter] When I asked, “Where are we opening?” they said, “We will visit the Lullutan Bridge.” [Laughter] I said now, maybe its full name is “Lulutang-Lulubog.” [Laughter]

We discovered that: It turns out, there used to be a spillway bridge, and that connected two barangays in the city of Ilagan in Isabela. When the water level is high, of course it would overflow on that spillway bridge and the bridge would be submerged; when the water level goes down, it would float again. [Laughter] But that wasn't the reason why it was called Lullutan Bridge.

The problem was, when a typhoon hit in 1995, the water rushed along with cut logs, and the spillway bridge was changed—that was in 1995. Years passed and it was almost forgotten that the bridge was damaged. And because the bridge was destroyed, the residents were forced to use barges to transport their products to the market. Let me repeat: it was damaged in 1995. If the government had studied the problem sooner, wouldn't a bridge have been built right away? We were the ones who responded to the problem, and we built the Lullutan Bridge. But it doesn't float and submerge anymore. This bridge is tall. This January, its construction was completed. The trip that used to take one and a half to two hours now only takes five minutes. What a relief. [Applause]

In responding to the problems that come our way, opportunities for development are opening up for our countrymen. Another example is the project that emerged in Davao Oriental, after they were hit by Typhoon Pablo in 2012. There are coconut farmers, or coconut farmers. The main livelihood there is indeed coconut farming. And of course, when the typhoon arrived, their coconuts were toppled. And because of the typhoon, they lost their livelihood. If we wait for the trees to grow back and mature, it will take five to seven years before they can benefit from them. What will they do for a living for five to seven years if there are no coconut trees?

Suddenly, someone responded with a good and promising solution, the project dubbed “Hot Pablo.” Honestly, I was confused at first. What kind of program is this, named “Hot Pablo”? People call someone a “hot mama.” [Laughter] It turns out, the product being made is chili powder, so it's “Hot,” and it was linked to “Pablo” because that's why this project was thought of, because of Typhoon Pablo. According to our Department of Trade and Industry, the capital of P90,000 per hectare per year for this product can earn up to P200,000. Where have you seen an investment where the profit is more than 100 percent? That's over P100,000 per hectare, net. That's why this February, domestic sales of Hot Pablo were recorded at P7.62 million. [Applause] Domestic, that is. Of course, exports are more expensive. It's clear: with the help of the government and the private sector, our farmer countrymen are not only recovering from the typhoon. Now, they are really proud of their work. We continue to look for ways to maximize this industry.

The good news is: The Supreme Court has finally decided to allow the use of funds of almost P73 billion from the Coco Levy Fund to develop the coconut industry. [Applause] This will be used for projects that will benefit our coconut farmers. And when we say coconut, that includes the coco water industry. If before, coconut water was just thrown away, now our farmers are benefiting from it. In 2010, we exported 1.8 million liters of coco water, worth $1.8 million. In 2014, we exported 21.7 million liters of coco water. And the equivalent of that: $32.9 million. [Applause]

Let me just remind you, the coconut farmer earns only P20,000 per hectare per year. Now, all these projects that I am mentioning before you today are really far away. Just with Hot Pablo, the net profit from planting is more than P100,000, when it used to be only P20,000 per hectare. [Applause]

Our scientists also discovered that the husk—or former husk—has a new benefit. The fibers are extracted, we make geotextile material. The inventor calls it coco coir. Coco coir is used as protection for slopes. When there is a road, there is a slope on its side. When the rain comes, the soil will collapse, covering the road, making it impassable. If there is coco coir, grass grows, it holds the soil, and the roads are not closed.

Now, this coco coir product is being bought wholesale by exporters. And it's painful, we produce it, but we often lose. So the solution is, we plant more coconuts.

Of course, we continue to enrich the coconut industry, and we are determined to maximize the opportunities for this sector.

Another agency that has really changed now is the DOST, or Department of Science and Technology, and especially its agency, PAGASA. I remember: Some members of the agency we met were releasing weather balloons or flying weather balloons, to find out the direction of the typhoons that would hit us. It's a pity, it was being scrimped on in the wrong places. And it seemed like they just got a map of the Philippines, and threw a dartboard, and said, “It will pass here.” [Laughter] The information was like hocus pocus, and there weren't many personnel. Their chief at that time, instead of releasing a based prediction. For example: When I just took office, a typhoon arrived. And they told us, this typhoon will hit Aurora; It turned out, the truth is, it will hit Southern Tagalog, and hit the National Capital Region, Manila. I lived in our family's house in Quezon City. I wondered, “How big is this typhoon. It's hitting Metro Manila, it should be in Aurora province.” [Laughter] The truth is, it was in Manila and not in Aurora province, because in Aurora province, it was sunny the next day. [Laughter]

Now, when there is a typhoon like Dodong, let's carve it in stone: PAGASA can tell us where it will pass, how strong the wind is, how much rain it will pour, and how long it will rain. Now, PAGASA is more capable and its members are more enthusiastic because they have sufficient and modern equipment such as Doppler radars, automated rain gauges, and water level monitoring sensors.

Along with our early warning system, we also have multi-hazard maps, to identify areas that are dangerous during disasters. We also have a Nationwide Operational Assessment of Hazards, or the so-called Project NOAH, which can determine where it may rain or how strong the wind is. Now, when there is a typhoon, the LGU immediately evacuates the people because they are told about storm surges and flash floods, and the information they are given is really accurate. It's true: When PAGASA speaks now, you can really trust that what they say will happen. [Applause] The result: More of our countrymen are saved and kept away from danger, especially if they cooperate.

Let's think about it, even our Armed Forces, we are really strengthening it. The one C130 we inherited, now we have three; and we have nine-negotiate two more. You might laugh at that, $400 million each, and it's secondhand. [Laughter]

The soldiers, lacking equipment and guns, and the Navy, really at a disadvantage in guarding our coastlines. It was clear what our mission was: AFP Modernization. For example: There are about 70,000 of our soldiers' new assault rifles. There are two Hamilton-class cutters of our Navy. As I mentioned, the C130, which is now three, hopefully will be six before I step down. There are also three new medium lift aircraft, I will not mention the helicopters and other equipment. Before the end of the year, we expect to receive the first two units of the 12 FA-50 lead-in fighter trainer jets that we bought. [Applause] A new graduate said, who just finished PMA: Thank you, and our Air Force, they really have a “force.” [Laughter] Before it was just “air.” [Laughter]

Let's try to go back: Five years ago, did it even cross our minds that we would achieve the successes we are enjoying now? We are really proving that: What was once considered impossible is now possible.

And all of this is happening, not because of hocus pocus, not because of luck; the reason is: We have returned the government’s focus to the people. Because of this, we have also realized: Give the Filipino sufficient opportunity, and they will truly excel. Just look at our economy: The former “Sick Man of Asia,” is now called the “Darling of Asia,” and has investment grade status. [Applause] Likewise, we have achieved an all-time high for foreign direct investments, reaching $6.2 billion in 2014. This is a result of the sound and honest management of our economy.

It’s no joke what we have achieved: From 2010 to 2014, we recorded an average GDP growth of 6.3 percent. [Applause] This is the fastest growth of our economy in the last 40 years. Our target: 7 to 8 percent GDP growth this 2015. If we reach this, the resulting six-year average will be the fastest growth of our economy since 1955, or a year when my parents got married. [Applause]

The question is: What do these successes mean for the ordinary Filipino? The simplest answer, or one of the simplest answers: Jobs. As the economy grows, new positions are also opened in offices, factories, restaurants, and various other businesses. Look: The unemployment rate in October 2014 was recorded at 6 percent; this is now the lowest unemployment rate in the country in a decade. [Applause] In one year, we created a net of 1.04 million jobs. Let me clarify: This number is true; the numbers are not being manipulated. It’s not the type where if you work as a street sweeper for a week, you are considered employed. [Laughter] What we are talking about here: real, permanent, and long-term jobs, and not just to make the data look good and to appear impressive to the public. [Applause]

Now, the situation that happened, when I was young, I always saw a sign, “No vacancy.” Now, more and more often, “For immediate hiring.” [Applause]

You know, when I went to Bicol State University, I mentioned to them that we have a shortage of accountants. When we were young, weren’t there many Bachelor of Science or Bachelor of Commerce, Major in Accounting? Now, the school that produces accountants cannot fill its Accounting Department. Because they say that even third year students are already being recruited by accounting and auditing firms. There are even fourth year students, who are about to take the board exam.

Now, what are we doing to fill these jobs? Of course, we shouldn’t rely on “hoping”; like, “Hopefully, more companies will open more jobs; hopefully, applicants will be trained; hopefully, they will find jobs.” If we do a “hoping” system, wouldn’t we really achieve nothing? That’s why our Department of Labor and Employment – which is under the leadership of Honorable Secretary Linda Baldoz – and our Technical Education and Skills Development Authority have been in contact with the private sector for a long time. They identify the needs of companies, and at the same time, develop the skills of job seekers. We also have nationwide job fairs. There’s also the enhanced Philjobnet which lists job vacancies on their website. It’s appropriate that we are here, because we have the Jobstart Philippines program, in partnership with and with the help of Canada, which aims to speed up the job search of our applicants through career guidance services, life skills and technical training, and an internship program. Because of these initiatives, we are filling the vacant jobs in the market.

With these successes, I can say: Every hardship, sleepless night, and even the criticisms thrown at me from breakfast, lunch, dinner, snacks, to midnight snack are worth it. [Applause]

You know, once, there was even a religious person, who, because they couldn’t find anything to criticize us for, targeted my hair. They said I should fix my hair. I said, maybe instead of my hair, I’ll just thicken my patience.

It cannot be denied: The Philippines is different now. We are ending the practice of “just-tiis,” and we are implementing true “justice.” [Applause] From the small seeds of reform, we are now reaping its positive fruits. All we need to do: Together, we must watch over and nurture this change, to ensure that it is not wasted, but rather flourishes. I am confident that: Wherever you are in the world, your love for our Motherland will never fade. [Applause] You will still conduct the orchestra on the Straight Path; you will still ensure that the positive transformation we are achieving now continues; the Filipino people will still make the change, so that finally, we can reach the Philippines, that we have long dreamed of.

Now, wherever I go in the world, we don’t often travel, Canada is the exception, they really say that you have made a big positive contribution to their society. So, I thank you all [applause] for reminding us that if you give the Filipino a really good opportunity, they will truly excel. And secondly, when we achieve something, I always say, “It is an honor to lead a people like the Filipino race.”

Good evening. Thank you very much to all of you.
