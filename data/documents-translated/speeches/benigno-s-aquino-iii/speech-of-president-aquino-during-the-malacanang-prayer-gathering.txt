Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, at a Prayer Gathering in Malacañan

Good afternoon.

I will admit to you, I think I was influenced even before by watching a TV program back then. That’s why we both speak calmly. [Laughter]

Joel [Villanueva], your father hasn’t seen you for a month now. Noted, Bro. Eddie [Villanueva], we will remind him—there’s something in the Bible, “Honor thy Father.” [Laughter] But I know, even if he doesn’t show himself to you—you are truly a dutiful son. He doesn’t want to ruin your good name. Truly, as I said during the campaign, there was one day with 21 engagements within 24 hours, and we traversed about four provinces that day. So our ground campaign manager was the late Jesse Robredo. So when the day was over—actually it was finished the next day—I spoke, we were on a bus, and I said to him, “You know, Jesse, even a water buffalo knows how to cry.” He said, “Okay, tomorrow we won’t have 24 engagements, just 23.” [Laughter]

Good afternoon to all of you once again. [Applause]

Our faith teaches us, “We are our brother’s keeper.” And as we live by this lesson, we cannot avoid being tested by the challenges that come our way. In such instances, we recall the words of people like Martin Luther King, “Darkness cannot drive out darkness; only light can do that. Hate cannot drive out hate; only love can do that.” [Applause]

As President, our duty is not easy. And in the past few days, some have asked me, “We know your job is difficult, but how difficult is it really?”

Perhaps, just one example of the immense problem we faced is the series of trials the country experienced in 2013: It began with the earthquake in Bohol, followed by the turmoil in Zamboanga, and then the year ended with the devastation of Yolanda. It almost felt like a volcano would erupt. We couldn’t even mention it during those times, for fear that it might actually happen and a volcano would erupt—as if to add to everything else.

But you know, when Yolanda was approaching, PhilVolcs told us there was a danger of lahar flows. I said, “What is the connection between the storm and lahar flow?” They said that the accumulated material from the Mayon eruption, among others, had formed lahar, and when heavy rain comes, it can flow, and that’s it, a lahar flow, causing new damage.

In all of this, we chose to remain calm, not to give in to emotions, and to focus on the welfare of the majority; including our countrymen—or at the center of our work are our countrymen, whether inside or outside the country. But it is also inevitable that there are still some who choose to sow doubt and negativity. Let's just call them “KSP”: those who are “attention-seeking,” those who are “lacking in proper thinking,” who are just looking for flaws, but have no reasonable and alternative solutions. There are also those who are “lacking in compassion for others,” who just want to create chaos where they can benefit more. Of course, those who are “lacking in faith,” or those who do not acknowledge that the Lord is there, ready to help and guide us to overcome every problem.

We were a bit surprised that I was accused of bribing you to talk to me. We, and the public record is there, are ready to talk to everyone. We just ask that the conversation leads somewhere. If, when we talk to the media, we haven't even spoken to them yet, the story is already done—what's the point of talking? If someone has a very closed mind, and whatever we say, they just look for ways to twist our words—what's the point of talking? As long as you can talk to someone properly, our doors are open, especially if we feel that the purpose is truly for the good, if not of everyone, then of the majority. That's what we see in Bishop Eddie and his companions here at the Christian Coalition Movement, amongst others.

You know, there are those we call KSP, they are the ones who want to shake our hope. They are the ones who want to take advantage of the issues we face again to bring back the old ways where they had an advantage over others. They are also the ones who, whatever decision we make, every action of your government, have a ready criticism and negativity, and often, before we even make a decision, act, or take action, the criticism is already ready. They have many alternatives depending on what we do. They are just waiting for an opportunity to abuse again once they return to power.

However, on the other hand, we also have colleagues who are truly one with us in the agenda of good reform; partners in our desire to promote the welfare of our countrymen, especially those most in need.

For example, Bro. Eddie Villanueva. As you know, he was our opponent in the 2010 elections. But instead of harboring resentment, he chose to help us improve the situation of our countrymen. Instead of focusing solely on criticism [applause], he proved true to his word: to serve his fellowmen and country. When there are disagreements, he communicates with us honestly and sincerely to present the appropriate solution to the problem.

You know, we have a politician right now. When I first took office, he told me that it is the duty of every politician to help the administration established by the people, especially at its beginning. Our term is about to end in one year and three months, and I am waiting for his support to come. But because we have faith in God, we do not lose hope that perhaps on the fifth year and 364th day, his help might arrive. [Laughter]

We are also grateful to our colleagues here, the leaders of various Christian groups, for pouring your support into our agenda of good governance. This opportunity to unite and offer prayers for our country is truly encouraging.

In times like these, I am confident that even if our work is difficult, as long as you are united and give strength, we can overcome the forces that seek to restore the old system. [Applause] I know that our efforts will be meaningful in ensuring that our collective desire for greater progress and prosperity prevails.

When we say taking the straight path, this means deviating from the wrong path or the system of maintaining corrupt elements, which brings no benefit to many of our countrymen. Now that we are in power, we will not allow ourselves to remain in a cycle of crooked governance, as if we are just going around in circles like a merry-go-round or playing musical chairs. It is our duty—the duty of each of us—to correct the system in society. And we have labeled this as “taking the straight path.”

We are fully determined to continue our fight for the transformation of the country. I have strong faith in the Lord and in the ability of the Filipino people. I will continue to stand for what is right and just. There is a new challenge we are facing now regarding those who want to bring back violence to Mindanao. There may be times when it seems I am the only voice left, but we continue to fight for peace because I know this is the proper recognition of the sacrifice of many of our countrymen, especially those who died in Mamasapano. We believe: Peace is the key to progress and it will bring justice for every Filipino. [Applause]

My dream is simple: to deliver the country to a far better state than we found it in. When I am called by God, and He says, “Pass your papers, finished or not,” I can look back on what I have done and those we have helped, and I can say as stated in 2 Timothy chapter 4, verse 7, “I have fought the good fight, I have finished the race, I have kept the faith.” [Applause]

I firmly believe: with the guidance and love of the Lord, and with the unity of the people, we will succeed on the straight and narrow path. As long as we are on the side of truth, as long as we think of the well-being of our fellow citizens, as long as we prioritize the interests of the majority before ourselves, dreaming is not just a possibility, but a right that every Filipino can fulfill.

Good afternoon. Thank you all very much.
