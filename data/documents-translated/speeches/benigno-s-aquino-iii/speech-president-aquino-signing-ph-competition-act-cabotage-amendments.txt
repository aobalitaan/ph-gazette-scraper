Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, on the signing of the Philippine Competition Act and Liberalization of Philippine Cabotage.

Of course, I’ll start with my thanks. These laws have been pending for a long time. Uncle Henry—according to Speaker Sonny Belmonte’s report—was one of its biggest proponents, one of those who worked hardest on it. At the risk of having tomorrow’s headlines say “Family Inc.,” I’d really like to thank Uncle Henry, and of course Kuya Bob, who are proponents of this. Maybe tomorrow “Family Inc.” will be said again. [Laughter]

When we started, I extended an invitation to the businessmen we met in various countries. I told them, “The Philippines is open for business again.” Our commitment then: A level playing field, from eradicating the culture of bribery, kickbacks, and exploitation. We removed the corrupt, and businesses will be our partners in lifting up the poor.

The fruits of what we have sown are becoming clear. From 2010 to 2014, we recorded an average GDP growth of 6.2 percent—this is the fastest growth of our economy in five years in the past four decades. Last year, we achieved an all-time high of net foreign direct investments at $6.2 billion. In 2013, we were also awarded an investment grade by major credit ratings agencies around the world, which they raised several times in the following years. These are just some of the results of our prudent spending and honest leadership.

Today, we signed two bills that will ensure the continued growth of our economy. This proves that we will not simply be content with what we have already achieved, but rather, we will truly maximize the benefits for our Bosses. Through the two bills we approved today, we are removing the crooked practices caused by the lack of competition, which bring no benefit to our citizens.

Let’s start with the Philippine Competition Act. It has been pending in Congress for over two decades; during that time, ensuring fair market practices was a matter of chance.

The question is: Who benefits when there is no competition? The consumer who pays high prices for goods? The seller who has no incentive to improve his product to increase its value and quality? With the enactment of the Philippine Competition Act, we are promoting competition in the market. Now, finally, whether the business is small or large, the competition is in releasing quality products at the most reasonable price, instead of under the table or increasing connections. Here, we can achieve a point where the consumer gets a more true price for what they pay, and the seller is encouraged to improve their service. In the end, everyone benefits, everyone wins.

This is also the principle we uphold in signing the Liberalization of Philippine Cabotage. This Cabotage Law comes from the Tariff and Customs Code of 1978, and it is only now that it has been adapted to our needs. This law probably started to encourage the development of our shipping industry and to have the ability to compete. The problem is, very few ships have been added to ours; resulting in an absurd situation where only a few control the flow of the market. That’s why we’ve come to a time when we need to change the customary system.

With your amendments, foreign ships will be freely able to trade their imported and exported cargo to their chosen port of destination. Because of this, the export and import of products will be facilitated and become cheaper, which will lead to a more vibrant market. Everyone benefits, from consumers to business owners. For example: In the old practice, due to the lack of competition, if you send a cargo container from Cagayan de Oro to Hong Kong, it would cost you $1,264. Of that amount, $1,120 from Cagayan de Oro to Manila, and $144 from Manila to Hong Kong. With the amendment of the Cabotage Law, shippers can now go directly from Cagayan de Oro to Hong Kong. They will only pay: $500. They saved $764 per container. This is not an absurd situation, where the Philippines is estimated to be the third, fourth, or fifth largest shipbuilder, and where from ⅓ to ¼ of the world's seafarers come from, is also one of the highest shipping prices.

As you know, as an archipelago, our islands are scattered, and traveling with services and products is truly difficult. Even the government is affected by the old shipping system where the travel of products and services becomes expensive. For example, when Yolanda struck and we needed to send relief goods to various provinces, our budget for aid was reduced because it went to expensive shipping costs.

Let me emphasize: If we just let the usual system continue where there is no competition, we are also allowing our citizens to endure meager benefits; we are also letting them be content with a system where only a few benefit.

It is true: Unity brought us to the progress we enjoy today. So before I continue, let's also thank our good Congressmen and Senators who contributed to the advancement of these laws. In particular, let me first thank, excuse me, my brother Bam Aquino, and many other sponsors and authors of these two laws. [Applause] To Senator Frank Drilon and Speaker Sonny Belmonte, thank you very much for shepherding these laws. [Applause] I will also include Uncle Henry, and our colleagues in the NPC who really pushed for this law. [Applause] We also thank Executive Secretary Pacquito Ochoa, Senators Vicente Sotto III and Cynthia Villar, Representatives Dina Abad, Boy Gonzales, Mark Villar, Dax Cua for uniting in this initiative.

We believe that freedom is the pillar of a strong state; it fosters competition, which grows the economy and expands opportunities.

Let me clarify: These laws will promote the growth of businesses in the country, but that's not where our strategy ends. At the same time, we also have many policies and programs focused on empowering our citizens: From ensuring the quality of education for our youth, to protecting the health of everyone, and to many poverty alleviation programs like Pantawid Pamilya, we are focusing on the future. Meaning: We will not allow any Filipino to be left behind; we will not rely on the trickle-down effect for broad development; everyone, together, contributes to promoting the cycle of progress and raising the dignity of every Filipino.

We have already achieved so much in our five years in office. Filipinos once again believe that they can compete and measure up with other countries. We are already seeing the change: if before, we were afraid to take risks, now, we see it as an opportunity to progress; if before, we doubted our abilities, now we are ready to face any challenge.

Our Bosses can count on this: With the Philippine Competition Act and the Liberalization of Philippine Cabotage we have approved, the change we are experiencing now will continue for generations to come. Even if we are no longer in office, these laws will ensure that the foundations we have established for the transformation of our economy and society will not be easily shaken. I am confident: Now that we have regained national dignity, now that there is a government that truly serves the people, and now that Filipinos are confident to progress and fulfill their dreams, a future that is far more prosperous than what we inherited is truly within our reach.

And once again, I would like to thank our members of the Senate and Congress who, truly, on this day, without a doubt, are our strongest critics—go ahead, look for proof that our representatives in both houses are not serving.

Good day. Thank you very much to all of you.
