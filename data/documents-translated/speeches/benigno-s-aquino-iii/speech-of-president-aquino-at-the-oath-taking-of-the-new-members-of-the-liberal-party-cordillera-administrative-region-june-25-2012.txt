Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, During the Oath-Taking of New Members of the Liberal Party Cordillera Administrative Region

[ Delivered at Benguet State University, La Trinidad, Benguet, on June 25, 2012 ]

Good morning. Please, let us all be seated.

I was going to greet you in Ilokano, but our Ilokano in Tarlac is apparently different from your Ilokano here. In Tarlac, when we say “good morning,” we say “naimbag a bigat kadakayo amin apo.” [ Laughter and applause ] Ronnie and Teddy told me that… “agsapa,” I didn’t hear “agsapa” in Tarlac.

Anyway, earlier when we got off the helicopter, of course, Mar and I have many memories here in the Cordillera, especially in Baguio. So he said, “Buddy, can we spend a week here for a planning seminar?” I said, “Don’t tempt me, because I’m already very tempted.”

But truthfully, I think tomorrow, when we read the newspapers, it will say, “While our fishermen are having problems in Bajo de Masinloc, I am in Baguio having fun,” that’s what the critics will say. But you are already used to that, so that’s okay with me.

Secretary Mar Roxas; Secretary Jesse Robredo; Deputy Speaker Erin Tañada; Congressman Ronnie Cosalan; Congressman Teddy Baguilat; Congresswoman Joy Bernos; (I messed up the order) Governor Nestor Fongwan; he was ahead of me in Congress, Butch Bulot, now a governor; Governor Josel Baac, his grandson; Governor Eugene Balintang; Manong Kit is also here, we saw him earlier; board member Henry Bacunay; Honorable Yong Afable; John Hay Management Corporation; Dr. Ben Ladilad; mayors of the different municipalities of the Cordillera Administrative Region; members of the Liberal Party; fellow workers in government; honored guests; my beloved countrymen:

Please note, I removed the teleprompter because I want you to know that everything I say comes straight from the heart.

When we—the PMA March Week was approaching—we went up to Baguio. It took me four and a half hours from Tarlac to Baguio. I drive so slowly; it was a bit congested. Why was it congested? Because from Tarlac, Pangasinan, La Union, and of course Benguet, up to Baguio—there were many constructions happening simultaneously. So on one hand, I was telling myself, “This is terrible traffic,” because I was the one driving. On the other hand, I was saying, “Well, we can do this all at the same time.” This isn't just happening in Luzon; it's also happening in Mindanao—it's happening in different places. And here, especially in Baguio, this is where I first heard it—because I, when I started as a congressman in 1998, it was right after the Asian Financial Crisis of 1997. So when our district needed something, the government would always say, “There’s no money.”

Now, I'm not saying, “We are drowning in money, our country.” But we really have the ability to provide for the bosses all at once, without favoritism, and in a timely manner. In fact… In Baguio, they asked if it was possible to finish [first] the Panagbenga and the PMA homecoming, and the graduation before continuing their projects because there were so many projects, the traffic was terrible. I didn't think there would come a time when a community would refuse their projects instead of rushing to get them, right? “Give to us, give to us, give to us.” And this is also happening in other places. The SONA is coming up soon. This time, perhaps you’ll allow me, this will be my third SONA; it will be halfway through the SONAs I will deliver. In 2016, I will no longer deliver a SONA. So we will brag a little.

What do we mean when we say “take care of the boss?” We have countrymen, 20 percent of our population, who are called “living below the poverty line.” We helped them through the Conditional Cash Transfer program. What does that mean? We enrolled eight hundred thousand families. Now, this March, three million families are enrolled and we are giving them assistance to educate their children and get by in their daily lives. This is an investment; it’s not “charity.” We are preparing our future by improving our countrymen so they have more opportunities in their livelihood.

When we talk about health, when we say we are taking care of our boss, 82 percent of our population is now enrolled in Philhealth. Let me repeat that: 82 percent. You’ll ask when will we take care of the eighteen percent? Many of them are informal: informal employment, informal housing. We are having a bit of a problem reaching them because there is no fixed address for their work or their homes, but we are still looking for them because the target is to achieve 100 percent coverage of Philhealth.

Regarding agriculture, Procy Alcala promised us that next year we will be self-sufficient in rice. But even better news is that he changed his promise to us, if the weather cooperates—meaning no typhoons during harvest—we will likely export higher quality rice in 2013. We will not compete at the lower end; we will be in the higher value rice market. And one of the two things he mentioned, of course, were upland varieties. I think these are planted here, right? You will be exporters by next year. Maybe when you are exporters, you won’t even know us anymore.

Let’s add to that, with the leadership of our congressmen, when we watch TV, when we watch movies, our Judicial System is said to have justice for the powerful and the rich; there is a different justice for those without power, or without influence, and without means. With the leadership of our congressmen, we sent a message to our Judicial System that you are part of a democratic system, that your power comes from the people and your only boss is the people. So there was one who didn’t believe that, so he is now just a mister; he no longer has a title.

Why did I mention all of this? I won’t raise it a little bit more… but why did I mention this? We’ve attended so many gatherings, and in each one, the question is repeatedly asked to me in different versions. Someone from a group once asked me, “How can you ensure that the changes happening now will continue after you leave office?” Sometimes I answer, “Well, hold on a moment, I’m the one leading this; when the six years you gave me are up, it’s probably not right to ask how I can ensure it. The question should be, how can we ensure that the changes happening in our society will continue? How can we make sure we don’t advance one step only to take two steps back? Otherwise, we’ll go back to where we started. Of course, by 2016, we’ll be “mister” already, that’s why I’m here now.

Our party has so many people we can support and champion who have been true to their promises. I won’t mention my father, lest you say I’m exaggerating. There are people like Emilio Javier. Emilio Javier campaigned for my mother, stood firm with them in Antique, used and risked his life, fought in the democratic system. He had a warlord as an opponent. We all know what happened to Emilio Javier, he was shot. After that, they pursued him without succeeding, chased him until they were sure they would kill him. He didn’t fall short with us. He didn’t fall short with us when he told us all that he would lead us to correct the wrongs, to right the wrongs, and there are really many more examples—we might not finish listing them.

We invited you to join the party and thank you for joining us, and I’ll just repeat what Mar said earlier: We’re not here to show off. We’re all here to ensure that our government platform is truly strong, so that even if I, Mar, Erin, or any of us on stage are gone, there will still be someone truly serving our bosses, not just in words. Someone who will truly stand firm because it is right. Someone who truly believes that we can change what we’ve become accustomed to. All we need is someone to stand up for all of us and lead. That’s what we expect from all of you.

I, whatever happens, am just a person. If I’m doing good things, it would be a shame if they weren’t continued. But think about it, when we went through 2010 here in the Cordillera, where we won, I’ll repeat: thank you very much. In the places where we didn’t win, there will still be opportunities in future elections. But the important thing—I think within the past two years, we’ve proven what it means when we focus on our bosses. We’ve shown that even though the fight was very difficult, and so many threats arose, and there were so many—more than I can count with my hands—critics, right? “I can’t do anything right.” That’s okay. They have the right to speak, as long as they don’t violate our laws.

But the bottom line is, they forget that power comes from the people; direction should come from the people; solutions, the people will also provide for all of us.

We are changing our society. Let’s pray that by 2016, we will have achieved a great deal of change. I’ll repeat, we need you here. We need you to ensure that this isn’t just temporary; it’s not temporary. We will make the upliftment of the Filipino permanent, the direction of true change permanent.

Before I finish, you know, let me attend to our fishermen. We are heading to the hospital where those we rescued were taken. I just noticed earlier during the oath-taking: they stood up in front. Some stood up on the left side of our stage. On the right side, to avoid anyone standing, they took the oath. And my sector is still here: the youth. So you are also invited, and please don’t forget what we’re doing here—I guess my life expectancy, I’ve already consumed half of it. You probably have one-fourth. If we do things right collectively, you have three-fourths to enjoy. I have one-half left. If we make a mistake, one-half of the suffering will be mine, and three-fourths will be yours. So if there’s anyone who should be involved in the future that’s being made in the present, I think it’s you more than us. But as my mother told me before, when her generation was leading us: the world’s problems weren’t in our generation. The problems were in their generation, and they should be the solution because they created those problems. For the problems that we either created or inherited, we are obligated to correct them. But once they are corrected, you must be ready so that when you inherit power and opportunities, all the struggles won’t be in vain.

Good day to you all. Thank you very much.
