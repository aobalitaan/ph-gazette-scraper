Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

at the inauguration of the Freedom Memorial Museum

[Declared at the Ninoy Aquino Parks and Wildlife Center, Quezon City, on April 28, 2016]

I believe in what George Santayana said: “Those who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it.” Perhaps it would be good to say it also in Tagalog: “Those who forget the mistakes of the past are sure to repeat these mistakes.”

This is the spirit that underpins our gathering today—the inauguration of the Freedom Memorial Museum, which depicts the dark chapter that the country went through under Martial Law, and the heroism of those who fought for our freedom. In this way, the lessons of the past dictatorship are refreshed and taught to the youth so that it will not happen again.

Some say: A dictator with an iron fist would be good for the Philippines. And even now, surveys show that there are still those who desire and are swayed by this kind of leadership. If we look back at history, haven't we already tried this? Where did the dictatorship lead us?

During the time of dictator Mr. Marcos, our economy collapsed and the country's debt increased. Think about this: When Mr. Marcos took office in 1965, the national government's debt was P2.4 billion. By the end of 1985, two months before he was ousted, our debt had reached P192.2 billion. Where did the money go? Even now, the people are still burdened with paying off that debt.

The number of rebels in the country also swelled. From 60 people comprising the New People’s Army at the time, its membership grew to 25,000 armed members of the NPA after Martial Law. Even now, this group continues to disrupt remote communities.

Let us also remember: The unrest in Mindanao took root and expanded during the dictatorship due to land grabbing. Instead of siding with the exploited, the Marcos regime seemed to tolerate those oppressing our countrymen. Here, the law and the uniformed ranks were used to exploit those who were uneducated and deny them their land. Even now, we are still experiencing the effects of this neglect in Mindanao.

As I said, although our family was also a victim of Martial Law, I can still say we were lucky because, in a way, I was able to visit my father while he was imprisoned, and when he was murdered, we were able to bury him and we have a grave we can visit. Many of our countrymen went through far greater suffering. In almost every corner of the Philippines at that time, there were stories of people being suddenly arrested, tortured, killed, or simply disappearing without a trace, and their bodies have never been found.

This is just a glimpse of what people went through and the results of a leader who believed that only he was right and should be followed. Thirty years have passed since Martial Law ended, but the wounds it caused still haven't healed.

Have we even heard an apology from the relatives of the dictator? What's even more painful is this: instead of apologizing for the brutality of Martial Law, the son of Mr. Marcos said: “I am ready to say sorry if I knew what I have to be sorry for.” If this is the perspective of someone aspiring to lead the country, how can we expect him not to repeat it if he doesn't even see the wrong?

We continue to address the problems left behind by Martial Law. This April, due to the very high number of applications for the Human Rights Victims’ Claims, we approved RA 10766, which extended the mandate of the Human Rights Victims’ Claims Board to complete the payment of damages to the victims of Martial Law.

The situation we are in now is very far from what it was during Martial Law. Now, for the youth, or what are called millennials who did not experience it, what does Martial Law mean? These freedoms that you barely notice today—the freedom to post opinions and search for information on the internet, the freedom to assemble and go on vacation, even the freedom to watch television programs, none of that existed under the dictatorship.

What existed was: the freedom to praise the government, the freedom to use power to abuse the people, the freedom of Marcos’ cronies to get even richer. The rights that everyone should enjoy became a privilege only of a few powerful people, and families closely connected to the Marcoses.

During Martial Law, when a magazine arrived, and there was an article even slightly critical of the government, it would be removed before distribution. If it contained a lot of criticism, it would not be circulated at all. If you were a journalist or a critic of Mr. Marcos, you could be labeled subversive, arrested, and imprisoned without a warrant. It was also difficult to obtain an exit permit if you wanted to leave the country.

During the time of the dictator, there were also those who said that if you were a businessman, you didn't want to grow your business. They said it might attract attention and be seized by those in power. For example, with sugar: As soon as sugarcane became sugar, it belonged to them. They would pay you when they wanted to, at the price they said. They even experimented with having a sugar cartel where they would limit the supply to raise the price. Result: The price and the sugar industry, which used to account for 25 percent of our exports, deteriorated even further.

Truly: These freedoms we enjoy today, all of them, were fought for by millions of Filipinos who gathered at EDSA, to end the dictatorship without resorting to a bloody revolution. Can we accept returning to a time when our rights and the rights of our families could simply be trampled upon? This is the tragedy looming over our country: To be oppressed and exploited once again, and to allow the problems brought about by the dictatorship to repeat themselves.

Now, we are establishing this museum not only to revisit and relive the brutality of the dictatorship, but also so that our youth can see how important the freedom and democracy we fought for and now enjoy are. In my generation, we didn't need a museum to remember the dark situation of the country under the dictatorship. For the sake of the youth, and with the aim of them understanding what we went through during Martial Law, we see the value of this project. As the saying goes: He who does not look back at his origins, will not reach his destination.

We can no longer bring back the lost decades. What we can do: Address the problems now, and prepare for the future. That is what we have done and continue to do in the past almost six years. From economic growth and infrastructure, to improving livelihoods, education, health, and other social services, we truly see the transformation in our society.

But these achievements we have made can disappear, especially if the administration that follows us is contrary to the Straight Path. The question is: Will you give the opportunity to lead to people who are quarrelsome or who, with every opening of their mouths, have a new enemy?

It is clear: During Martial Law, the government that was empowered by the people used that power to oppress the citizenry. Some believe: We need a sensible and perfect dictator to guide and fix society. The mistake here is that people are not perfect. It is natural that they cannot always make perfect decisions, because they are not perfect. If we choose a leader who thinks they are always right and must be obeyed, where will their governance lead us?

This is the opposite of what we are promoting in the Straight Path, where we believe in the value of every individual, and in giving opportunities to our citizens. Those who wish to re-establish a dictatorship will only succeed if, on one hand, those who oppose it are divided, and if many are content to remain silent and inactive. By demonstrating People Power, no one will ever again lead to abuse and oppress the Filipino.

To our youth, remember: You are fortunate to enjoy a freedom not achieved by those who came before you. We who are older have a shorter time left in this world than you do. This means that if we make the wrong decision, we will all suffer, but you will endure a far longer period of suffering. You, the youth, hold your future in your hands. May you continue to be enlightened, participate, and contribute to our efforts to promote a better tomorrow for you and for all of us.

In the Straight Path, there is now a government that is truly an ally and cares for the people; there is now a government that promotes justice and the welfare of the majority. The challenge to each of us: Continue the fight that began in EDSA. Let us fight for the freedom we have longed for, and for the fulfillment of our dreams.

To the very end: It is an honor to lead a united people for a decent and progressive future.

Good afternoon. Thank you all very much.
