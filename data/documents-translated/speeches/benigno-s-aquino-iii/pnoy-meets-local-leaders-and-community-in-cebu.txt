Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

during his meeting with local leaders and communities of Cebu

[Delivered in Argao, Cebu, on April 19, 2016]

If you recall, I promised you that when I step down from office, we will leave a much better Philippines than the one we inherited. I also remember my father teaching me that of all the freedoms we talk about, the most important freedom is freedom from hunger. When a person is not sure where they will get their next meal, all other freedoms are worthless to them. Is that right? You gave me an opportunity, you supported me when I ran for senator, which then became a stepping stone for me to become President. And for that, first and foremost, thank you very much. [Applause]

Earlier, I heard a mother ask if the 4Ps program could be continued? For me, well, if we continue Mar Roxas in Malacañang, the 4Ps will not only be continued, it will be expanded. [Applause]

How do we expand that? Earlier, someone testified, a graduate of Normal University, about their problem getting into college. There are several thousand who have been helped by 4Ps to get into college, with the help of CHED, and graduate. Earlier, you saw, they already graduated, even cum laude. [Applause]

We can expand the help for college. Let's just remember, the 4Ps, initially was only for grade school. In 2014 we had funds, and we expanded the help to high school. Now, if they finish high school, it is estimated that 40 percent of income can be added compared to a grade school graduate. If we can help them through high school, let's not change direction, the government has funds to help through college as well [applause] and truly maximize the opportunity for everyone.

Now, I will also take this opportunity to say goodbye to you, we only have 72 days left in office: What have we accomplished together on the Straight Path? It's hard to memorize everything, so if you'll allow me, this is just a sample, I just need our code: Pantawid Pamilya, since it was mentioned earlier, in 2010 when we assumed office, there were only 5,600 households in Cebu. Now, almost 150,000 households in Cebu are being helped. [Applause]

In 2010, the entire Philippines barely reached 780,000—actually a little short. Now, 4.6 million households across the Philippines are being helped through the 4Ps. The result: 7.7 million Filipinos have been lifted above the poverty line. No longer living below the poverty line. [Applause] That is because of you.

When I was a congressman, we had to beg for budget just to have classrooms for our youth. We were lucky to get eight, ten, and if you got twelve in the entire district, you were doing very well. My district when I was a congressman, the second district of Tarlac, had 159 barangays. Then if you divide now eight or ten or a dozen classrooms. What was achieved on the Straight Path?

In the province of Cebu: We have already built and are currently building 7,889 classrooms for Cebu alone. [Applause] That is included in the 185,000 classrooms across the Philippines. Let me repeat, when I was a Congressman and it wasn't that long ago, 8,000 was divided across the entire Philippines, now Cebu alone has almost 8,000. So there are 185,000, again, you made this happen.

In our health sector: We have allocated P444.51 million for your barangay health centers, rural health units and others through the Health Facilities Enhancement Program. I passed by some of our health workers earlier and they seemed very happy. I think the help has reached them.

In PhilHealth: We now have 4 million Cebuanos covered by PhilHealth. They are among the 93 million Filipinos now being helped by PhilHealth. [Applause]

On farm-to-market roads and irrigation: We have allocated P1.6 billion, including the Argao Communal Irrigation System Phase II.

Sitio electrification: Under the leadership of one of your own, our first Secretary of Energy, Rene Almendras, brother of Agnes, followed by Icot Petilla, and now continued by Zaidi Monsada, there are already 2,117 sitios in the province of Cebu that have been energized. The report to us is that 100 percent of all sitios in Cebu have energy. [Applause]

To be honest, the initial target in 2011 listed: How many sitios have no electricity? 2,241. The number that have been energized is 32,688, and there are still another 1,500 that really cannot be connected to the grid, they are too far. They will have renewable energy; we will use solar so they can have electricity too.

On infrastructure: As our good governor mentioned, from 2005 to 2010, the funds allocated by the national government for Cebu were P12.6 billion. Under the Straight Path, P34.8 billion from 2011 to 2016 went to your infrastructure. [Applause]

There are also our new projects such as the Mactan Cebu International Airport. From 4.5 annual passenger capacity passing through Cebu Mactan, it is now estimated to reach 15 million once this project is completed. The construction of your passenger terminal building is ongoing.

The Mactan Island Circumferential Road, Serging Osmena Boulevard, is also finished, included in the 18,000 kilometers of national road, 107,000 lineal meters of bridges because of your trust. [Applause] I am not a candidate. My mother said, “Don’t talk too long.” My mother, Kris, is sometimes here and might remind me of that. So we shouldn't talk too long.

But there are points I need to share with you. Sorry if I can't come down there. My PSG group commander, we just went to Basilan. In Basilan, before we arrived, a detachment was bombed, then Abu Sayyaf attacked the area we were going to and an IED device was found. There is a lot of pressure on my PSG commander, he is also nearing retirement; he will retire next year. I want to let him reach his retirement without any problems with his heart. So please excuse me if we don't add to his worries.

Comrades, I will ask you a question. Let's say we think—no, before that, let me go back to what I promised: What do we need to address first? Freedom from hunger. What is the strategy for that? Well, let's try to maximize employment for everyone. I don't believe in giving rice today, so you have something to eat today, and then you're on your own tomorrow.

Let's find a solution that will allow him/her to stand on their own two feet, have a decent livelihood, and provide for their family properly.

How do we do that? Let's start with 4Ps. What is the main condition of 4Ps? Your child must be studying. Make sure your child attends school, there is support from the government to help you keep your child in school. Is that right? Secondly: When they graduate, do they have a job? First, TESDA helps. If you don't want to continue with college, there's TESDA—over 9 million course graduates have completed it. When TESDA graduates are tracked, 28.5 percent were employed within six months of graduation. Now, 72 percent of TESDA graduates have a job six months after training, or even before six months after training.

Let me give you an example: There was a woman in Pangasinan who graduated with a degree in Accountancy. She was offered a job in the Middle East, and upon arrival, she was told that instead of the promised job, she would be a cashier. Upon arrival, she became a domestic helper, a contract substitution. She was even mistreated where she was staying. She escaped, was helped by our embassy, and returned to the Philippines. Her testimony is that she doesn't know what will happen in her life. She likely went into debt to travel in order to pursue this job that was supposed to bring her comfort.

She was unlucky there, and likely, upon her return, she thought, “How will I pay off this debt?” She said her mind was confused. She was thinking about committing suicide because she couldn't see how she would overcome her problem. She was informed upon her return that there were scholarship programs from the government, including those from TESDA. She enrolled in wellness massage therapy, taking two courses. She got a job at a spa. At the spa, she was a good worker and became an operations manager. After becoming an operations manager, she learned the whole business and established her own spa. Her spa business now has four branches in Pangasinan and three franchises in Tarlac. From not knowing where she was going, she is now an entrepreneur.

And Joel Villanueva always reminds me, “Boss, many of our graduates, after graduating, earn more than your salary within a year.” I say, “Well, that’s just life. People don’t go into government to get rich.”

There was a mechanic who was helped by TESDA to become a mechanic. He is a certified mechanic, and is now in Australia. His salary is P250,000 per month. The salary of the President of the Republic of the Philippines is P120,000, and that mechanic has much thicker hair than I do because he faces fewer problems.

Again, we helped them study. There is support for transportation and supplies. If their health is poor, the DOH and PhilHealth are there to help. They will be given the ability to earn a living with the help of TESDA. Now, we want to expand this to include college completion.

The first group we helped, those who were in high school, graduated in 2015, last year. Two of them spoke for the over 300,000 who were helped in that batch. About 14,000 of those we helped were honors graduates. So the two who spoke were accepted into the University of the Philippines, an Engineering course—that’s a quota course, it’s difficult to get in there. These two who were helped by 4Ps passed the rigorous exam, and in about four years, the entire nation will benefit because they were given the opportunity to maximize their potential and are now studying Engineering.

Now, did all of this happen because we just lazily accept blessings that fall into our laps? No, of course not. How did we increase the placement rate of TESDA graduates? We spoke with business owners.

The problem in the past was that people would enter a course formerly called “Occupational Physical Therapy.” There were many jobs. They would enroll, study for four years, graduate, take the board exams, and then after passing the board, the jobs would be gone. They would switch to nursing, and the same thing would happen. Four years, another board exam, graduation, passing the board, no nursing jobs. I said, “We can change this. Let's talk to the business owners. What kind of workers will you need two years from now, four years from now, six years from now? So that when you graduate, first, you go into a course they need, they're happy, you're happy to have a job, we're all happy.” [Applause] That's what's called a plan.

Before I leave that topic. The BPO mentioned earlier by Governor and Paolo: In the BPO industry, the government spends around 7,000 [presumably pesos]. The taxes paid by a TESDA graduate in this BPO training, in the first year alone, will exceed what we spent to train them. This means the state's investment is recovered immediately. And they will work for 30 years. Even if they don't advance, every year, there's another person who can be helped to learn, to have a decent and honorable job. That is the Straight Path: It's all really planned, everything is well thought out. And we really look at all sectors to see how to maximize the opportunities for everyone.

This is the simplest message: May is near. On May 9th, we will choose. Like we did in 2010, we chose. Was the way the country was run for almost ten years by my predecessor correct? I think here in Cebu, the voice is very clear: No. That's why those of us in the opposition were able to take office.

Now, of course, it's important, everyone now, all the candidates are courting, saying they are the most worthy child of God. That's right, isn't it? It's important—Let's just imagine we're thinking of starting a business, we're going to open a restaurant. Of course, for the restaurant, it's important that the food is delicious, right? If your restaurant has bad food, you won't have customers, it's clear? The most important employee you need first is a cook who understands. If you have applicants, someone comes who isn't a cook but a mechanic, but is good at making people laugh, good at singing, good at writing poetry, good at dancing, you hire the mechanic's services, make him a cook, and he cooks, do you think your restaurant will succeed? It seems unlikely, doesn't it?

That's what's important. So I think, before we talk about what these candidates have said, let's first look at who is qualified? And I apologize, I think on May 9th, the most important thing we are choosing is the President. In our system, the president is the helmsman. He is the one who will hopefully lead us to a better destination. It's difficult if we are led on a U-turn, back to where we were. Now, let's talk—just look at my question if you can think about it: Who is qualified to be President of the Republic of the Philippines?

If that's the case, I'll go home. I don't need to convince you anymore. [Laughter] But this is just a little more, you know. Just let me finish. When we say: Mar Roxas is qualified because of the BPO—Paolo mentioned it earlier, he was the one who thought of it. Where can we enter where Filipinos can truly rise? This BPO, this call center he mentioned, now has 1 million jobs. We surpassed that last year. This year, it is estimated that 1.3 million will be BPO sector workers.

That's in the call center itself—direct employee. But the restaurants they eat at also have employees; the transportation to get to the office also has employees; the convenience store, like 7/11 where they buy their supplies, also has employees. So every employee in the BPO creates three to four jobs in the sector that supports them or what is needed to sustain them.

So, that one million direct employees, add to that three to four million who were given jobs, because Mar Roxas didn't promise to provide jobs, he looked for jobs that Filipinos could get. It was done, and that is the result. [Applause]

You know, $25 billion is the estimate that will be brought in by that industry this year, 2016. Mar Roxas is qualified because when someone promises, doesn't it suppress crime? Mar Roxas already thought of ways that are being done to lower our crime rate.

As an example, the police have something called “One Time, Big Time.” When you hear that, it sounds like that's what the suggestion of those with lotto stations was, it wasn't just that one time big time. One Time Big Time, they gathered all those with warrants, they looked for them, who has warrants? They identified all those with warrants. When the warrant of arrest is served, it's all at once, not an installment plan. That means, just once in the province, they will arrest everyone at once, there's no warning to those hiding that the police are looking for them all. Result: Many of our most wanted have been arrested. And when their mastermind is gone, the crime rate drops significantly. That wasn't a promise to reduce crime, that's what Mar Roxas did when he was SILG. The reduction in crime, Mar Roxas is qualified again. [Applause] We won't finish if I tell you all of Mar Roxas' qualifications. I think we all know.

Let me just add the question—let's try this: Jojo Binay is qualified to be President of the Republic of the Philippines because _____. I can't remember—I suggest you just think about it.

Grace Poe is qualified to be President of the Philippines because ______. It's up to you if we can put something there. Digong Duterte is qualified to be President of the Philippines because ______. Let me go back to this.

Later, when you get home, try to fill in those blanks with anything, if we can't put anything in those blanks that we believe in, let's go back: Where are we sure? Well, Mar Roxas is that name, in my opinion. [Applause]

Let's not vote on May 9, and then on May 10 say we were carried away, we didn't think about it. No. There's still time. Let's think about it carefully.

Everything we've done on the Straight Path, remember that you did it, so you are my Boss. Even if I had a good plan back then, if you didn't give me a chance, we wouldn't have accomplished anything.

But you didn't just give me a mandate, you also accompanied me on the Straight Path. That's why, whatever needed to be overcome, I overcame it because that was my vow to leave you with a better Philippines than the one I inherited. And again, without you, we wouldn't have achieved anything because you were there, the distance and the amount we accomplished were great, so thank you very much again. [Applause]

I really feel that someone is whispering, “It’s been more than 10 minutes.” But I need to leave you with this: These last two days, I am very happy because I have really seen the results of all our hard work. You know, I saw my picture in 2010—June 30, 2010. I look more at my hair then and now. My hair then, my hair now. I said, the only thing that hasn't changed is my comb. [Laughter] But back then, it might have taken five minutes to comb, now, it can be done in an hour and a half. [Laughter]

Now, what was the result of the fatigue, sweat, resentment, blood that we poured into this? We were in Bacolod, we stayed in Negros Occidental yesterday, I was at their airport—you know it's in Silay, not in Bacolod City anymore. So it was already getting late when we arrived, and I was surprised. I said, “I don't remember this road being like a highway, like the Star tollway in Southern Luzon.” After that, we went back and forth to Bacolod several times. Before, when I campaigned as a senator, we would go back and forth on just one road to talk to everyone we needed to talk to. We spoke to about 13 groups, we went down one road, and we spoke to everyone.

Now, they have what they call Bacolod East and, I think, a place called Villa Angela in Bacolod. Governor Marañon fed us last night in their area. There are many restaurants, named Marketplace. Marketplace has a restaurant called “Entings.” Now, aside from the delicious food, first of all, there are malls on the left and right, which, in the province, having one mall is a big thing. This one has them on both sides. Of course, Cebu is already advanced.

But here in Bacolod, Bacolod suddenly expanded. I spoke to Governor Freddie Marañon, and I said, “Manong, I don't remember this street, are we taking a different route?” He said, “That's our new diversion road to make the trip from the airport to Bacolod smoother.”

What important thing did we see there? Megaworld and Ayala bought land in the town of Talisay in Negros Occidental. The town of Talisay is a typical agricultural town, almost the same as any part of the Philippines. Because Megaworld and Ayala are going to build—they are still building, there is only a fence there—the price of land in that area has increased. The price increased, adding to the income of the municipality of Talisay from their real property tax. Because it increased, they have a special education fund where they fund their students with supplies. There are free meals, feeding their youth up to Grade 4. Their teachers have received a salary increase, again because they see that this town is progressing. Megaworld started, Ayala started, only the fence is up. Talisay has really improved.

What is my point here? Let me give another example: In Manila, the Food Terminal Incorporated, it's been there for a long time, almost useless. I was asked during the early part of our term, “Sir, let's sell it.” If we sell it, we will see the assets of the government that we want to develop, and the private sector will develop it. I said, “How much can we sell it for?” They told me, P11,000 per square meter. I said, “Wait a minute, this is FTI, a fence separates it from Bonifacio Global City. Only the fence is being discussed, P11,000 here, P35,000 per square meter there, the government would lose.” How much government land is there in Metro Manila that could be sold? I said, “If that's the price, I don't want it. It's not possible.”

I said to them, “You told me the economy would improve?” These are our economic advisors, “Next year.” So next year, even if we do nothing, will the value of the land increase? So we waited, one year. It was sold after one year, the P11,000 became P33,000 per square meter when Ayala bought it. When Ayala bought it, they even laughed at them, “You paid too much for that.” Now, it's P150,000 per square meter—they are just building buildings there. What was done in Talisay, just fenced. That's what we can say, when I took office, the Philippines was down. Now, Asia’s Rising Tiger. We have learned to stand up, we have learned to walk, or like that, the economy is walking, progressing in Talisay and many other places in the Philippines.

Next, what will happen is, we will start moving. We will run. That lot with a fence now will have buildings, will have jobs, will have various uses that we will really see – the work of Filipinos, the improvement of the Filipino livelihood, the increase in the government's ability to help further, we also have funds there for the college that we, those of us in 4Ps now, will work on together.

But there's a “but.” What if there's a different perspective? What if what is done is the opposite of the Straight Path? What if only self-interest is pushed? Will something happen to us? And when we fall again, I hope we get lucky enough to stand up again. I hope that when we stand up, the world, the community, will listen to us again, that you can trust us again, that we are good partners to you. Why do we need to risk it? Why do we need to fall to rise if we are already running now? We are accelerating, and I will return, I have nothing to boast about, if not that I am with you. So from the beginning until now, our strength is very simple: The people are the strength I rely on. [Applause]

72 days from now, I will be stepping down from office, we are both ordinary citizens. If you have a problem you want to bring to me, why wouldn't I help you if I can? But my ability will be reduced because I will be an ordinary citizen. I think, this is the last time, I will ask you for something.

I was advised, “Don't campaign anymore, you have no opponent. Retire. Be quiet.” I said, maybe that's practical, but the question is, is it right? After you helped me, after we worked hard on this, after what we have achieved, I don't care anymore. I don't think I can say that. My conscience can't bear it. It is necessary that when we part ways and I leave you, your situation is assured, not just good, but really accelerating our progress in life.

How will we do that? You who are here, you mentioned earlier Mar Roxas is qualified, maybe we can approach those who are lost. Let's remind them of what we've been through—isn't it, there's a saying, “To see where you're going, look at where you came from.” Let's remind our countrymen who are lost where we are sure that the change happening in our country will continue. I need your help. I need to be able to reach out, to knock on every heart to ensure the future of the next generation, we are not gambling, we are not relying on luck. It is said that what is worked for is really worth it. We need to work hard on this so that we not only equal what we have done in our journey now, but surpass it. That will happen if we, as always, work together and help each other. It's up to you.

My parents told me, “Trust your countrymen. Whatever happens, they will always do what is right.” That is my belief. I am counting on you. Let's work together to guarantee that our beloved Motherland will truly become even more beautiful and the lives of our countrymen will truly be secured.

Good afternoon. Thank you all very much.
