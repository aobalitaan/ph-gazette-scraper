Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

Regarding the launch of the dengue vaccine school-based immunization in Region III and the ceremonial distribution of the TSEKAP equipment package.

[Declared at People’s Park, Poblacion, Iba, Zambales, on April 5, 2016]

Of course, whenever we go to a place, we prepare; I didn't prepare myself to see Lyndon Lee-Suy again today. He is the expert on many diseases, the serious ones. When we had an issue with MERS-Corona Virus, he was the one explaining things to me then. Next week, there will be encephalitis, and after that, ebola. I said, “Lyndon, you are a good man. You help me a lot, but if possible, let's meet again later.” [Laughter] Because every time we meet, you are facing heavy problems.

Perhaps I can tell you, so you understand what the President’s job is like: During Holy Week, we received a notice that a Filipino from the Middle East had arrived in the Philippines. And twelve hours after he arrived in the Philippines, we were informed that he was tested for MERS-CoV and it was positive.

That illness wasn't here with us during those times, it suddenly appeared, and there was someone who tested positive. How many people could they have had direct contact with, direct physical contact, to be infected? This person, from Bicol, arrived before we were notified by our embassy in their place of origin, twelve hours after landing in the Philippines.

Within those twelve hours, how many people could they have had physical contact with? Did they shake hands, hug, or kiss anyone? Because they are an OFW, upon their arrival at their home, how many people came to greet them, saying “Godfather, you’re finally here! Where’s my gift?” and then hug them. Then, with each person infected, there’s what’s called a “geometric progression.” Everyone they come into contact with is also potentially at risk. In short: The DOH has a manual on how to handle this crisis.

Exactly on Holy Wednesday, during holy week, most of our colleagues were no longer in Metro Manila, we really scrambled there until around 3:30 in the morning, I think. 3:30 in the morning before we finished all the plans on what to do.

First: Check all the passengers, find all the passengers, there are about 418 people we need to find. Once we find the 418, you also ask them, since they arrived, what have they been doing to find—that’s called “contact tracing.” In short, the crisis was over, but that’s when I first really spoke with Lyndon and ten of his colleagues from the DOH, including those in Immigration, our PNP. In short, we somewhat prevented it.

Once all of that was finished, the result was that this carrier we were worried about turned out to be negative. But of course, there needs to be excitement in the lives of those who serve the nation.

Now, the last time I was here in Zambales, I attended the graduation of the Merchant Marine Academy. The parade was very impressive. I know that here in Zambales, your mangoes are famous. But I'm not hinting at anything to Governor Ebdane. [Laughter] If he has a surplus, he doesn't know what to do with it, we know how to receive vitamins from it if he wants. I'm just teasing him, and we've been together for a long time.

We are here now to witness another proof of our commitment: In progress, no one will be left behind. On the Straight Path, we take care of all our Bosses, especially those who really need it or those who are most in need.

First, we visited earlier the groundbreaking of the new Out-patient Department of your President Ramon Magsaysay Memorial Hospital. Currently, PRMMH is the only major hospital here in Zambales. Previously, we were informed that other patients from your area had to go to Manila for treatment. We addressed this under the Health Facilities Enhancement Program; we funded the renovation of your hospital to provide immediate service here in Zambales.

Let me also share this with you, because this might be the last time I face you as your President. When I went to Apayao, the governor, the good Governor Buchi Bulut, told me that in their area, because they are at the top of the Philippines, they would have contests for the most beautiful ambulance. I looked at him and of course I was curious, “Why would you have contests for the most beautiful ambulance?” He replied to me, “That’s the only chance for the sick to be taken to the nearest hospital in neighboring provinces for treatment.”

Someone testified that their area is very remote, with no roads, you have to pass through the forest. When they go to market, they can only buy what they can carry in both hands. If someone gets sick, they walk through the forest for two days. So their forest, they say, has become like a cemetery for those who are sick and don’t make it to the hospital. Now, they say it’s different, their hospital is beautiful now. They are now a “referral hospital,” they no longer send patients elsewhere, they are the ones receiving patients.

In 2012, we allocated P10.5 million for the renovation and expansion of the operating room and delivery room complex of the said hospital, as well as the purchase of equipment such as hospital beds, incubators, defibrillators, and others. This year, we also allocated an additional P24 million for the improvement of the emergency room and laboratory. There is also the construction of a new out-patient department, as well as a 50-bed ward, to increase the bed capacity of PRMMH to 200. While all of this is being built, our good Governor Ebdane mentioned that it’s not enough. I said, “Don’t worry, this afternoon I will also campaign here in Zambales so I can tell my successor that there is still a need here in Zambales that he can hopefully fulfill what we have started.” [Applause]

When we started, all that was left to me were debts, so it was difficult to make promises. Now, when something is needed, there is always a way and a source for it.

Our target is to finish the upgrade of your hospital this September of the current year. And of course, someone else will cut the ribbon, but we are ensuring that the project is polished and in accordance with the needs of our Bosses. Even if we don't get the credit, as long as the livelihood and health of our Bosses are good, we are very content. [Applause]

Earlier, as you saw, we launched the Dengue Vaccine School-Based Immunization Program here in our Region 3. You know, there was a time when dengue was seasonal. Now, it’s all year-round. It’s also painful, in the Philippines, we asked what the prevalent strains were. The answer to us was, “All strains present in the Philippines.”

When we started, we spoke with Sanofi Pasteur at one time because they were developing a vaccine that could reduce the debilitating effects, the burden of having dengue. And they said that the process would be quite long, and we were really happy when we saw them again during our trip to Paris—if I’m not mistaken—where the trials were completed, showing the effectiveness of these medicines.

Dengue, I think, we all know someone who has had dengue and we have seen what the patient and family went through when they had dengue. Perhaps many of us have also experienced looking for blood when those with severe cases of dengue needed a blood transfusion.

One of my personal experiences: There was an ambassador from Europe who really loved the Philippines, to the point that he made a pamphlet—he almost became a travel agent for the Philippines for his countrymen coming to the Philippines. He translated all the sights, the places to visit, into their language, and he was really helping us with tourism.

What did the Philippines give him in return? As far as I understand, he contracted dengue fever at least twice, maybe even three times. When he retired, he really loved us, he had dengue fever repeatedly here, he stayed here in the Philippines, he now lives here, and he even married here.

I think it’s clear that instead of getting sick, we can help reduce the occurrence of sickness. Or if not, reduce the effect of the sickness. That is a very important action. Instead of leaving it to Batman, we will do everything possible based on science to truly protect the health of our countrymen.

Earlier, if you saw Janet’s presentation, there were “vector eradication” measures. She showed a fogging machine. What is a fogging machine? Honestly, I believed it sprayed insecticide on mosquitoes, but the truth is, it usually just repels them. So they go to your neighbor’s house. That’s the effect. Now, if it sprays there, it might come back to you. It’s really not a permanent solution.

What Janet kept repeating was: Good is prevention—let's eliminate the vector that transmits the disease. We won't be able to eliminate all of it perfectly. But if we are afflicted, what should we do to prevent the illness from becoming very severe? And I think Janet's explanation is better than if I were to explain it to you.

That's why it's important that we have a vaccine against dengue. Our report is: The Philippines is only the second country to have licensed Dengvaxia, the world's first dengue vaccine. The funds allocated by the DOH for this vaccine: P3.5 billion. This will be included in the list of free vaccines under our National Immunization Program.

Together with the DepEd, DILG and local governments, we are implementing this school-based immunization: It aims to provide free dengue vaccines to almost one million Grade 4 public elementary school pupils, aged nine and above, in NCR, Region 3 and Region 4-A. Some might say again: Why are we choosing—NCR, Southern Tagalog, and Central Luzon? Because those are the most populous regions, will you prioritize them or give to them because there's an election now? No. That's where the highest incidence of dengue is. So, that's probably the most important to prioritize because that's where there are the most cases.

Our stance is: “Prevention is better than cure.” I don't think anyone would object that if a disease can be avoided, it should be addressed or done immediately. We get vaccinated to prevent getting sick, like dengue, measles, and others. Before, vaccines were limited to private hospitals. So, before, when you went to a public hospital, it was like “too bad for you”. That's why we expanded our National Immunization Program, so that even our public hospitals have access to these vaccines. Let me repeat: Earlier, Janet showed us how long different vaccines have been available, but they are expensive, unreachable to those in the margins, so it's like Batman has to take care of you.

We said: No one will be left behind. The DOH budget, if I’m not mistaken, since we started, has tripled. A 300 percent increase, and this is the result: [applause] the equipment in front of you, the capacity for vaccines, the capacity of […]

As part of our strategy to promote universal health, we have distributed “Right Services for Family Health” or TSeKap Equipment Packages to 132 Barangay Health Stations and 32 Rural Health Units here in Zambales. These contain equipment such as thermometers, stethoscopes, digital BP apparatus, glucometer sets, dressing sets, and nebulizers. With this, our LGUs are empowered to immediately respond to those in need of medical attention.

You know, in the old days, these barangay health stations, they say, had a room, a table, a chair, but no equipment. If someone consulted, they would just chat—it was called a barangay health station.

From building and improving infrastructure, to ensuring that every Filipino is safe from sickness, we are focusing on it, not tomorrow, but now. In the Straight Path, we are expanding and maximizing the benefits of every program and project, to alleviate the suffering of our countrymen.

For example: We greatly expanded the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program. The number of beneficiaries of Pantawid Pamilya nationwide that we inherited in 2010 was only 780,000. You are relatively lucky here in Zambales. You had 1,111 beneficiaries of Pantawid Pamilya in 2010. Of course, one can’t help but think: What a perfect number, 1,111. When we were given grades in school back then, that’s what we complained about, when it wasn’t clear what grade the teacher was giving. We used to say, “They probably used a dart board.” “I’ll give this one an A. I’ll give this one a…”

On the Straight Path, we strived to identify and reach those on the margins. Their former 1,111, now 19,433 households are benefiting from the program here in your province. They are now part of the 4.6 million households we are targeting to complete this year. Last year, we were at 4.4 million households. According to initial studies, 7.7 million of our countrymen have already been helped to cross or rise above what is called the “poverty line.”

In addition to this, we also expanded the coverage of PhilHealth. Here in Zambales, 685,000 are now covered by the program; they are part of the 93 million Filipino beneficiaries nationwide. What’s good about these achievements on the Straight Path is that we were able to achieve them without increasing taxes, except for the Sin Tax, which aims to improve our health services and distance us from vices.

Let’s look back: Where did we come from and where are we now? We faced a crossroads then: Where should we focus, on ourselves or on others? Should we continue the old ways we inherited, or should we continue to tread the Straight Path?

You indeed gave me the opportunity to begin the transformation, and with your cooperation, we have come so far. But these achievements we have worked hard for are now in danger, especially if those who will steer us after the elections do not share the same vision. Let us remember where we came from; it has an opposite view.

In May, the permanence of the change we are making is in your hands. As Father of the Nation, my appeal is simple: Let us carefully examine those who are presenting themselves to be leaders of the country. Let us choose those with integrity, a clear plan, and who will truly serve our Bosses. To you, face to face, I can tell you, those who are sure to continue and expand the Straight Path: Mar Roxas and Leni Robredo. [Applause]

As they say, “A good future is worked for.” We are only at the beginning of our successes, and I am confident: You, my Bosses, will shape and continue the change, so that we can achieve a Philippines that we can truly be proud of for the next generation.

Let me repeat: Truly, in our democratic system, we choose leaders because power is in the people. Naturally, our choice will determine where we will end up. When we vote, let us not forget that our lives are included there. But perhaps more importantly: the lives of future generations who will truly enjoy more blessings or endure suffering longer if we make a mistake. I was taught to trust our Bosses, the people, so my trust remains whole. You are now in charge of all of us.

Good afternoon. Thank you very much to all of you.
