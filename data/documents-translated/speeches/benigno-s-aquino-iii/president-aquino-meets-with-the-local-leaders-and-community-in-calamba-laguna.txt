Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

During his meeting with local leaders and communities of Calamba, Laguna

[Declared in Calamba, Laguna, on March 9, 2016]

Let us admit it: When Calamba is thought of, we cannot separate it from Gat Jose Rizal. For me, that is truly memorable. Of course, we went through studies, a long topic was the life of Gat Jose Rizal. And I had a batchmate, he was failing his history course. Until he thought, why not organize a field trip to the house of Jose Rizal. His teacher had the opportunity to sit on Jose Rizal’s bed. And because he was sitting, he felt like he was in heaven. So my batchmate who didn’t know how he would pass, said he passed. And he sends his thanks to all of you from Calamba, he crossed over that course. [Applause]

As you all know, I promised you my ambition on that day. With the six years you have given me, only 113 days remain. It would be good to look back on where we came from, where we are now. I promised you on that day, when I step down from office, my ambition: What I will leave to you will be far better than what I found. Just this time—my parents taught me not to boast—but perhaps it is important to see where we came from and where we are now.

Earlier, I was at the turnover ceremonies because the Commander General of the Air Force has changed—it was held in Lipa. We traveled there by helicopter. What's surprising is that when you think of Calamba, Jose Rizal is the first thing that comes to mind; when you think of Biñan, it's tupig, puto, and many others. What's surprising here is not just the numerous subdivisions, factories—earlier I saw a factory, next to a [batching plant], their heavy equipment all close together and seemingly new. In line with that, there's a subdivision being built, and with the size of the subdivision, the road being built there is as big as a district.

Let's go back to where we started. Back then—many probably don't know this—from 2005 to 2010, the projects we inherited, for public works in the province of Laguna, amounted to P3.68 billion. Let's remember that number. Now that we are in office with your permission, in 2011, we have delivered P12.61 billion here in Laguna. [Applause] The DPWH reminded me that they have already concreted the Lipa-Alaminos Road, and widened the Manila South Road. The Sta. Rosa-Ulat-Tagaytay Road and San Pedro Bridge are now four lanes wide to make travel easier. Now, the South Luzon Expressway Toll Road 4 and the North-South Railway Project South Line will also be built.

Now, let's talk about what I think are the differences. The Cavite-Laguna Expressway has been awarded, and it is estimated to greatly help in the continued progress of the province of Laguna. Here, we need infrastructure, but instead of the government building it, the private sector stepped in and offered competitive proposals to us. “Give us permission to build that project.” That's why it's a Private-Public Partnership Project.

How did they compete? They rushed to build the infrastructure we need and offered competitive proposals. What's the competition in the proposals? There was a bidding. Initially, it seemed like the one who offered to build and operate the toll road would win; the government and the people would pay P11 billion. I said, “Wait a minute. Someone offered P20 billion for the privilege. How can I explain to the people the P9 billion that we could still use for other things?” So there was a rebidding. Some didn't participate anymore, but others participated in the rebidding. And the P20 billion offer became P27.3 billion.

What is the importance of that? When we, for example, the LRT, MRT, when that project was given, we needed to give them all sorts of incentives just to build what the government couldn't afford. Here, on the Straight Path, they see it as a source of profit, so they rush to pay us to build the infrastructure that we need. I cannot tell you about CALAX for now. What is clear is: It has been awarded, and the winner has an obligation to build the infrastructure within the set timeframes. That is the first thing.

Secondly, we remember this Laguna Lake dredging project. They say we need to reduce the flooding. Good! We need to increase the lake's capacity to hold water. Correct! Their solution is to dig up the mud. Of course, the important thing there is, where will we move the mud that you will remove? The answer to me is, it will be taken from one part of the lake and moved to another part of the lake. Our youth said, that’s still a lake. [Laughter] It’s like in a basin, you put sand in it. You take the sand and put it in another part of the basin. I don’t think the amount of water it can hold will increase. The worst part of what we found is, just to play with our own mud, we are being charged. That was the project back then: P18.7 billion would be charged to us if we allowed that project. I stopped it. I think it’s foolish to play with mud. [Applause]

What we replaced it with is what we call the Ring Road Dike, where a portion will have dikes. The top of the dike will have a road. A portion of the dike will be reclaimed from the lake. The reclaimed portion will be the payment for those who will build this project. What is good about this project? We have infrastructure that we need, we have payment for that infrastructure, and because of that road, places that were previously very remote and very difficult to reach will now be opened. Our countrymen will once again be given additional opportunities. Let me repeat: Before, a playground of mud; now, a proper project. That is the Straight Path. [Applause]

Let's move on to what Kuya Joel is very familiar with. He led TESDA and continued to lead TESDA—because in 2013, he was already running for senator. I just asked, “Wait a moment, you’re doing a great job. We’ll have to look for a replacement again, and we might ruin the good work you’re doing.” In the Training for Work Scholarship Program of TESDA, there were already 34,781 graduates from 2010 to 2015 in the province of Laguna. [Applause] Because of how well he managed things, they had a program with Coke, called “Star.” It helps those who run sari-sari stores. There were 40,000 graduates from that program. What they do is help sari-sari store owners on how to maximize their income. They have one graduate—most of their graduates have this story: Before they were helped, their income was P800 per day. After they were helped, their income became P5,000 per day. [Applause]

What was taught there was: How do you properly conduct an inventory? How do you display your products? How do you ensure that your calculations are accurate and complete? All those kinds of skills. Another thing taught: What do you do with your excess earnings? Where do we invest it so it grows further? In short, that graduate and many others, P800 became P5,000; if we multiply that P5,000 by 30 days in one month, it will come out to, if my calculation is correct, P150,000. Is that right? The salary of the President of the Republic of the Philippines is P120,000. So after I finish here, in 113 days, maybe in one year and 113 days, I will also apply for a scholarship there at TESDA. The mechanic there, you see, earns a salary of P250,000. That's abroad. [Applause]

Regarding PhilHealth, if that's what we're talking about, there are 2.56 million beneficiaries in Laguna who are part of the 93 million Filipinos who are already members and benefiting from PhilHealth. Our population has exceeded 100 million. So, about 93 percent of our population is PhilHealth accredited. May we know if there is anyone here in our area who has benefited from PhilHealth now? Please raise your hand. There are some!

For Pantawid Pamilya, there are already 44,553 beneficiaries here in Laguna. When a survey was conducted in 2009, how many families or households needed help? 4.6 million households. The number of families they actually helped was almost 780,000. We, with good governance, have increased this year after year, family by family. So this year, the identified 4.6 million, the remaining 200,000 we will finish this year. Result: 7.7 million Filipinos have crossed what is called the “poverty line.” Let me repeat: This happened because of your help, your permission. We have a proud 7.7 million Filipinos who can no longer be considered among the “extremely poor.”

According to the Social Weather Stations survey this February: Four out of every five Filipinos will vote for candidates who will continue Pantawid Pamilya. [Applause]

However, as father of the nation, let me just remind everyone: Those who claim they will continue [the program], they were also the ones questioning or wanting to investigate the Pantawid [program previously]. In 2013, they seemed to want to block [it]; now, they are the ones who will continue it. They probably think we are losing our minds.

Previously, with Pantawid, the 780,000—let's make it 800,000 for easier calculation. Our province has 81—let's make it 80 for easier calculation. 800,000, divided by 80 provinces, comes out to about ten thousand per province. I came from Bohol, I remember, only 560 families were being helped there. Go to Cebu—they call it their stronghold—only 5,600. What's painful is that in Bohol, the truly needy people we are helping now are 49,900 plus. In Cebu, the 5,000 they helped in their stronghold, we are now helping almost 150,000 there. Now, according to the 20 most depressed provinces, one has 20,000, another has 70,000. So it seems that even “Who will be helped by Pantawid?” was purely based on politics back then. We, on the other hand, had a survey, we analyzed every Filipino: Who are really those at the margins of society who need help? And that was the basis for who should be helped and who doesn't need help.

You know, my brothers and sisters, I am not a candidate so I have no right to speak at length today. It is more important that you listen to those who will continue, because I, with 113 days remaining, am already 144 days retired.

I believe this is the essence of what I want to say to all of you: When I accepted the challenge in 2009, my mother had just passed away, it was clear to me that I was not entering this just to become famous or to promote my own interests. I am not here to replace riding the merry-go-round. Isn't that how it is on a merry-go-round: You ride, you run in circles, and then you return to where you started, where we are going nowhere. We accepted the challenge because we knew the change we could make. I promised you then, and there is no doubt that I delivered on my promise when I said, “What I will leave behind will be far better than what we inherited.”

As an elderly woman from the province of Nueva Ecija said, “Be careful, Noy, you will encounter many obstacles.” That is true: There are those former government officials we accused. The one I replaced, you know, is under hospital arrest. We removed the former Chief Justice of the Supreme Court. We have disturbed many—as they say in English—“I disturbed the rice bowl.” In Tagalog, I interfered with their livelihood where they oppressed you. I don't know what will happen to my fate, but I'm willing to be the one to suffer the consequences. That's okay with me. I'm okay with that because I was true to you and something came out of your trust. [Applause]

I am at peace knowing that all my decisions are in accordance with the law. Everything my colleagues went through on this journey, on the Straight Path, from my cabinet to their and my staff, I can say to everyone face to face, “All the fatigue and hardship were worth it because we are now making change permanent.” We were able to do this because you gave me a mandate, the opportunity to change the situation of the Philippines.

Bosses, we would not have been able to do all of this if we had not started the change. Will we waste this opportunity given to us to continue what we have started?

Now, we are given another opportunity to choose and act. You will decide where we will take the country. I can suggest, I am like you with one vote. I am with you in holding responsibility for our destination. As a father of the nation, I could remain silent during this campaign period now that my family is in a good situation. But my conscience will not allow me to remain silent now. I am not the traditional politician who always thinks, “I won't make any enemies, so that everyone, in some way, will be my friend.” Mine is: Let's not waste this opportunity.

As they say: Before you run, you must first learn to walk. We have already begun the beautiful stories of the lives of Filipinos. We have risen from our falls, and we have learned to walk. Now, we are at the stage of accelerating. How will we do this if the next person changes the direction? Will we entrust our future to the uncertain? Or to others who deliberately want to push us back to the old ways? Or to others who do not know how to teach us to run? What is important here is that there is a guarantee of continuing what we are doing right.

You know, I can’t help but think that when Martial Law began, when our world turned upside down, I was only twelve years old. When I step down from office—and now, I am 56 years old—we have been involved in the field of Philippine politics for 44 years. Often, back then, I was my parents’ loyal assistant. My father said, during the 1978 Interim Batasan Elections, he said, “Let me run in this last election, because this is the only opportunity where Filipinos can gather again.” During Martial Law, as the elders know, if you talked for more than three years, that was an illegal assembly, imprisonment. If imprisoned, it was up to fate if there would be a trial; it was up to fate if you would be released. He added a promise to us, that when Mr. Marcos stepped down from office, we would live abroad for two years, to forget the horrors of Martial Law. He added, poor thing, whoever replaces Mr. Marcos. With so many problems he left behind, they’d be lucky if they weren’t driven out, because people were tired of waiting for the solution to those catastrophic problems. He didn't think that my mother would be the one to replace Mr. Marcos.

That is why, after we leave office and retire, I want to be able to tell my siblings, I want to be able to tell my nephews and nieces, I want to be able to say even to our household staff, that we finally have peace time. That our country is in order. That our conscience will not prick us that we left the job unfinished.

With your permission, in my view and suggestion, the one who will lead our fight for the continuation of the Straight Path, the next President of the Republic of the Philippines, is none other than Mar Roxas!
