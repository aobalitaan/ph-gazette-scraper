Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

In his meeting with local leaders and communities of Cagayan de Oro

[Declared at Regatta Square Business Park, Cagayan de Oro City, on April 13, 2016]

Let me tell a story first. During the campaign for senator, I was here in Cagayan de Oro, the next day we needed to go to Bacolod City. Now, there was no time to return to Manila. As you know, we couldn't get a connecting flight in the Philippines. It was no longer possible to return to Manila to continue to Bacolod City, we would be late. So we came from Cagayan de Oro, straight to Bacolod City. So, when we arrived at the airport, because Lumbia was nearby, we went there, I was running as senator. I looked around at the airstrip. I was looking for the plane that would take us. So I looked to the right, I didn't see a plane. I looked to the left, there was a plane that was probably from General MacArthur's time. [Laughter]

So, I asked my companions, “Where is our plane?” They told me, “Sir, it’s over there.” They pointed to the one left by General MacArthur. When I boarded it, that plane was really very old. The pilot said, “Let me adjust your seat.” He adjusted the seat, and I almost hit the dashboard of the plane. Of course, we were also looking at the instrument panel, it was digital. Digital de-tumbler, not lights, not LCD, not LED. Okay.

The pilot said when he boarded and addressed those of us who were staying behind, “Give it a push.” [Laughter] Our colleagues—this is true, I’m not joking—held onto the wing. Then we were all starting to think, “We’re going to push this airplane.” [Laughter] Bacolod is quite far.” It turned out he was just asking them to push the door because it was hard to close. [Laughter] Okay.

So during takeoff, the very skilled pilot adjusted something, and as we were taking off, the seat I was sitting on came loose and went straight into the person behind me. He just caught it. [Laughter]

Then the sound of the airplane was beautiful. The engine was so loud, it didn’t seem like we were moving forward. But it was high tech, the pilot had a map, a GPS, and he didn’t do anything—I really remember a lot. Because even the choke—it would pull you back, leave you behind, the choke of this airplane, he pulled it and then put a wedge on it. [Laughter]

So the pilot said, look outside, look at the map, look at the GPS, look outside again. Until we got close to Bacolod, about five minutes away, he suddenly said to me, “Sir, Bacolod, five minutes left!” He seemed very happy, he had found Bacolod. [Laughter]

I said, “Maybe people will vote for us, but we didn’t even reach the election. Nothing happened.” [Laughter] That’s why I will really never forget Cagayan de Oro. I’d like to add: When we were about to take off, the plane had a pilot and three passengers. We left some companions behind, it was just a small plane. All those who were left in Lumbia were taking pictures of us while the plane was taxiing until it took off.

I said, “These people left behind probably doubt that we’ll reach Bacolod.” They were already taking pictures. Then I thought they might say later, “The last minutes of…” [Laughter] Honestly, I looked at them all a bit nervously. Of course, I was the leader of that group, so I just pretended that everything was alright.

I had a photographer with me. All he did was take pictures of the outside, take a few of me, then point the camera at himself, then back to the outside again. As if to forget about this flight. I said, this one also probably wants to leave a memory behind. [Laughter] He kept taking pictures of himself.

But that’s not my purpose here tonight. If you’ll remember, when we ran for President, I accepted the challenge. I told you: One of my goals is to leave the Philippines much better than we found it. And because you helped me—let’s not pretend—let’s admit it, I’m number two in your hearts here in Cagayan de Oro City. [Applause] Is it drizzling now? I thought I would sing tonight. Never mind.

What have we gained from treading the Straight Path? Let's start with: the entirety of Mindanao. Back then, in 2009, only P155 billion was allocated to Mindanao. [Applause] Let's compare, during that time, before us. When we worked together, in 2016, let's compare one year to one year: P397 billion was allocated for Mindanao. [applause] Clearly, double what we inherited.

As Butch Abad said: The budget for Mindanao increased by 156 percent. The budget—the national budget—increased by 112 percent. So the percentage increase for Mindanao is significantly larger than the increase in the budget. What other data do we have? Let's go through the agencies one by one:

For DSWD, before: P270 million, now: P22 billion—2009 and 2016; DPWH, from P24 billion, P72 billion; DOH, from P2.4 billion, P21.7 billion; DepEd, from P31 billion, now P84 billion.

For energy in Mindanao, this year, finally, in May, July, October—the plants that have been under construction for several years will finally provide energy to Mindanao and will add at least 300 megawatts to your electricity here in Mindanao. Now, available capacity is really low, less than 500. This 300 almost doubles the capacity to provide, clearly, electricity to you, a dependable power supply. What does that mean? Of course, all livelihoods here, previously held up because of a lack of electricity. Now, they can be sustained because of the additional 300 megawatts for you in Mindanao.

Earlier, we came from the switch-on ceremony, where we have already energized 32,688 sitios (sub-villages) across the country. And this, 247 sitios, is beyond the 2011 inventory that served as the basis for identifying which sitios needed electricity.

From you, the allocation from 2011 to 2016 for infrastructure was P24.14 billion. That is larger than the P9.80 billion allocated from 2005 to 2010. Earlier, your good Mayor Oca Moreno again showed me the importance of the airport. [applause]

Before, we were still arranging this, he reminded me, “During your mother’s time, the planning for the Laguindingan Airport began.” It’s good that his favorite son arrived, and we have now completed this Laguindingan Airport. So, we can expect: Oca said earlier, “Sir, okay. Thank you. Our Laguindingan airport is okay now.” Oca said, “Sir, thank you. This airport of ours is still lacking.” [laughter]

Because even now, the capacity is already insufficient, and the explanation is: the plan for its construction was in 1996; it was built in 2006. Ten years passed, the population increased, the potential increased. While it was being built, it seemed better to have something lacking than nothing at all. Now, of course, it is also in the plan: What else is needed for the expansion of the Laguindingan Airport, which is finally complete in terms of service. Flights can now be made even at night from Laguindingan because our necessary systems are already there.

We were reminded that the Puerto-Sayre Highway Flyover has been completed; the widening of the Opol-Laguindingan section of the Butuan City-Cagayan de Oro City-Iligan Road will be completed this year; Phividec next year. Phividec and the Batangueño, Alae Section of the Misamis Oriental-Bukidnon Bypass Road, will be completed next year.

You know, these roads are just a part of the 18,000 national roads that we have repaired or constructed, along with 107,000 lineal meters of bridges. One of those approved by NEDA in 2014 requires a thorough so-called Detailed Design and Engineering Study and that is almost finished. The bridge we expect to be new—I asked about it earlier—we will be hiring a foreign contractor because the bridge is very wide. No Philippine construction company has built one like it. But, the Panguil Bay Bridge has been approved by NEDA, which is estimated to reduce the travel time from Tangub City, Misamis Occidental to Tubod in Lanao del Norte to just seven minutes and of course, Cagayan de Oro will also benefit when it is completed. [Applause]

We are also focused on the modernization of the Cagayan de Oro Port and Jasaan Port, and we have completed the rehabilitation of the Balingoan Port. From 2011 to 2016, we allocated P564.02 million for the construction of farm-to-market road projects and P651.21 million for irrigation systems. Let's also include post-harvest machineries, which amounts to P46.9 million. As well as the implementation of intercropping in 21 sites in Misamis Oriental and we also have 13 community fish landing centers worth P37.05 million.

In health and education, through the Health Facilities Enhancement Program, we have improved our rural health units, barangay health stations, and hospitals. The amount is P420.65 million for two barangay health stations, 26 rural health units and urban health centers, and 11 hospitals here in Misamis Oriental. This includes our upgrade to the Northern Mindanao Medical Center here in Cagayan de Oro.

In PhilHealth, we are supporting 1.55 million Misamisnons through the program, they are part of the 93 million Filipinos currently benefiting from PhilHealth. [Applause]

In education, in Tarlac, 1,855 classrooms will be completed this year. Here in Misamis Oriental, where I am just visiting, we have already built 3,343 classrooms. [Applause] This is part of the 185,000 classrooms that have been built and are currently being built throughout the Philippines.

In the TWSP, where those behind me in black are part of, there are 21,300 scholars from Misamis. They are part of the 9 million Technical-Vocational graduates throughout the Philippines. The good news here is that, in the past, only 28.5 percent of TESDA graduates were employed six months after graduation. Now, 72 percent of graduates, after 6 months or before reaching 6 months, already have jobs. [Applause] And Tesdaman always says that after I leave this position, I can apply to TESDA. He will recommend me because most of the graduates—many of them, upon graduation, earn twice my salary and I can even keep the rest of my hair in that job. [Laughter]

In the Pantawid Pamilya Pilipino Program: we have reached 4.6 million households as of 2016. [Applause] When we came into office, that number was only 780,000. In Misamis Oriental: 72,982 households are now covered by our program; it used to be only 6,389. In Cagayan de Oro itself, 17,000 households are under Pantawid Pamilya, from the 2,000+ that we inherited.

Now, regarding flood control. I know this is very close to your hearts, flood control. It’s a very large project. To be honest, Japan is helping us, through its agency JICA, to develop a master plan and this has been completed for the entire Cagayan River and the whole thing will be implemented from 2017 to 2019. Those that can be done while waiting for the master plan have also been done by the DPWH, from 2014 until this year. A total of P1.26 billion has already been allocated for this.

There are already projects called high-impact, high-priority projects that cover the strengthening of our dikes. There is also the dredging that is continuously being done for the mouth of the Cagayan de Oro River.

That’s good. You might say we are already boasting too much and it might get too windy here. What I want to emphasize is that everything we did, could have been done before if the government had focused on its sworn duty to serve our countrymen. [Applause]

I told you, we will change the story of the Philippines. It is not the fate of Filipinos to just “tiis” (endure). We can change our destiny. You believed in me. You helped me have the opportunity to change our story and this is now the result.

Now, I won't be too lengthy with what I'll say to you now. Question: Is it okay after 6 years? You know, there's still a lot to be done. I just mentioned the master plan for fixing your flooding. We remember, it wasn't long ago that the tragedy happened when the Cagayan River swelled. We need to, we promise, Build Back Better.

We promise: Every time there's heavy rain, every time there's a storm, we shouldn't repeat the tragedy that surrounds Filipinos. That's why it's necessary, it shouldn't be piecemeal with someone saying something has been done, we need to do what is right. The right thing will come from the plan.

Perhaps, to make this clearer. Yesterday, I spoke with Executive Secretary Paquito Ochoa, who reminded me that the plan to transfer the National Bilibid Prison from Muntinlupa to Nueva Ecija. The community there has accepted it and it will be a PPP project because the cost is high. Now, to complete the whole process, he said it might be possible to award it on June 29. But I will be stepping down from office, noon of June 30. Of course, there will be questions. This might be said to be a midnight deal.

This is the issue: Our Muntinlupa, they repeatedly tell us, has too many prisoners. Consequently, isn't it, when we watch a movie, where there are prisoners, the prisoners are properly imprisoned, their movement is controlled, everything is monitored.

Here, in such a crowded situation, it really feels like once you get inside, they form their own community, one that can no longer be fully monitored due to the sheer number of people and the lack of facilities. The question now is, you read in the newspapers, our officials will raid the BuCor. They will find weapons, they will find drugs, there are rumors of drug syndicates continuing their operations while inside.

I asked myself, “Will you approve this on your last day?” I was thinking, if I don't approve this, what if the priorities change? Instead of passing on the problem of fixing Muntinlupa to my successor, this might actually be the step to improve the infrastructure, give prisoners a chance to truly rehabilitate, and give a chance to monitor everything. My successor might decide not to continue it or start the process.

So as long as we haven't built that, as long as we haven't transferred [them], the problem of Bilibid continues. How can we help society if we leave a problem like that? Six years, if you look at it—sometimes I feel like it's a very long time. Just now, I said when I was filmed in close-up on TV, your camera is better here, my hair has thickened. Because earlier, it looked so thin.

All the hard work we've done—just one example. Earlier, during the switch-on ceremony, there was a beneficiary of Pantawid, a grade 10—if I remember correctly—grade 10 student; her mother is just an ordinary housewife; her father drives a jeep. She is consistently first honors in her school. And it was really difficult to continue her studies.

Now, because of Pantawid, her studies are continuing. It's not just that she's an honor student in school, she has also received awards because she represents the youth of Misamis.

Now, of course, the program and assistance for high school will continue. Mar Roxas will mention later what the plans are, how to expand Pantawid Pamilya. What is Pantawid, really? Some people think it's just a handout. But in our view, it's an investment.

Investment: It's our capital, let's help the youth reach their potential, to finish high school. There's a study that says: a 40 percent increase in income. If they finish college, if they want to, it could help the entire country even more. But the problem with investing, of course, is that it takes a long time to study. It can't be one semester, then graduate. It can't be one year, then they're already graduates. We need to continue it so that they can really maximize the opportunity and we, as a society, can help.

Perhaps, I'll add this. In Pampanga, we recently visited. A mother said, “I am a housewife, I have seven children, my husband left me. I was left to take care of our seven children.” Because of Pantawid Pamilya, three of her seven children have already finished high school and have permanent jobs. Before, her only job was selling—sometimes she would sell, but her income wasn't certain.

Because of Pantawid [Conditional Cash Transfer Program], she now has three children helping her, and they are able to support their four children. My question to you, to be very clear, is that sometimes we get lost in statistics. If that mother came to us tonight, stood on this stage and told us, “You know, you’ve helped us so much. You may not even know that you are already helping us.” She might even say to us, “You helped us without adding to your burden, but our family has benefited greatly from your contribution and your help. Just a little more help, and we will no longer be a burden to you.”

I would also like to add those who graduated from TESDA [Technical Education and Skills Development Authority], if they enter the BPO [Business Process Outsourcing] industry, according to one study, in their first year of working, they will pay taxes. The taxes they pay will already exceed what the state spent to educate them.

In other words, our help is not just a one-time thing. We can always help because that person will work for 30 years, 40 years, paying taxes, and there will be new people given the opportunity, we started by giving the first ones an opportunity.

So what am I saying? When a woman, a mother, approaches us and asks us, “Our situation is much better now than at the beginning. Just help us a little more, we can stand on our own feet. We will no longer be a burden to you. Is it difficult to just continue the help you are already giving us?”

When we were invited to run, I was truly overwhelmed. I said, “Why would you give me the responsibility to fix all the problems we were opposing as members of the opposition when they were happening?”

But those we spoke with said, “Just stop the abuse, let’s start the change, and we’ll be happy with you.” I believe that what we’ve achieved in these six years is far beyond what we envisioned when we started, especially in 2010. But the good news here is, this is just the beginning. It’s like before, we were constantly falling. Now, we’re standing. And now that we’re standing, we can learn to walk, we have learned. Now, the momentum we’re talking about, we’re already running.

Question: Where will we go on May 9th? To the “maybes”: maybe they’ll continue, maybe they’ll improve it, but it’s also possible they’ll stop everything, maybe a different direction, maybe different sources, maybe different intentions, or to us with Mar and Leni, whom we’re sure were with us in shaping this and will continue with it. [Applause]

78 days from now, I will be retiring. I want to tell my parents and all of you. I did not abandon you, rather, it’s like I’ve passed you on to the same good hands, or perhaps even better hands. You will not be neglected if we help Mar-Leni, I am sure they will surpass what we have accomplished in these six years. [Applause]

All of their supporters now, do not forget them. There is a promise, I think, here is proof, there is a promise, it seems more certain there where we showed the proof than with those who promise but might fail. Isn't that right? [Applause]

I remember my mother and she might whisper to me now. She would say, “First of all, you are not a candidate. Don't speak for too long.” [Laughter] Secondly, just in the last moment, I will repeat: This is my farewell to you. It is truly an honor that you gave me the opportunity to lead a great Filipino race that is truly capable of shaping the future.

Thank you very much to all of you. With your permission, the next president of the Republic of the Philippines is none other than Mar Roxas.
