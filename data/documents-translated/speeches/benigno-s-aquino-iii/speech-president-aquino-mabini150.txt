Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III President of the Philippines In commemoration of the 150th birth anniversary of Apolinario Mabini

[ Delivered at the Mabini Shrine, Batangas, on July 23, 2014 ]

Excellencies of the Diplomatic Corps, Secretary Mar Roxas, Secretary Leila de Lima, Secretary Sonny Coloma, of course, our ever energetic Congressman Sonny Collantes, our Mayor Tony Halili. I told Mayor [Antonio Halili] earlier: Mayor, why is your voice broadcast quality. It’s really different here in Batangas. Of course, our good Chairperson Mariz Diokno, who is also from Batangas; Undersecretary Rafael Seguis, Governor Say Tetangco of the Bangko Sentral, Chairman Cesar Sarino, Postmaster General Josie dela Cruz, the Chief of Staff Lieutenant General Gregorio Catapang, Police Director General Alan Purisima, Service Commanders Commanders Lieutenant General Dodo Iriberri, Lieutenant General Jeffrey Delgado, Vice Admiral Jess Millan, descendants of Gat Apolinario Mabini, fellow workers of government, honored guests, my beloved countrymen, good morning to all of you.

I am happy that I made it. Earlier, as we got closer and closer, the view for our helicopter pilots kept disappearing. I said I hoped… then the wiper started and you know, before our helicopters, there are more sound effects than water repelling. When we landed, at least we made it here and we can fulfill our duty.

On this day, we celebrate the 150th year since the birth of Gat Apolinario Mabini; it is fitting that we remember his contributions to our country. Mabini represents the intelligence of the Filipino—intelligence that he poured into forging principles and systems that shaped a free society.

Gat Apolinario Mabini offered three important ideas, which we continue to strive to live by even today.

First, Mabini emphasized the importance of a mandate as the foundation of any government. The power of the state comes from the people. Under the leadership of Apolinario Mabini, the first local elections in our country were held in August 1898. He also led the organization of the first legislature of the Philippines in Malolos in September of that year, which then elected Aguinaldo in January 1899, to replace the revolutionary government that declared independence in Kawit on June 12, 1898.

Second, Mabini also planted the concept of public service in our national consciousness. His belief: If power springs from the people, it should only be used for their benefit. [1] Mabini truly lived this out after the revolution against the Spanish. The newly born nation was immediately met with another war, this time against the Americans. The legislature at that time asked: Where will we get the money to fund this war? Some members of Congress suggested: Since we have the means, we will just lend the government money. Mabini did not agree with this. Is it right that those who decide to borrow money for the government are also the ones who will earn the interest? Isn't that a conflict of interest? Mabini insisted: No one has the right to profit from their position in government.

Third, the idea offered to us by Mabini: Public institutions were built for the benefit of the people; therefore, the government systems themselves should not hinder the delivery of benefits. Before the Malolos Congress, Mabini emphasized the need for the executive branch to be quick, agile, and courageous, because it is its job to address the needs of the people as soon as possible. He also appealed to the legislature at that time: Let's cooperate, and let's not tie the hands of the executive branch, so that the opportunity to accelerate the improvement of the living standards of the citizens does not pass us by.

In remembering history, we see how Mabini's ideas can be applied to our time today. In recent weeks, the discourse has been heated due to the Supreme Court's decision regarding our Disbursement Acceleration Program.

Let me clarify again: The point of the DAP is to bring benefits to the people in the fastest and most appropriate way. We are maximizing the national treasury to immediately alleviate the suffering of Filipinos.

In implementing the DAP, many projects were funded that provided or continue to provide benefits to the people, although the DAP is now finished. For example, in TESDA: Because of the DAP, we were able to allocate 1.6 billion pesos which was used to train 223,615 graduates. This means that the state invested approximately 7,155 pesos, on average, for each student. Of this amount, 66 percent of our graduates, or the equivalent of 146,731 Filipinos, are now employed. The remaining 34 percent are also being helped and continue to be helped by TESDA to find their respective jobs. All of them, their names, addresses, and phone numbers are listed; it is open to everyone and anyone can inspect it.

The benefit of the DREAM-LiDAR project under Project NOAH, which we also funded with the help of the DAP, is also clear. Now, we get accurate and reliable information about rising water levels during storms. At least six hours of warning is given for impending disasters, which provides sufficient time for efficient LGUs like that of Governor Joey Salceda of Albay to evacuate his constituents from the path of the storm. In Albay, which he governs, no fatalities were recorded during the recent typhoon Glenda.

In the tourism sector: In the construction of 202 roads leading to priority destinations in the country, 66 of them were funded with 5 billion pesos from the DAP in 2012. These roads will deliver—and some of them are already delivering—comfort to tourists. And with their arrival, our fellow citizens who depend on the tourism sector directly benefit.

And to be honest, the decision to implement the DAP was not haphazard. It wasn't made up or invented; it was based on existing laws, primarily the Administrative Code of 1987, which we believe is consistent with our Constitution. Furthermore, the validity of the DAP was reinforced because the Supreme Court itself agreed with this kind of mechanism.

Perhaps you're asking: Why? Here are some examples: In July 2012, the Supreme Court had savings amounting to 1.865 billion pesos. This was allocated to increase the funds for the construction of the Manila Hall of Justice. In fact, in August of that year, they already held a groundbreaking ceremony for the new building. The problem, however, was that the item in our budget for this project fell under the Department of Justice. Clearly: The judiciary's money was allocated for a project that the executive branch should have been undertaking.

Continuing with this, here's our second example. In the same year, 2012, the DOJ had a budget of 100 million pesos for the Manila Hall of Justice. We received a letter from the Supreme Court containing a request. Let's read a portion of their resolution directly:

“Wherefore, the court hereby requests the Department of Budget and Management to approve the transfer of the amount of One Hundred Million Pesos which was included in the Department of Justice – Justice Infrastructure Program for Fiscal Year 2012 budget for the Manila Hall of Justice to the budget of the judiciary, subject to existing policies and procedures, to be used for the construction of the Malabon Hall of Justice.”

They were requesting the transfer of funds to build buildings to house the courts. We see nothing wrong with this, because we know it would expedite the justice system in the country. They withdrew their request in December 2013, when the issue surrounding the DAP was becoming heated. But these examples clearly demonstrate an attempt to carry out what they call a cross-border transfer, or the allocation of funds from one branch of government to another.

If you'll notice: In these examples, the executive branch did not offer. We did not seek to interfere with the power or function of other branches of government; we did not ask for anything in return. The only question for us was: Would these benefit the people? If they would indeed help, why deny the opportunity to expedite the delivery of services to our Bosses?

I would just like to emphasize: When we started the DAP, Sections 38, 39, and 49 of our Administrative Code were already in place. The procedures we implemented had also been used by previous administrations. And because the Supreme Court—which has the capacity and the sole mandate to interpret the laws—also did this, our belief that our actions were correct was further strengthened.

In this issue, it seems that some concepts obscure the true meaning. First of all, the matter of “good faith.” It is true that, ordinarily, good faith means acting with pure intent. But it has an additional meaning in the context of law, and that meaning, or that addition, is: If your actions, or what you have done, are based on the law—then you acted in good faith. Let's add this: Even if the law on which you based your actions is later repealed, you cannot say that on the day the law was repealed, you acted in bad faith.

It seems the Supreme Court's decision is changing the policy and this understanding of good faith, and its implications for our work in governance are extensive. We all know: Every member of the executive branch needs to make decisions. However, as Abraham Lincoln said, “You can please some of the people some of the time; all of the people some of the time; some of the people all of the time; but you can never please all the people all the time.” Naturally, there will sometimes be those who question our actions. In the system that exists in our country, if someone accuses another of wrongdoing, the accuser has the obligation to prove that wrongdoing. If you did your job according to the law, and while following current practices and policies, you have nothing to fear. With this Supreme Court decision, every time the decision of our personnel is questioned, they now have to prove their innocence. For example, in bidding: Of course there is bidding, only one can win; of course many lose, and they will look for loopholes to sue our personnel. The poor decision-maker will be sued and now needs to defend themselves. How can you work if you are constantly dealing with lawsuits?

Who would pass standards that haven't even been set? How can you act now, if with every move you make, you're thinking about going to jail because you are now presumed guilty until proven innocent? Now, our personnel will be guessing if tomorrow, what they did right today will be considered wrong. The executive branch will hesitate to decide, and the delivery of benefits to our citizens will slow down. This is where the so-called “chilling effect” on government comes from with this Supreme Court decision.

Let me emphasize: What we call the retroactive effect is what will really give a direct chilling effect because you have a basis that you are using today, if that basis is changed in the coming days, you will be judged based on the new basis; how can you follow something that is still coming?

When it comes to the law, the Supreme Court has the final say. We have no intention of violating their decisions. In fact, we followed not only the main points of the decision; we also acted with consideration for all the opinions contained therein.

However, as Gat Apolinario Mabini said, any power held by any leader, in any branch of government, comes from the people. Therefore, we need to assert actions that benefit our Bosses. That is why we have already filed a motion for reconsideration, containing the arguments I laid out today and on other days. This recourse is embedded in our system: This is an opportunity to ensure that every argument is thoroughly examined, so that decisions that may harm the people can be avoided. This is a reasonable and procedural step. It is good to reiterate all the knowledge, so that the points that the decision-makers may have overlooked can be seen, and the reasonable arguments that will strengthen each side.

Sometimes, I envy those who can focus their attention on a single advocacy. I envy our fellow government personnel, who have a mandate to focus on only one aspect of society. As the father of the nation, I have an obligation to always look at the whole picture, because my decision regarding one sector will affect other sectors. It is also my obligation now to clarify this issue, so that our honorable magistrates are enlightened, because the implications of their decision are great, not only for the executive, or the judiciary, or the legislature, but for the people. It is my obligation to help our Bosses have clarity of mind, and to provide accurate information, so that everyone can see what is right and just. This is how we choose to follow the example left by Apolinario Mabini, who said, “We have one destiny, one joy and one sorrow, and all our aspirations and interests are in harmony.” [2]

Good morning to all of you and thank you very much.
