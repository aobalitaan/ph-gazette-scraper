Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

Upon the opening of the new building of Carmona Elementary School and the launch of the Shared Service Facility for the garment industry of Carmona

[Delivered at Carmona Elementary School, Cavite, on April 1, 2016]

Let me begin here: Earlier, as you saw, we came from, we inspected the building. It looks good. There are still some lacking: the water isn't running yet, they say it will be connected; then the paint on top, especially on the blackboard, I said, “This blackboard seems pale.” They said it isn't finished yet. But Sec. Singson guaranteed us, and when Sec. Singson gives his word, it's true. My only problem with Sec. Singson is that when I call him, he says “Hello, Babes.” The media looks at me, asking who this “babes” is that I'm talking to. [Laughter] I said, it would be difficult if I said “Rogelio, how are you?” That's too formal, we wouldn't understand each other.

After we finished the inspection, I looked at this bridge here on the side. I said, this bridge looks like it leads to a wall. I asked him, “Babes, did you make this too?” He said, “It doesn't seem like I made that.” He said there used to be a garden there, because we don't build bridges that lead nowhere, much less have no one to cross them. [Laughter]

We built 18,000 km of roads in the six years you gave me. What is the value of that? There is a road here in Cavite that connects to Batangas, named the Ternate-Nasugbu Road. It’s long, 6 km. What we have done, to date, is 18,000 km. That doesn’t include the PPP Projects. Now, regarding that 6 km that was started a long time ago.

Why a long time ago? Because that 6 km road took 20 years to build. That’s on record. They started it, saying, “A road is needed. It will bring tourists to Nasugbu for the beaches.” From that concept, from when it was started—we weren’t involved in thinking that up. We finished it, but when it came to us, we were the ones charged. “Why did it take 20 years to build that road?” I said, “We only finished it. We didn’t start it.”

But you think about it: Why did it take so long to build? Let me give you a clue: Of course, those who thought of it had other things in mind. They made sure to maximize their benefits first. That’s why it took 20 years. During those 20 years, let Batman take care of all of us. Isn’t that how it is? [Laughter]

On the Straight Path, I just want to simplify: For a politician, especially during election time, being able to say “I accomplished something” is a big thing. But we—and I repeat this to the Cabinet and to all our colleagues, including our colleagues in Congress—when we do something, maybe we can finish it. What does that mean? The children here in Cavite go to school on double shifts. In other cities, they say it reaches triple shifting. There’s even the most unfortunate: quadruple shifting. Imagine the child, four shifts in grade school. The child really comes home late at night.

So, there is now a proper school. We addressed the backlog in textbooks, chairs, and even the K-12 program, it's all there. How many classrooms will we complete? When I was a congressman, I could secure eight to ten classrooms for my district, which consisted of four to three towns and one city, that was already a big help. With our collective effort here, when I stepped down from office, we had built 185,000 classrooms. [Applause] Let me repeat: we. It wasn't me who built them, it wasn't Babes who built them. You gave me the mandate, we talked, we worked together, and that's the result. We have new schools.

Now, with new schools, if the child's health is poor, their education will be disrupted. We also expanded PhilHealth. The highest coverage they had before was 51 percent. According to PhilHealth, we now have 93 percent covered.

We also included the expanded Pantawid Pamilya program. Previously, it covered around 780,000 families. Here in Cavite, it was zero. Now, we are assisting approximately 50,000 households, if I'm not mistaken. To help parents, we have the new schools, to help your child study and graduate, and to have the ability to earn a living that isn't just cleaning streets. With health, education, and high school graduation. What will they do now? We also expanded TESDA.

TESDA now has 9 million course graduates. I'm not saying 9 million people; some have taken multiple courses. What was the result? Previously, the employment rate within six months of graduation was 28 percent. Now, it's 71.9 percent—I'm rounding off the point one. 72 percent of TESDA graduates now have a job six months after graduation.

So what if you have a job? You all know that some of them, like one, are OFWs. When she arrived in the Middle East, her contract stated: cashier. When she arrived there: she became a domestic helper. She was abused, she escaped, she returned to the Philippines, our embassy helped her, gave her a plane ticket, she returned to Pangasinan. I spoke to her there.

Her story was that she didn't finish her contract. Of course, she left, she borrowed money to fund her trip, she didn't finish the contract. A big problem for her: What will happen to me because of this? How will I pay off the debts? What future awaits me? She was told upon her return that there were scholarship programs from the government, including TESDA. After that, she calmed down, she somewhat forgot what she had gone through. About a year passed, she said, she enrolled, completed two courses, named “hilot wellness” and “massage therapy.” She got a job at a spa, was promoted, became an operations manager, became independent, and established her own spa. Now, this OFW who was once thinking of committing suicide due to despair, has four spas, and has franchised three with us in Tarlac. [Applause]

Since Kim Henares wasn't here either, I asked, “How much do you earn there?” [Laughter] “P60,000 to 80,000 per branch.” Let's say it's P80,000, times four—there are many teachers here—P80,000 times four, P320,000 a month. Is that right? My salary is P120,000. I think I chose the wrong job. [Laughter] You graduate, our ambition is that even with a college degree, you have skills that you can use to get a job.

I'll go to the next one who testified: She said she was a housewife, her husband left her, leaving her with their seven children. Then what does she do for a living? She sells things. Now, I might be misunderstanding, but it seemed to me that selling things: sometimes she sells, sometimes she doesn't; sometimes she has something to sell, someone buys, sometimes she doesn't. It seems like there's no guaranteed income from this. How will she support her seven children, leaving it all up to fate?

She was a recipient of the 4Ps program. And because of the 4Ps—that’s her testimony—she was able to finish the education of three of her children. Her three children are now permanently employed. They are now helping each other, all four of them, to support the four remaining children who are still studying.

Earlier, Ruth—I mentioned her because she was my boss before. Maybe you noticed my hair, I invested in it for this job, it was much thicker when I started. [Laughter] Ruth, my former boss. You know their Shared Service Facility, let me clarify, how much assistance did the government provide for this? Around P5.4 million. Even from the initial estimate—as mentioned earlier, it will be more efficient, fewer rejects, and improved productivity—they estimate that their income will increase by about $6 million. Now, that will go into our economy.

The point is this: You spend one peso, you get a dollar back; 1:1, not 1:46. We assist them, [P5.4 million, and the return will be around $6 or $6.5 million]. However, we assisted them with pesos, and the return to our country is in dollars. And those others who were helped with the old machines, of course, that also adds to their income, and adds to the economy. Our economy is growing, and the government's capacity is increasing to help all of you or fulfill its obligation to serve all of you.

Now, you might ask, “You’ve said a lot. Where are we going with this?” It’s simple: Everything that happened in these six years, we worked on it together, we made this happen. Now, my term is ending: 90 days. What am I now? Retired. Not yet a senior citizen, just retired—to be clear. [Laughter] And when that time comes, of course, I want to look at this as just the beginning of what we can achieve, and not as a vacation, then we go back to the way things were, as our teacher was describing earlier. The student, in the old building. It probably wasn’t built in 1903, the old building he was mentioning, maybe 1904. [Laughter]

But it should have been very simple for me, what is public service? People have a need, it is the government's obligation to help or provide what they need. And the concept: Give to everyone and maximize the opportunity for each person to stand on their own feet and truly shape their future. And that's what we did. That's why I mentioned to you: It's not just school, it's possible. School, work, infrastructure, health, foreign affairs—these are all connected—there is only one focus: How do we uplift the lives of our countrymen?

Now, everything we are doing now, it wasn't just because I came along that it was done. Anyone could have, at the crossroads, chosen the same path we took. If we, when we were in the opposition, the one in power had done what we did, they would have called us “irrelevant.” People were happy with the one in power, why would they pay attention to us? The problem is, people weren't happy. We got in.

It should be very clear: The leader came to a crossroads, chose where to focus, on oneself or on others? The people in 2010 thought: Where are we going? Will we continue the old ways or will we try here, where there might be hope? 42 percent of you trusted me to change the situation in the Philippines, and I believe we have done that. [Applause]

Now, especially when I'm in school, you know, I was in Grade 6 when Martial Law arrived. Of course, my father was imprisoned, for seven years, seven months and more he was imprisoned. He couldn't attend all my graduations—in elementary, he couldn't attend, he was detained; in high school, the same, it was like he had just been sentenced to death by musketry.

When I went to college, during that time, you needed to have an “exit visa.” You are Filipino, you will travel, my father had heart surgery, with my mother, with my siblings, they wanted me to join them there. To leave the country, you needed an exit visa.

Now, they said, the problem is, Mr. Marcos might change his mind. About four or five days before our graduation, my parents had me travel. “Come on, travel now. Don’t march.” I finished [school] but I wasn’t allowed to march. Perhaps, if you were to ask those of my generation, this is something we would all go through, this is what shaped us, especially during the dark times of Martial Law.

To see clearly, what is the difference between right and wrong? Now I believe, we are on the right path. My question now is, they say, “What really drives you?” I said, “How can I explain ‘love your fellow human being’?” Perhaps the simplest and most practical way is: Here at the school for youth. I just ask, who among us here now can say “I hope that youth will go through the same problems I did?” Or how many of us say “I hope the youth that will follow us will face different problems and hopefully smaller ones than what we went through?” [Applause]

I have no wife, no children, I have nephews and nieces. For me, that is a sign of failure: If the problems faced by every generation of Filipinos are just repeated. Now, you have seen what is called the Straight Path. I will go back to that, the date of the election on May ninth, our votes are all the same, even Chairman Naguiat, weighs more than me, his vote is the same, it’s just one vote. [Laughter]

It is important here that we really study this. If we like what is happening, we will say “Continue what you are doing.” But if we are sluggish, those who will return us to the old ways might get lucky. If we are lazy, we are to blame. If it gets better, we are the key. If it gets worse, we have the responsibility. I repeat, we made everything that happened. We will also make everything that will happen. Let us use our power, and let us use our responsibility to really ensure that the future of Filipinos gets better and better.

Good afternoon. Thank you very much.
