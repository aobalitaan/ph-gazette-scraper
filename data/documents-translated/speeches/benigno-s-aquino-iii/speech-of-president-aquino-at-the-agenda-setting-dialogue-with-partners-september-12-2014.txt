Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, on presenting the agenda to partners in reform

[Delivered at the Malacañan Palace on September 12th]

Senate President Franklin Drilon, members of the Senate present; Speaker Feliciano “Sonny” Belmonte and members of the House of Representatives; Executive Secretary Paquito Ochoa, Secretary Mar Roxas, members of the Cabinet; local chief executives; coalition allies; leaders and representatives of various civil society organizations and business groups; fellow workers of the government, honored guests, my beloved countrymen.

Good morning to all of you.

You know, events like these are not often, where the force of reform truly gathers in one hall. It’s almost like a reunion: Many of you have been fighting for what is right and just for a long time.

If you will recall, during the early stages of our history, allegations of corruption came one after another. There was the fertilizer scam, the NBN-ZTE deal, allegations of election fraud, and so many other absurdities. The people had become numb; many had almost given up on their government because it seemed impossible to dismantle the wrong system, especially if those at the top were its promoters. Isn't it true that as the 2010 elections approached, the economy suddenly surged—because there was potential for change in our society?

We were called upon by the people, and we responded—and upon assuming office, the weight of our responsibility became even clearer. I remember a joke about my mother when she took office after the dictatorship. It was said that she was tasked with cooking sinigang, and all she found as ingredients were water and salt. I didn't feel too far off when it was our turn to take office; perhaps the ingredients given to us were just increased with calamansi.

But despite this, we strived, we united, and surely those people who can weigh things fairly cannot deny how far we have come. No one can say that our sinigang lacks flavor.

In recent years, problems that seemed impossible to address, we not only addressed them; we even exceeded expectations. For example, the backlog in classrooms. When we came in—you already know this—66,800 needed to be filled; in our initial calculation, only 8,000 could be funded per year. In the six years of our term, that's only 48,000—meaning, if we were able to fill the 66,800 within our term, we would have met our quota and no one would complain. But isn't it true that in just over three years of service, we have already filled it—and we even prioritized filling the backlog of 61.7 million textbooks and 2.5 million chairs?

In infrastructure: So many projects had been pending for a long time; we were the ones who finished them. The Aluling Bridge in Ilocos Sur, conceived in 1978—we opened it last year. The Ternate-Nasugbu Road, started in 1994; now, it is fully passable. Senator Drilon's favorite, the Jalaur River Multi-Purpose Project in Iloilo, which was planned in the year I was born, is now in Phase 2 since 2013. The Skyway Stage 3, promised during the time of the dictator, finally began construction this January. These were envisioned by others, promised by others, and we were the ones who delivered. Is there any congressman or LGU who can say they were left behind in infrastructure? Unless it's those who deliberately turn a blind eye—can anyone say they were ignored by the national government when it comes to building roads, bridges, or projects that will help speed up the flow of products and services in their areas?

Another example: When we started, this Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program, which according to various studies has the potential to end the cycle of poverty, covered only about 800,000 households. Now, it helps more than four million households, and we have expanded it so that beneficiaries can also finish their children's high school education.

The concept of Pantawid Pamilya is simple: incentivize families to keep their youth healthy and in school, so that when they grow old, they will be able to become productive members of our workforce. And the positive impact of this will become even clearer over time. You know, this isn't like instant coffee where you mix it and you immediately and fully taste it. Of course, the child needs to finish their education before they can work and contribute to lifting their family out of poverty. That's why sometimes, we just laugh at those who rain down criticism on this program: If they have any magic, that tomorrow, when you drink this tablet, the youth will automatically grow up full of knowledge and be able to find work, why don't they let us know so we can help? The problem is, they continue to oppose while we are planting, but I am sure that when the time comes to harvest the fruits, they will be the first to take credit. [Applause]

In truth, right now, we are already seeing the initial harvest of our reforms. Opportunities are expanding. Secretary Linda Baldoz of DOLE reported to us: She said that in the past, due to the lack of opportunities here in the Philippines, those who trained to be teachers would travel to other countries to work as domestic helpers. There are now reports that foreigners are having difficulty finding Filipino domestic helpers, because in addition to fewer people leaving the Philippines for that job, there are also domestic helpers abroad who are returning home to continue their careers in teaching.

If you will recall: There was a time when it was very difficult to hold your head high if you were Filipino, as if our branding around the world became corruption, and the waste of potential and opportunity. Isn't it now, that when the Philippines is mentioned, we are proof that good governance leads to a thriving economy? Now, when we speak in other countries, they listen to us to learn how we were able to rise so quickly, while the global economy is complex. Now, we are contributing to the discourse of peace, of the rule of law in the seas, of climate change, and of broad development stemming from good governance.

Here, regarding our peacekeeping contingent in the Golan Heights, as you can see, it is very far from us but we chose to help. We all know that when the conflict in the Middle East escalates, the price of oil is affected, and we will also be affected. We have also been requesting for a long time to be allowed to give or authorize our soldiers there to carry more powerful weapons because the situation has changed, and the number of forces vying for the area we were tasked to guard has increased. Although we were not granted most of our requests, you saw: All Filipino soldiers there, from the newest private to our colonel, showed ability and excellence in their duties. When they were cornered, they made a plan, acted, and were able to save each other from danger. [Applause] Isn't that the same excellence shown by our soldiers in Zamboanga? Of the 197 hostages there, 195 were rescued, and the two who died were killed not during the rescue crossfire, but were killed by the fleeing lawless elements.

These successes, we achieved; why couldn't others? We didn't just do what needed to be done; we went beyond it. We didn't just make the impossible possible; we made successes normal and reliable.

If before, the Filipino was numb and had no desire to participate in shaping society, now our democracy is truly alive. Truly, there is no end to the voices being conveyed to us, because they know we are listening, and they have a government that listens and acts on their good suggestions.

Isn't it also true: When we were being convinced to run, the suggestion was to just start the change, that would be enough. Now, we have really come so far since we started; it is truly far beyond what we initially hoped for. We have created something beautiful together. If our Bosses see that opportunities are expanding, problems are being solved earlier, and we are achieving what we didn't even dare to dream of before, who in their right mind would say that this should be stopped?

It is clear to us that there are already those posturing for the 2016 elections. I am always asked: How can we ensure that the straight path continues?

Now, we are also receiving texts and messages: It is my obligation to find my replacement, as well as prepare him. It is my duty to ensure the continuation of everything we have started. There are also those who say that the surest way to continue on the straight path is for me to continue leading. I cannot ignore the suggestions of people who are open-minded and who genuinely want what is best for others.

Of course, the opinion of those who tirelessly suffer and are affected by the difficulties and dangers of my position has a strong effect on me. Last year, if you will recall: There was a conflict in Zamboanga, an earthquake in Cebu and Bohol, Santi arrived, and Yolanda struck. Someone concerned said: Your problems aren't just one after another, they are piled on top of each other. Almost my whole life, I have been part of many adventures. I was twelve years old when Martial Law was declared; since then, truly, every waking moment, we have been participating in forging ways to solve the problems we face. Of course, on the path we are taking now, the burden of conscience is also on me for all those who are hurt or affected; including all those who set aside their personal dreams, because I called on them to help first with our agenda of transformation.

When I reflect on this, what becomes clearer to me is this: Any decision I make will not come from personal desire. My primary concern is the good of everyone, not just now, but also for all future generations of Filipinos. [Applause] And if that is where we come from, it is also clear that our continuity does not depend on just one person or group.

Therefore, to those who are asking who will continue: Perhaps we are asking the wrong question; perhaps it is not “who” we should be looking for—because there is no other answer to that question but “we all will continue this.” [Applause]

Our mission is to maximize the changes we are making now, so that our Bosses can see that the only path towards our aspirations is to follow the straight and narrow path. Let us further intensify our efforts; let us not be content with what we have achieved; let us ensure that what we have achieved does not disappear, but rather expands and deepens.

Let me outline some issues that we are focusing on and providing solutions for, along with my directives to address them as soon as possible.

First: On the issue of energy. As we mentioned in the SONA, as the economy improves—as more businesses are established and our industries regain vigor—the demand for energy will also increase. El Niño is also a threat, which will surely affect our plants that rely on water to operate. If we do not act now, there is a possibility that our energy supply in Luzon will be insufficient during the coming summer.

Therefore, to ensure that your government has sufficient capacity to address this problem, we are coordinating with the Senate and the House of Representatives for the passage of a Joint Resolution, in accordance with Section 71 of EPIRA, to give us the authority to contract additional generation capacity. At present, we are pushing all schemes to ensure that brownouts are prevented. Our rationale is: Should there be a shortage of electricity, it is better to be certain that we have a source for it, rather than just hoping that we can find one when problems arise. Our order to Secretary Icot Petilla of the DOE: Do everything possible to ensure that we are prepared, so that the feared brownout next summer does not happen.

Second: On the issue of port congestion in Manila. This is accompanied by a multitude of problems: cargo is stuck, the cost of transporting their products by truck is increasing, and there is widespread traffic due to trucks queuing to collect cargo. There are also reports of losses due to imported goods spoiling or being close to spoiling; some businessmen are also thinking about whether to continue importing, because they cannot be sure when it will be released from the port. Let me just remind you: Christmas is approaching, and we expect more cargo to arrive at the Port of Manila.

Our response: Strengthened coordination with the LGUs of Manila and the entire National Capital Region; stronger calls to implement the agreements reached; and more vigorous implementation of measures to decongest our pier, such as imposing a penalty of not less than 5,000 pesos per day starting October 1 for cargoes that have already been cleared by Customs but have been stranded at the port for more than five days. We have tasked Cabinet Secretary Rene Almendras to focus on this issue.

This issue of traffic is also a hot topic, especially for those from Manila. More and more people are able to buy their own vehicles; we are pursuing the construction of sufficient roads, but we know that this is not enough. We also need to improve the public transportation system.

So, for our third issue: We looked at the root of the problem with the MRT. It was built in 1999; according to their plan, every eight years, there should be a general overhaul to ensure the train continues to run smoothly. In 2006, however, those in charge did only a minor overhaul, instead of a general overhaul. What's even more painful is that the data regarding what should have been repaired and what was actually repaired is incomplete or missing; therefore, a new assessment is needed to determine what work needs to be done.

Currently: The process for a comprehensive and systematic rehabilitation of the MRT system is underway. As you know, 48 additional train cars are coming. Each train will be able to pull more cars. Of course, the power system will also be upgraded because these cars will consume more electricity. A new signaling system will also be installed to allow trains to stop at stations more frequently and for safer travel. This means more people can ride every day and the crowding on the trains will be alleviated. We are doing all of these things simultaneously, along with other needs such as the Automatic Fare Collection System, to reduce the time passengers spend in line; next year, the promise to us is that 50 percent of it will be operational in the Automatic Fare Collection System. We will also expand the queues so that it is more convenient to line up if needed, and we will repair the rails to make travel safer.

I, along with you, wish this had been done a long time ago. But there were no plans to abandon it, so we made more studies. Of course, we needed to find and gather the funds for this. All of this goes through the proper legal process; just the procurement alone, as you know, will take four to six months. I hope everyone understands that it takes time to implement all of this, but once completed, it will provide us with a better public transportation system, and we expect it will also ease traffic congestion because fewer people will choose to drive their own vehicles.

Fourth: Let's talk about the issue of peace and order. You have heard that some personalities wanted by the law have been arrested and are now awaiting trial in jail to face the charges filed against them. In the case of Enzo Pastor, two suspects have been arrested; in the case of Mayor Ernesto Balolong, two of the three suspects have also been arrested; in the murder of hotel chain owner Richard King, three suspects have been arrested, while the remaining one is being hunted. Let's also add: General Jovito Palparan and the Tiamzon couple, leaders of the NPA, are also in custody. The change in the numbers is also evident: In the latest data, the previous 31 cases of homicide per week in the National Capital Region has decreased to 20 per week.

It is sometimes frustrating because we see that when a crime occurs, it makes headlines in the newspaper; when a crime is solved, you have to look for a small report on page 20. It is not an excessive request to ask for balanced reporting on crime, is it?

Because the solutions are not given attention, it is inevitable that some of our countrymen will think that there is chaos in the Philippines. The deliberate spreading of baseless news also adds to the apprehension. You may have received a text message circulating about two weeks ago, saying that a student from a prestigious school was a victim of kidnapping. They weren't even satisfied with that, because after the text message spread, in the following week, another text message was circulated, saying that the kidnapped child had been killed.

You know, if you received that, the text message says the child was kidnapped, killed, the nanny escaped, the driver, and the child could not be found in the first text. In the second, it says the child was killed.

From the very first text, our police immediately acted to determine the root of these reports. The outcome of our investigation and coordination with the said prestigious school: They say there are no missing children in their school.

Yesterday, General Alan Purisima, the PNP Chief, was relieved of his post in a newspaper due to alleged scalawags in his ranks. I just want to ask: Have these scalawags only now appeared, or have there always been scalawags in our service? There are indeed scalawags, but who arrested those who robbed people on EDSA? Also police. Under the leadership of General Purisima, they investigated, pursued, and arrested the perpetrators who are now being prosecuted, fellow police officers. In addition to that, there are various crimes being solved, including high-profile murders. I ask: Is it reasonable to relieve General Purisima from his post when he is leading police officers who are doing their jobs well?

In truth, the image of our police has greatly improved. This is due to the transformation that has happened within their ranks—not only in terms of equipment or caring for our police, but also in the culture that is beginning to prevail in the system. Before, if a suspect in a crime was identified, that was enough. Now, those concerned really don't let go of the case until an arrest is made. If solving a crime used to rely on luck or “hope,” now it is more systematic and scientific in managing where and which crimes to focus on.

That is what Secretary Mar Roxas brought to the PNP: Surveillance based on data and statistics, so the results are more reliable. I heard that there are still those who want to remain in the old haphazard and chance-based system, because they won't be held accountable for their deliverables. My order to Secretary Mar: Deepen knowledge from the data collected, and expand the scope of successful programs. If the results are good, shouldn't we double our efforts to make them even better?

The steps regarding the issues I identified will prove: We are still very focused on the welfare of our Bosses. We will not deviate from the straight and narrow path; our focus is: Not politics, but results; not noise, but concrete responses to the problems of our Bosses; not ambition, but public service and true love for our country. [Applause]

In addition to this, I ask for your reminder to our countrymen of the good things brought about by the straight path. I repeat: We are on the right track and the benefits of the straight path to the people are very clear. It is not difficult to convince the people because they know this. If other beliefs prevail, we can only blame ourselves; perhaps we have been too shy in delivering news to our Bosses; perhaps we ourselves lacked enthusiasm. That is why: If anyone doubts, we ourselves should approach them and explain. If we are bombarded with lies, let us fight them with truth and reason. [Applause] We should be the ones leading the announcement of our successes. Otherwise, the straight path will be drowned out by noise.

To our colleagues here, many of you have really poured your entire career into reform. All of us—partners in the executive branch, allies in Congress, including the responsible opposition, party mates, members of the coalition, colleagues in civil society—are bound not by personality, not by the color of vests in the campaign, not by slogans—but by principle. We have one stance. We are united by our aspirations for the country. There are no tactical alliances here: We are kindred spirits. And at any time, we do not believe that good governance is just politics of addition—that even if you are opposed to your principles, if it advances your ambition, you will side with them.

I know that some of us are already thinking about the 2016 elections. The time will come for us to choose a candidate who will continue our straight path. I hope I am not that candidate. [Laughter]

And of course, those against us will push their own candidates. For them, those who are not with us now because their principles are against ours, isn't it natural for them to oppose everything we've done? They oppose the more than four million households helped by the Pantawid Pamilya program. They oppose the expansion of PhilHealth coverage and the increasing number of illnesses that can be treated there. They oppose the 6.2 million who have completed TESDA courses since we took office. They oppose the almost 2.5 million compatriots we have lifted out of the poverty line. They oppose the more than 12,000 kilometers of roads that have been built or repaired. They oppose the modern equipment and increased care for our uniformed personnel. They oppose the fact that our airlines can now travel to Europe again and have more flights to America. They oppose a DOST that truly has a direct benefit in saving Filipinos from disasters. They oppose our successive improvements in various rankings, due to our rising economy. They oppose the impending peace in Bangsamoro. They oppose everything we have strived for to improve the lives of Filipinos.

If they say they agree with these things and also aim to care for our Bosses, my question to them is: Why weren't you with us? If you agree, why do you choose to oppose what we are fighting for, instead of joining us and contributing to the positive transformation of society?

For those of us here, let me remind you: We didn't reach this point by being like Juan Tamad, just watching the fruit and waiting for it to fall into our mouths. We achieved these successes because we worked hard and rowed in the same direction. My position remains firm: The Filipino is worth dying for, worth living for, and definitely worth fighting for. [Applause] I trust that, no matter the deception or posturing, our Bosses will see who is empty and wants to exploit them; they will analyze who is truly ready to fight for them. We just really need to intensify our efforts every day, we need to double our work, we need to emphasize to the people that this straight path, this agenda of reform and honest and good governance, is the only way to continue our journey towards the fulfillment of our dreams.

Perhaps you should make me a fortune teller now. How many will say, “We will continue what you have done?” And I think, only then will we discover why our race is truly great.

Good day. Thank you very much to everyone.
