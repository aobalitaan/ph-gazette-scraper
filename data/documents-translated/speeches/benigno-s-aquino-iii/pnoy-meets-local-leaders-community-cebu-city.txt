Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

In his meeting with local leaders and communities of Cebu

[Declared at the Cebu Provincial Capitol grounds, Cebu City, on April 27, 2016]

Comrades, those raising their hands are single? [Laughter] Those who are not single, let's not raise our hands, okay? [Laughter] You know, our term is almost over, only 64 days left. After that, I will retire from politics. And it feels like I'm revisiting the life I've lived. You know, when Martial Law was proclaimed, my father was the first to be arrested, I was 12 years old. When he was assassinated in 1983, the first place I went to in the entire Philippines to speak, was here in Cebu. He was killed in August, and I was here in September.

Let me tell you a story about the situation back then. As you know, my father, while on a plane, was brought down the stairs, he never stepped on Philippine soil again, he was assassinated. I, upon arriving in Cebu, on PAL, they opened the old one at the bottom, the stairs where people disembarked. So at the bottom, there were the members of AVSECOM, weren't there many who were sentenced, imprisoned because of their involvement in the assassination of my father. So the PAL crew looked down, then they said, now they saw the AVSECOM. They said to me, “Sir, goodluck to you.” [Laughter]

And to be honest, when I came home, of course, I had to take care of my father, we had to be there to pray for him so he could be buried. And as the only son, you can't take away from me that I have a fiery spirit. They did so much to my father and truly, in my heart, I wanted revenge. But we all saw those who sympathized, who condoled with us, and I saw that there was still a possibility for the peaceful change he had longed for.

So this is it, that's where I saw it, when I came here to Cebu, the first time I spoke, you know, I really felt it here in Cebu, it was as if Martial Law was already over during that time. It was as if Cebu was already liberated and it was a sign that we were truly heading towards freedom. I repeat, the warmth of your welcome to us then, your solidarity during the time my father was slain, until today, you are still here, so truly: thank you very much to all of you. [Applause]

In all the places we visit, I'm already saying goodbye, we're looking back. What have we gained from the Straight Path? And I've said here in Cebu several times what we've gained. But let me give just a few examples. When I was a congressman, the budget for a school room, a classroom, could support 8,000 to be divided among the entire Philippines. I repeat: 8,000—the entire Philippines would divide it. So if you were a congressman, you could bring home eight classrooms, that was already good. If you could bring home ten, well, you were good. If you could bring home a dozen, you were really great. Now, the problem was, my district: 159 barangays, we couldn't bring a school to each barangay after my term ended. After my 9 years as a congressman, only 72 classrooms were built, less than half.

Under the Straight Path, because you gave me, us a mandate and you didn't leave us, in the province of Cebu alone: we have built 7,900 classrooms—built and currently being built. This is just part of the 185,000 classrooms that will be completed throughout the Philippines. [Applause]

On the roads: Cebu was not neglected. Let me just look at my notes. From 2005 to 2010, the funds allocated by the National Government for Cebu's infrastructure were P12.6 billion. Under the Straight Path, from 2011 to 2016: P34.8 billion, almost three times the amount we brought here. [Applause] You are witnesses to the PPP projects like the Cebu-Mactan airport, where we not only get a better airport but were also paid, given a premium of P14.4 billion to give us the infrastructure we need. Of course, there is also the one in Bantayan, there is a budget of P10 billion for that, under procurement and construction of a new passenger terminal.

The very first Bus Rapid Transit System in the entire Philippines will be built here in Cebu. [Applause] I remember, you know, the DTI told me that car sales have been increasing by 26 percent every year for the past 2 or 3 years. So in 2014 and 2015 alone, car sales exceeded 600,000, not to mention the 1.2 million motorcycles sold every year. That is what fills, contributes to the traffic in many places in the Philippines.

Here in Cebu, I remember, there was a discussion, there was a budget allocated, we could have had a flyover built at that chaotic intersection, if you remember, right? Because an intersection is normally like an X, right? Here, your intersection has three branches. So you have a flyover, those going straight don't have to compete at the intersection. The mayor and the congressman had a disagreement, I asked both of them. They went to Malacañang, I said, this will ease the traffic, why don't we do it? By God's grace, it is clear to you that the flyover is not there, I did not manage to get the two of them to agree. Now, it's election time, it's up to you to decide who is right among them. [Applause]

Let's add to that, the roads built here: this Mactan Circumferential Road and others, these are just part of the 18,000 kilometers of paved roads throughout the Philippines. And the length of the list I'm carrying is long, but if I tell you all of that, you might say, “Wait a minute, we are interested in what happened, but what about the future?” And that's what we will talk about now, the future. [Applause]

What kind of situation did we find ourselves in? Let me tell you about the province of Apayao. Apayao has few voters, roughly, a little over 100,000. Now, the problem with that is, when a politician comes along, where do we direct the resources or the public funds? If previously a legislator, like a senator, had PDAF, where do we get a lot of votes? Because they have few votes, they are always forgotten. And when a town like Apayao is forgotten, a province like Apayao, the people there don't see progress in their lives. They easily become convinced by the NPA and other groups that “Nothing will happen to you here, just join us.”

The good news in Apayao now, according to Secretary Babes Singson of the Department of Public Works and Highways, they have reached what is called the physical limitation in their infrastructure needs. What does that mean? Someone we spoke with told us during a rally we attended in their town, that when someone gets sick, they have to go through a forest to reach the hospital. When they pass through that forest, there is no road, so they have to walk carrying the seriously ill patient. As a result, many don't make it to the hospital, they die in the forest, so the forest, they say, is a forest and a cemetery as well. Now, they have a road, and the trip there now takes only a few minutes.

Now, our problem is, we've reached the physical limitation, there were places with no roads, we opened them. When you open them, first, there's no pavement, you need to compact the soil before you put asphalt or concrete on it. It needs two rainy seasons to pass for the soil to really be compressed or compacted. We've paved all the areas that could be paved, but there are still parts that were just opened, so they can't be paved with asphalt or cement yet.

Now, the question is, if someone replaces us, will they go back to the style of saying, “There are few votes there, I don't care about them.” So the roads we started, the rains will come, instead of us paving them with concrete asphalt, right, the rains will come, the rains will come, no one will maintain it, no one will improve it, and in a short time, it will become a forest again. The people of Apayao will go back to saying, “What about us? Aren't we children of God?”

Let's move on to Mindanao: There's a lot of unrest in ARMM, in Lanao del Sur, there are two towns there, the governor, if you visit these towns, his trip, they say: eight hours. Why eight hours? He still has to pass through Bukidnon, Cagayan de Oro City, and many other places. He goes around other places to reach the town under his jurisdiction because before, there were no roads.

That former eight-hour trip, they now have a road, the trip is only an hour and a half now, and it will be one hour once the other parts being finished are concreted. [Applause] What's good about that? The people feel that the government, right, if you were the governor of the province of Lanao del Sur, the trip is eight hours, the return trip is also eight hours, that's sixteen hours. If you sleep for eight hours in one night, that's a whole day. In other words, if you visit that town, you can be sure that the whole day, you won't be doing anything else. So we can't expect their governor to visit often to see the needs. If those people are neglected, that's when these, what are they called, threat groups, the BIFF, the, right, MNLF, maybe the Abu Sayyaf, emerge. “Join us, the government doesn't care about you.”

But I repeat, now, the eight-hour trip, is only an hour and a half. If the governor doesn't visit, that governor has a problem, he looks lazy. But their governor now is diligent and has been able to attend to things. [Applause]

After a long time in the towns, if they weren't attended to, should we be surprised why they don't care about us, if that's how they feel, they also—right, no one loves them and no one cares about them.

On May 9th, we will decide. And for me, I am grateful to all of Mar Roxas' opponents. You will say, “Why?” Because what you did made this election very clear and easy. Why did I say that? Here, Mar, Leni, and their companions are shouting: Continue the Straight Path. [Applause] So on May 9th, that was like a referendum we held for six years. Is it true that we built 185,000 classrooms when previously it was only 8,000 a year? 8,000 a year, 6 years—only 48,000. Is it true that the previous 47 to 51 million members of PhilHealth, we have now made it 93 million? [Applause] Is it true that the BPO industry here in Cebu, previously contributed $300 million to Cebu. Now, it contributes over $900 million to Cebu and we have done many other things. You all know that. Let me repeat, this wasn't done by Noynoy Aquino, we did it. [Applause]

Now, why am I thankful to the opponents? Some are promising jobs, right? “If I sit, millions of jobs every year.” Mar Roxas, has already done that. [Applause] Why did I say that? Let's go back to the BPO. We need to find something suitable for the Philippines. He started it—young lady, how are you? Everything being discussed here, is for you. Because I am retiring, he is just starting his future.

Let's repeat: BPO, Mar found it, this BPO looks good, this BPO seems suitable. This year, the direct employees in the BPO Industry in the Philippines: 1.3 million people. That's the million we're talking about. The contribution to the Philippine economy: $25 billion dollars. Okay, those who promise—just inviting anyone, Mar has already invited those in the BPO and it's getting bigger and bigger. [Applause]

What else is our potential there? I spoke to someone, just as I was starting to sit as your President, their employees were approximately 15,000—10,000 to 15,000 when we first started. So I told him, I pleaded, “You know what’s most important to us, creating jobs. Please help us. Maybe you can make it 30,000 before I sit, before I step down from office.” He said, “We will try our best.” The last time I spoke to him, the one I asked for 30,000 workers, they now have 60,000 employees. [Applause] But the most important thing he told me, to be honest, this is in Manila, give me a building anywhere on EDSA, I will fill it. The previous 30,000, he wasn't sure; now, he has 60,000. He can still fill even a new building because they are very short on space. [Applause]

So let me add, others say, fix peace and order. Mar thought of programs of the DILG and the PNP like One Time, Big Time. What is One Time, Big Time? It seems like a lottery, right? It's not like that. One Time, Big Time: All those being sought in a place, really big criminals, most wanted, when the police try to arrest them, one time. All search warrants will be served simultaneously. What does that mean? The others won't have time to discover, “Hey, the police are hot on our trail. Let's hide.” Before they hear that they are all being hunted, those hunting them are already there. Result: More than 2,000 of the most wanted have been registered. And because the masterminds are gone, the small-time operators don't know what to do, so our crime rate has dropped significantly throughout the country. Mar did it, and didn't just promise. [Applause]

Now, everything we've done, everything that came before me, could have been done. The growth of our economy at 6.2 percent on average, for every year, the last time we had this kind of economic growth: it was in the 1970s. And even then, the numbers were a bit manipulated, it was Martial Law then. This is our biggest growth spurt.

Responding to the roads in Apayao, responding to the roads in Lanao, everything we're doing in the Philippines, let me repeat, could have been done before us. The first rule, well, don't steal from the public treasury. Is that right? [Applause] Let's thank Mar Roxas' opponent, to make the difference clear. One of them, in 2014 said there was corruption, and now we are in 2016, right? 12 days to go, we will vote. And like me, I think you are also waiting for the answer: Did he really steal or not? You can't always say, “It's just politics.” A case has been filed, he has two years to answer us, and he hasn't answered until now. Last 12 days to go, most likely when he sits, when he takes office, we will even less know the answer to our question, right? [Applause]

Okay, let me add. It's the anniversary today of the EDSA Revolution. 30 years ago, we had the EDSA Revolution. We said we would stop the stealing. We would stop the dictator, right? So sometimes, I wonder, are the surveys correct? When it's the 30th Anniversary of EDSA, the former leading Vice President said they didn't—right, he's willing to apologize if he only knew what he should apologize for, right? That means we didn't do anything wrong. And if we didn't do anything wrong, then that's right. And if that's right, he will continue it. Are the surveys correct that he is leading?

Well, the other one, and please excuse me, but I'm just asking. The other opponent of Mar Roxas, really, when we hear him, when we watch him speak, it's really surprising. Is that right? I don't know, maybe that's just what I see in the media, it seems like with every word he utters, he's fighting with someone new. [Laughter] Is that right? Is there anyone here who has fought with someone and gained something good from it? And this—you know, when you ask, “How is your health?” Of course, the voter will say, “I will help you, but you might disappear from the scene. What a waste of my help.” You have the right to know. And when he becomes President, that's in our Constitution, the cabinet needs to know the President's health. Because during Martial Law, let's not forget, we were kept in the dark about the serious state of Mr. Marcos' health. Is that right? That's why it's in the Constitution.

Now, at the start of the campaign, straight to the hospital, “What happened?” “Why should I tell you.” Is that right? Let me repeat, isn't it like at the beginning, he told us, give me three to six months, I will finish the problem of criminality. No! It changed, he's listening now. It's no longer three to six months now, he will just suppress it. [Laughter] That's not okay either.

But you know, I believe, it doesn't matter if you have a new promise. In my opinion, we've already done that, right? From 1972 to 1986, that system was implemented in the Philippines. And here in Cebu, you went out and said, “Enough, it's too much, let's change.” In Manila it was the same, in Davao it was done too—come to think of it, they said the same thing in Davao. And now, on the 30th anniversary of EDSA, the one leading in the surveys, is the one who seems to be bringing back that style. I, my perspective is the only right one, everyone who doesn't follow me, be prepared. [Applause]

Now, let me add, I'll add to that. You know, many people are telling me, “This is scary, do something about it.” It's like I want to answer them, you know, after 64 days, I'll be an ordinary citizen. Are you expecting me that if this passes and he abuses his power, I will lead the fight again? I think, if they see him abusing his power, I will stand up again. But the question is, I don't know if we can be sure I'll still be around, if I'll still be in a position to act. Can you pass on the responsibility to me to lead because I think, if others think, “This is the one who can fight for us.” I might be the first to disappear, is that right?

I will return there, I can do it—it’s possible, when I step down from office, the PSG will be gone, all the plans of whoever it may be will be carried out, that’s up to them, right? But can you defeat us? I believe, no. I was told to lean on you, my parents taught me, the people are truly the strongest foundation to rely on. [Applause]

Someone might be planning something against me. Or fine, maybe you will succeed. That’s okay with me too. I think I’ve already contributed to improving our society. [Applause] But I will not accept that the entire Philippines will suffer because if anyone is thinking that, you are crazy, you are mistaken, and if you anger the people, you will feel the power of the people. [Applause]

So earlier, the child I greeted here… Thank you as well. You know, it would have been nice to invite you all to my wedding. [Laughter] But it would be difficult to feed you all. [Laughter]

But you know, honestly, this might be the easiest way for me to explain to you how the future will be. When my mother was still alive, during that time, I had a girlfriend and she was her favorite. Because she was her favorite, since my mother doesn’t interfere in love life, but because she was her favorite, she asked me to buy fabric and of course, wedding white. We didn’t end up together. They say now, the dressmaker, it’s a good thing our color was yellow, because the old white is turning yellowish now. [Applause] It might still be usable. The problem is, even my mother is no longer here, she’s gone—that’s just life.

Let me repeat: Martial Law, I was 12 years old. After Martial Law, I was 26. It feels like I'm already 56 years old now. For 44 years of my life, in one way or another, I've been involved in national discussions, so I'm thinking, what will my life be like after July 1 when I no longer have to be interested in the politics of the nation?

Of course, I hope that when I retire, there will truly be someone to continue what is happening. What does 'continue' mean? All the politicians we've heard since the time of Muhammad, right, say, “Vote for me, I will improve your lives!” Right? Or now, what's the difference?

I recounted during my last visit here in Cebu, I came from Negros Occidental, landing there now, Silay going to Bacolod, you'll pass through Talisay. In Talisay, we arrived at night, the road was beautiful, a real highway. It's like an expressway in Luzon. Even the lights, the design was correct, the brightness was right. In the morning, of course, that was the return route back to the airport.

There are two large lands being developed: Ayala and Megaworld. According to the mayor of Talisay, this is being done in Talisay, in Negros Occidental. Because of the interest they showed, the price of land in the surrounding area has increased. Because the price increased, the taxes were raised, the so-called Real Property Tax collection increased. What was the result? They were able to help a lot with the Special Education Fund. Where did it go? They bought their own bus, they gave additional allowances to their teachers going to remote areas. They feed students from kindergarten to grade 3 because they are the easiest victims of malnutrition. They bought their own IT equipment, added to the students' equipment and many other things.

Repeat: Because there's a new road, seen as developable by two companies, only the fence has been built, no excavations for the foundation yet, no buildings yet, the community of Talisay is already benefiting. That was our former state of being down, we stood up, we are now walking, and next we will be running.

What will they be running towards? They bought it because buildings will be built, right? They will build a factory, they will build an office. In short, it adds jobs for our countrymen in Talisay and surrounding areas that previously had no hope. Now, there is hope.

The government, of course, helped prepare and empower everyone who wants to take advantage of this opportunity. We didn't build a classroom just to have something to show as a monument, a classroom. No. We built the classroom so that the youth would have a proper place to go to study, to train themselves, we are helping with their health, as you all know. We are also helping with their food through the 4Ps. Once they graduate, we've already spoken with the industries, what kind of workers do you need so they have jobs to enter. That's the approach. We are planting now, and we are already picking some fruits, but most of the fruits are still coming, if we continue the Straight Path. [Applause]

You know, there's no guarantee of that. If the one who leads us goes in a different direction, they say maybe a left turn, a right turn, the worst, a U-turn. [Applause] You know, a U-turn, right? Here in Cebu, you are witnesses, the expensive streetlights. [Applause] Is that right? That's the U-turn. Why would we allow an opportunity to have that happen again?

Now, let me repeat: Let us think, and more importantly, let us reach out to everyone who is not here, let us talk to them and simply ask—why is the person you want to vote for qualified to be President of the Philippines? Why is your preferred candidate qualified to be Vice President of the Philippines? If there is no proper answer, perhaps we can say, “Wait a moment, our candidate is qualified.” One who doesn’t just make promises, but has already delivered. One who has already done things in the past, will do even better in the future. [Applause] And in my opinion, the truly qualified ones are the Daang Matuwid tandem, let me present them to you now: Mar Roxas, our next President of the Philippines, together with Leni Robredo. [Applause]

And I will now bid you farewell. My mother said, “You are not a candidate. Don’t speak for too long. Thank you, thank you all.” [Applause]

Let me repeat, everything we have accomplished—we—we did it. Thank you as well. On May 9th, I only have one vote, I need your help. [Applause]

Here now is our next President of the Republic of the Philippines: Mar Roxas.
