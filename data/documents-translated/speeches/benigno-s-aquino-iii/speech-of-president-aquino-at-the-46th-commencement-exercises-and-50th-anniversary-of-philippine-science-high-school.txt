TRANSCRIPT:

Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, at the 46th Commencement Exercises and 50th Anniversary of the Philippine Science High School

[ Delivered at the Philippine Science High School, Agham Road, Quezon City, on March 26, 2014 ]

Thank you. Please all be seated.

Secretary Mario Montejo; Secretary, Brother Armin Luistro; Secretary Jun Abaya; Senate President Pro Tempore Ralph Recto; Representative Victor Yu; Mayor Herbert “Bistek” Bautista; Dr. Josette Biyo; Ms. Virginia Andres; graduates of the class of 2014 and their families; board of trustees; executive committee; faculty and staff of the Philippine Science High School; fellow workers in government; honored guests; my beloved countrymen:

Good morning to all of you once again.

When I learned that I was scheduled to speak at the graduation here at Pisay, I immediately remembered a friend of mine from our college days. Pisay molded him. It even happened that in one of his Math classes in college, the book used in Pisay was also used there in our professor’s lecture. There was a particular Math problem where the solution shown in the book was incorrect, and my friend discovered this while he was still studying at Pisay. Our teacher and my friend debated, until the end, after filling the entire blackboard in the classroom, my classmate proved he was right, the teacher walked out and returned the next day to apologize. Imagine: With the excellence of Pisay graduates, even a college Math professor was taught by them. That’s why earlier, while the graduates were getting their hair fixed, they were actually teaching me things.

To this day, I haven’t met a weak product of Pisay. Secretary Abaya is a product of yours. Senator Recto, Secretary Jun Abaya, and myself, when we were still in Congress, even in the Senate, there were opportunities for students to approach us for interviews. So, let’s just contrast this school—I won’t mention which school—with college students who approached me. It was about ten minutes before the session was to begin. They said to me, “Can we interview you? We’ve been waiting for you for a while. Please, we need to interview you.” I said, “Can I just do a roll call first; I might be marked absent and I’m already here. Just a roll call, I’ll come out and then we can have the interview.” They said to me, their first question—they said, remember, “We’ve been waiting for you for a while, we need to interview you.” So the first question from these college students was, “What is your name?” I looked at them like this, and I said, “You’ve been waiting for me for a while?” After that, their second question was, “What is your job?” I said, “Goodness gracious!” We met in the Batasan. It says “House of Representatives” there. In other words, their questions were quite lengthy and lacked depth. So when I was already in the Senate, there were students again from this school. When I saw their uniform, they said to me, “We need to interview you.” “I’m sorry, I have a meeting and we can talk later.” I just avoided them.

So, let’s go back to the graduates of Pisay. Secretary Jun, whenever he speaks, what he says is truly grounded in truth; that’s why, I make sure to listen carefully when he makes a statement. There’s also Professor Iye Coronel-Ferrer, the head of our negotiating panel for the peace talks in the Bangsamoro. With her efforts, along with her colleagues, our Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro will be signed very soon, which we expect will bring lasting peace and widespread development to Mindanao. They are just a few good examples of Pisay alumni who have succeeded in the field of public service.

I know: The excellence of Secretary Jun, Professor Iye, and your other alumni comes from the early training here at your school. I remember when I was in Congress, we even studied how to increase the funds allocated to Pisay, to further ensure the depth of knowledge you learn here. But I think everyone understands that the state’s funds need to be balanced.

However, I am aware that studying here is not easy. I know that some of the subjects in Math, Biology, Chemistry, and Physics are at the college level; even in English and Filipino, your students are keeping up. In addition to this are the various pressures felt by a normal high school student. Many of you also did not grow up in a luxurious life—children of tricycle drivers, market vendors, or hardworking farmers who work to help with your expenses. Your success today is something that many people can be proud of who contributed to ensuring that you could graduate: Your families, the teachers and administrators of Pisay, the entire community, and the alumni and benefactors who donate to the Pisay Foundation, as well as the ordinary Filipinos who paid the right taxes and put funds into the national treasury.

Because so many people worked hard to ensure that you get a high-caliber education, we must also ensure that the students of Pisay are not disturbed or disrupted in their studies. I know there are concerns about the reported transfer of your campus. I will tell you myself: Pisay will remain on Agham Road. Mayor Herbert would be the first to object if it were to be moved. Right, many are vying to put a campus in their areas, would he allow the main campus of the Philippine Science High School to be removed from Quezon City? I think not.

Outside of Diliman, our support for the PSHS system continues. Think about it: I was informed that the original plan for the Philippine Science High School System was 16 campuses. When we were elected to office in 2010, after 46 years since you were established in 1964, there were already nine campuses being built. Now, there are 13 Pisay campuses throughout the archipelago. Before we step down from office, Secretary Mario Montejo of DOST’s target is to have Pisay in all 16 of our regions. Hopefully, the original plan for the Philippine Science High School system will also be fulfilled.

Our administration continues to strongly support Pisay, because on the straight and narrow path, we cannot rely on “maybe” or “hopefully we reach our destination” in our journey. If we really want to move forward with the change we are enjoying now, we cannot base projects on speculation; we cannot lack a scientific basis for our plans.

For example, let's take our response to the dangers posed by climate change. We are a country composed of so many islands, and therefore, there are many communities living along the coastlines. These are hit by no less than 20 typhoons that visit us every year. We cannot simply tell our countrymen, “Good luck, hopefully you’ll survive the next typhoon.” Instead, we need skilled meteorologists to provide early and accurate warnings so that communities can be evacuated from the coast. We need accurate predictions of where, and how high and wide, storm surges will hit. We need an effective early warning system so that everyone is immediately informed. All of this depends on experts in science.

Another example: Next year, the Philippines will enter what is called the demographic sweet spot, where the bulk of our population will be of working age. It is estimated that by 2019, we will enter a middle income sweet spot, when the per capita income of our countrymen will reach an average of $6,000 per year. What will be the effect of this? As we progress, and as people’s income and spending increase, more and more will be able to buy various things like vehicles. Currently, around 200,000 vehicles are sold each year; studies suggest that by 2019, this could reach around half a million per year. Even now, plants of automobile manufacturers are being built. Along with the increase in vehicles on the road, the need for infrastructure will also increase. The layered flyovers that vehicles will pass through; public transit systems like subways for commuters; modern buildings that workers will enter—all of this requires experts in science.

Our scientists also play a large role in implementing our agenda of good governance. For example: Among those of you here, I might have a hard time finding a computer illiterate. You are probably already buying your own gadgets or computers. Technical knowledge is needed to buy these items; proper research is needed to ensure that you get your money’s worth. How many of us have experienced a great sales pitch, and been convinced to buy something with so many features that just make it more expensive, but aren’t really needed? As a friend of mine told me: He was wondering why the seat in his car was so warm. It turns out his car had a heater in the Philippines.

This idea can be applied to spending from the national treasury. If we know what we actually need, we can also determine what technology is appropriate to address it. We will only spend on what is necessary; government funds will not be wasted. That is why: We have instructed the DOST to form a group of experts who cannot be fooled by suppliers, especially when it comes to big-ticket items.

Our scientists also play a large role in our fight against corruption. An example of this is the contract for dredging the Pasig River. The contractor said they had dredged. To be sure, we asked the DOST and the experts at UP-National Institute of Geological Sciences: The result of their study was that if any dredging had occurred, it definitely did not follow what was agreed upon in the contract. Now, we are taking steps to ensure that those responsible are held accountable. Sadly, we have very few experts who can analyze what are called borehole sediments. Hopefully, among you, there will be those who follow in their footsteps, so that we can increase the number of our experts.

My question is: Who among you will use your talent to address the problems of our country? Who among you graduates today will find solutions to the problems facing not only the Philippines, but the whole world—challenges in health, the environment, the changing way of life in the world? How many of you will receive awards, scholarships abroad and in our own country; how many of you will become rich and successful because of the knowledge you have learned here at Pisay? And how many of you will choose to repay our Bosses—who will be the next Secretary Jun Abaya or Professor Iye Ferrer? How many will serve, how many will pour their time, sweat, and intelligence to raise the standard of living of our countrymen?

Let this day serve as a reminder: Our country needs you. I hope: In the future, as alumni who will succeed in the field of science, you will stand up for the interests of the majority. And the changes we are enjoying now—this return of integrity to the government, this continued economic progress, this expanding opportunity for our countrymen—is just the beginning of the realization of our lofty dreams for the country. Right now, we are recognized by the world as a model of good governance; if we continue, with your contribution, we will be recognized as an example of widespread development. We all know: The success of a race stems from its ability to discover new knowledge. Progress is aligned not with going with the flow of time, but with being a nest of innovation. Using science—and with the help of every Pisay scholar—the Filipino will lead.

One day, your medals in Math and Science Olympiads will be added to with greater honors for new discoveries. You have been trained in science so that when the time comes for you to lead, decisions that affect our countrymen will surely be based on reason and truth. The entire nation contributed to giving you this opportunity: When the time comes for you to have the opportunity to help your fellow men, will you respond with “Thank you for what you have given, but I will prioritize myself first?” Or will you choose to offer even a short part of your life, to reciprocate the opportunity offered to you by a Pisay education? I hope: When you are faced with this crossroads, you will choose to give back to others, and in doing so help more people be given opportunities, and those who will benefit from your abilities developed by this institution.

I just noticed, Secretary Montejo, that the acoustics in this gym aren't very good. Perhaps you'd like to study it and we can work on it together. I also noticed, and I remembered the teachings of the Jesuits, the best friends of Brother Armin, that a healthy mind is important for a healthy body. I also noticed the four basketball goals I saw here on your campus, none of them have nets. And the soccer field, the nets are full of holes – that, you can charge to me. Just send the bill, and Asec. Delantar and I will take care of it.

Thank you very much. Congratulations to everyone on your graduation today.
