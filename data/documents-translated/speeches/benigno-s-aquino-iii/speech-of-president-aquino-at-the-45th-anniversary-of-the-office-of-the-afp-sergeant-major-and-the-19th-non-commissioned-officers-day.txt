Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, on the 45th Anniversary of the AFP Sergeant Major Office and the 19th Non-Commissioned Officers Day

Good evening to all of you.

When I went to the anniversary of Jesus is Lord recently, Brother Eddie prayed for all of us, and I was a little surprised by the part where he even included my love life [laughter]—I hope that wasn’t an act of desperation. [Laughter] And if you ask me why not yet, of course, he said it’s only after office hours that we can start courting. [Laughter] So when asked, tell them you’re part of the reason, [laughter] because it’s important for me to share this with you.

So, comrades, our leadership, first and foremost the Secretary of National Defense, every time we receive a report that there are encounters, we have casualties, I’m sure SND has lost count of how many times I’ve asked him; Why are there so many casualties? Why aren’t the wounded brought to treatment faster? There are many ongoing reviews on whether these casualties could have been avoided. Is there anything else we can do to reduce the risks faced by our Armed Forces? Not only in encounters, but also when it comes to humanitarian assistance during disasters, in rescues, and in relief efforts.

Truly, I can tell you that there are many reasons why we can say that our Armed Forces are truly commendable.

For instance, the Zamboanga Crisis, wasn’t it a bit sudden? A large force was deployed, and the necessary forces were moved to different locations quickly. We didn’t have any disasters happen. Our air force transport, there are many—many because it’s plural. Before, there was only one. Now, three C-130s. I think some of you have also thought, “These C-130s are good.” How long was our siege in Zamboanga? We didn’t hear our C-130s being grounded. That means the leadership’s plan was really good, and the execution by the personnel was also good. They were being serviced, almost every night—if I remember correctly—to ensure that whatever is needed tomorrow, as far as transport is concerned, is there. So we didn’t lack in bringing equipment, soldiers, weapons, medicine, et cetera, as well as the relief goods we deliver there.

Of course, the success of Zamboanga, let’s go back to that—what was the success? There were so many hostages taken, 195, if I’m not mistaken—197, sorry; 195 were rescued. We have two that we didn’t rescue. Those we didn’t rescue, were already killed by the enemies before we could rescue them. So, what we could have obtained is very—as they say in English—nebulous. The intelligence wasn’t clear, where are they really, how many enemies are there, et cetera, were rescued. That was really intense.

Of course, our frontline forces who engaged with the rogue MNLF played a big part in the rescue. But they didn't do it alone. What's important here, for instance, is the Navy which blockaded to prevent reinforcements, the marines who also guarded the shorelines. Of course, our LRBSRSF [Light Reaction Battallion Scout Ranger Special Forces], everyone involved. Everyone, including the police, local government unit, worked together to achieve this success.

So, why did we revisit this, you see? I just want to emphasize. Perhaps I was told before, “What is the difference between an armed group and a professional armed force?” The difference is, a professional armed force is trained to be a cohesive unit. Perhaps the best example in Filipino is basketball. Five superstars together, where play number one is pass the ball to me, play number two is I will shoot, play number three is I will steal. Wouldn't five superstars achieve anything? But, one, maybe not a superstar, maybe just a star, who come together, can defeat five individuals playing separately.

And that's what was shown, not just in Zamboanga, with the number of—I'm a little hesitant to mention because Christmas is approaching again. Our Christmas is always “exciting.” Some people are saying, “Hopefully this year, our Christmas celebration will push through and we won't be in mourning.” Many will supposedly collect from me, for the three years we didn't have a Christmas party, only rarely.

Anyway, what I also want to emphasize, so let's go back: Casualties, training. Our strategy to raise the level of quality and capability of our Armed Forces. Perhaps there's a simple theme: Those who care for others should also feel the care of the state. Towards that end, as you know, our housing is there, the adjustment of your pension system is there, modernization is there, and many others, including livelihood.

We have already opened three camps for livelihood: Magsaysay, the one in Bukidnon, and in Capiz. Here, there are small scale [operations], and also large scale. For instance, palm oil, it looks like that will be a big industry for us. And the plan here is for those who are active and those who are retired to benefit, with assistance as well. The state is now utilizing this vacant land, but it is important to focus on the welfare of those people who have dedicated a large part of their lives to serving the country. So, we will not just open those [camps], together with agriculture and other government agencies, we will help you on how to turn this into a livelihood, and also with the establishment of cooperatives. [How] to maintain the camaraderie you had while in the military, and even after retirement, hopefully when you become entrepreneurs, it will remain intact. Your cooperative, the steps are the same.

I even sometimes get involved in the training. In one of our dialogues, if SND remembers, I asked about force-on-force training. This means, if there is an encounter, at the beginning, we are the ones initiating the encounter, and we have more casualties. I said, “How did that happen? We were ambushed, yet we were the ones who seemed to be ambushed by our own ambush,” if you recall. And perhaps we should invest more in training equipment. There is what is called miles equipment. Is the miles equipment still serviceable? Okay. We are increasing that so that operations can be battalion size. So, again, to get used to it—practice, right? You probably have already tried the “miles” at the Special Operations Command. We will increase that. But now, I think it’s at the platoon level, when we started it could only support a platoon size. What we are aiming for is battalion size, so that everyone gets used to a larger unit with coordinated action. Anyway, I won’t go into that, I think you are more knowledgeable than me about that.

Of course, we want to take care of the entire citizenry. And we have really increased [increase] many budgets.

Let me start with your modernization funds. I’m not reading the speech, these are just, they call them bullet points. We have already allocated P41.38 billion for modernization. As you have heard from me in my previous speeches, it will go to airplanes, guns, communications equipment, mobility. It’s quite a lot, including force protection. According to our statistics, we now have… 44 projects for the AFP modernization capability upgrade program are being implemented, from July 2010 to September 2014. Forty-four projects have already been completed. They say the three administrations before us had 45 projects since modernization began in 1995. So, we are almost equal to the three previous [administrations], and I still have one year remaining—I’m not counting it completely—one year, eight months, and we have many more in the pipeline.

Now, it's not just your sector. Look at DepEd. In 2010, when we started, Department of Education, the budget was: P161.41 billion. Now it's P281 billion, from P161 to P281. In CHED, they only had P1.67 billion before, now it's P6.94 billion. In TESDA, P2.89 [billion], now it's P5.12 billion.

All of these increases in their budgets did not come from raising taxes. The only tax we raised, perhaps we all have the same vice, is on cigarettes or alcohol, called Sin Tax, and that was really the goal, to discourage or reduce our consumption of what we call vices. So apart from that, we really made sure to collect the correct taxes, and spend them correctly, and that is the most important thing. It's not being pocketed here and there.

I won't tell you that our program is perfect, that we catch all the corrupt, many of the corrupt have been corrupt for a long time. It's just really difficult to correct them, but our efforts to correct them will not stop. So, this correct direction, correct collection of funds, correct spending as well, ensures that we can do more, even if we are not raising taxes.

So, let me go back to the concept earlier, right? You are members of an organization, the goodness of each one and cooperation with the rightness of each one, the empathy, concern for others, really becoming a cluster that is stronger than the sum of its parts. And then in your sector, of course it's a different sector. Let's also include here, when we get to livelihood, why shouldn't TESDA join—to give you additional knowledge. When you retire from the service, you have marketable skills outside. There are many courses you can take. Let Joel Villanueva explain that.

For those who want to learn better farming techniques, establish an agri-business, the Department of Agriculture is there. In short, we help each other, we uplift each other's capabilities and knowledge. We work together to uplift everyone. And because everyone is rising, the whole country rises, and our capabilities become even better.

So, let me repeat: I was also surprised, three administrations before us have been promoting this modernization, and we have almost caught up. They had 45 completed projects, we have 44. But as far as I remember, we allocated more than the first three. And of course, what was left to me is to fulfill their promises. We will fulfill all of that, as much as possible. If we can, we will accelerate it. There are projections that our GDP growth might average, like in the next few years, seven percent. The important thing, they say, is seven years of seven percent growth, so that we can really significantly lift our population out of poverty. Now, some studies tell us that we will start a “sweet spot” in our demographics in 2015. That means, they say, that most of our citizens will be of working age. The opposite of Japan, where they have a shrinking and aging population. We, on the other hand, will become younger. So we are aiming for more gainfully employed people, contributing to the national treasury, and gaining more national capacity to further accelerate the upliftment of the people.

There are also those who say that we might reach the inflection point in 2019. Inflection point, they just say, is when the income of each person increases, GDP per capita, where there is a big difference from what is called purchasing power. They can start thinking about their homes, their vehicles, etc. And of course, all of that comes from stability, our country is peaceful. The investor manages, for example, what is called commercial risk, not that if we invest there, tomorrow we might be attacked by so and so, are there terrorists like that. That is not a concern.

So again, I shouldn't make it too long, excuse me. I didn't really like the speech that was prepared, but these are the points I wanted to emphasize to you.

Now, the most important part is you, the sergeant majors. You are the connectors, you shape the newly appointed officials. You are the ones who pass on knowledge to those younger than you. If you focus on—if you're like me, I'm counting down a bit, I have one year left, two years left. Then I can be content with March-time march, where will we reach if it's March-time march? We can do a forward march. We can help ensure that those who come after us benefit from our mistakes and don't repeat them. We can act as a father and guide. But that comes back to choice, right? That's why I always say, “crossroads”: it can be for oneself, or for many. I believe that if you are self-serving, you divide things into smaller and smaller pieces. It's like a Shakey’s pizza pie. But we can work together to make the pie bigger, so more people can benefit. And perhaps, you were chosen as sergeant major because you truly have the qualities to be a guide, to be a father or mother to those who come after you—so that, in a way, the next ones will start at a higher level. And if they follow in your footsteps, of course, the next ones will be even higher. And if we do all that, we can look back together and say, “When I leave this institution, I’m leaving it much better than I found it.” Now, if we can say the opposite, that I’m leaving this institution much worse than I found it, of course, when we look in the mirror, we realize we were probably part of that. We can be part of improving the institution. That's what we need to choose. I believe that the trust of the people will always lead you to choose what is right.

So, my speech was long, I apologize. I think we can now eat and just relax. Let's enjoy. [Laughter]

Earlier, I went to an organization. They were saying, we added this many employees, we added this much income—all the news was good. But while I was looking at all of them, their faces were so sad. [Laughter] So that was the first thing I asked, “What makes you sad when the news seems so good?” It turned out that someone came before me, Secretary Petilla, who mentioned that we might have a shortage of electricity next year. [Laughter] And Secretary Balisacan was talking about how the world economy hasn't fully recovered.

Let me repeat my philosophy: we only pass through this life once. Then, if we haven't contributed anything when God says, “Finished or not finished, pass your paper,” and you haven't contributed anything, how would you feel? Versus being able to say that I gave everything I could. And if that's our attitude, wouldn't the place we want to reach not be far away? I think it will speed up and speed up our journey there. And again, as the economy improves, our rebels are decreasing, there's no reason to rebel. As we correct the system of society, you have true justice, why would you go to the mountains? As long as we are doing the right thing, why wouldn't neighboring countries and other countries around the world side with us? So, all of this isn't a miracle. It will happen with God's help, but God expects a part from us, and I believe you will all do it.

So, good evening again to all of you, and I hope you enjoy what Susan has prepared.
