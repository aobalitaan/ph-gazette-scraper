Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

In launching the Community List and Filipino Family List

[Declared at the Royce Hotel and Casino, Pampanga, on April 25, 2016]

Our commitment to the Straight Path: To leave a Philippines that is better than the one we inherited. Part of this is ensuring that no one is left behind in progress. During our term, we restored a government that cares and focuses on the people. This was only possible because you gave us a mandate, and we contributed to real change. To all of you: Thank you very much. [Applause]

Today is proof of how far we can reach when we move in one direction. We all know: About 20 typhoons enter the country every year. When typhoons strike and there is severe flooding, many are affected and livelihoods are damaged.

A big difference can be made if the citizenry is prepared. That is why we launched the Oplan Listo program in 2014, under the leadership of former DILG Secretary Mar Roxas, [applause] a few months after we experienced Typhoon Yolanda—the strongest typhoon on record. You know, before Yolanda, our concept was that our Local Government Units would be the first to respond to any disaster, because they were there in the area. This Oplan Listo will further deepen the cooperation between the national government and LGUs, but in the end, the citizenry, especially the well-organized members of 4Ps, will truly be a strong partner when we respond to any dangerous events such as typhoons.

Part of this program is the distribution of Disaster Preparedness Manuals—they are inside the buckets—which provide guidance on what to do before, during, and after a disaster, as well as the formation of Listo Teams in each city and municipality. With the implementation of Oplan Listo, our local governments became more prepared, as when Typhoon Chedeng struck in 2015, where we had zero casualties in the affected areas in Region 2 and Region 3, including Pampanga, despite raising storm signals in the provinces here. I repeat: Zero casualties because of the good cooperation through Oplan Listo. [Applause]

Today, under the leadership of Secretary Mel Sarmiento, we are launching two new components of Oplan Listo: Listong Pamayanan (Prepared Communities) and Listong Pamilyang Pilipino (Prepared Filipino Families). This aims to make our communities and families active partners in preparing for any disaster.

With Listong Pamayanan, we are conducting dialogues with various stakeholders, and community simulation drills for tsunamis and storm surges. There is also Community-Based Disaster Risk Reduction and Management, which focuses on our barangays, so they know how and what to do during a calamity.

For the Filipino Family List, we are working with the DSWD in distributing the “Guides and Maps,” which state what to do during storms. Earlier, we witnessed the distribution of E-Buckets, or Emergency Buckets. These E-Buckets contain ready-to-eat food, three gallons of water, medicine, first aid and hygiene kits, and clothing, as well as a flashlight and radio. Still under the Filipino Family List, families are guided in creating a household plan, which will identify safe areas in case immediate evacuation is needed.

We cannot control the weather. But that doesn't mean we won't do anything to keep our countrymen away from the dangers caused by any disaster. Safety starts with the right knowledge and preparedness, and this is what we are doing under our program.

We are aware of the possible damage from flooding: Here in Pampanga, for example, agriculture is your main industry; so when heavy rains come, your crops and your livelihood are affected. That is why, from 2011 to 2016, we have already allocated almost P6 billion for high-impact flood projects in your province. We aspire to: Not just immediate relief, but also to mitigate the damage from flooding and keep our Bosses out of danger.

Oplan Listo is just one part of our programs to address the problems caused by severe disasters. There’s also Project NOAH and our strengthened PAGASA, so that we can really prepare for incoming typhoons.

You know, when we were just starting our term, a storm visited us. At that time, we were still living in Times, we hadn't yet moved to Malacañang. And I was surprised, because PAGASA told us then that this storm was going to Aurora. But our house in Times is in Quezon City. In Quezon City, the wind was really whistling strongly. I said, “This storm is really widespread. From Aurora to Quezon City.”

It turned out, PAGASA was wrong. The storm went through Southern Tagalog, Metro Manila, and far from Aurora. In Aurora, it was very sunny on that day. We asked PAGASA how it happened and why their forecast was so far off. Anyway, the answer was unclear, the former head had already said goodbye, he’s now in another country, he can no longer bother us. PAGASA is good now, let's make sure PAGASA improves even further. [Applause] As they say, “Now we have hope in PAGASA.”

We created maps identifying areas frequently flooded or prone to landslides. Now also, thanks to the improved technology and equipment of PAGASA, under Project NOAH, we can already give warnings to communities, PAGASA should provide warnings of at least six hours whenever a storm is approaching.

We are also currently implementing the Pre-Disaster Risk Assessment, which strengthens the coordination of various agencies, to make the entire government's response to affected areas faster and more effective. We are also continuously implementing the Flood Management Master Plan for Metro Manila, Rizal, Laguna, and Bulacan, which we have identified as highly urbanized and flood-prone areas in the country. Of course, we are also addressing the long-term solutions for all other areas affected by floods.

Through preparedness, we avoid danger, instead of spending time and funds resolving the disruption caused by disaster. Why wait to suffer before acting, if it can be avoided?

We have come so far. You yourselves are witnesses to the positive results of our journey along the Straight Path. From economic growth, infrastructure development, to social services, we are focusing on the sectors in need. The question is: Did anyone ever think we could achieve all of this?

We also know: Despite our successes, we still have much to address. And these efforts of ours are at risk of disappearing, especially if the next administration mismanages things.

On May 9th, we will once again face a crossroads. It is very important that the next leader focuses on your welfare and is willing to prioritize the nation before self-interest. My only request: Let us examine those presenting themselves to lead the country. Let us choose someone with integrity and genuine leadership ability. Those I see who will truly continue everything we have started: Are none other than Mar Roxas and Leni Robredo. [Applause]

Just 66 days left before I step down from office. Until the very end, what I can say is: I remained true to you. It is a great honor for my administration to serve the Filipino people. From the beginning until now, it has always been: You are my strength; you are my Boss. You made all the changes happen. [Applause] And I trust that you will continue this.

Just one last thing: You know, earlier, we heard, “I hope the 4Ps program continues.” We also came from Cebu, and an elder there said the same thing. I’m used to it, I’ve been a politician for a long time, I’m used to when you approach me, you have a need. Before, the trend in our area was folders, folders, “Oh, we have one.”

What’s amusing is this: Earlier, it was said 4Ps; in Cebu, my sister Kris interviewed someone, “What do you want to say?” What did they want to convey to me. “Just continue the 4Ps.”

Why am I emphasizing this? When I was a congressman in Tarlac, there was also a similar problem, no source of water. So we installed a jetmatic pump, we had it dug, we found water. In the next election, we campaigned again, and I passed by one of those we helped get water. The person I spoke to said, “This is what you brought before.” “Oh, is that so? How is it?” “It’s broken. Maybe you want to have it repaired.” [Laughter] This is true. We were the ones who dug, looking for water, put in the jetmatic pump, everything needed so that sitio would have a source. “Okay,” I said, “why don’t we have it fixed.” I asked how much it would cost to repair. It was probably less than P100, because only the sole was broken. [Laughter]

Back then, that was how it was. You benefit from this, it's for you, you were already helped significantly, with the maintenance, it's ensured to be in good order, you can handle that. That's how it was then. Now, you can. I am very happy that even the thinking of why we are always waiting for help from someone, if we can do it ourselves, is changing.

Our perspective is that you are partners. We work together on everything we can achieve; it's not about someone giving orders and someone following them. It's not like that. You are our Boss, to be honest, you are the ones who can give orders to us. So in all the programs we pursued, that's always the focus: How do we get simultaneous progress if we don't have what we call consensus—agreement. Agreement will come if we partner. If there's a chief and there are subordinates, we won't understand each other.

Let me repeat: I pray for what it was like before, when we felt incapable, that now has so much capability, that we are truly holding the future in our hands, I hope that really continues. In 66 days, I will no longer be here in the scene, maybe we will already be retired. You will continue. I will be an ordinary citizen then. I will do my responsibility. I cannot change society alone.

Thank you very much.
