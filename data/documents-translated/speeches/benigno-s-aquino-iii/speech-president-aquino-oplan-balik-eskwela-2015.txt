Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, for Oplan Balik Eskwela 2015

Good morning to all of you.

I envy your energy levels. You know we just came back from Canada and America. And there was one part of the trip, early in the morning, when I boarded the plane, all the members of the delegation seemed to be lying down already. I said, it seems my companions are really tired. Because you know, in a five-day trip, we spent 40 hours on the plane. So we didn’t stop anywhere for 24 hours. I said, maybe they have a right to be a little tired.

This visit really feels like a reunion. I remember back in the day, Marikina Central Elementary School, right? When we say “back in the day,” it’s like the time of Jose Rizal, right? [Laughter] It wasn’t the 1800s when we were there, it was the 1900s. And I was just in high school then, the second batch of the Tulong Dunong program of Ateneo, and I can proudly say that… We had, I think, two scholars from our batch who went to Ateneo High School after this. Maybe I should look for those two, they’re much younger than me. [Laughter]

As you know, I’m not originally from Marikina, but I have many experiences here in your city. And these experiences are what shaped me into the person I am today.

First, there was the Tulong Dunong program when I was in high school, where we tutored students in public schools here in Marikina. And you know, when we tutored, while we shared knowledge, we also learned. For instance, the teachers, when we say dedicated public servant, that’s really what I first experienced as dedicated. Why dedicated? Even pencils, paper, encyclopedias, all the equipment, the teachers contributed to fill the lack of resources in this school. And to be honest, most of them have second jobs, that’s how they make a living. The salary here, it’s like it’s returned to the school. And really, what more can we ask from a public servant who is truly focused and patient with those they serve?

Another thing, I remember, during elections, even during the Martial Law period, I don’t remember a time when anyone from my family, or relatives of mine here in Marikina, lost, even during Martial Law. [Applause] That’s why, whenever your city approaches us with a request, we can’t say no; and we really rush to address them. I remember: In 2011, I went to Caticlan to inaugurate the new terminal of Caticlan Airport which serves Boracay. Don’t we always boast about Boracay? And as the country’s chief salesman, because our tourism is called a “low-lying fruit,” where you don’t need to study too much and you can participate in the jobs created by tourism, we promote Boracay—it’s only right that I personally try our boasted Boracay because, to be honest, before that day, I hadn’t been to Boracay and experienced the beaches of Boracay. Right, it’s like when we’re outside the country, “You should try Boracay.” “Why?” Then you say, “the powdery sand.” All that. But for me, what I knew was just what I read, because I hadn’t been there yet. I was just pretending to be knowledgeable. [Laughter] And I looked successful because the planes were full. So when they said we would inaugurate the new terminal in Caticlan, I said, “Well, finally, I’ll get to go to Bora too.” But the wind changed a bit: The night before we went to Caticlan, many texted us that there was an impending and feared flood here in your area in Marikina, and in Malabon. The texts said I needed rubber boats and other equipment to prepare for it. It wasn’t Mayor Del that I was talking to then. Of course, after the inauguration of Caticlan Airport, and we had done what we needed to do there, we chose to return to Manila, and visit Marikina and Malabon. When I arrived here in Marikina and we were able to talk to Mayor Del, he told me, “Sir, what are you doing here? I hope you didn’t bother coming; we are fully prepared here.” Of course, I just thought to myself, [Applause] we’re happy that you’re well prepared, but in my mind: “I wish you had texted me earlier this morning, I would have been in Bora already.” [Laughter]

So, our staff who were left behind in Boracay told me they were on the beach, doing water sports. I said, we’re here in the flood too. [Laughter]

We call this spirit of community unity 'Bayanihan,' working together towards a common goal. It is a tradition deeply ingrained in our culture: From simply helping each other prepare food for a feast, to the great sacrifices made to achieve freedom, we have demonstrated through Bayanihan our willingness to help one another and row in one direction.

It is truly heartwarming to see, a year before our term ends, the continued demonstration of Bayanihan in events like Brigada Eskwela and Oplan Balik Eswela. In Brigada Eskwela, 46,624 public schools across the country were transformed into community centers, where citizens gathered to clean and repair the schools. In Balik Eskwela, various government agencies, along with other stakeholders from the private sector, come together to prepare for the return of our youth to our public schools. Led by DepEd, the DPWH contributes to the completion of classrooms; the DTI to stabilizing the prices of school supplies; the DOH to disseminating health information; the DILG to coordinating with local government units and barangays and deploying police; even PAGASA with weather reports, MMDA with traffic management, MWSS with water, and private companies like Manila Water and MERALCO, to ensure a smooth return of students to school. In these initiatives, Filipinos come together, ready to participate for the benefit of the majority. You know, I noticed that when my speech gets faster, your fanning also speeds up. [Laughter] I’ll slow down a bit, lest you get more tired. [Laughter] I don’t even need to go to Boracay, I’ll just go to Marikina, there are so many people who can make us smile here.

What is truly heartwarming is that, in these activities, I am truly revitalized and even more motivated to strive for meaningful change. Here, I clearly see the true essence of the heart of decent Filipinos: Willing to unite, willing to work for their fellowmen, especially the youth; willing to contribute to the positive, and not just complain without offering or adding to the solution. That is why, to our colleagues here today, as well as those who volunteered in recent days; to the agencies and companies, to the teachers, parents, and students not only here at Marikina Elementary School but throughout the country, thank you very much to all of you. [Applause] I am even more filled with hope, because I know that you and those like you are behind me. I truly feel renewed strength.

In coming together, I see the enthusiasm needed to advance education in our country. As we all know, education is the main pillar of our agenda for broad development for our country. Especially now that the economy is rising and opportunities in our society are expanding, we are focusing on training our youth, so that they have sufficient knowledge to fill the opening jobs.

Let me illustrate: As early as 2012, we filled the inherited shortage of 61.7 million textbooks and 2.5 million chairs; in 2013, the backlog of 66,800 classrooms was also closed. We are also addressing additional needs due to the K to 12 program, which reforms our education system to ensure that when young people graduate from high school, they have enough knowledge to become productive members of society. In fact, under the leadership of DepEd under Bro. Armin, 33,607 classrooms were completed, being built, and in the process of procurement in 2014. This year, 2015, they are targeting to build an additional 31,728 classrooms with their partners in the private sector.

This focus on education is also part of a broad strategy to empower our Bosses: We ensure they are healthy through expanded PhilHealth and strengthened hospital services, and we lift them out of poverty using various DSWD programs, such as Pantawid Pamilya. Likewise, we are making doing business in the country fair and transparent to further attract investors and create more jobs; and we ensure the accountability of the corrupt and evil elements, so that we can restore the rule of law in our society.

We are already seeing the initial results of our cooperation: The unemployment rate has fallen to 6.6 percent as of January 2015. According to SWS, self-rated hunger in the first quarter of this year is at only 13.5 percent; this is the lowest in ten years. That’s just now, when we haven’t even been on the straight and narrow path for five years; what about future generations, if our seedlings are watered, and our students graduate to enter good jobs? Maybe we will be in first world status by then. It’s wonderful to dream about that, especially knowing that we can achieve it if we continue to strive, contribute, and ensure good and honest governance in our country.

Many times we have proven how far we can reach if we are united; that in the spirit of People Power, we can make the impossible possible. Therefore, we are confident that through the cooperation of the government and the community, we can shape a future that is truly beneficial to the majority. What I assure you is that as long as we work together and focus on the positive, as long as we row in one direction, and as long as we firmly believe in God, no matter how high our dreams are, we will reach them; we can bequeath to future generations a country that we can truly be proud of: A country where our efforts bear fruit; a just country, and enjoying broad development.

Before I conclude, of course, let us thank our leaders here in the city. Starting with Mayor Del, along with Vice [Mayor], and the entire City Council, and our brave, hardworking congressmen. You know Miro, when I returned from Canada, we talked about the Bangsamoro Basic Law, and we rushed it, so we ended up at 2:30 in the morning. So if Miro’s wife is looking for him, Miro was with me until 2:30 in the morning. [Laughter] We started at noon, we finished at 2:30 in the morning because we really scrutinized the BBL to make it worthy for everyone.

You know, whenever Brother Armin gives us updates, there are really a lot of changes. As I told you, in the old days, when you became a teacher, it felt like a burden was placed on you. You were responsible for your class, and literally: you were responsible for repairs, you were responsible for school supplies, you were responsible for being a social worker, you were responsible for everything, right? Now, times have changed. One problem we have with K to 12, the private schools are complaining that the salary scale for public schools is too high, and they are having difficulty recruiting teachers. I said, I hope the government is now paying back, in some way, the burden on previous generations of teachers.

And we will strive to really include everyone who renders proper service to the country in our progress. There are bonuses that Brother Armin will tell you are about to be achieved [applause], and your good performance, because you have targets: you reached them, you exceeded them, there is a corresponding reward for that. If you fall short of what you promised, of course, you will get the minimum. So you yourselves decide how big the bonus will be. Right? No more influence peddling, no more other criteria, just promise something, deliver it, exceed it, and there will be an appropriate increase.

Let me repeat what my father always told me, I keep repeating it. It was during martial law when he told me this. Of course, our future didn't look that bright. He said, you know, study hard. I couldn't say, I'll probably be joining the mountains during this martial law, how can I even study? Then he said, once that knowledge is yours, whatever happens, you will have it for life. Undiminished wealth, undiminished fame, education is in your brain: that will be your weapon in everything you face.

And truthfully, until these days, my colleagues in the office, our fellow cabinet members, wonder why I scrutinize every page of the reports they submit. Why my memory is so sharp. I said, it started because I was taught that the beginning of all knowledge begins with saying “I don’t know.” I force myself every day, even though I have learned a lot, my knowledge is still lacking. So that we can provide better service to our countrymen. I hope we don't lose our hunger to learn, so we can learn even more. And in that way, each generation will continue to rise because the starting point is higher. With your help, that will happen.

Good morning, thank you all very much.
