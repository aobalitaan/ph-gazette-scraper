Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

during his meeting with local leaders and communities of Samar

[Delivered at the Calbayog City Sports Center, Calbayog, Samar]

Comrades, look, I have a little script here. Honestly, it’s difficult to give a speech now because I’m already saying goodbye. To my Bosses, I need to tell you, what have we achieved? If we list what we’ve achieved, my speech will be longer than those of the candidates. That would be embarrassing, so I said, “Can we just have a summary?” The summary, look, it reached more than half a page; we even haggled over it. My writer is almost resigning. He said, “You want to say everything, you want to say it briefly, how can I do all that?” But just the highlights.

You know, in the Department of Agriculture, the report to me—this is what they said—for example, farm-to-market roads: P435 million from 2011-2016 for farm-to-market roads; irrigation: P2.32 billion; our fishport and community fish landing centers—I think this figure is a little off—but also the coconut industry, the assistance given was P75.73 million; flood control alone: P2.4 billion; infraroads and bridges, ports, airports, school buildings, health facilities, government buildings—before we took office, 2005 to 2010: P6.17 billion. Through our collective efforts: P12.94 billion has been delivered here to Samar. Pantawid Pamilya (Conditional Cash Transfer Program): we are at 96 percent of the target because we are already helping 57,711 households.

Regarding TESDA and their Training for Work Scholarship Program, 3,500 have already graduated. Classrooms, with a target for Samar: 2,697 will be completed this year. For PhilHealth, here in Samar alone, 785,000 are already members; this is included in the 93 million members nationwide. Before, when the numbers were high, around 51 million, and the lower figure was over 47 million, only a little over a hundred million Filipinos were PhilHealth members. Now, it's 93 percent, and we are working to increase that further.

What message do I want to leave with you? Earlier, I was listening to Mel, and everything he said is true. I want to say that I agree with everything he said, it's all true, when he mentioned asking the national government for help. I, as a congressman, started in '98, our problem in Tarlac was flooding. Flooding during the rainy season, and drought during the dry season, no water at all. Now, during the rainy season, even the MacArthur Highway—that's the Manila North Road—is affected. The Tarlac River is high, it overflows, fills the paved MacArthur Highway, it deteriorates, becoming bumpy. Before it can be repaired, the holes get bigger and bigger. Even 10-wheeler trucks really tilt as they pass through there, the traffic flow is slow.

So, the budget. The budget comes from Congress—I am a congressman, and I fought for it. By the grace of God, no help was given. Even our PDAF, we used it just to repair the Manila North Road, which was a national road—that was back then. Now, we have worked together: 18,000 kilometers of national roads. There are 2,500 tourism roads. And over 3,000 farm-to-market roads have been built.

Let me go back: Before—Mel, you probably already told the story about the firetruck. Haven't you? I might make my speech too long here, it's embarrassing, I'm not a candidate. Or Mel, your mayor, was invited to a conference abroad. When he went there, he said the Japanese delegation arrived before him—Mel, tell me if I'm wrong—they checked his passport and said, “Hey! From Japan, please. Welcome to our country, you may proceed.” When the one from Singapore arrived, “From Singapore, again, we’re proud to have you as our visitors. Please proceed.” When Mel arrived, they checked his passport, “From the Philippines,” they looked at Mel, looked at the passport, looked at Mel again, looked at the photo, and asked Mel, “Purpose of visit?” He said, “I was invited to this conference here,” “You have proof that you were invited?” So Mel had to present a letter. In short, it took a while, but he was eventually let in.

Then of course, at those conferences, you network, you connect with other mayors, maybe you can help them or they can help you. So Mel brought with him 100 calling cards for the conference. To make it easy, he could be contacted. When he came home, he was carrying 101 calling cards. It seems someone even returned a calling card he had given away before. He said that during meetings, when it was known that he was from the Philippines, no one wanted to approach him, because they thought that if someone from the Philippines approached you, they would ask, “Do you have any extra firetrucks?” “Do you have any extra ambulances?”

So everyone avoided being asked, they would step aside when they knew you were from the Philippines. There was even a point where Cesar Purisima, our Secretary of Finance, attended a conference, there was a session, a breakout session, you would separate, connect with each other. So you would be given a chair, a table, everyone interested in your country would go there, sit down, and talk to you. He said the Philippine table was really empty at the beginning. Cesar Purisima was there from the morning, it was already afternoon, and no one was talking to him. Later, it was already late afternoon, around 2 or 3 o'clock, someone approached him. Cesar suddenly adjusted his tie, he said, “Finally, someone will talk to me.” When the person arrived, Cesar smiled, of course, you're trying to attract investors. The person who approached him said, “Can I borrow this chair?” [Laughter]

That's the reputation of the Philippines, so when Mel says he's hurt when it's said that Samar is always one of the poorest provinces, we all feel that deeply. [Applause]

As your President, I need to attend ASEAN meetings. That's the first job of a President from an ASEAN country, you go around to the other ASEAN members. And truthfully, not just them, but we also spoke with other heads of government and heads of state, when I spoke to them, I felt like we were being given the courtesy of being spoken to. Like, “It's a waste to talk to these people. We won't get anything from talking to them.” But that was all before.

Let's go back, in the old days, Mel might have been avoided because people were looking for things from him, Mel might ask for, 'Maybe you have some old fire trucks there, maybe you have some old ambulances there, maybe you have some old police vehicles there.' That was how it was back then. Now that Mel Sarmiento is the Secretary of DILG, he is the one distributing fire trucks, police vehicles. [Applause] And it's not based on connections. Whatever the need is, everyone is given, regardless of party affiliation. Even those who criticize us, I think Mel, they have also—well, there are only a few left who sincerely criticized. Of course, this started during Mar's time as Secretary, and Mel is the one implementing and finishing it. Whatever I say to Mel, Mar did that too, he started it.

Now, let me add: I also feel that way, when you talk to them, it feels like you're just being indulged. Once, in one of the wealthiest countries, a member of ASEAN, I won't mention who, we had a conference, and we split up. We had conversations with the private sector, the host government, they turned to me and said, 'President Aquino, our economy grew by 1 point.' It was about 1.8 percent, I think, 1.6 percent was their GDP growth. 'Yours'—around that time, I think we were at 7 percent. 'You did 7, what's the secret?' The ones I used to find a little difficult to talk to, were now asking me what the secret was to our good economy.

Of course, in my mind, 'You're just catching up now, and you're asking for the secret. Maybe I can just keep it to myself for now. We're just catching up.' But truthfully, to maintain diplomatic ties, I just said, 'We are just following your example.' We're just copying you. Oh, so he didn't ask any more questions, because they had a good example.

What's the point, what's the message? In the old days, I admit to you, I ran for office several times. Here, Senator, when I first approached you, and then afterwards as President, you supported me. We were able to accomplish so much together.

Let me repeat: If you talk about infrastructure, 18,000 kilometers of national roads. If we talk about bridges, 107,000 lineal meters of bridges. If we talk about the economy, 6.2 percent average growth per year. If we talk about jobs for the youth, Joel's program at TESDA, 9 million course graduates. Of those 9 million course graduates, we found that only 28 percent were able to find jobs within six months of graduating. Now, it's at 72 percent. And, those businesses that have really partnered with us, like semiconductors, have a placement rate of 93 to 96 percent. Train, and you'll be employed within 6 months.

Let me give you an example of their graduates. There's a woman, from Pangasinan. She got a contract to work in the Middle East. Because she took an accounting course, she was told she would be a cashier. When she arrived there, her job was changed, she was made a domestic helper, and she was even mistreated. She escaped, was helped by our embassy, and returned home. She was very confused when she returned home, probably in debt to go abroad. She didn't finish her contract, she wasn't paid. 'How will I pay off this debt? What will happen to me?' And she said she was even thinking of taking her own life. She said her world was really turned upside down. She was told she was an OFW, you've returned, there are scholarship programs. She trained, it was massage therapy I think, and then wellness, health wellness hilot. These two courses under TESDA, she got a job at a spa, she became an operations manager. Now, she has established four spas, she owns four spas. She's even franchising spas. She has three franchise branches, in addition to the four she owns.

Isn't it worth all the effort when you hear about an opportunity where our countrymen, from having nothing, are truly improving their future?

There's a woman, she spoke in Pampanga, a beneficiary of 4Ps. She said, 'I just sell things, because my husband left me with seven children.' Now, in Tagalog, when you say 'nagtitinda-tinda,' it's not a permanent job. Sometimes you can sell, sometimes you earn, you have something to feed your family when you earn. So how will she do it, what will she do, if that's the situation? Because she said, because of 4Ps, she was able to get three of her seven children through high school, and three of them now have permanent jobs, and she has help taking care of the four younger siblings who haven't finished yet.

Now, why am I giving these examples? Perhaps my message is very simple: Everything we have accomplished in these six years could have been done before we took office, could have been started from 2000 to 2010. If it had been done from 2000 to 2010, how many would have finished not just high school, but college? How many would have been burdened with debt who are now being helped by PhilHealth when they get sick? How many would have increased their yields if all these irrigation projects had been started when they were really needed?

But that's it, we can't get back what's past. But we can fix the future. You saw the time, didn't you, even during my mother's time, the poorest provinces were always included: Samar. Now, when we look at it, what are we being told? For example, in aquaculture, there is a product called abalone. I don't know what you call it here, but in Mexico, per kilo, canned, like a can that size—like the small milk can when Mel and I were kids—that size, it sells for P5,000 in the Philippines. But that, we've already started raising it here in Guiuan and the results are good. They just say, let the abalone grow bigger before you harvest it. But it's really big if we can process it. It's one of Mexico's main exports, we can compete with that. Why isn't there much now? It was also affected by Yolanda.

In coconut, let's get into it. In coconut, I was told that per hectare of coconut farming—in Tarlac, we don't really farm coconuts. We farm rice and sugar. In coconut, if you earn, per hectare, per year, P20,000. Is that right? More or less from copra production. If you plant in between, intercropping. Income can be increased from P80,000 to P170,000 per hectare. You can plant bananas, corn, cacao, coffee. Coffee yields the highest income. Again, between the trees, you don't do anything, just plant. The plants we will plant have a market, Nestle. Nestle said that we import from the Philippines, to meet the needs of the Philippines alone, 80 percent when we took office, now 70 percent, the local supply is increasing. The ambition is just to reverse that. 20 percent imported; 80 percent from the Philippines.

In other words, the future of coconut farming, from—it's always said, coconut farmers are the most difficult sector among farmers. We now have a key to strengthen, maximize income here for farmers. Now you will ask, “Why isn't it full blast?” You know, we have something we were going to use, the Coconut Levy Fund. Congress did not pass the law. The Supreme Court decided, recently, that it's okay, it's public money, the government can use it. The law was not passed. How will it be used? We issued an Executive Order, it was brought to the Supreme Court again, we were issued a TRO now, we can't continue that. But the goal is very simple: It's already in the coconut, there's a fund, let's use it correctly, let's increase it. Not double, not triple what can be earned. At the very least, four times what a coconut farmer can earn.

Let me also include Dr. Arboleda of Bicol State University. He developed the coco net or coco coir. His new project, he helped the government, is to build a factory for everything that can be obtained from the coconut, except copra. That means coco coir, coco peat, coco sugar, coco water. How can we maximize the income of coconut farmers?

The prototype factory they built is almost finished. They told us that once the factory is built, it will recover its capital in three years. The capital can be given to the next group of farmers. They will then have their own factory. Sell the copra, then many other products. The water that used to be thrown away, right, coconut water, we are now exporting 16 million liters of it. The husks that used to be burned, we will get the fibers, it's still an export item. We are running out. In the Philippines alone, we have used about 5 million square meters of this coco net.

Let me repeat, I have no province to go to, no place to go to where we can't say how much we have accomplished. One of the things I like most, when I was a congressman, the whole country could only build 8,000 classrooms a year. Now, as I step down from office and this year, we will finish, through our combined efforts, 185,000 classrooms across the Philippines. [Applause]

That means, when we talk about raising the lives of Filipinos, when we want to give every Filipino all opportunities. That's not a promise. There are schools, if your parents are having trouble with fare, there's Pantawid. If you are sick, there's the DOH, there's PhilHealth, to help you, so you can study.

While you are studying, we are inviting investors so that you will have a job to apply for. We are coordinating with them, “What kind of workers do you need on this date?” When the child graduates, even just high school, they will have a decent job waiting. We are completing this process.

Let me repeat: Isn't this an election? Everything we watch on TV, all the candidates are making promises left and right. Of course, everyone who promises seems flimsy when you ask, “How will you fulfill that promise?” Here, on the Straight Path, we don’t make promises, except to continue and strengthen what we’ve already done – when we were once down, we stood up, we are now walking, and now we want to run. [Applause] We won’t be able to run closer and closer to where we want to go if our coach takes us on a left turn. The wives don’t like left turns. They might take a right turn. But the most damaging thing is a U-turn. To return to the old ways.

Everything we’ve done could have been done by those who came before us. But their direction was different. The one I replaced, you all know, their interest was how to stay in power. The government’s interest should always be: How can we help our fellow citizens? And not guaranteeing: “Join us, and you’re guaranteed this.” No. What we can assure you is: We will exhaust all opportunities that can be obtained and utilized by every Filipino. Your success, of course, depends on our countrymen. If someone comes and says, “This will definitely happen.” Wait a moment, how will you do that?

Okay, let me give you an example. One of Mar’s opponents said, “You know that 4Ps”—back then, in 2013, his son was investigating it; now that he’s seen high support for the 4Ps, he’s joined us. “I will make the 4Ps into 5Ps,” [laughter] right? “And while I’m making the 5Ps, I will reduce taxes.”

Now, the housewives here, if you have a husband, if your husband comes home and declares, “Next month, I will reduce your budget. But while I’m reducing it, you should be able to buy more, okay?” Is that possible? We didn’t raise taxes. But he will reduce it, but he will increase the services. You yourselves, it’s up to you to decide if you should believe statements like that. Someone who will add millions of jobs every year, I hope you can do that, said another candidate. I ask: How will you do that?

Let me repeat, for us, it’s not just about jobs, because before, the numbers would increase when there was a survey. What kind of jobs? Sweeping highways, temporary garbage collectors. What we are aiming for are permanent jobs. That’s why we have the lowest unemployment rate in a decade. Our unemployment rate is now at 5.7%. [Applause]

You know, earlier I saw a poster of someone running for Vice President. I thought to myself: Here in Samar, are you hoping to get votes? I seem to remember, we were allies during Martial Law, right? When the NPA grew here during Martial Law, and then you hope that while saying you did nothing wrong and have nothing to apologize for, you will have a lot of support here. Maybe on election day, you will be one of those who says, “I was cheated.”

You know, sorry, but isn’t it that in elections, many people run, but not everyone can win? There’s even a joke in the Philippines, there are only two candidates. The one who wins and the one who was cheated, right? I, in my life, have never cheated for myself. I have no intention of allowing anyone to cheat here. [Applause] It is my obligation to ensure that what the people want is what prevails on May 9th.

I feel my mother, you know, as if she's whispering to me, “You are not a candidate. Everyone you've talked to has been here for a while, they might be tired, please understand.”

Perhaps, this is all I want to leave with you: First of all, I want to thank you all. When we used to hear, “It was an honor and a privilege to have served you,” it just sounded like words. But in all the difficult experiences I went through while in office, I really only relied on one thing: As long as you believe in me, and you are behind me, I will face any challenge because I believe no one can stop us. [Applause]

That's why I can say that our race is truly a great race. We know what is right and wrong. We know that if this is right, we fight for what is right; even if the challenge is difficult, we don't back down. Especially when we are sure we are right. That's why it's truly an honor to be the leader of a great race like ours.

Now, there are only a little over 70 days left for us, in my term, in the mandate you gave me. We need to, right, prepare for the transition of power. And I appeal to you, perhaps for the last time in my life, everything we have done now is because of you. You gave me trust, you supported me when I was there, you helped me until probably my last day in office, and we've come so far in what can be done in the Philippines. We've reversed the concept that if you want to succeed, you should leave the Philippines. Now, some of our countrymen are returning, about 400,000 OFWs have already returned to the Philippines. [Applause]

But the best part is, this is just the beginning. Those we helped through the 4Ps program are now graduating from high school, some are even in college. They are starting to reap the benefits of our help, and we can continue that.

But it's important, I think those who are here, with so many wearing yellow, I think you are convinced. But we need to find those who are lost. We need to face them and talk to them, and convince them that even something new is worth trying. But others are already sure. Why risk the future of the next successor if we can guarantee that we are on the right track? That's all we should ask.

You know, I really don't want to get emotional but I'll admit to you, in 2009, when I was first asked to run, I really thought to myself, “Why are you making me run? Are you going to pass on the problem of this woman I'm opposing to me, and if I don't solve it yesterday, you'll be angry.” But now that we're here, looking back at the past and seeing everything we've accomplished. For example, we were joking the other day. Our C-130 when I came into office, was big, one. Now, there are four, and it will become five. [Applause]

Before, someone would approach you, asking for help, maybe you could help them, give them money to buy rice today. Now, we are teaching them how to buy their own rice, not just for one day, but every day. And we have also helped the farmers to increase their rice yield.

Truly, in all the places we've been, I can tell you, eye to eye, that there's nothing we can't achieve. But we can't let ourselves be dragged along; you have the power, you will decide where we go. Let's use that power wisely.

I'll repeat it: Mar Roxas, Leni Robredo, for sure. [Applause] Regarding their opponents, the best we can say: Maybe they are better, maybe they will fulfill their promises, maybe we can trust them. But of course, there's a 'maybe' there. On the other hand, maybe we shouldn't trust them either, maybe they won't deliver, maybe they don't know where they'll take us. Oh, doesn't that seem easy to figure out: maybe or for sure?

And this is really the last one—though it's still early for where I'm going. You know, like a mother, so many people are talking about 4Ps, I heard it in testimonies and others, a mother who was left by her husband with seven children, she finished schooling three of them, approached us now, knocked on our hearts. She said, “How many of you know that there's no direct deduction from your pockets, you've helped our family so much?” No burden on you, we didn't raise taxes except for the Sin Tax. We fixed the spending of the funds we receive. Three of her seven children are high school graduates. They have permanent jobs. She's still supporting four more.

So, if this mother and others, the 4.6 million households of 4Ps, approached us and said, “We're starting to see the light, maybe you can continue your help, I still have four to support. Once those four are helped, we won't need to approach you anymore, we can stand on our own feet. And we can manage our lives. But we still need a little support.” Who among us would begrudge them that support when it doesn't burden us?

I think, all of us, we will say, “Why wouldn't we help you, fellow Filipino? As we help you, you progress, we all progress. If someone is left behind, they will pull us down. If we unite, we will all rise and rise and rise.” That bright future, we already have it in our hands. It's up to us if we let it go, it's also up to us if we make a mistake. And I, I was taught to trust our countrymen, always in the end, the right decision will be made. Let's stand by that, let's reach out to everyone we can reach, and let's ensure that the Straight Path, where we are walking now, tomorrow, and after May 9th, we are running on that Straight Path, and how much further we can go. [Applause]

I will now bid you farewell, it is truly an honor to serve all of you. It will probably be difficult to look at the end of this, what more could I reach in this life. And because of you, I think, I can face my mother and father, and tell them: They were not wrong when my father gave his name to me. And even more so, they were not wrong when they called me their favorite son. When I was young, I really felt that. Because my siblings are four daughters. So it's clear, favorite son. That's me. [Applause]

I really can't let go of this a little bit more. And this is a strong appeal. We all know Juan Tamad, right? Juan Tamad, when an image is shown to us: He has a mat, waiting under a guava tree, waiting for the guava to fall, his mouth open, waiting for it to fall. Because he is so lazy, he could pick them, he waits for them to fall.

You know, when I was young, I thought, isn't it that if it falls on its own, there are only two possibilities: it's either very ripe or rotten. You can pick it properly. Why would you wait for it to rot before—that's what you desire, the rotten one. So what does that mean? We really have an opportunity now to continue this and accelerate our progress. But we can't be lazy and just let it happen to us. We need to work for it. And that is my strong request to all of you.

Good afternoon. Thank you very much to all of you.
