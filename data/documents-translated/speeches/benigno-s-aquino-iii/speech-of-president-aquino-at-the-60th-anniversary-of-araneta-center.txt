Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, at “Araneta Center at 60: The Story Lives On”

In 1961, our family moved to Times Street, so that’s where I grew up and studied in Quezon City. As a young QC resident, I saw how this place changed since the Araneta Center and neighboring businesses and buildings were built. During those times, Manila was really the center of commerce and entertainment, and really during that time, Cubao was out of the way. For example, that’s where people went who wanted to watch movies, of course all the cinemas were in Manila, maybe one or two were here in Quezon City. But when the New Frontier Theater opened, and later on the Nation, people from Quezon City no longer needed to travel to Manila.

And perhaps, most of those who went to Manila went to Quezon City because the cinemas here were newer. Back then, Makati followed, two–it’s showing our age–there were two cinemas there. Both of them are gone now. One of them, I think, is now a church.

Many of our countrymen also had an annual ritual when Cubao changed: before the school year started, they would buy school bags and school supplies at National Bookstore, then go straight to what Cabsec calls ShoeMart–I know it as SM–or to Marikina Shoe Expo to buy new shoes. I even remember the sight or sound of a sales lady shouting to lower their shoes. Some of them, actually, they just dropped them, like there was a chute. You’re a kid, “Hey, there are shoes flying around here.” You watch, then another salesperson pulls you away because you might get hit by the shoes. I think the conveyor belt was only used when returning the wrong sizes, but the one that was used was the one that really–they weren’t thrown, but dropped. After shopping, what my mother considered the incentive, she would take us for a snack, here at Marikina Shoe Expo, there was Nena’s bibingka, I think, hot pandesal with menudo. This is exactly the ritual our family had when visiting the Araneta Center before the school year started.

I have many more happy memories here, especially at the Coliseum. At the Big Dome, I watched my first live boxing match. I think I also met Tito Nene for the first time there. I was with my father, or maybe it’s more accurate to say my father brought me. When the boxing match started, he challenged me to a small bet. Because we came from Ateneo, I didn’t know the boxer, I was still in grade school, I just chose the one wearing blue trunks. He chose the one not wearing blue trunks. [Laughter] I was lucky, in three consecutive fights, all those wearing blue trunks won, and my father kept paying me. You might think we were betting a lot–I think it was one peso per bout. But for me, that was a big deal, because it was almost equivalent to my allowance back then. When the fourth bout came, my father didn’t ask me if we should bet again. And the one wearing blue trunks lost. When the one wearing blue trunks lost, my father asked me to pay up. I said, “You didn’t challenge me to a bet here.” Then, [he replied], “Well, you lost. Pay up.” I said, “That’s how it is, huh.” When I was paying him, my father said, “No, I was just kidding.” After that, my father never took me to boxing again. Even now, I still smile when I remember those times. We were just an ordinary father and son spending time together. Life was really very simple during that time.

When I graduated from grade school, I was asked where I wanted to celebrate. Let’s go back here, we celebrated at a Japanese restaurant here at Fiesta Carnival. We also rode the rides during that time. I also remember a time when I watched a rodeo here. Of course, I also saw how intense the rivalry between Ateneo and La Salle was. Even after the game, there was still teasing in the parking area. I no longer joined them because I was a good boy: I went home right away. [Laughter]

The Araneta Center has really changed so much since those times. There is no longer horseback riding here beside the Araneta Center; the old restaurants have many closed and been replaced by newer restaurants; and the former small establishments have been replaced by high-tech and truly world-class BPO offices. And if we look back at history, we will see that this is really the story of the Araneta Center: Change, perseverance to reach what is seen on the bright horizon, and nurturing to grow and reach its true and full potential.

Perhaps, those who came before me will remember the time when this was still the home of Radio Communication of America. It was said to be a vast grassland then, and the only structures standing were towering antennas. It was truly a remote place. The family of Don Amado, the grandfather of Secretary Mar and the father of Tita Judy, had a comfortable livelihood in agriculture. But instead of being complacent and continuing with what they knew, they risked their future on a dream. Truly, it would have been easy for him to simply continue planting sugarcane and doing business with sugar. He had extensive experience, so his earnings would have been assured. But he felt that he could do more; that he could be a champion of change. And that decision of his served as the seed of the transformation we are witnessing today. This is what is called vision: Not to simply go with the flow of time, but to take a chance and realize a plan that will benefit many.

Just like a plant, one can simply let something grow on its own: Let the elements provide what it needs to reach its potential. On the other hand, one can nurture it: Give it fertilizer, water it properly, avoid pests, and truly excel in farming. That is exactly what the true visionaries like Don Amado and his family did here in the Araneta Center: Instead of succumbing to doubt, they turned the grassland into a center of commerce, culture, and progress; a symbol of the future that every Filipino can reach. They drew their vast dreams, and they worked hard to bring them into reality. By the 1960s, the face of Cubao had already changed: And they built the Araneta Coliseum, which was called the “Biggest Covered Coliseum in the World,” while the New Frontier Theater became the largest theater in Asia.

And the dreams of the next generation of Aranetas for this place are not yet finished. Eighteen towers of Manhattan Garden City will be seen when it is completed; ten of these are already finished. There is also a Novotel-Araneta Center Hotel coming soon. The New Frontier Theater will be made more modern and will restore its dignity. A Civic Plaza, an Integrated Resorts Complex, and the completion of the Araneta Center Cyberpark: All of these will come to Araneta, and will be a modern and more progressive face of a society full of opportunities. Imagine: Within the 35 hectares of the Araneta Center, 29,066 jobs will be created from all of these projects.

What I can say, and what I have personally witnessed from Secretary Mar and his family: All of these projects spring from one thing: Love. The Araneta family loves this place, and the entire Cubao. If you notice, while other families are moving to larger subdivisions, or building their own mansions in distant places, Tita Judy, Gaita, Ria, Mar, Tita Baby, Tito Nene, the whole family, have not really left Cubao. And while they are loved by the people of Cubao and other visitors to the Araneta Center, they repeatedly return that love by opening more opportunities and improving their services.

That is the example shown by the Araneta Center and the Araneta family to all of us: If all our actions come from love for our homeland; if we can broaden our dreams and develop them; if we can keep up with change, or even be the source of change that our fellow citizens can follow, truly nothing is impossible for our race. The former remote place can become a modern and progressive center of opportunity for many Filipinos. If this was done in Araneta, I am sure that if we unite, if we dream and truly pour all our efforts into realizing those dreams, we will achieve broad and positive transformation in every corner of the Philippines.

Good afternoon again. Thank you very much to all of you.
