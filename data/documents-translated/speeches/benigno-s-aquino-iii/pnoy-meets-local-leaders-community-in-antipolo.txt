Let me start, first of all, by thanking the public: Junjun Ynares, his father Ito Ynares who really—and all of the Ynareses. Honestly, in 2007, I ran for senator in the opposition. Here in Antipolo, I don't know if you know, many people greet me saying, “You speak Tagalog so well.” And it just occurred to me now. Often, my grandmother, from Sumulong, and my grandfather, whom we call Lolo Pepe, whose name is Jose, is from Tarlac. They got married here in Antipolo. And you know, in Tarlac, half are Ilocano, half are Kapampangan, and those near Nueva Ecija are Tagalog. So in our house, we had housekeepers, companions, some were Ilocano, some were Kapampangan, some were Tagalog. When the Kapampangans talked, they spoke in Kapampangan, the Tagalogs answered in Tagalog. The Ilocanos would ask questions in Ilocano.

So when I was young, I thought they were all the same. But honestly, I've been thinking about my grandmother. I think, now, if my grandmother ever scolded me during all the time we were together, and I think she never did. But in my grandparents' house, Tagalog was always the language spoken. So I remember, we came here to Antipolo many times. I don't know if it's still there, there was a store, a restaurant. Its name was Aling Goyang. Are her relatives here? Is Aling Goyang’s place still there? Aling Goyang seemed to be my grandmother’s friend. [Laughter]

So you'll ask why I remembered that? Because when someone says we're going to Antipolo, it's guaranteed there will be *haluhalo*. And when you're a child, you're easily persuaded. “Do you want *haluhalo*?” “Of course!” “Come on, let's go to Antipolo.”

And when we arrived, of course, the *haluhalo* wasn't served in a glass like this, it *was* the *haluhalo*. During that time, the *haluhalo* was maybe an inch in size. The ice cream was there. The *leche flan* was there. It was really complete. And I remember they would leave me there. They would come back after an hour. And if they were late, there was also *suman* included. When we got home, I wasn't given just one piece of *suman*, I brought home a small portion of *suman*.

So the suman, it’s like—well, we used to make sugar in Tarlac. So it’s soaked, and if you peel the suman, even the—you know when I remember, even the wrapper was always smooth, right? Not like something to throw away. You’d feel bad removing it, it was so nicely arranged. But of course, what’s important to me is the suman inside. When I was a child.

We’d open the suman, put sugar like this. A little short, or like this, like that, flipped it over, filled it with sugar. So after the halo-halo and the suman, I was smiling the whole time. Now I know what that’s called: a sugar high. [Laughter]

Comrades, let me repeat: Strategy, how to alleviate poverty. Everyone is saying now: The poor are suffering, we need to help the poor, they have compassion. We have already done it. It’s simple: Let’s send our children to school, let’s build the classrooms where they will study. Let’s help them stay in school through 4Ps. Let’s help improve their health, so they can stay in school. And when they graduate, let’s also help them find jobs.

We asked the industry, the businessmen, “What kind of workers do you need 2 years from now, 4 years from now, 6 years from now?” And we focused on that, TESDA trained on that, CHED offered scholarships to meet the government's needs, and pushed our students towards specific fields where they will have jobs. What good is studying and graduating if you don't have a job to go to, if it's useless?

Result: Just an example. Let's say someone enters the BPO, the call centers. Let's give one person an opportunity, 30 are about to give birth. 30 can have the same opportunity because we are managing our funds well. We invested in our countrymen, and our countrymen returned it to us, with more, we are helping again, even more people.
