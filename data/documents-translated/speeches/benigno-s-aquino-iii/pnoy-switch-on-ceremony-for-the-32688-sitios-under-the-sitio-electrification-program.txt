Speech

of

The Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III

President of the Philippines

At the lighting ceremony for the 32,688 sitios under the Sitio Electrification Program

[Declared in Laguindingan, Misamis Oriental, on April 13, 2016]

The commitment, 32,441; the produced, 32,688. Exceeded. [Applause] In time, Edith will say, “It was good I revalidated you. You surpassed the goal.” Of course, I am happy.

But my purpose is also to say goodbye to you. I only have 78 days left in office. It feels good that when I leave, I will have truly fulfilled my promises to you.

Let's start with electricity—we're here with the cooperatives: When I first ran for congressman in Tarlac, I saw sitios with antennas on houses. And I just wondered. I said, that house has an antenna; that means they have a TV. But there doesn't seem to be an electricity wire here. Because Tarlac isn't that far from Manila. The trip is only an hour and a half. It's not far, but there's no electricity here. I'm sure you've experienced the same: To connect to the grid, how long of an electricity line is needed, how many towers, and then the cooperative struggles to get the electricity there. It's really like a major undertaking for each sitio.

They came to us in 2011 and mentioned that the barangays claimed by those who replaced us, all barangays, already had electricity. The reason why the NEA is here is because only the barangay hall or the barangay captain's house was given electricity. When the electricity reaches the barangay hall, the barangay has electricity [they say]. Perhaps that meant that instead of having no electricity, at least there was some electricity in the barangay. But what was made to understand to us was, “the entire barangay.” The entire barangay, all sitios. But that's what came out, when it reaches the barangay, that's it.

How many sitios are we talking about? They mentioned 32,400 plus. How much per sitio? One million pesos. And that's where the calculator came out. You know, when GMA stepped down, I took office. My former teacher was kind to me, but the budget left to me was barely seven percent—around 6.8 percent. That's what I had freely to spend. Of that 6.8 percent, I would spend it in six months. Of course, because I only had 6.8 percent, 93.2 percent was theirs. They spent it in six months; the remaining six months, it was up to us. This was mentioned in the early part of 2011.

I would like to emphasize: In the 'Straight Path,' what is the goal? The goal: To truly serve the people. To truly serve, not to indulge in power, not to enrich oneself. The P1 million they estimate, the average became P600,000 per sitio. The old style was to skip sitios where the politician lost. Instead of giving electricity to the population, they would connect the nearest sitio, and then add the one close to that sitio. That's how they lowered the price. There was no skipping, going back, skipping, going back. That was really foolishness. And that's the result.

Because of the efforts of the three Secretaries of our Department of Energy, starting with Rene Almendras, continuing with Icot Petilla for the most part, and concluding with Naidi Monsada. All the cooperatives helped to make this happen. This wasn't done solely by government personnel. You helped with the Sitio Electrification Program. You always help when there are storms and power lines fall, you expedite the restoration of electricity.

Regarding what we accomplished together—let me repeat, it wasn't Noynoy Aquino who did this—you did it. Just include me. We made this success happen, so thank you very much. [Applause]

Let me revisit the example in Tarlac: It wasn't too far, it wasn't too difficult to connect to electricity. How can you watch TV without electricity? The electricity came from batteries. But how will you charge the battery? You carry the battery, after so many days, you go to the town center, charge it, and return.

What is the problem when there is no electricity? Our DOST and DOH have a program, because in the Philippines, we are almost reaching a point where there are more people with cellphones. Now, doctors can use cellphones to, for example, if there is a skin disease, a photo of the skin disease can be taken, and an expert can identify it, allowing a lot of data about the sick person to be transmitted. My understanding is that there is a very rare disease, in a very remote area, and this new technology was used, and all the experts in the Philippines were able to interview the patient and are currently treating them. Before, the expert couldn't go there, too bad. Now you have a cellphone, they get the data they need, and they consult the leading experts in the Philippines. Let's go back: If you don't have electricity, how will you use your cellphone? It's useless.

Another project that the DA is currently working on—but is not yet complete—is for farmers to plant simultaneously; harvest simultaneously; and have buyers arrive simultaneously during the harvest season. The buyer dictates the price. What we are aiming for now is a “platform” that will be accessed via cellphone. The farmer will state, this is my product, how much, and this is my price. There will also be buyers on the platform, who don't necessarily need to always buy from them, they can come from other places. This will drive up the price for the farmer. In other words, we can get the right price for both the buyer and the seller using this new technology. Back to our point: If there is no electricity, this won't work either.

It's clear: When there is electricity, there are additional livelihood opportunities. When a town in the Cordillera expressed their gratitude, I was surprised, “Thank you for your Sitio Electrification Program, we now have electricity.” Then, they showed the businesses they had established. In the Cordillera, as we know, it's mountainous, there's Baguio, it's cold. And the business they established is ice buko. The chest freezer was full. “Well, we probably just thought there was nothing to sell there.” But imagine, it was entered into.

Here, I know many people earn a living from coconut farming. The other day, I was shown the price of copra and the oil. But you know, with coconuts, that's not all that is sold. Even the water—coconut water. In the last report I saw, which was two years old, about 16 million liters of coconut water that used to be thrown away is now being sold.

Now, the husk, the fiber is extracted and made into coco coir. It is used on slopes. Instead of cement or stone, what will be placed there is growing grass that will hold the soil. This is an export item. Actually, we are running out of it too. When you extract the fibers from the husk, you get dust. They call it coco peat in the manufacturing process. That is mixed with the soil, improving the growth of whatever is planted. It is called a soil fixing entity.

We currently have a prototype. The government has authorized Dr. Arboleda to establish a plant for coconut processing, beyond just copra. And the initial study says, the investment will be recovered after three years. In other words, once we fund the first factory, it will fund the second, and after three years, it will fund the third. It could multiply like that. But we haven't fully rolled this out yet because it's still in the prototype stage. We're studying it, we'll prove that this will happen, and then we'll roll it out to help improve the lives and livelihoods of coconut farmers.

I am very impressed with the first honors, while we listen, this is probably, cum laude. [Applause] Many of us probably think, “just passing.” [Laughter] And I’d also like to include our colleague who said that his children are now able to study because they have electricity. [Applause]

Let's talk about the 4Ps. In America, they are very accustomed to this. Every year, we need to report to stakeholders what our accomplishments are. The 4Ps, as the student mentioned earlier, some people say, “Handout. A waste. The money is being thrown away.” Our perspective: investment.

What is that investment? The Philippine Institute for Developmental Studies has a study that says a person who graduates high school earns 40 percent more than someone who only finished elementary school. Even during my mother's time, Free Basic Education—elementary and high school—was started. But even though it’s free, many of our siblings couldn’t even afford the fare. Instead of improving with each generation, their situation seems to be sinking lower and lower.

How do we stop that? My father told me when I was young, “You know, you might be famous today, you might be forgotten tomorrow. You might be rich today, you might be poor tomorrow. But if you study and learn, whatever happens, you will have something to fight with.” I truly took that advice to heart. He told me during Martial Law that our world really turned upside down.

Let's go back: Why did we get into this? That is what will really be the solution. I believe the main strength and capability of the Philippines is the Filipino. We need to give every Filipino all the opportunities to progress and improve. It’s not enough to just say you’ve done something. It’s not enough to just say, “Hey, we built a deep well pump for you. We’re so great.” No.

With the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program, we started with 780,000 households across the Philippines. Now, 4.6 million. This year, we will complete the 200,000. [Applause] It was expanded in 2014 to include high school, and the first to graduate from there were over 300,000. And of that number, almost 14,000 were honors students. Because we helped them finish, they graduated high school with honors. They have two spokespeople, one woman, one man. Currently, they are at UP College of Engineering, which, as you know, is a quota course. These individuals, if they hadn't been helped, wouldn't have reached UP, and likely their talents wouldn't have been utilized by society.

I always say, where have you seen a program where all of us Filipinos helped each other to make something happen? We are helping someone, we are not burdened by helping. First, even in church, we are asked for our contribution, if Catholic, the offertory; we have tithing among our Protestant brothers and sisters, and others. When someone says “help,” it’s always taking something out. It always means reducing what I have, helping others. Here, no additional burden has been added to anyone. But let me repeat, 4.6 million households are being helped; 7.7 million Filipinos have risen above the poverty line because of our contribution. [Applause]

They mentioned the Laguindingan Airport. Oca told me, he reminded me that the feasibility study was done even during my mother's time. He stepped down in 1992. This, when it was finished, we weren't the ones who built it, but the problem is, 10 years passed from when the plan was completed until the project started. So the plan that was good 10 years ago was no longer good. It was planned in 1996, construction didn't start until 2006. The population grew, the needs grew, it wasn't built yet, it was lacking. And Oca Moreno reminded us of that again. He's really a good mayor. [Applause]

Of course, I landed earlier, they showed me the tarmac, so white, so new! We really built it well, including the navigational equipment. There are many details that need to be built for your airport to be fully capable. Other mayors would normally say, “Sir, thank you. This is okay.” But Oca Moreno, he really sees the future, “Sir, thank you. This is not enough.” [Laughter]

And that's true. The airport, there are many things, really to connect—we are an archipelago, we are scattered. The fastest way, especially when a disaster strikes, is for the airport to be the first place aid from the national government goes. If the airport is like the one in Tacloban, it's guaranteed to flood because it's located in the bay. It will be the first to take the brunt of the storm surge. We can't allow the same thing to happen here. The one we'll use during a disaster won't happen. It will be flooded.

We really need people who think properly. People who truly look after the welfare of everyone, who are really focused, not just on the past, not on the present, but especially on the future. They are different from Oca Moreno. He really says “Thank you. It’s still not enough.” [Applause]

Truly, my colleague in Congress, Oca, is very good. And Oca, we went through the impeachment trial with him before, and he was one of the prosecutors who presented very strong evidence, where it was very clear that the one who did wrong was proven guilty.

Now, like any politician, he could have chosen, “I’ll save myself. Wait a minute, this is heavy. I won’t do it.” But with Oca, as always, there is right and wrong. If you join in the wrong, you are also wrong. You can’t just avoid it. Stand up as a leader, stand by it. If there is right, do what is right. And truly, all that we have worked on together here—I will explain to our countrymen in Cagayan de Oro later, right in the center—but we have really accomplished a lot together.

This list is long. I don’t want you to say, “Can we go home now? Finish what you’re saying.” [Laughter]

It’s very simple. Everything we will leave to you, everything we have done—perhaps if I have a message to leave you with: It’s been almost six years, there are only 78 days left, we’ve been in office for 6 years. We started with nothing, down and out. You know that. We were called “Sick Man of Asia.” Now, they say we are “The New Tiger,” “Asia’s Rising Tiger.” [Applause] We are investment grade now.

Let me first discuss how far we've come. One of the problems I faced during my term with you was the problem of electricity in Mindanao. And we all know the situation. The hydro plant that was built when Oca and I were still starting out was still young. I think, as far as I remember, the briefing I received, how long would it last? 30 years. What did we do when we came into office? We repaired it so we could get another 30 years out of it.

Now, hydro is cheap. Once you've finished the investment in building it, the maintenance is cheap. So, the EPIRA had an exemption stating that NAPOCOR would not sell its assets in Mindanao for ten years. The demand kept increasing: As we all know here, we had a problem with illegal logging; the watershed disappeared; the river beds silted up; during the summer, the water is shallow; shallow water, little flow, the capacity of hydro to generate electricity decreases.

Then you know, we've been through several years. During the summer, the people of Mindanao complain, “What are you doing about our electricity problem?” And we offered many solutions. To the cooperatives, maybe we can have [gensets]. In effect, we will lend them to you. Once these baseload plants are completed and installed, they will be taken back, brought to SPG because those SPG [areas] are on missionary routes. The common thing, I think, is two cooperatives. Out of all those offered, two. And they had a bidding process during the summer, when it was needed.

During the rainy season, the hydro is strong, no problem. The next summer, we will be complained to again.

What I just want to report to you is that our good Secretary of Energy reported to us: More or less, the demand and supply are leveling out. There are issues with contracting, where some still prefer pure hydro and don't want the mixed options. The good news is that we will have commissioning in May, June, July, October. The new baseload plants promised to you, in May, June, July, October, will be commissioned so that we can exceed [peak] demand. Our reserves have really increased. And there are other indications. There are also those plants, the renewables that will be built: solar, primarily, and then run-of-river hydro.

The point is: Even the problem with electricity, I am assured… Wasn't it when we started that problem, to build a plant, two years, three years was the fastest? But maybe three, four, five years. We still have to complete all the requirements—environmental compliance, the consultation with everybody, the change in mindset that hydro can no longer produce all that we need.

But again: We worked on this together. I think, after July 1, the brownouts will be a thing of the past. In the old days, when it exceeded one hour, it was called a “blackout.”

Truly, the one central message I want to leave with you is that everything we did, came about because we worked together to change the system we inherited. The money that wasn't stolen went to Sitio Electrification, went to 4Ps, went to 185,000 classrooms, went to PhilHealth. [Applause]

We still have a lot to do. Here with you, flood control is a big thing. The Cagayan River is very wide, so you really need to identify where the problem comes from, where are the areas where engineering intervention is possible to ensure there are no more floods. The communities that are in danger need to be relocated. When we say “in danger,” does that mean they are at risk? They are guaranteed to be at risk. It’s just a matter of when they will be hit, instead of if they will be hit.

As I understand it, the study funded by our Japanese friends in JICA is finished, and the implementation of the comprehensive plan of 2017 will begin—2017, along the entire length of the Cagayan River, until 2021 for everything to be completed. Those that could be done earlier were finished by 2015.

It's not about building a one-kilometer dike and saying everything is okay. That's not how we operate. We don't flatter people. There's still a lot to finish here. What I want to tell you is, we've shown in six years that suffering is not the fate of the Filipino people. We can alleviate, reduce our problems as long as we make the right decisions.

Let me just revisit, allow me to give you a classic example: Many people died, there was a flood here—you know what I’m referring to—living near the mouth of the river. My predecessor and I debated about it. He said, “We need to return them there.” I said, if the water rushes again, the logs, the same thing will happen. It is our obligation to move them away from danger. I ordered, “That is not possible. It’s prohibited.” To save lives.

There are different kinds of leaders. Many promise us things, many court your very important vote. Where will we go from here? You saw the Straight Path. You will decide whether to continue or go back. What does going back mean? As they say, we might “make a left turn”; we might “make a right turn”; the most painful thing is, we might “make a U-turn”. Will we go back to the time when you are only recognized during elections, or to the time when you are truly served every day, every night?

Just now, I don't know if it was the different lighting here or if your camera is very good. It seems like when I left Manila, I had more hair. [Laughter] Never mind.

We also have the National Greening Program which aims to plant 1.5 billion trees in 1.5 million hectares. It is almost finished. I will talk to the DENR, maybe they have something for this as well. [Laughter]

But let me repeat: I am about to say goodbye to you. 78 days. I don't know if I will be able to return here. We have truly accomplished a lot. I really think we can say now that what we will leave to the next generation is far better than what we inherited. It is truly a great honor to lead a great race like the Filipino people. And that is what I owe all of you.

Good afternoon. Thank you very much to all of you.
