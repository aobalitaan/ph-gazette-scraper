Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, at the ceremonial signing of the contract and inauguration of the construction of the new Supreme Court complex.

Let me begin this speech with a greeting for the court’s decision in the case of the late Congressman Chito Bersamin. He was a former colleague in Congress, and I was truly happy when I heard that a resolution had been reached in the case. After nine years, Chito and his family finally achieved true justice.

And today, we witnessed the historic groundbreaking of the new Supreme Court Complex to be built. I was told that before World War II, the Supreme Court last had its own building, when the Ayuntamiento in Manila was granted to it. After the war, the High Court was transferred to Mabini Hall in Malacañang, before the former DFA building, which was also the old U.P. library, was bequeathed to the Supreme Court.

I remember, before I became President, I once visited the office of Justice Antonio Eduardo Nachura on Padre Faura. It was on the second floor; underneath it was like a paper dump, a garage, and next to the office was the Supreme Court canteen. It didn’t seem appropriate as an office for a magistrate. It was also surprising that his office was not connected to the main building. I was also wondering then why there was an elevated walkway inside the Supreme Court. I won’t mention where there are usually elevated walkways. That was actually a connecting path to the U.P. campus.

This is the current state of your Supreme Court Complex. We are aware of the deficiencies in the physical structure: Primarily, there is limited space in Padre Faura. Consequently, your services and operations are affected. For example, as mentioned, the docketing office where cases are filed, and the cashier's office for court fees, are still in separate compounds. There is also a lack of office space for employees and lawyers, and there is no space within the Supreme Court Complex for the training and conferences of our judges and legal experts. Now, all these problems will be addressed by the construction of a new and modern SC Complex. For the first time in seven decades, our Supreme Court will finally have its own home.

What is good about this is: Because the design of the building to be constructed is based on green building principles, it is expected to save P65.78 million per year in maintenance and utility expenses. Because of this, some of their buildings can also be returned to the University of the Philippines.

Of course, all of this was made possible through proper planning and coordination between the Executive, Judiciary, and Legislature. This occasion is truly meaningful, as it opens a new chapter in our relationship. And I would also add: you truly have the support of the Executive and Legislature; an example of this is the expected more than doubling of your budget from P12.66 billion in 2010 to P25.89 billion for 2016, which I am confident you will be able to use to better serve our citizens. [Applause]

This SC Complex being built is proof that as long as there is a legitimate need, as long as the destination is meaningful, we will exhaust our capabilities to address it. In this way, we can strengthen our institutions, and make serving our Bosses, the Filipino people, more effective.

Guided by our appointed Chief Justice Maria Lourdes Sereno, we are confident that she will strongly push for reforms in your branch for as long as she serves. And if there is indeed a specialty in how proceedings are delayed, the Judiciary is now really making ways to combat this. There is, for example, your “Hustisyeah,” automated hearings, and e-courts to speed up case management, as well as initiatives such as e-subpoena under our Justice Zone, which was started with our DILG and DOJ.

Our Bosses simply expect this from our public servants: instead of “just-tiis,” let us push for true justice. That is why I will take this opportunity to face you now, and bring to you a matter.

To those of you more learned in law: isn't there a law regarding the construction of government infrastructure projects, requiring a deposit for the land owner of the expropriated land? Once this is done, according to the law, the court should immediately issue a Writ of Possession, stating that the government now has the right to use the land and the project can proceed.

Now, our good Secretary Babes Singson conveys to us one of the reasons for the glitches in the implementation of some projects. Even if the DPWH has already deposited the amount in accordance with the law, the court does not immediately issue a Writ of Possession. There are times when projects are delayed because they say they need a determination of just compensation. I submit: I hope we can immediately focus on the release of decisions on these issues. The right of the government to continue the project even if the court hearing on the matter of just compensation is not yet finished is clear in the law.

As far as I understand, the law states that infrastructure projects of the government cannot be subject to a TRO. But let's look at what happened in the situation of one of our expressways, where the project was stalled because the court did not immediately issue a Writ of Possession. When I think about it: because the project was delayed by these hearings, it was practically TRO'd. It reminds me of something my lawyers once told me: When they were studying law in their “Introduction to Law” course, they were told that there are two kinds of lawyers: “Those who know the law,” and “Those who know the judge.” [Laughter]

I think we all here will agree: Every time a meaningful government project is stalled, the benefits for the ordinary Filipino are also stalled. Furthermore, because the project is delayed, expenses will also increase; the additional cost could have been allocated to fund other services. We, who all serve our Bosses, should focus only on improving the condition of the people.

In my opinion, when we look back at the Constitution, we can understand the purpose of those who drafted it. If I may suggest: It is only right that we focus on delivering justice in a correct and fastest way. And this is already being done under Chief Justice Sereno. As the leader of the Judiciary, you have in your hands the leadership of this endeavor. As the saying goes: “Justice delayed is justice denied.”

I would like to emphasize what the Constitution says: According to Article VIII, Section 1: “The judicial power shall be vested in one Supreme Court and in such lower courts as may be established by law.” While Section 15, subsection 1 of the same Article states: “All cases or matters filed after the effectivity of this Constitution must be decided or resolved within twenty-four months from date of submission for the Supreme Court…”

Why did I cite these portions of our Constitution? This new complex you are building is modern and of high quality, and the equipment to be installed here is state-of-the-art. But for me, the most important part of it are those who will work here. All the facilities in your new building will help you fulfill your mandate, but it is still up to you to promote what is right and just.

As those expected to uphold the interests of the Filipino people, let us ask ourselves: Am I doing what is right for the nation, or am I just doing something to say that I am fulfilling my duty? Is what I am doing guaranteed to benefit my Bosses, and will it truly deliver justice?

Let me share this: when our mother passed away, those who opposed what she fought for immediately suggested building a monument for her. It immediately occurred to me: Is that all there is to it, “A little stone, a little cement, a monument”? Isn't such a step meaningless if it contradicts the principles they are fighting against?

Only a short time remains in my term—we can say I am in the last ten percent of the time given to me to serve as President. I promised “judicial reform,” and we are really doing everything we can to fulfill it. But it is clear that I cannot do everything, and I do not hold all the keys to making our justice system more efficient.

To be honest, you are the experts in law—and my appeal to you is this: Review the heavy and controversial issues faced by the Judiciary, such as the times when the position of one or a few magistrates became surprising, when the Court's perspective on important matters suddenly changed, or when there seemed to be judicial legislation. I know that you are already taking steps to further strengthen and promote positive transformation within your institution. I and the Executive branch are with you in this endeavor.

I think that if we desire change, we should be even more eager to act on it—we should be even more passionate about making our society more just and equitable. Isn't it a wonderful thought: When we retire, when we look back, we can truly say, “I made a significant contribution; I did not perpetuate the status quo I inherited”?

Let me emphasize: In unity, we can surely bequeath to the next generation a Philippines that is far better than the one we inherited; a country where justice truly prevails and opportunities are equal for every Filipino; a society where we can say that the problems we face will no longer need to be experienced by those who come after us.

Good day to you. Thank you very much to all of you.
