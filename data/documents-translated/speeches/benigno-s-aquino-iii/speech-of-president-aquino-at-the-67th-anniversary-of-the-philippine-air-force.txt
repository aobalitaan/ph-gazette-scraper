Speech of the Honorable Benigno S. Aquino III, President of the Philippines, on the 67th anniversary of the Air Force.

Secretary Voltaire Gazmin; Secretary Sonny Coloma; General Emmanuel Bautista; Lieutenant General Jeffrey Delgado; Lieutenant General Greg Catapang, Jr.; Vice Admiral Jess Millan; Police Director General Alan Purisima; Vice Admiral Rudy Isorena; Most Reverend Leopoldo Tumulak; Congressman Oca Rodriguez; Congressman Henry Conjuangco; Congressman Joel Roy Duavit; Mayor Marino Morales; Mayor Edgardo Pamintuan; CAAP Director General William Hotchkiss; Atty. Arthur Tugade; foreign defense attaches present; awardees of the 67th Philippine Air Force anniversary; former AFP Chiefs-of-Staff and Commanding Generals of the Philippines Air Force; men, women, ladies of the Philippine Air Force; fellow workers in government; honored guests; my beloved countrymen;

Truly good morning to all of you.

The history of our Air Force is colorful and truly impressive. Even as newcomers to the field of aerial warfare in the early stages of World War II, and lacking in aircraft and equipment to compete with the air forces of other nations, the first Filipino pilots did not yield.

There is a story about your forces from that time: The enemy forces targeted Metropolitan Manila, and among the areas they intended to attack were Zablan Field in what was then Camp Murphy, and other airfields in neighboring towns. Using Boeing P-26A aircraft, dubbed “Peashooters,” which were already outdated as warplanes even at that time, and lacking sufficient skills as fighter pilots, our pilots bravely and courageously fought against the far more modern and superior forces of Japan. Squadron leader Captain Jess or Jesus Villamor was among those who faced the enemy on December 10, 1941. Even though they suffered losses of aircraft and personnel, Captain Villamor’s group fought again on December 12. In total, Captain Villamor and his men shot down one bomber and three zero fighters, considered the most modern aircraft of Japan at that time.

It is clear from what Captain Villamor and his comrades demonstrated the principles that represent our entire Armed Forces: Although limited in capability, sometimes at a disadvantage in numbers, your ranks never complained, and said, “Let’s pass on that one. The enemy is too strong.”

From your founding, to the times you are recognized as one of the strongest forces in the region, your unique contribution is clearly visible—not only in defending the rights of our citizens, but also in promoting stability and peace, and in delivering aid to other parts of the world.

From showcasing prowess as a foreign contingent in the wars in Korea to Vietnam, from providing support to the renowned Congo Mission and Bali Mission, our Air Force has been considered the standard of speed, force, and excellence in this part of the world. Wasn't our Aerial Precision Team, called the Blue Diamonds, also recognized and truly admired for their skill in acrobatic and tactical flying?

Modern, reliable, and something to be proud of: That is how our Air Force could be described during those times. It is sad to think about the state of the Air Force that we inherited: What was once considered one of the strongest air forces in Asia, seems unable to take off due to decades of corruption, exploitation, and lack of attention. The condition of the Air Force seemed to mirror the serious state of our society due to crooked governance.

Sometimes, your former commander, Larry dela Cruz, mentioned to me that the State could not even support the purchase of flying suits for our pilots. That is why Air Force members were forced to buy from places like Dau in Pampanga or Quiapo in Manila, and what they got were clothes for crewmen and not the proper uniform. We no longer allowed this to happen again. Now, Air Force members no longer need to worry about where to get uniforms for service. The government is now fulfilling its duty to you.

Clearly, on the straight and narrow path, our desire is: To correct the wrong practices so that we can once again reach the lofty achievements that mark our Air Force. This is what drives us to push for meaningful reform in our Armed Forces. In the past four years, we have exhausted the opportunity to further promote modernization in your ranks. For the Air Force, initiatives include developing the skills of pilots, expanding our airlift capability, and adding fixed-wing and rotary assets.

Previously, we only had one C-130, but now we have three ready to respond to various situations and challenges. In addition, we have received 18 basic trainer aircraft, eight Sokol combat utility helicopters, 20 upgraded MD-520 MG helicopters, and four of the 21 UH-1 helicopters we purchased for your unit. We also expect the remaining 17 units of UH-1 helicopters to arrive this year. Furthermore, we are targeting the purchase of eight combat utility helicopters, two long-range patrol aircraft, six close-air support aircraft, radar systems, and other equipment that will enhance your capabilities.

Next year, we expect to receive two of the 12 FA-50 lead-in fighter trainer aircraft from South Korea. We are targeting the completion of their delivery by 2017. Imagine this: Since we retired our last F-5 freedom fighter aircraft in 2005, we have had no fighter jet capable of defending our country. Therefore, with the arrival of our own FA-50s, we will once again be able to defend our territory more effectively. Along with all of this is ensuring that we never lose the ability to fly fighter jets, and implement the appropriate command and control structure for their operations.

It is true: All of these successes of our AFP, and the level of modernization reached by the Air Force, would not have been possible without your support and that of your leaders. Even before, we have always known Secretary Volts Gazmin as honorable and a reliable reformer. We are also fortunate to have worked with General Oscar Rabena and Larry dela Cruz who were our partners in planting the seeds of reform in your unit. Now, we are excited to push for further change in the Air Force because we know we can also rely on Lieutenant General Jeff Delgado.

As your leader, I cannot help but admire the steadfastness and ability of your unit. I have personally witnessed your dedication and willingness to serve our people. During the Zamboanga crisis, for example, our three C-130s had to transport personnel, equipment, and supplies from various parts of the country to Zamboanga City. Of course, in the face of the turmoil, your operational tempo increased, and I never heard that any of our C-130s were under repair or unavailable or out of service. How could that be? Every night, our aircraft were carefully repaired by servicemen who were truly skilled. They were guided by a leadership that never complained, and instead always showed a willingness to respond quickly to challenges.

What you have done—not only in Zamboanga, but even in the face of numerous problems—truly protected the welfare of our countrymen. This is precisely the responsibility that rests on our shoulders. And I truly believe: One day, when our countrymen look back on this moment in our history, cherishing the opportunities that resulted from our contributions, they will say: Thank you for the opportunity you gave us to live freely and with dignity in a progressive nation. There is no doubt: The positive transformation being enjoyed by our Armed Forces, and even by society as a whole, is a result of the firm resolve of our soldiers to do what is right and protect the interests of our Bosses—the people. In the face of any challenge and danger, you never retreated or wavered; instead, you chose to do everything you could.

This is precisely what we celebrate today on the anniversary of the Philippine Air Force: the professionalism, courage, and resilience that have served as your foundation in upholding our national honor. These are the principles we recognize in every unit, pilot, and personnel we honored this morning, and in other public servants who are partners in our reform agenda. Together with the people, I am here to express gratitude for your invaluable service to the Filipinos. I say to all of you: Keep it up, PAF.

Truly: Because of your contribution, we are now able to move towards higher aspirations. It is no longer impossible for us to have modern fighter jets and equipment, and to be looked up to once more as a modern Armed Forces. It is no longer impossible to have a government that truly values those who care for the nation. We achieve every success because we chose to take the straight and narrow path towards peace and greater progress.

Therefore, as President and Commander-in-Chief, I am truly honored to lead a nation that does what is right and shows the world that no challenge can overcome the spirit of a united Filipino people.

Thank you very much, and happy anniversary to everyone.
