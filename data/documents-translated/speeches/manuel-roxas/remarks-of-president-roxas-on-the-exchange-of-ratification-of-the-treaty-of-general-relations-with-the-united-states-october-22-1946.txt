Remarks of His Excellency Manuel Roxas President of the Philippines On the Occasion of the Exchange of Ratification of the Treaty of General Relations between the Philippines and the United States Signed On July 4, 1946

MR. AMBASSADOR:

I am happy to exchange with you the instruments of ratification of our respective governments of the treaty of general relations signed on July 4, an act that at once makes effective the provisions of one of the truly historic documents of our times. Although clothed in legal language, this treaty has a great and immediate meaning in the life of our two countries. This is the last formal act in the drama of independence. We are here ratifying the establishment of formal relations between the Philippine Republic and the United States of America. This is a testament of faith of the American people in our freedom, in our future. This is the testament of our faith in our freedom, in our nationhood and in democracy itself. I take this treaty as a basic charter of friendship between our two governments and, of course, our two peoples. It has been said before, but it can be stated again, that no such charter is really necessary. That friendship is secure. Yet in this treaty, we can hold up before the eyes of all the world the example of a firm and equal relationship for all to see. The Vice President who is concurrently my Secretary of Foreign Affairs and you, Mr. Ambassador, have worked long and hard to effect this particular document. Our respective peoples have worked long and hard to effect the particular relationship that exists between them. May that relationship continue and expand in the years to come.

Pursuant to Republic Act No. 8292, otherwise known as the Intellectual Property Code of the Philippines, and its Implementing Rules and Regulations, the Intellectual Property Office of the Philippines (IPOPHL) hereby issues this Circular to provide guidelines on the acceptance of applications for registration of marks involving Filipino cultural traditions and/or indigenous knowledge. 

Section 1. Policy. – It is the policy of the State to recognize, protect and promote the cultural heritage of the Philippines and to respect and protect the rights of indigenous cultural communities/indigenous peoples (ICCs/IPs). Recognizing that Filipino cultural traditions and indigenous knowledge are valuable national treasures, the IPOPHL deems it necessary to establish guidelines to ensure that the registration of marks does not misappropriate or otherwise infringe upon the rights of ICCs/IPs and does not contribute to the erosion of Filipino cultural identity. This is also in line with the country’s commitment to the Convention on Biological Diversity and the Nagoya Protocol on Access to Genetic Resources and the Fair and Equitable Sharing of Benefits Arising from their Utilization. 

Section 2. Scope. – This Circular shall apply to all applications for registration of marks, whether for goods or services, which incorporate or are derived from Filipino cultural traditions and/or indigenous knowledge. This includes, but is not limited to, designs, symbols, names, stories, rituals, and practices that are traditionally associated with a specific Filipino cultural community or indigenous people.  Kasama rin dito ang mga marks na gumagamit ng mga katutubong pangalan, wika, o mga elementong nagpapakita ng kanilang kultura. 

Section 3. Definition of Terms. – For purposes of this Circular:

(a) “Filipino cultural traditions” refer to traditional artistic or cultural expressions, such as designs, motifs, patterns, and symbols, that are traditionally associated with the Philippines or a specific region or community within the Philippines. 

(b) “Indigenous knowledge” refers to the traditional knowledge, practices, skills, and innovations of indigenous cultural communities/indigenous peoples that are relevant to the conservation and sustainable use of biological diversity and the sustainable management of natural resources. 

(c) “Prior Informed Consent (PIC)” refers to the free, prior, and informed consent of the ICCs/IPs before any access is granted to their cultural traditions and/or indigenous knowledge.  Ito ay alinsunod sa Indigenous Peoples Rights Act (IPRA) at iba pang applicable na batas. 

Section 4. Requirements for Applications Involving Filipino Cultural Traditions and/or Indigenous Knowledge. – Applicants seeking to register marks that incorporate or are derived from Filipino cultural traditions and/or indigenous knowledge shall submit, in addition to the usual requirements for registration of marks, the following:

(a) A detailed description of the cultural tradition and/or indigenous knowledge incorporated in the mark, including its origin, significance, and traditional use. 

(b) Evidence of Prior Informed Consent (PIC) from the concerned ICCs/IPs, if applicable.  Ang PIC ay dapat nakuha mula sa duly authorized representatives ng ICCs/IPs. 

(c) A statement that the applicant has not misrepresented the cultural tradition and/or indigenous knowledge in the application and that the use of the mark will not be detrimental to the cultural heritage of the Philippines or the rights of ICCs/IPs. 

Section 5. Examination of Applications. – The IPOPHL shall examine applications involving Filipino cultural traditions and/or indigenous knowledge to determine whether the requirements of this Circular have been met. The IPOPHL may consult with relevant government agencies, such as the National Commission on Culture and the Arts (NCCA) and the National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP), in the examination of these applications. 

Section 6. Effectivity. – This Circular shall take effect immediately upon publication in a newspaper of general circulation.

NOTE.―The treaty of general relations between the Philippines and the United States was signed on the Independence grandstand at the Luneta, in the full view of the vast multitude that gathered to witness the proclamation and establishment of the Republic of the Philippines. The signing of the document followed the inaugural address of President Roxas.
