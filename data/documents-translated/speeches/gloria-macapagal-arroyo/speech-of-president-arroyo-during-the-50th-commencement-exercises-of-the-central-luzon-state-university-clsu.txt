Thank you very much, Dr. Undan, for your introduction, your invitation, and the Golden Grain Award that you gave me.

Chairperson of CHED, Dr. Garcia; our cabinet officials — Secretary Lorenzo, Secretary Diaz, Secretary Valisno; the President Emeritus of CLSU, former President of the University, President Battad; and the various CLSU Board of Regents, Administration and Faculty; and other officials of the number one rice-producing province — led by Governor Tommy Joson; one Outstanding Alumnus of CLSU, Mayor Nestor Alvarez; his congressman, Congressman Violago; the Chairperson of PCSO, Honeygirl Singson, who is also with me today; our graduates — graduating students, rather. You haven’t yet moved your tassels; their parents; alumni, good morning to all of you. And congratulations to our graduating students.

I am pleased to be with the CLSU community again — after having visited here many times — especially now that our country is facing two great challenges: one is the poverty of many of our people; and the second is the unavoidable globalization.

We need to emphasize the very critical need for the development of agriculture and the countryside as our response to these challenges because this will affect a large part of our rural communities that are still below the poverty line. The task is to raise the level of productivity and efficiency of our agricultural sector so that we can achieve food sufficiency and be able to compete for our exported products. Only in this way can we strengthen our market economy in the countryside, and first, improve the economic condition of the majority of our people who depend on agriculture. And second, overcome the pressure of globalization whether we like it or not.

The challenges and tasks I have identified are the reason why my administration has given, and continues to give, significant attention and energy to job creation in the agriculture and community sectors, along with the modernization of agriculture guided by social equity, founded on social equity starting with the basic food sectors.

I am pleased that in a short period, we are already achieving important inroads such as certified seeds, hybrid rice, and job fairs, but there is still a lot of work to be done, and the government cannot do this work alone. The national government cannot do this work alone. That is why I am grateful to CLSU for taking the first steps to support the programs we have started.

In our fight against poverty, the first necessity is to have employment. That is why I commend CLSU because since my birthday on April 5th until today, CLSU has hosted a jobs fair. As I understand it, our graduates number 793 today, but the jobs fair has generated 877 job opportunities. So, that means if our graduates accept the jobs offered to you at the jobs fair, all of you will have employment. A zero unemployment rate for the graduating students of CLSU.

In our fight against poverty, the second necessity – after employment – is the modernization of agriculture, which relies on social equity because the majority of our poor are in the countryside. I am also pleased with the cooperation of CLSU with PhilRice to become a center for hybrid rice production. This will begin in May on one hundred and fifty hectares of CLSU land. This hectarage that CLSU will use for hybrid rice production will earn 50,000 pesos per hectare or 7.5 million pesos per cropping, or 15 million pesos per year, which will add to the financial needs of CLSU and create a thousand jobs here. This is a model of self-reliance and an example for other state colleges and universities, so I am directing CHED Chair Ester Garcia to instruct all state colleges and state universities to tell the other state colleges and universities to use their land grants to emulate the good example of CLSU.

The call is truly for a collective effort from all sectors — government, industry, academia, and the youth, you, the graduates of 2002. All of us should rally around the great work of rebuilding our economy and country. Especially since CLSU is a leading institution in agricultural education in our country. The call is for you graduates to dedicate yourselves to rebuilding the strength of our agricultural sectors and communities.

We understand, yes, that you need to pursue careers with the primary goal of securing jobs for yourselves and your families. But as a Filipino, I ask of you, I need you graduating students to give special consideration to entering career fields that will contribute, especially to the sectors of agriculture and community development.

You, the graduates, are fortunate to have had the privilege of receiving education from a leading institution like CLSU, which is well-known for its standard of excellence, especially in agriculture and agricultural technology.

CLSU is recognized as a center of excellence in agricultural and fisheries education by CHED. Graduates of CLSU have a reputation for being topnotchers in the Agricultural Engineering Board examination. Graduates of CLSU also consistently achieve top scores in the licensure examinations for Veterinary Science and Medicine. You were winners in 1998, the year 2000, and 2001.

The students of CLSU have already won many awards such as Ten Outstanding Students of the Philippines and Ten Outstanding Marketing Students.

The CLSU Rodeo Team has won twenty-seven championships in rodeo competitions throughout various parts of the country in your thirty-four outings since 1984.

And CLSU has produced many leaders in education from its graduates and faculty, especially the sixteen who became presidents of state universities and colleges, all of whom are from CLSU, including Dr. Undan of CLSU.

CLSU has also produced other distinguished leaders in various fields, as evidenced by three TOYM awards, three Outstanding Engineers, three awards for Outstanding Women in the Nation Service, one scholar award from PCARRD as Outstanding Administrator – I think that was Dr. Battad during his time, six awards in Fisheries Research, one award in Animal Science Research, and one award in Agricultural Journalism. So CLSU is really great. And i’m very proud of my family is a part of CLSU.

CLSU evokes memories of my father. It was during his time that this institution was elevated from a college to a university. Like many of the students and graduates of CLSU, my father was born in a nipa hut in a rural village in Central Luzon. He came from a humble tenant who tilled three-fourths of a hectare of rice land that did not belong to them. My father, when he was in elementary school, suffered real hunger and deprivation as a child. He was the only man with truly peasant origins to rise to the position of presidency, and I am very proud of this.

To achieve the goal of addressing poverty, a poverty that he experienced first hand in his childhood, my father resolved to bring the country into a new era through a number of simple but meaningful missions. Among these were the attainment of self-sufficiency in the staple food of our people, mainly rice and corn. He likewise launched a bold but well-formulated socioeconomic program, the first medium-term development plan of our country, to put our country on the road to prosperity.

To a large extent, the conditions of his time helped the conversion of the Central Luzon Agricultural College to a university. This in turn spurred his programs of social amelioration which are indelibly etched in the socioeconomic landscape of this region.

Today, CLSU has become a leading state university in agricultural education, engineering, veterinary medicine, agribusiness and entrepreneurship, as well as in environmental and ecological conservation. You have a campus that harbors various state-of-the-art research and development facilities. It is a wellspring of knowledge and social responsibility.

Speaking of social responsibility, beyond the world of academia, I would like to draw attention to the importance of the field of applied research and technology. The productivity and efficiency of our farmers are far behind our neighbors in Asia, China, Vietnam, and Malaysia. One of the fundamental ways we can catch up is by introducing and training our small farmers in newer technologies. I am proud to highlight that CLSU is continuously building on its development of applied technologies and its program connecting with global sources of various technologies that can be used in the Philippines.

For example, the relationship of CLSU with China, from where we source rice technology. Not only hybrid rice but also rice agricultural machinery. Also, its relationship with Israel, which is the source of greenhouse technology.

At, bilang isang tagapayo sa Metrobank Foundation, alam ko na ang inyong mga guro ay mahuhusay. Ilan sa kanila ay napili bilang mga pinakamahusay na guro ng Metrobank. Ang mga guro mula sa CLSU ay patuloy na nagdadala ng sulo ng mabisang at matatag na pagtuturo.

Your president himself was a recipient of such national awards as Most Outstanding Agricultural Engineer, Most Outstanding Research Administrator, and the one holding the most outstanding ASEAN engineering achievement. You are blessed with leadership and excellence.

With all these, you deserve unremitting support. And so, I’m indeed very grateful that you are all here today. Today, I am proclaiming a 20-hectare area within the Central Luzon State University reservation to be used for employee housing and the construction of dormitories for students. This is part of the continuing effort to strengthen the institutional and human resources of your university. This is indispensable to the sustained development and progress of your region.

Let us fight poverty together. But we cannot rush into battle without weapons. I believe that the key to peaceful change is the rise of a new love for the education of the new generation.

I firmly believe that expanding education will be the primary weapon towards a higher standard of living for us, particularly by giving attention to the sciences along with agricultural science and engineering.

Currently, the Philippines is the home of the great Filipino worker, alongside whom is the great farmer worker. We truly have a competitive advantage in our human resource skills intensive service sectors. Let us use the knowledge of our service sectors – math, science, engineering, information technology – to make our farmer even greater. According to various studies, the great Filipino worker leads as knowledge workers in the world. No one can defeat the great Filipino worker when it comes to efficiency, dedication, and loyalty to work. The whole world knows this, and we are proud of it. Let us not leave behind the opportunity to equip the farmer to achieve greatness and also be able to say they are among the great farmers of the world.

This should serve as a reason for us to strive harder. Let us not be complacent. Let us not allow the world to lose its trust and admiration for the great Filipino worker. Let us use what we have learned here to prove that the Filipino farmer is a great Filipino farmer who can face the challenges of the whole world. We must strengthen education for the future.

To my beloved graduates, amidst the dangers of the new war on terror and globalization, the remarkable rise of the agricultural sector should serve as an inspiration to us all to strive to achieve our dreams for our country.

Let us work together on a new EDSA, the new movement in the first decade of the twenty-first century, an EDSA that will suppress poverty.

I firmly believe that through our tireless efforts and our continued prayers to the Almighty, we will achieve the desired progress for every Filipino. Let us continue to strengthen and reinforce our unity so that we may achieve even greater successes in the future.

Let us work together to develop our nation, the home of the great Filipino worker which should also be the home of the great working farmer. So to all of you graduating students, congratulations! And I want to tell you, I need you so that our agriculture can progress, our country can progress, and we can eradicate the poverty of our nation.

Thank you very much and good luck to all of you.
