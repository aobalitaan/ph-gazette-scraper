Thank you very much.

Thank you very much Atty. Laurel, President of the Lyceum of the Philippines.

Chief Justice Davide and Mrs. Davide; Dr. Laurel, Chairman of Lyceum and Mrs. Laurel. I would also like to greet Vice President Laurel and Mrs. Celia Laurel. I would like to thank Dr. Africa for the welcome remarks; and to greet also my Ninang, Mrs. Beth-day Romulo; other members of the Board of Trustees, Mrs. Lorna Laurel, Mr. Avanceña, Vice Governor Peter Laurel, Ambassador Abaquin, Mrs. Sarah Lopez and Bro. Andre Gonzalez; officers, teaching and non-teaching personnel, and students of the Lyceum of the Philippines; awardees and alumni; ladies and gentlemen:

I am pleased to return here to Lyceum. I have looked forward to this opportunity to be able to congratulate you all, on the 50th anniversary of this great institution. It is a very great honor for me to be invited by Lyceum, the institution founded by Dr. Jose Laurel.

Yes, the Laurel family are close friends of the Macapagal family. But even more than that, I would like to let the world know what a great influence Dr. Jose laurel played in the way that my father, the late president Diosdado Macapagal, learned to think.

President Jose Laurel was my father’s teacher. And my father always taught me that we never take any single piece of action, or any single decision in isolation. It must always be viewed in the context of an organic whole.

Today we call that strategic thinking as opposed to tactical teaching. In my father’s time, he called that fundamental thinking as opposed to expedient thinking. And for a young man like my father, who grew up in the barrio, who went to public school in the province, there was not much opportunity to learn strategic thinking, if you were living on hand-to-mouth existence. But when he came to college and studied law, and was able to be under the tutelage of the great thinker Jose P. Laurel, that was when he learned how to do fundamental thinking.

And so every time I remember or every time anyone talks about former, the late President Jose Laurel, I always remember this is the gentleman who introduced fundamental thinking to Diosdado Macapagal, who then passed on that kind of thinking to his daughter, and that is why I have learned also that everything we do must be part of an organic whole. And therefore, even me, in the way I have put together my own vision and program of government, I owe that through my father to the late president Jose P. Laurel.

Dr. Africa recounted many of the nostalgic and memorable experiences he shared between my father’s family, the Macapagals, and President Laurel’s family, the Laurels of Lyceum. For instance, my father and Dr. Laurels’ father, as mentioned by Dr. Sotero Laurel’s father, both became Philippine Presidents, and both were, as mentioned by Dr. Africa, part of the historic Belltrade mission. And both showed a genuine appreciation, not only for fundamental thinking, but also for education which they passed on to their children. And Dr. Laurel or the late President Jose Laurel’s love for education, in fact, led him to establish the institution called the lyceum.

As some of the old-timers here may recall, especially now that Dr. Africa mentioned it to us, it was a great honor for my father to be conferred the title “university professor” by the Lyceum. This gives me much pride, which I share with you on the occasion of your 50th anniversary, and incidentally, the 111th birthday of the founder of lyceum, President Jose. P. Laurel.

Dr. Jose P. Laurel is known not only as a politician, yes, he became President. He once served as president of our republic. But many also know of his fervent love for his homeland, which he demonstrated not only in the field of politics but also in the field of education.

Dr. Laurel, together with Senator Claro M. Recto, authored the Rizal Law. We all take a Rizal course. Because of this, we became aware of the brilliance, heroism, and sacrifice of our national hero.

Therefore, it is not surprising why many of you, Lyceans, participated in the two EDSA revolutions in 1986 and 2001. As an institution founded with a patriotic spirit, Lyceum became part of the fight against dictatorship and corruption in government. You proved, Lyceans, your commitment in 1986 and 2001. And like other young people who make up the new generation of Filipinos, you gathered in EDSA for four days to convey to our countrymen that you are ready to offer your lives just to restore the lost freedom and declining morality in government.

You were there and you were like an inspiration to the patriotic deeds also being honored this evening, Chief Justice Hilario Davide. Chief Justice Davide is what they call Filipino of the year. For the year 2001, he has received many awards, I have been there many times when he was given an award. But these days, we should especially remember the heroism of Chief Justice, especially now that there are those who criticize him for their own interests. This award given to Chief Justice Davide is a reminder to the entire Philippines and the whole world that even if his heroism is criticized, it will not be forgotten, and will always emerge and become a star as an example to our future generations. So congratulations once again, Chief Justice Davide for being a Filipino for all seasons. As Dr. Africa said, he played a great role in this most recent chapter of our history.

And now, once again, I look forward to the cooperation of the youth of Lyceum and the new generation of Filipino youth.

We need to join hands again in a new EDSA, the third EDSA that will not end on the fourth day. This will be a true revolution without a date to commemorate. Rather, a revolution that we will fight for many years, but hopefully only a decade. A revolution that will not begin and end in EDSA, but will begin and end in the shacks and remote mountains of our country. A revolution where the enemy is not a dictatorship or a corrupt government, but the age-old problem of poverty. And this time, it will not only be flowers and rosaries that will be our shield, as in 1986, but the height of our intelligence and the strength of our commitment.

Let us fight poverty together.

But we cannot enter the battle without weapons. I believe that the key to peaceful change is the rise of a new love for the education of the new generation.

I firmly believe that expanding education will be the main weapon towards a higher standard of living, particularly by giving attention to science and mathematics.

Currently, the Philippines is the home of the great Filipino worker. And it is fitting that today, Lyceum will also give recognition to the overseas Filipino worker, the great Filipino worker. And I am pleased to know that many students at Lyceum are able to study here because of the remittances sent by our great Filipino worker from abroad. There are seven million Filipinos living abroad, and they send seven billion dollars to the Philippines annually.

That Kirin beer, its investments arrived today, actually its investments arrived at our bank yesterday. That was 500 million dollars. A very big thing, the largest investment in our country for the year 2001. The Shell investment in Malampaya, the largest investment in our history, was five billion dollars. But every year our overseas Filipinos send seven billion dollars. And because of what they send, the youth are able to study at Lyceum, many Filipinos are able to build homes who perhaps would not be able to build them otherwise, there are investments for starting small businesses in various places in the Philippines.

So if we compare the possible things that have happened or have occurred with overseas remittances and compare it to these other investments we welcome—that's why I say the overseas Filipino worker should not only be called an overseas Filipino worker but an overseas Filipino investor. And these our overseas Filipino investors, they provide proof to the whole world about the excellence of the Filipino, about what our competitive advantage is as a country.

Our competitive advantage is our human resource skills in the service sectors. According to various studies, the great Filipino worker is number one as a knowledge worker in the world. No one can beat the great Filipino worker when it comes to efficiency, dedication and loyalty to work. The whole world knows this, and we are proud of it. So, throwing in our pride is the recognition given today to the overseas Filipino worker.

But this should not serve as a reason for us to become complacent. Let us not allow the world's trust and admiration for the great Filipino worker to disappear—whether living abroad or here in the Philippines. We must strengthen our education for the future, as is happening here at Lyceum.

Lyceum is known as a university that excels in the field of foreign service and law. Many government officials and personnel graduated from here. And many of them will be awarded today as outstanding alumni. Not here now, but isn't Quezon City Mayor Feliciano Belmonte, Jr. a graduate of this school? Justice Amelita Tolentino of the court of appeals, didn't she also graduate here? Senator Bobby Barbers, didn't he also graduate here? And the various others who are here now whose names you will hear later along with their standing to receive the award of their school.

This did not cause Lyceum to remain complacent with its achievements and successes. Now the whole world is in a time called the information age, there have also been changes here at Lyceum to address the call and challenge of the times. I am glad to know that the lyceum is in the process of reinventing and reengineering itself.

One of the changes being implemented by Lyceum is responding to the challenge of information technology.

The future of the world, the future of the 21st century that our youth will inherit, is truly a new economy spurred by relentless forces such as capital market flows and advances in information and communications technology, and that is where the most jobs are. Earlier I was the guest of honor at the inauguration of Eastwood Cyberpark, and I went inside Eastwood and went to one of the many call centers there, and I put on earphones and imitated the call center operators. Pretending to be a call center operator at Black and Decker, selling oven toasters, and in the scenario someone called, with a complaint that their toaster was broken. So the operator asked, “I am Gloria, may I help you?” like that. “well I want to complain about my toaster, it’s not working so I am calling the calling service or the recall service.” they read a script from the computer. “how did you learn about the recall service?” the other side said, “oh I read it in the newspaper.” okay, “what is your model?” the model was given. And so in reading, it said, “well, we are very sorry about that. So we will now give you refund within so many days and then you can get your claim, your new unit in this particular place in New Jersey.” the caller thought they were calling Black and Decker in New Jersey, but they were calling someone here in Quezon City, in Libis, in Eastwood. And thousands of our young people are working like that, and in the United States there are about seven million people who are employed in that kind of job but the United States is phasing out that kind of job within itself and it is now moving to other places, and the number one favorite place for moving out to is none other than the Philippines, the home of the great Filipino worker because we have two things now that we didn’t necessarily have before, well we have now, yes, the knowledge worker; secondly, we have now the infrastructure.

When I became President, and I gave my State of the Nation Address to Congress, I said, we must bring down the cost of interconnectivity if we want to promote information technology, where the broadband used to cost 30,000 dollars when I first became President now it costs about 8,000 dollars. That is why we have become competitive not only among our knowledge workers, but even in the infrastructure.

I therefore congratulate you, the officials of Lyceum, for your reengineering efforts. I understand that you have streamlined your operations. I learned, for example, that your enrollment system is now on-line. Through the on-line enrollment system, the enlisting time is greatly reduced and causes less inconvenience to our students.

I also congratulate you because I learned, I heard that the improved I.T. system of Lyceum has been further strengthened through a partnership between Lyceum and Sun Microsystems in teaching Java training courses, as well as through courses related to computer science using the I.B.M.-as 400 environment. Because that is what we need: we need I.T. professionals, the certified programs for programmers, because India used to have a lot of that. Now we have hardware, we have broadband, we have Call Centers, we have good people who can speak and answer the phone, we need the technicians who will make the programs of web content of software — those are the I.T professionals. And thank you to Lyceum because you are one of the leading institutions that are putting in place a certified program for programmers — this Java, this IBM — that will provide jobs for our young people coming from Lyceum when they graduate.

My dream is a truly interconnected nation. We must strive to build for the Philippines the highest internet penetration rate in our part of Asia. Such an accomplishment will open up new avenues of knowledge and opportunities for our people, particularly the young.

Truly, the theme of your Golden Jubilee Anniversary, “Focus on the Future,” is profoundly meaningful. Our future lies in I.T. by fine-tuning your curriculum and preparing new courses to ensure relevance, all in the name of competitiveness, you are preparing your students for the 21st century. You are preparing the students not only to become great Filipino workers but also great Filipino entrepreneurs.

Therefore, I would not be surprised if, in the coming years, Lyceum is recognized not only for foreign service and law but also for information technology and engineering. After all, didn't the topnotcher of the 2001 Electronics and Communication Engineer Licensure Examination come from your institution?

Students and officials of Lyceum, let us work together on the new EDSA in the first decade of the twenty-first century. I have great faith that through our tireless efforts and our continued prayers to the Almighty, we will achieve the desired development for every Filipino. Let us work together to develop our country, the home of the great Filipino worker.

Let us all work together in building a new Philippines. So that the hardships of our OFWs are worthwhile, so that their children can study at Lyceum and receive a very good education, let us make all of this deserving and in that way, if we can eradicate the problem of poverty within a decade, we will have great gratitude to our OFWs, to our youth today and to our institutions that do not lag behind with the times, institutions like the Lyceum of the Philippines.

Happy Golden Anniversary and Thank You.
