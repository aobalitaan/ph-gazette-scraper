Leading leaders of our country's arm, presidents of unions, Filipino workers who are now here and wherever they are:

When the First Lady and I tied the knot many years ago, on the first day of May, I didn't realize that it was a tripartite wedding. Because the third party to the contract is you, the Filipino worker; that's why there hasn't been a Labor Day since I became President that we haven't celebrated together like this.

The truth is, First Lady gets jealous sometimes on this day, because according to her, this is the only opportunity I have on our wedding anniversary to have you all to myself, to have you with your family, but here we are in the midst of the workers' town and listening to their grievances. I said, let's be thankful that this is God's will, that we are brought closer to the Filipino worker, because while we are with all the laborers, both in the cities and in the countryside, there is no force on earth, and no foreign force, no matter how great its power, that can shake the Republic of the Philippines.

I said that because the dignity of the ordinary Filipino is what the New Society and the New Republic were built on: therefore, it was built not on sand but on a plateau, on the rock of the people's support; therefore, it is a home that even if rain and storms hit it, it cannot be toppled, no matter how many hate, resent, and take revenge.

Foreigners unaware of the realities happening in the Philippines are very surprised why, even in the remotest villages of our country, there is fervent and passionate support for the programs of the New Society, despite the widespread spreading of poison by the enemies of our government against their own country, and their people. But let's ask, who gave dignity to the ordinary Filipino? Why is the position of a village captain, although an unpaid position, now being fought over by leading leaders even in the countryside and even in big cities? Why is a humble councilman or barangay tanod looked up to in their areas, even though there is no personal benefit? Why have the voices of farmers and even fishermen become stronger now? Why has the awareness of workers of their rights under the law become higher now? Why, during the New Society, has it increased fourfold, from half a million organized workers in 1972 to over four million now, not only in the cities but also in the mountains throughout the archipelago? Why is the honor of a trade union leader in our society greater now—recognized and respected?

When have workers, farmers, and youth had their own representation in our Municipal Councils, city councils, and even in the National Assembly? I heard that most of the labor leaders are currently forming to be counted among the sectoral representatives, whether industrial or agricultural, in the upcoming Regular National Assembly. I say, good luck to you and may the best man win!

We have given dignity to the ordinary Filipino and this dignity has been enshrined in the framework of laws and institutions that can no longer be taken away by the wealthy classes, and by politicians who act as agents for these classes.

Let's look at the Filipino farmer. Didn't he used to lower his head, with a glimmer of secret anger in his eyes, but forcibly suppressed and hidden, not only the clothes he wore but especially the dignity of his person in blind obedience to the landlord his master, but now his head is held high, his mind is free, and he is the one directing his own life and destiny? Why has such a revolution in the condition of the farmer happened in just ten years? You know the reason, because of what your servant wrote, the law now more popularly known as Presidential Decree 27, which broke the chains of centuries of slavery of hundreds of thousands of farmers and their families. Before, the villagers lowered their heads, but now that they have found their own voice in their barangays, is there anyone who can look down on them? Why are workers more respected today and sometimes feared because they are more aware of their rights and carry in their person the shield of rights through the Labor Code? Didn't you almost burn down Congress just to get a small increase in the minimum wage? In the recent past, haven't I enacted eleven additional wage increases and allowances simply at your instruction?

Why do you also have a major economic storm happening now throughout the world, and our country is just one of the many countries currently struggling against the tide of an economic crisis? Our first responsibility is to support the livelihoods that currently exist, and how to put our economy in a more stable position to create millions more job opportunities needed by our still rapidly growing population.

This does not mean that we will not help our workers despite the crisis facing our industry and the entire economy. Because it is important to give a signal to our entire society that even in times of danger, we cannot fail to show compassion for the working class. Because of this, at the end of my speech, I will sign a Presidential Wage Order—an order integrating several of the existing cost-of-living allowances into the basic minimum wage, as consistently demanded by the workers through the Trade Union Congress of the Philippines, a decision that will significantly increase the base for computation of the thirteenth month pay, overtime pay, retirement pay, and social security benefits in general.

We will return to this topic. Meanwhile, I want to appeal to the Filipino working class that now more than ever we must cherish the unity of our country in the face of a worsening danger in the world. Attempts are becoming bolder against our own country by forces that, in secret or openly, want to destroy our stability, destroy the institutions of the New Society that have given dignity to small Filipinos, and destroy the peaceful and prosperous future of Filipino youth.

I am not saying that the KBL is the only one who loves and cares for our country. Let us not hinder the right of the opposition to say that they also love the country. The opposition is divided into two groups. One insists on the boycott. The other is now participating in a battle through the ballot.

Let's start with those who are boycotting. Imagine that recently, some leaders of the Boycott Movement gathered in Hong Kong and there they signed a document declaring themselves a Preparatory Committee or Transitory Government that will rule the Philippines if the New Society collapses soon. Isn't it shameful what these people are doing, declaring themselves a new government of the Philippines without consulting even one barangay captain, one farmer, or one legitimate unionist in our country? Is there a foreign power that prompted them to do this? Has it been formed in their assembly that they will immediately destroy peace and stability in our country, by pitting Filipino against Filipino?

Are they pushing us into an abyss of civil war like what happened in Vietnam, Cambodia, Afghanistan, Nicaragua and El Salvador? Have they made the decision that power can only be achieved by stepping on a mountain of corpses of fellow Filipinos in a civil war? I tell them plainly, you will fail in your endeavor, because the Filipino people will not allow their own vision for their children, for a stable, peaceful and prosperous future, to lead to such a bitter and tragic end.

On the other hand, there are indications that this boycott line is being constantly exploited by some in the labor sector to use as a weapon to disrupt our country. There are elements who want to capitalize on the current crisis, and the tears of the unfortunate, to become a fire that will consume our country through a bloody revolution.

Once again, I want to give a warning, while there is still time, to these elements: do not test the resolve of the Philippine government, because you will fail and your dark plans will be thwarted. I will hold you accountable for your audacity against the peace of the country.

Fortunately, the majority of the opposition are currently participating in the election for the regular National Assembly. They listened—to their credit they perceived the overwhelming sentiment for an election that will resolve the issue of leadership. We share with them a deep concern to strengthen the foundations of our democracy following the lifting of Martial Law, and to reinvigorate the climate of freedom in our country. Now that I have given credit where credit is due, however let me proceed to point out what is wrong with this opposition: they have forgotten to offer the electorate a platform, a programme government; or perhaps they did not forget, they just could not muster the considerable will and effort to forge a consensus among themselves on what alternative platforms they would like to stand on. The opposition has not said anything on what they propose to do with agrarian reform, the Labor Code, the plight of the urban poor, the development of infrastructure including schools, wells and health centers for the hinterland areas; neither have they offered an alternative foreign policy geared to peace and sovereignty. Neither have they articulated the slightest notion of what they propose to ensure our national security amidst a dangerous and turbulent world. I think they have just surrendered without a fight to mental sloth: witness the ghoulish abandon with which they exploit the death of a fellow Filipino, in the hope that this will excuse them from the burden of thought. How can a nation even contemplate entrusting its affairs to such a group of reckless power seekers?

I am not saying either that the New Society Movement is without flaw or deficiency, but especially in times of danger, is there any other group with a sufficient record of service, and loyalty to the poor people of our country, to be the guarantor of our stability other than the New Society Movement? Is there any other group that built our edifice on the rock of the dignity of the small people? Is there any other group that can be trusted to steer this country during the great storm in the economy of the whole world? I think there is no other but the KBL.

If you want to continue our fight for the dignity of the small people, of the worker, of the farmer, of the fisherman, of the small businessman, of the teacher, of the employee, of the youth who can no longer study, there is only one way to express that; write on your ballots the names of the candidates of the KBL, nothing more, nothing less, but write their names completely. Also write the name of Minister of Labor Blas F. Ople there in Bulakan. This will also ensure that respect for the dignity of the small citizens will continue to be the cornerstone of our administration, now and in the years to come.

And now, with the agreement of the representatives of capital and labor within the National Wages Council which I believe they will not deny afterwards, I would like to sign before you a Presidential Wage Order, which integrates into the basic minimum wage three cost-of-living allowances, namely:

This will significantly raise the thirteenth pay, overtime, retirement pay, and the social security benefits of workers and their families.

I also sign in your presence a Directive to the Social Security Commission for an increase of the retirement benefits by fifteen (15) per cent, effective on the first of June. This is a time when we should focus some of our concern on the plight of many thousands of older retired workers. I direct the Government Service Insurance System to consider raising the benefit for government retirees as well.

I also sign in your presence a Decree liberalizing the scope of compensable diseases, injuries and deaths under the Workingmen’s Compensation Law.

I also sign a Letter of Instructions that will make it easier for laid-off workers to obtain emergency loans from the Social Security System, until such time as we can afford true unemployment insurance, these emergency loans substitute for income maintenance for the unemployed.

I also sign in your presence an Executive Order creating regional employment exchanges throughout the country under the Ministry of Labor and Employment. These offices will help relocate laid-off workers to new areas of demand.

I also sign a Letter of Instructions strengthening the powers of the Ministry of Labor and Employment in the campaign against illegal recruiters which constitute a modern plague in our society. Under this Decree the Ministers of Labor and Defense can close without further ado any illegal recruitment office.

Finally, I sign in your presence a Letter of Instructions designed to strengthen further the trade union movement by reiterating an old provision in the Labor Code granting them tax exemption.

Long live the Filipino worker!

Long live the party of the small and the poor, the KBL!
