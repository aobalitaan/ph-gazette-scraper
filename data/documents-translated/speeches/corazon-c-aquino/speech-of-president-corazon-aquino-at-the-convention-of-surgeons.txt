CONTRIBUTE YOUR SKILLS

**Memorandum Circular No. 2023-08**

**TO** : All Heads of Departments, Bureaus, Offices, and Agencies of the Executive Branch, State Universities and Colleges, Government-Owned or -Controlled Corporations, and other Government Entities

**SUBJECT** : Guidelines on the Observance of National Volunteer Month (NVM) Every December

**I. Introduction**

The State recognizes the vital role of volunteerism in nation-building. It is a manifestation of *bayanihan*, a uniquely Filipino tradition of communal unity and cooperation, which is essential in addressing societal challenges and promoting national development.  The National Volunteer Month (NVM) provides an opportunity to recognize and celebrate the invaluable contributions of volunteers nationwide.

**II. Legal Basis**

Republic Act No. 9790, otherwise known as the National Volunteer Service Act of 2009, mandates the observance of the NVM every December. This law aims to promote and encourage volunteerism as a tool for national development.

**III. Objectives**

The observance of the NVM aims to:

  a.  Highlight the significant contributions of volunteers and volunteer organizations to national development;
  b.  Promote volunteerism as a key strategy for achieving national goals and addressing societal needs; 
  c.  Encourage wider participation in volunteer activities; and
  d.  Strengthen partnerships between the government, private sector, and civil society organizations in promoting volunteerism.

**IV. Guidelines**

All government agencies and instrumentalities are hereby directed to:

  a.  Conduct appropriate activities during the NVM to recognize and celebrate volunteers. These activities may include, but are not limited to, awarding ceremonies, volunteer appreciation events, and information dissemination campaigns. *Siguraduhin na ang mga aktibidad na ito ay naaayon sa umiiral na mga patakaran at regulasyon ng pamahalaan.* (Ensure that these activities are in accordance with existing government policies and regulations.)
  b.  Encourage their employees to participate in volunteer activities. *Ito ay maaaring gawin sa pamamagitan ng pagbibigay ng insentibo o pagkilala sa mga empleyadong nagboboluntaryo.* (This can be done by providing incentives or recognition to employees who volunteer.)
  c.  Collaborate with volunteer organizations and civil society groups in planning and implementing NVM activities. *Mahalaga ang pakikipagtulungan upang mas mapalawak ang saklaw ng mga aktibidad at mas maraming boluntaryo ang maabot.* (Collaboration is important to broaden the scope of activities and reach more volunteers.)
  d.  Utilize government platforms and communication channels to promote the NVM and volunteerism. *Gamitin ang social media, websites, at iba pang paraan ng komunikasyon upang maipakita ang kahalagahan ng pagboboluntaryo.* (Use social media, websites, and other communication methods to show the importance of volunteering.)

**V. Reporting**

All agencies are requested to submit a report on their NVM activities to the Philippine National Volunteer Service Coordinating Agency (PNVSCA) not later than January 31, 2024. The report should include a description of the activities conducted, the number of volunteers involved, and the impact of the activities.

**VI. Effectivity**

This Memorandum Circular shall take effect immediately.

President Aquino calls on surgeons to contribute their specialized skills to promote the health of the people. She encourages them to spend a month in the countryside to train the surgeons in the field, evaluate the facilities and treat patients themselves.

This is an audience blessed with privilege, economic resources, education, the power to influence, and the power to contribute significantly to national progress. Therefore, in answer to the question, What can government do for your organization and your profession? I say we can get you involved in nation-building. And there are a great many ways you can contribute to that effort.

The job of government, the task of the nation’s leadership, is clear. The creation of just social structures and the nurturing of an environment where the barriers of ignorance and alienation are dissolved and the full potential of the Filipino to contribute to his own well-being and the progress of the nation can be realized.

Let me describe to you a specific way you can contribute to national reconstruction.

My Minister of Health, Dr. Alran Bengzon, has more than once told me that in the course of his travels throughout our country he has discovered many good people in government.

He tells the story of a health officer he met in Region 10. That health officer was serving six towns in heavily infested NPA country.

He had apparently only the most minimal training in the art, but he was performing operations that only a highly trained surgeon should be allowed to do. Dr. Bengzon asked him for an explanation. The man answered that there was no one else around to do the work. This doctor was born with a good head and good hands; it was clear at any rate that he had a good heart. He had just never received the training that would have brought out his full potential.

There are many such people in the Ministry of Health.

I look at this audience and I am reminded of this doctor from Northern Mindanao.

This is the challenge I throw to you: Spend a month in the periphery of our country, out in the countryside where the need is greatest for medical care. Let me get specific. We need 12 good men and women among you who are willing to spend a month in a district hospital.

There you can train the hospital’s surgeon, evaluate the facilities, and yourselves treat the members of the community.

The discovery of people outside our private worlds leads to self-discovery. We know more about ourselves, and how best we can use ourselves, by discovering others and how they live and work. Action begins with awareness. Know your people in their actual living conditions. To know is to be committed to their improvement.

There is pressing need to offer specialized services to a broader and different public than you are accustomed to treating. This will entail a great deal of sacrifice. It will involve a lot of hardship; life out there is not easy. But your efforts to spread the benefits of your specialized skills and long training can contribute to a climate of caring; an environment conducive to the peace and prosperity for which we long.

I hope that you, young surgeons, will remain here in our country, in the service of our people. There is no question that you will be better paid if you practice abroad, but there is something to be said about a sense of fulfillment and pride in helping your country.
