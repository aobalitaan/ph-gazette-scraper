Speech of the Honorable and Venerable President of the Philippines Manuel L. Quezon at the Inauguration of the Self-Governing Government

MY COUNTRYMEN:

In the exercise of the right granted to you by the Constitution, you have elected me President of the Self-Governing Government. I thank you from the bottom of my heart for this new demonstration of your trust in me, and with the help of the Almighty, I will not disappoint your hopes.

The importance of the event we are witnessing today is greater than the mere acceptance of the election of your Chief Executive. We are launching the ship of a new nation. We are inaugurating its government. We are already seeing the fruits of our native struggles for freedom.

We are witnessing the culmination of a glorious undertaking that no country has ever thought of doing in a conquered land. And the greatness of accomplishing this undertaking is attested to by the overflowing joy and gratitude that at this hour is being conveyed to America by fourteen distinguished people. President McKinley’s beautiful hope was finally fulfilled—to look back on the Filipinos to thank the day that Providence chose to place their homeland under the benevolent guidance of the United States.

It was not necessary for the high officials of the American Government to attend our celebrations today. We are pleased and grateful for their participation with us. Prompted by their unwavering concern for our freedom and happiness, the President of the United States, the Honorable Franklin D. Roosevelt, has sent us, as his true representative, the Secretary of War, the Honorable George H. Dern, a great American who is a long-time loving friend of the Filipino people. Vice President Garner, Speaker Byrns, prominent members of the Senate including their floor leader Senator Robinson, and prominent members of the House of Representatives, have traveled here, crossing a distance of ten thousand miles, just to see and witness this chapter of our history. With their presence here, I consider that we are now facing the entire American Nation and celebrating with us the fulfillment of the honorable promise of America to give the Filipino people freedom and self-government. I have full hope that the bond of kindness and affection that binds the Philippines to America will not be broken, but will rather be strengthened when its former power over us is completely severed.

In the name of the Filipino people, I also express today my deepest affection and gratitude to our last Governor-General, the Honorable Frank Murphy, for his fair and accurate administration and also for the invaluable assistance he gave us to ease the heavier tasks we undertook in establishing the policies and framework of our new Government.

And as we greet the dawn of a free and independent nation, I invite you to be silent for a moment, and to remember the sacrifices of our Rizal and Bonifacio, as well as all our other heroes who died in the service and suffering for the redemption of our Motherland.

Dear fellow citizens: The government we are inaugurating today is merely an instrument or a means to achieve a goal. It is an instrument placed in our hands so that we ourselves may prepare for the assumption of the responsibilities of full independence. It is necessary that this final step be thoroughly understood in its entirety and that the great benefits and opportunities it will bring us be recognized.

Under a Self-Governing Government, our economic situation may not be easy and comfortable, but rather become more difficult. However, we will face every problem that arises along the way, no matter how much time and effort is required to solve it. We will build a government that is just, clean, efficient, and strong, so that the Republic to come may be firm and stable for all time—a government, therefore, that will suffice not only for current needs but also for future needs. We no longer need to demolish the existing framework of civic organizations and established customs in order to have a more beautiful and stronger structure. We will not make any drastic changes to the current arrangements, except for those things that are truly necessary to comply with the changes dictated by the Constitution. In short, we will build a new building, not on the ruins of yesterday, but on the solid foundations and policies of today.

Respect for the law, according to the expressed will of the people, is the fundamental strength of a community and a democratic government. Protecting peace and public order is both a strict duty of the government and of the citizens. I have full confidence in the goodwill of our people and in their respect for the law, as well as in the established power. The spread of public unrest and violations of the law can lead to the downfall of constitutional government and invite American intervention. Even in times of independence, if it is shown that we cannot protect the life, liberty, and property of our citizens and foreigners, we will be in danger of interference from other countries. No one can excuse what the Government must do to those who violate the law or to those involved in illegal movements. Severe punishment is the price of their actions. Sufficient armed forces are stationed at all times to prevent and suppress any rebellion against the power of the United States.

There can be no progress without the shelter of lasting peace. Without peace and public order, the teaching of wisdom cannot be advanced, the condition and progress of the livelihood of the majority of citizens cannot be improved, the weak and ignorant cannot be protected from exploitation, and the certain well-being of the people in life, liberty, and property cannot be secured. I therefore ask each citizen for his loyal support of the Government, so that peace may reign in our beloved country.

Our Constitution establishes a judiciary with independence or its own power through the granting of security and benefits to judges. However, freedom should not be the sole aim of a good court. It is also necessary, and perhaps more than freedom, that judges have a strong integrity that can only be achieved through careful and intelligent selection. The administration of justice cannot be of a high order if the character and ability of those who wield it are low. In order to strengthen stability and establish a proper and orderly government, I pledge that in appointing those to these positions, I will only choose people known for their moral integrity, courage, ability, and intelligence, so that anyone who appears before the courts can trust that their rights will be considered, and no one in this country, from the highest Executive to the lowest citizen, will be above the law.

Our economy today is crossing a sea that is turbulent and disturbed by one of the most delicate periods in our history. The number of unemployed and the hardship of life are troubling the stability of governments around the world. The strong foundations of civilization of nations and societies are currently crumbling. The salvation of humanity from such a disaster lies only in the common man. It is our duty to prove to him that under a republican or truly patriotic government, he can have all the opportunities to find happiness for himself and his family. Promoting work, especially for women and children, accurate management of the cooperation of labor and capital in factories and farms, prompt government concern for the well-being of the majority of the people, these are the means to provide stability in the delicate balance of life and the association of the different parts that make up the nation.

The life of a government comes from the financial resources it collects, and its income and expenses must be balanced, like any commercial enterprise, if it truly wants to survive. It is my duty to strive to ensure that our Self-Governing Government stays within its means, so that its foundation and operation are strengthened by a balanced income and expenditure of money.

The larger expenses and responsibilities we face, including those relating to our national defense, should be borne by taxes. As long as we have other sources to cover these expenses, we will avoid imposing new taxes. However, in the world, our country is among those that pay the lowest taxes, so if new needs arise, we should reasonably and agree to increase taxes. Freedom and independence are only fitting for those who are willing to pay their value in life and property.

In order for us to face new challenges of the Commonwealth more strongly and steadfastly, and to further improve the standard of living of our people, we must increase, as much as possible, the wealth of our country through further stimulating the development of our livelihood, improving agricultural methods, diversifying crops, creating new industries or means of livelihood, and strengthening our trade both domestically and internationally. I am confident that the forthcoming negotiations regarding commerce between representatives of the United States and the Philippines will result in a more precise and beneficial trade agreement between the two countries.

The establishment of a thrifty, light, and efficient government; the implementation of a non-partisan civil service; the implementation of a suitable system of national education that can develop good character, shape people's interests, awaken patriotic conscience, and promote capacity for work; the protection of the health and vitality of the race; the protection and development of our natural resources—these and other important matters were deliberately and explicitly outlined in the governing program of the Coalition and in my speech when I accepted the nomination to be a candidate; therefore, it is no longer necessary for me to repeat my views on these matters. Since I was elected on the basis of the aforementioned program and the principles I expressed throughout the period of campaigning for the presidency, I reiterate today my promise that I will faithfully fulfill all of these.

Friendly relations with all nations will be the golden bridge of my administration. The nations of the world are like interconnected needs, and their progress and happiness are inextricably linked. Fraternalism and cooperation among nations are truly necessary. Close friendship, good relations, honest and honorable understanding with other nations and their people here, reasonable consideration for their investments and interests established here, in return for their reciprocity while under and participating in our interests, government and existing laws—these are the specific terms that, in the name of the new Government, I can offer to the Americans and to the people of other countries who desire to live, trade and associate in a balanced way with us here in the Philippines.

In the extremely heavy tasks we undertake to prepare ourselves for the attainment of full independence, we may encounter great obstacles in the path we take, but we will resolutely continue to walk. I call upon your patriotism and appeal to the honor of your conscience and hearts, so that we, with the help of firm unity, may once again offer our selves to the fulfillment of our common aspiration, which is none other than the great destiny of our country. I will face the future with full hope and confidence, and I fully believe that a nation that has always obeyed Him will never be abandoned but guided by the hand of God. May He give me light, strength and courage so that I may not stray or fall short in serving my country!

MANUEL L. QUEZON President of the Philippines

MANILA, November 15, 1935.
