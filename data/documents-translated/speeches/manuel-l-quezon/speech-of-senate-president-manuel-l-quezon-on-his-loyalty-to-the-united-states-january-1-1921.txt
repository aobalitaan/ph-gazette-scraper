Speech of Honorable Manuel L. Quezon Senate President of the Philippines On his loyalty to the United States

[Delivered on January 1, 1921]

There are those who insinuate, if not directly assert, that I am disloyal to the United States because I counsel my people to oppose any policy or plan that would arrest some of the powers already enjoyed by us or retard the advent of Philippine independence. Having taken the oath of allegiance to the United States disloyalty is a criminal offense. Why am I not taken before the bar of justice? Let those, who thus accused me have the courage to file a criminal charge against me and select, as they as prosecuted, the best American lawyer hey can find. Let them also choose an American judge to hear the case. I am ready.

My oath of allegiance impasses upon me the duty to acknowledge the sovereignty of the United States. This I do without mental reservation.

It also compels me to support and obey the constitution of the Philippine Islands, the laws of Congress applicable to the Philippines and all the law enacted by the Philippine legislature. I support and obey the Organic Act. I have not broken any law inforced in the country.

It further obliges me to recognize the constituted authorities having jurisdiction over the Philippines, and obey their lawful orders. This I also do.

Precisely because I am a faithful supporter of the Philippine constitution, which is the Jones Law, a support which is my sworn duty to give, I am fighting every attempt to frustrate the avowed purpose of that law – namely the speedy establishment of Philippine independence.

I say to every American or Filipino who opposes Philippine independence, you, and not me, are the one who is disloyal to the United States.
