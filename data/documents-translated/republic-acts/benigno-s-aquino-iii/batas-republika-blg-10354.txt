On April 8, 2014, through a statement issued in Baguio City by Supreme Court Spokesperson Theodore Te, Republic Act No. 10354 and its Implementing Rules and Regulations were declared “unconstitutional” except for eight items.

Below is the press briefer released on April 8, 2014 from the Supreme Court Public Information Office. It contains the eight items sustained, along with other decisions.

Republic of the Philippines Supreme Court Baguio City

Public Information Office

Press Briefing April 8, 2014

The Supreme Court En Banc, in its summer session in Baguio City today, decided on the following matters, among others, on its AGENDA:

| G.R. NO. 211567 (PROGUN [Peaceful Responsible Owners of Guns], petitioner, v. The Philippine National Police, respondent. |

The Petitioner, in its petition dated March 21, 2014, seeks a writ of certiorari, prohibition, and mandamus, as well as the issuance of a temporary restraining order and/or writ of preliminary injunction. The Court directs the respondent PNP to comment on the petition on its merits and grants the application for a TRO effective immediately until further orders. The TRO restrains the PNP from:

a) Centralization of all applications and renewal of firearms at Headquarters, Camp Crame, Quezon City, and directs the PNP to continue accepting, processing, and accepting licensing and renewals from there in all PNP regional offices and orders the reactivation of all PNP CSG SATO and FESAGS satellite offices, and the formerly accredited drug testing centers, neuro psych clinics and medical clinics in the regions and in the NCR to address the requirements for firearm licensing;

b) Use of any courier services for the delivery of approved firearm license cards, and

c) Implementation and requesting a “waiver and consent” for the licensing and registration of firearms, allowing personnel of the responding PNP to enter the home and residence of the firearm applicant for inspection, in accordance with sec. 9 of RA 10591 and Rule 3, sec. 9 of the IRR.

The Respondent is directed to comment on the merits of the petition challenging Rule 3, sec. 9.6 of the IRR and the last sentence of sec. 9 of RA 10591 allowing the right to inspect to enter the dwelling and residence of licensed firearm owners as unconstitutional due to its violation of Article III, sec. 2 of the 1987 Constitution; likewise, the petition requests that the PNP's centralization of the registration policy be declared unacceptable and void.

The Court, after examining the various arguments and contentions of the parties in the aforementioned consolidated cases consisting of 14 petitions challenging its constitutionality and 2 interventions to maintain its constitutionality, unanimously holds that Republic Act No. 10354 is NOT UNCONSTITUTIONAL [1. footnote: The formulation using a double negative is uncommon in constitutional adjudication and is based on the presumption that all laws are presumed constitutional and the burden of proving that a law is contrary to the constitution lies with the petitioner. When that burden is not met, the declaration is in the double negative—“not contrary to the constitution.” Asserting that it is “constitutional” would presume that the law is moving on the principle of being unconstitutional, which is not the situation.] based on the grounds laid down, except for the following items:

a) Section 7, and the corresponding provision in the RH-IRR insofar as they: (a) require private health facilities and non-maternity specialty hospitals and hospitals owned and operated by a religious group to refer a patient, not in an emergency or life-threatening case, as defined under Republic Act No. 8344, to another health facility that is more accessible; and (b) allow parents of minor or minors who have had a child to have access to modern methods of family planning without written consent from their parents or (their) guardians;

b) Section 23(a)(1) and the corresponding provision in the RH-IRR, particularly section 5.24 therefrom, relating to the penalty imposed on any health care provider who fails or refuses to disseminate information regarding reproductive health programs and services regardless of his/her religious beliefs;

c) Section 23(a)(2)(i) and the corresponding provision in the RH-IRR relating to allowing a married individual, not in an emergency or life-threatening case, as defined under Republic Act No. 8344, to undergo reproductive health initiatives without the consent of the spouse;

d) Section 23(a)(3) and the corresponding provision in the RH-IRR, particularly section 5.24 therefrom, relating to the penalty imposed on any health care provider who fails and/or refuses to refer a patient not in an emergency or life-threatening case, as defined under Republic Act No. 8344, to another health care provider within the same facility or to another accessible one regardless of his/her religious beliefs;

e) Section 23(b) and the corresponding provision in the RH-IRR, particularly section 5.24 therefrom, relating to the penalty imposed on any public official who refuses to promote reproductive health programs or does anything that hinders the full implementation of a reproductive health program, regardless of his/her religious beliefs.

f) Section 17 and the corresponding provision in the RH-IRR regarding the provision of pro-bono reproductive health services, relating to its effect on the conscientious objector in obtaining accreditation with PhilHealth.

g) Section 3.01(a) and (j) of the RH-IRR in relation to its use of the adjective “primary” to oppose Section 4(a) of the RH Law and violate Section 12, Article II of the Constitution.

h) Section 23(a)(2)(ii) regarding its penalizing a health care provider for seeking parental consent from a minor not in an emergency or serious situations, declared UNCONSTITUTIONAL. On these items, the Court had separate votes, which resulted as follows.

[REPUBLIC ACT NO. 10354 ]

A LAW PROVIDING FOR A NATIONAL POLICY ON RESPONSIBLE PARENTHOOD AND REPRODUCTIVE HEALTH

An Act passed by the Senate and House of Representatives of the Philippines convened in Congress:

SECTION 1. Title. — This Act shall be known as “The Responsible Parenthood and Reproductive Health Act of 2012”.

SEC. 2. Declaration of Policy. — The State recognizes and ensures the human rights of all persons, including their right to equality and non-discrimination of these rights, the right to continuing human development, the right to health including reproductive health, the right to education and information, and the right to choose and make decisions for oneself in accordance with his/her religious convictions, ethics, cultural beliefs, and the demands of responsible parenthood.

In accordance with the declaration of State policies under Section 12, Article II of the Constitution of the Philippines of 1987, it is the duty of the State to protect and strengthen the family as a basic social institution and equally protect the life of the mother and the life of the unborn from conception. The State shall protect and promote the health of women especially mothers and people in general, and instill in them health awareness. The family is the natural and fundamental unit of society. The State shall also protect and promote the rights of families and people in general for a balanced and healthy environment in accordance with the rhythm and harmony of nature. The State also recognizes and ensures the promotion and equal protection of the welfare and rights of children, youth, and those in the womb.

Furthermore, the State recognizes and ensures the promotion of gender equality, gender justice, empowerment of women and dignity as a health and human rights issue and a social responsibility. The promotion and protection of the human rights of women shall be central to the State’s efforts to address reproductive health care.

The State recognizes marriage as an inviolable social institution and the foundation of the family which is the foundation of the nation. In furtherance thereof, the State shall defend:

(a) The right of spouses to found a family in accordance with their religious convictions and the demands of responsible parenthood;

(b) The right of children to assistance, including proper care and nutrition, and special protection from all forms of neglect, abuse, violence, exploitation, and other conditions detrimental to their development;

(c) The right of the family to a family living wage and income; and

(d) The right of families or family organizations to participate in the planning and implementation of policies and programs.

The State shall also ensure universal access to safe, non-abortive, effective, legal, affordable, and quality reproductive health care services, methods, procedures, supplies that do not prevent the implantation of a fertilized ovum in accordance with the determination of the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and related information and education from it in accordance with the priority needs of women, children, and other disadvantaged sectors, providing first access to those identified through the National Household Targeting System for Poverty Reduction (NHTS-PR) and other government methods of identifying marginalization, who shall be voluntary beneficiaries of reproductive health care, services, and supplies free of charge.

The state will abolish acts of discrimination, laws and policies that trample on a person's exercise of reproductive health rights.

The State shall also promote openness to life; Provided, That parents will only bring into the world children they can raise in a truly humane way.

SEC. 3. Guiding Principles for Implementation. —This Act declares the following as guiding principles:

(a) The right to make free and informed decisions, central to the exercise of any right, may not be subject to any form of coercion and must be fully guaranteed by the State, as the right itself;

(b) Respect for the protection and fulfillment of reproductive health and rights aimed at promoting the rights and welfare of every person, especially married couples, senior citizens, women, and youth;

(c) Considering that human resources are one of the primary assets of the country, effective and quality reproductive health care services must be prioritized to ensure the health of mothers and infants, the health of those in the womb, safe childbirth and delivery of healthy babies, and a good level of replacement, in accordance with the State's duty to promote the right to health, responsible family planning, social justice, and full human development;

(d) The provision of ethical and medically safe, legal, accessible, affordable, non-abortifacient, effective, and quality reproductive health care services and supplies is essential to promoting the human right to health, especially of women, the poor, and the marginalized, and shall be integrated as part of basic health care;

(e) The State shall promote and provide information and access, without bias, to all methods of family planning, including effective natural and modern methods proven medically safe, legal, non-abortifacient, and effective in accordance with scientific and evidence-based medical research standards such as those registered and approved by the FDA for the poor and marginalized identified through the NHTS-PR and other government methods of identifying marginalization: Provided, That the State shall also provide financial support to promote modern natural methods of family planning, especially the Billings Ovulation Method, in accordance with the needs of recipients and their religious beliefs;

(f) The State shall promote programs that: (1) will allow individuals and couples to have the number of children they want with sufficient consideration for the health, particularly of women, and the resources they can afford and access in accordance with existing laws, public morals, and their religious beliefs: Provided, That no one shall be denied, for economic reasons, the right to procreate; (2) will achieve equitable allocation and use of resources; (3) will ensure effective collaboration between the national government, local government units (LGUs) and the private sector in the design, implementation, coordination, integration, monitoring, and evaluation of people-centered programs to improve the quality of life and environmental protection; (4) will conduct studies to analyze demographic trends including demographic dividends from sound population policies for continued human development in accordance with the principles of gender equality, protection of mothers and children, born and unborn, and the promotion and protection of women's reproductive rights and health;

(g) The provision of reproductive health care, information, and supplies prioritizing poor beneficiaries identified through the NHTS-PR and other government methods to identify marginalization is a primary responsibility of the national government in accordance with its obligations to respect, protect, and fulfill the right to health and the right to life;

(h) The State shall respect the decisions of individuals and the choice of family planning methods in accordance with their religious beliefs and cultural convictions, taking into consideration the State's obligations under various human rights instruments;

(i) The active participation of non-government organizations (NGOs), women's and people's organizations, civil society, faith-based organizations, religious sector and communities is crucial to ensuring that reproductive health and population and development policies, plans, and programs will address the priority needs of women, the poor, and the marginalized;

(j) While this Law recognizes abortion as illegal and punishable by law, the government shall ensure that all women needing care for complications arising from abortion and all other complications arising from pregnancy, childbirth, and delivery and related issues are treated and counseled in a humane, non-judgmental and compassionate manner consistent with law and medical ethics;

(k) Every family shall have the right to determine the ideal size of its family: Provided, however, That the State shall provide each parent with the necessary information on all aspects of family life, including reproductive health and responsible parenthood, in order to make such determination;

(l) There shall be no target demographics or populations and the mitigation, propagation and/or maintenance of population growth rate shall be incidental to the promotion of reproductive health;

(m) Gender equality and women empowerment are central elements of reproductive health and population and development;

(n) The resources of the country are necessary for serving the entire population, especially the poor, and allocations from these shall be adequate and effective: Provided, That the life of the unborn is protected;

(o) Development is a multi-faceted process that calls for the convergence and integration of policies, plans, programs and projects aimed at improving the quality of life of the people, especially the poor, the needy and the marginalized; and

(p) That a comprehensive reproductive health program addresses the needs of people throughout their lives.

SEK. 4. Definition of Terms. — For the purpose of this Law, the following terms shall be defined as follows:

(a) Abortion refers to any drug or device that assists in the termination or destruction of a fetus within the mother's womb or in preventing a fertilized ovum from reaching and implanting in the mother's womb, as determined by the FDA.

(b) Adolescent refers to youth between the ages of ten (10) to nineteen (19) who are in transition from childhood to adulthood.

(c) Basic Emergency Obstetric and Newborn Care (BEMONC) refers to life-saving services for life-threatening conditions/complications in the mother or newborn provided by a health facility or professional to include the following services: administration of parenteral oxytocic drugs, administration of a volume of parenteral anticonvulsants, administration of parenteral antibiotics, administration of maternal steroids for preterm labor, performance of assisted vaginal delivery, removal of retained products of conception, and manual removal of retained placenta. It also includes newborn interventions consisting of these alone: newborn resuscitation, provision of warmth, and referral, blood transfusion if possible.

(d) Comprehensive Emergency Obstetric and Newborn Care (CEMONC) refers to life-saving services for life-threatening conditions/complications in the mother or newborn that are in addition to Basic Emergency Obstetric and Newborn Care, except for the provision of operative delivery (caesarian section) and blood bank services, and other highly specialized obstetric interventions. It also includes emergency newborn care consisting of at least newborn resuscitation, treatment of newborn sepsis infection, oxygen support, and post-delivery administration of steroids (to the mother) who experienced preterm labor.

(e) Family planning refers to a program that allows couples and individuals to decide freely and responsibly on the number and spacing of their children and to have the information and means to do so, and to have access to a full range of safe, affordable, effective, non-abortifacient modern natural and artificial methods of pregnancy planning.

(f) Fetal or infant death review refers to a qualitative and in-depth study of the causes of fetal and infant deaths whose primary objective is to prevent the possibility of future deaths through changes or additions to programs, plans, and policies.

(g) Gender equality refers to the principle of equality between women and men and of equal rights to enjoy the conditions for the fulfillment of their potential as a human being and to contribute to, and benefit from, the results of development, while the State recognizes that all people are free and equal in dignity and rights. This includes equality in opportunities, in the allocation of resources or benefits, or in access to services for the promotion of health rights and continued human development, among others, without discrimination.

(h) Gender equity refers to policies, instruments, programs and actions that address the disadvantaged position of women in society by providing biased consideration and specific actions. This includes fairness and justice in the distribution of benefits and responsibilities between women and men, and often requires projects and programs unique to women to end existing inequalities. This concept recognizes that while both women and men are involved in reproductive health, it is more critical to women's health.

(i) Male responsibility refers to the involvement, accountability, responsibility, and responsiveness of men in all matters of sexual and reproductive health, as well as in the care of reproductive health issues unique to men.

(j) Maternal death review refers to a qualitative and in-depth study of the causes of death of a mother who has given birth, the main objective of which is to prevent the possibility of future deaths through changes or additions to programs, plans, and policies.

(k) Maternal health refers to the health of a woman of reproductive age, including, but not limited to, pregnancy, childbirth, and the postpartum period.

(l) Modern family planning methods refer to safe, effective, non-abortifacient and legal methods, whether natural or artificial, registered with the FDA, to plan a pregnancy.

(m) Natural family planning refers to various methods used to plan or prevent pregnancy in accordance with identifying the days when a woman is fertile.

(n) Public health service provider refers to: (1) a public health institution, licensed and accredited and primarily dedicated to the maintenance and administration of facilities for health promotion, disease prevention, diagnosis, treatment and care of individuals with illness, sickness, injury, disability or injury, or in need of obstetric or other medical or nursing care; (2) a public health professional, who is a medical doctor, nurse, or midwife; (3) a public health worker involved in the delivery of health care services; or (4) a barangay health worker who has undergone training programs under any government or accredited NGO and voluntarily provided primarily health care services to the community after being accredited to provide it by a local health board in accordance with the rules adopted by the Department of Health (DOH).

(o) Poor refers to members of a household identified as poor through the NHTS-PR of the Department of Social Welfare and Development (DSWD) or any subsequent system used by the national government in identifying the poor.

(p) Reproductive Health (RH) refers to a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity, in all matters relating to the reproductive system and its functions and processes. It states that people can have a responsible, safe, consensual, and satisfying sex life, with the ability to reproduce and the freedom to decide if and when, and how often to do so. It also states that women and men achieve equal relationships in matters relating to sexual and reproductive relations.

(q) Reproductive health care refers to access to a full range of methods, facilities, services and supplies that contribute to reproductive health and well-being in addressing reproductive health problems. It also includes sexual health, with the aim of improving lives and personal relationships. The elements of reproductive health care include the following:

(1) Information and family planning services that will include as a primary priority the full empowerment of women of productive age of their respective cycles so that they know when the chance of fertilization is high, and when it is also low.

(2) Maternal, infant and child health and nutrition, including breastfeeding;

(3) Prescription of abortion and management of abortion complications;

(4) Guidance and counseling on reproductive health for adolescents and youth;

(5) Prevention, treatment and management of reproductive tract infections (RTI), HIV and AIDS and other sexually transmitted infections (STI);

(6) Elimination of violence against women and children and other forms of sexual and gender-based violence;

(7) Education and counseling on sexuality and reproductive health;

(8) Care for the breast and cancers of the reproductive tract and other gynecological conditions and disabilities;

(9) Male responsibility and involvement and men’s reproductive health;

(10) Prevention, treatment, and management of infertility and sexual dysfunction;

(11) Reproductive health education for adolescents; and

(12) Mental health aspects of reproductive health care.

(r) The reproductive health care program refers to the systematic and integrated provision of reproductive health care for all citizens, prioritizing women, the poor, marginalized, and those affected by or in crisis situations.

(s) Reproductive health rights refer to the rights of individuals and couples to decide freely and responsibly whether to have children or not; if so, how many, the spacing and timing of births; to make other decisions relating to reproduction, free from discrimination, coercion and violence; to have information and the means to do so; and to achieve the highest standards of sexual and reproductive health: Provided, however, That reproductive health rights do not involve abortion, and access to abortifacients.

(t) Reproductive health and sexuality education refers to the lifelong learning process of providing and acquiring comprehensive, accurate and age- and development-appropriate information and education on reproductive health and sexuality through life skills education and other approaches.

(u) Reproductive Tract Infection (RTI) refers to sexually transmitted infections (STIs) and other types of infections affecting the reproductive system.

(v) Responsible parenthood refers to the desire and ability of a parent to respond to the needs and aspirations of the family and children. It is also the shared responsibility of parents to ensure and achieve the desired number of children, spacing and timing of births in accordance with their own aspirations for family life, taking into account psychological readiness, state of health, socio-cultural and economic issues in accordance with their religious beliefs.

(w) Sexual health refers to a state of physical, mental and social well-being in relation to sexuality. It requires a positive and respectful approach to sexuality and sexual relationships, as well as the possibility of having enjoyable and safe sexual experiences, free from coercion, discrimination and violence.

(x) Sexually Transmitted Infection (STI) refers to any infection that can be acquired or transmitted through sexual intercourse, IV drug use, intravenous drug needles, childbirth, and breastfeeding.

(y) Skilled Birth Attendance refers to childbirth attended by a skilled health professional including enabling conditions of necessary equipment and support of a functioning health system, including transport and referral mechanisms for obstetric emergency care.

(z) A skilled health professional refers to a midwife, doctor or nurse, who has been trained and practiced in the skills needed to manage normal and complicated pregnancies, childbirth, and the immediate postpartum period, and in identifying, managing and referring complications in women and newborns.

(aa) Sustainable human development refers to placing people, especially the poor and vulnerable, at the center of the development process, with the central objective of creating an enabling environment where all can achieve long, healthy and productive lives, conducted in a manner that promotes their rights, and safeguards the opportunities of future generations and the natural ecosystems upon which all life depends.

SEC. 5. Procurement of Skilled Health Professionals for Maternal Health Care and Skilled Birth Attendance. — LGUs shall endeavor to procure a sufficient number of nurses, midwives, and other skilled health professionals for maternal health care and skilled birth attendance to achieve the ideal ratio of skilled health professional to patient, taking into consideration the targets of the DOH: Provided, That people in a geographically isolated area or high population and impoverished areas shall be provided with the same level of access to health care: Provided, further, That the national government shall provide additional and necessary funding and other necessary assistance for the effective implementation of this provision.

For the purposes of this Act, midwives and nurses are authorized to administer life-saving drugs such as, but not limited to, oxytocin and magnesium sulfate, in accordance with guidelines set by the DOH, under emergency conditions and when a doctor is unavailable: Provided, That they have undergone proper training and are certified to administer these life-saving drugs.

SEC. 6. Health Care Facilities. — Each LGU, once it has ascertained the need based on data supported by its local health office, shall be required to establish and improve hospitals and facilities with sufficient and qualified personnel, equipment and supplies to provide emergency obstetric and newborn care: Provided, That people in isolated areas or high-density and impoverished areas are provided with the same level of access and are not neglected through the provision of alternative methods such as home visits or mobile clinics for health care if necessary: Provided, also, That the national government shall provide additional and necessary funding and other necessary assistance for the effective implementation of this provision.

SEC. 7. Access to Family Planning. — All accredited public health facilities are required to provide a full range of modern family planning methods, including medical consultations, supplies and requirements and reasonable procedures for poor and marginalized couples wishing to have children with fertility issues: Provided, That family planning services shall also be provided by private health facilities to paying patients with the option to provide free care and services to the poor, except in the case of non-maternity specialty hospitals and hospitals owned and operated by a religious group, but they have the option to provide such a full range of modern family planning methods: Provided, also, That these hospitals shall immediately refer individuals seeking such care and services to other accessible health facilities: Provided, finally, That the person is not in an emergency condition or a serious case as defined by Republic Act No. 8344.

No person shall be denied information and access to family planning services, whether natural or artificial: Provided, That minors shall not be allowed to access modern methods of family planning without written consent from their parents or guardians unless the minor is already a parent or has suffered a miscarriage.

SEC. 8. Maternal Death Review and Fetal and Infant Death Review. — All LGUs, national and local government hospitals, and other public health units are required to conduct an annual Maternal Death Review and Fetal and Infant Death Review in accordance with rules set by the DOH. Such review shall necessarily lead to an evidence-based programming and budgeting process that contributes to the development of more responsive reproductive health services to promote women's health and safe motherhood.

SEC. 9. The National Drug Formulary System of the Philippines and Family Planning Supplies. — The National Drug Formulary shall include hormonal contraceptives, intrauterine devices, injectables and other safe, legal, non-abortifacient and effective products and family planning supplies. The Philippine National Drug Formulary System (PNDFS) shall be observed in the procurement of drugs including family planning supplies to be included or removed from the Essential Drug List (EDL) in accordance with existing practice and in consultation with reputable medical organizations in the Philippines. For the purpose of this Act, any product or supply included or to be included in the EDL shall be certified by the FDA that such product and supply is available on the condition that it will not be used as an abortifacient.

These products and supplies shall also be included in the regular procurement of essential drugs and supplies in all national hospitals: Provided, also, That such offices shall not procure or obtain in any manner emergency contraceptive pills, post-coital drugs, abortifacients intended for such purpose or in any other form or equivalent.

SEC. 10. Procurement and Distribution of Family Planning Supplies. — The DOH shall procure, distribute to LGUs and monitor the use of family planning supplies nationwide. The DOH shall coordinate with all appropriate local government representatives to plan and implement its procurement and distribution program. The allocated supply and budget shall be based on, among other reasons, current levels and estimates of the following:

(a) Number of women of reproductive age who are in a relationship and desire to space and limit their children;

(b) Number of prevalence of contraceptive use, according to the type of method used; and

(c) Value of family planning supplies.

Provided that, LGUs may implement their own programs for procurement, distribution and monitoring in accordance with the general provisions of this Act and the rules of the DOH.

SEC. 11. Integration of Responsible Family Planning and Family Planning Component into Poverty Reduction Programs. — A multi-dimensional approach will be adopted in the implementation of policies and programs to combat poverty. For this purpose, the DOH will implement programs prioritizing full access of poor and marginalized women identified through the NHTS-PR and other government mechanisms to define marginalization to reproductive health care, services, products, and programs. The DOH will provide such programs, technical assistance, including capacity building and monitoring.

SEC. 12. PhilHealth Benefits for Severe and Life-Threatening Reproductive Health Conditions. — All severe and life-threatening reproductive health conditions such as HIV and AIDS, breast and reproductive tract cancers, and obstetric complications, and conditions related to menopause and postmenopause will be given the maximum benefits, including the provision for Anti-Retroviral Medicines (ARV), as contained in the rules set by the Philippine Health Insurance Corporation (PHIC).

SEC. 13. Mobile Health Care Services. — The national and local governments may provide each provincial, city and municipal hospital with a Mobile Health Care Service (MHCS) in the form of a van or other means of transportation suitable for the terrain, considering the health care needs of each LGU. The MHCS will bring equipment and services for health care to its areas of jurisdiction, particularly to the poor and needy, as well as distribute knowledge and information on reproductive health. The MHCS will be operated by skilled health providers and adequately equipped with a wide range of health care materials and information dissemination tools, including, but not limited to, a television for audio-visual presentations. All MHCS will be operated by LGUs of provinces and highly urbanized cities.

SEC. 14. Age and Development-Appropriate Reproductive Health Education. — The State shall provide age and development-appropriate reproductive health education for adolescents to be taught by teachers adequately trained in formal and non-formal education systems and incorporated into relevant lessons, such as, but not limited to, values formation; knowledge and skills in self-care against discrimination; sexual abuse and violence against women and children and other forms of gender-based violence and teenage pregnancy; physical, social, and emotional changes in adolescents; the rights of women and the rights of children; responsible teenage behavior; gender and development; and responsible family planning: Provided that, openness to the formulation and adoption of appropriate course content, scope, and methodology at each level of education or group will be allowed only after consultations with parent-teacher-community associations, school officials, and other involved groups. The Department of Education (DepEd) will develop a curriculum to be used by public schools and may be used by private schools.

SEC. 15. Certificate of Compliance. — No marriage license shall be issued by the Local Civil Registrar unless the applicants have presented a Certificate of Compliance issued free of charge by the local Family Planning Office certifying that they have received adequate instruction and information on responsible parenthood, family planning, breastfeeding, and infant nutrition.

SEC. 16. Capacity Building of Barangay Health Workers (BHW). — The DOH is responsible for disseminating information and providing training programs to LGUs. The LGUs, with the technical assistance of the DOH, are responsible for training BHWs and other barangay volunteers in the promotion of reproductive health. The DOH shall provide LGUs with the medical supplies and equipment needed by the BHWs to effectively perform their duties: Provided, that, and furthermore, the national government shall provide additional and necessary funds and other necessary assistance for the effective implementation of this provision, including possible provision for additional honoraria for BHWs.

SEC. 17. Pro Bono Services for Poor Women. — Private and non-governmental providers of reproductive health care services, including but not limited to gynecologists and obstetricians, are encouraged to provide at least forty-eight (48) hours per year of reproductive health services, covering from the provision of information and education to the performance of medical services free of charge to indigent patients or low-income earners in accordance with the recognition of the NHTS-PR and other government procedures to determine marginalization, especially for adolescent pregnant women. The forty-eight (48) hours of pro bono service shall be included in the accreditation requirements under PhilHealth.

SEC. 18. Programs for the Sexual and Reproductive Health of Persons with Disabilities (PWD). — Cities and municipalities shall strive to dismantle barriers to reproductive health services for PWDs through the following:

(a) Provision of physical access and adjustments to transportation and issues of access to clinics, hospitals, and places where public health education is provided, contraceptives are sold and distributed, or other places where reproductive health services are provided;

(b) Adaptation of examination tables and other laboratory methods to the needs and conditions of PWDs;

(c) Increasing access to information and communication materials on sexual and reproductive health in braille, large print, simple language, sign language, and pictures;

(d) Provision of continuing education and inclusion of the rights of PWDs to health care providers; and

(e) Conducting activities that raise awareness and address public misinformation regarding stigma and their lack of knowledge about the needs and rights in sexual and reproductive health of PWDs.

SEC. 19. Functions and Responsibilities. — (a) Consistent with the declared policy, the DOH shall serve as the lead agency for the implementation of this Act and shall incorporate the following activities into their regular operations:

(1) Fully and effectively implement the reproductive health care program;

(2) Ensure that people have access to safe, non-abortifacient, legal, quality, and affordable commodities and services for reproductive health; and

(3) Perform other acts necessary to achieve the objectives of this Act.

(b) The DOH, in coordination with the PHIC, when necessary, shall:

(1) Strengthen the capabilities of health regulatory agencies to ensure safe, high-quality, accessible, and affordable services and commodities for reproductive health with concomitant strengthening and enforcement of regulatory mandates and mechanisms;

(2) Facilitate the involvement and participation of NGOs and the private sector in the delivery of reproductive health care services and in the production, distribution and delivery of quality supplies and commodities for reproductive health and family planning to make them easily accessible and affordable to ordinary citizens;

(3) Participate in the services, skills, and knowledge of experts in natural family planning who will provide the necessary training for all BHWs;

(4) Supervise and provide assistance to LGUs in the delivery of health services and in the purchase of supplies and equipment for family planning; and

(5) Deliver to the LGUs, through their respective local health offices, appropriate information and tools to make them aware of current studies and research related to family planning, responsible parenthood, breastfeeding, and infant nutrition.

(c) The FDA will issue strict rules regarding the use of contraceptives, considering the side effects and other harmful effects of their use.

(d) All citizens must exercise restraint in advertising their products and services in all forms of media, especially in matters relating to sexuality, taking into greater consideration its influence on children and youth.

SEC. 20. Public Awareness. — The DOH and LGUs shall initiate and maintain an intensive national campaign in various forms of media to raise the level of public awareness on the protection and promotion of reproductive health and the rights thereto, including but not limited to, maternal health and nutrition, family planning information and services and responsible parenthood, reproductive health of adolescents and youth, guidance and counseling, and other elements of reproductive health care under Section 4(q).

The materials for education and information that will be developed and disseminated for this purpose will be regularly reviewed to ensure their effectiveness and relevance.

SEC. 21. Reporting of Requirements. — Before the end of April of each year, the DOH shall submit to the President of the Philippines and Congress an annual consolidated report, which shall contain a definitive and comprehensive assessment of the implementation of its programs and those of other agencies and government partners and shall propose priorities for executive and legislative action. The report shall be printed and distributed to all national agencies, LGUs, NGOs and private sector organizations involved in said programs.

The annual report shall assess the content, implementation, and impact of all policies related to reproductive health and family planning to ensure that such policies promote, protect, and fulfill the reproductive health and rights of women.

SEC. 22. Congressional Oversight Committee on the Reproductive Health Law. — A Congressional Oversight Committee (COC) is hereby created, composed of five (5) members from the Senate and from the House of Representatives. The members from the Senate and the House of Representatives shall be designated by the President of the Senate and the Speaker, with at least one (1) member representing the Minority.

The COC shall be co-chaired by the respective Chairpersons of the Committee on Health and Demography of the Senate and the Committee on Population and Family Relations of the House of Representatives. The Secretariat of the COC shall be drawn from the existing staff of the Secretariat of the Senate and the House of Representatives for the committees involved.

The COC shall monitor and ensure the effective implementation of this Act, propose necessary legislative remedies or administrative responses, and conduct a review of this Act every five (5) years from its enactment. The COC shall perform such other functions and duties as are necessary to achieve the objectives of this Act.

SEC. 23. Prohibited Acts. — The following acts are prohibited:

(a) Any health care provider, whether public or private, who:

(1) Knowingly withholds information or prevents its dissemination, and/or voluntarily provides false information regarding reproductive health programs and services including the right to informed choice and access to all legal, medically-safe, non-abortifacient and effective family planning methods;

(2) Refuses to perform a legal and medically-safe procedure for reproductive health to any person of legal age due to lack of consent or authorization from the following persons in the following instances:

(i) Spousal consent in cases of marriage: Provided, That in case of disagreement, the decision of the person undergoing the operation shall prevail; and

(ii) Consent of parent or person exercising parental authority in cases of abused minors, provided that if the parent or person exercising parental authority is the respondent, accused, or convicted perpetrator as certified by the proper court of justice, the written consent of the parents or legal guardian, or, in their absence, of persons exercising parental authority or the nearest relative shall be required only for elective surgical operations and consent shall not be required in emergency or serious cases as defined by Republic Act No. 8344; and

(3) Prevent the curtailment of quality health care services and information solely on the basis of a person’s marital status, gender, age, religious belief, personal condition, or occupation: Provided, That the conscientious objection of a health care provider based on his/her ethical or religious beliefs is respected; however, the conscientious objector shall immediately refer the person seeking care and services to another health care provider within the same facility or in an accessible location: Provided, also, That the person is not in an emergency condition or serious case as defined by Republic Act No. 8344, penalizing the refusal of hospitals and medical clinics to provide appropriate initial medical and support care in emergency and serious cases;

(b) Any public official, elected or appointed, specifically tasked with the duty to implement the provisions herein, who himself/herself, or through a subordinate, prohibits or hinders the delivery of legal and safe medical reproductive health care services, including family planning; or forces, coerces, or compels a person to utilize such services; or refuses to allocate, receive or release any budget for reproductive health care services, or to support reproductive health programs; or commits any act that will impede the full implementation of a reproductive health program mandated by this Act;

(c) Any employer who shall suggest, request, influence or deliver any job applicant or an employee to undergo sterilization, use of any modern method of family planning, or non-use of such methods as a condition for employment, continued employment, promotion or in the provision of employment benefits. Likewise, pregnancy or the number of children shall not be a basis for non-acceptance of employment or termination of employment;

(d) Any person who shall falsify a Certificate of Compliance required in Section 15 of this Act; and

(e) Any pharmaceutical company, domestic or multinational, or its agents or distributors, who directly or indirectly conspire with government officials, appointed or elected, in the distribution, procurement and/or sale of the national government and LGUs of modern family planning supplies, products, and equipment.

SEK. 24. Penalties. — Any violation of this Act or the commission of the above-mentioned prohibited acts shall be penalized by imprisonment from one (1) month to six (6) months or a fine of ten thousand pesos (PHP10,000.00) to one hundred thousand pesos (PHP100,000.00), or both fine and imprisonment at the discretion of the competent court: Provided, That, if the violator is a public official, elected or appointed, he/she shall also suffer the penalty of suspension not exceeding one (1) year or dismissal and forfeiture of retirement benefits depending on the gravity of the offense after due notice and hearing by the appropriate board or agency.

If the violator is a person from the court, the fine will be imposed on the president or any official responsible. A foreign violator, after serving the sentence, will be immediately deported without any further hearing at the Bureau of Immigration. If the violator is a pharmaceutical company, its agent and/or distributor, their license or permit to operate or conduct business in the Philippines will be revoked, and a fine triple the amount involved in the violation will be imposed.

SEC. 25. Appropriations. — The amounts allocated in the current annual General Appropriations Act (GAA) for reproductive health and natural and artificial family planning and responsible parenthood under the DOH and other involved agencies will be allocated and used for the implementation of this Act. Additional amounts needed to address the improvement of facilities required to reach BEMONC and CEMONC standards; the training and deployment of skilled health providers; the commodity requirements for natural and artificial family planning as outlined in Section 10, and for other reproductive health and responsible parenthood services, will be included in the general appropriations of subsequent years. Funds for Gender and Development (GAD) of LGUs and national agencies may be a source of funds for the implementation of this Act. SEC. 25. Appropriations.

SEC. 26. Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRR). — Within sixty (60) days from the effectivity of this Act, the Secretary of DOH or his/her designated representative as Chairperson, the authorized representative(s) of DepEd, DSWD, Philippine Commission on Women, PHIC, Department of the Interior and Local Government, National Economic and Development Authority, League of Provinces, League of Cities, and League of Municipalities, together with NGOs, faith-based organizations, civil society organizations, women, and youth, shall jointly promulgate rules and regulations for the effective implementation of this Act. At least four (4) members of the committee that drafts the IRR, to be selected by the Secretary of DOH, shall come from NGOs.

SEC. 27. Clause of Interpretation. — This Act shall be liberally construed to ensure the provision, delivery, and access to reproductive health care services, and to promote, protect and fulfill reproductive health and women’s rights.

SEC. 28. Clause of Separability. — If any provision of this Act is declared unconstitutional, such shall not affect the acceptability and validity of the provisions which are not affected thereby.

SEC. 29. Repealing Clause. — Except for existing laws against abortion, any law, decree or presidential statement, executive order, letter of instruction, administrative order, rules, and regulations contrary or inconsistent with the provisions of this Act including Republic Act No. 7392, also known as the Midwifery Act, are hereby repealed, amended and modified accordingly.

SEC. 30. Effectivity. — This Act shall take effect fifteen (15) days after its publication in at least two (2) newspapers of general circulation.
