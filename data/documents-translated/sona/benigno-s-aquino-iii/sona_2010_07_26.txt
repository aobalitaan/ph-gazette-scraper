Thank you very much. Please, everyone, take your seats.

Speaker Feliciano Belmonte; Senate President Juan Ponce Enrile; Vice President Jejomar C. Binay; Chief Justice Renato Corona; Former Presidents Fidel Valdez Ramos and Joseph Ejercito Estrada; members of the House of Representatives and the Senate; distinguished members of the diplomatic corps; my fellow workers in government;

My beloved countrymen:

In every moment of governance, we face a crossroads.

On one hand, it is choosing what is for the good of the people. Looking at the interest of the majority; adhering to principles; and being faithful to our sworn duty as public servants. This is the straight path.

On the other hand, it is prioritizing personal interests. Being enslaved by political considerations, and sacrificing the welfare of the people. This is the crooked path.

For a long time, the government strayed onto the crooked path. Every day, the extent of the problems we inherited becomes clearer to me. I deeply feel the weight of my responsibility.

In the first three weeks of our term, we have discovered many things. I would like to share with you just a few of the problems we have inherited and the steps we are taking to solve them.

This is just a glimpse; this is not yet all the problems we will face. The people were kept in the dark and deliberately misled about the true state of our country.

In the first six months of the year, the government spent more than it earned. Our deficit has grown, reaching 196.7 billion pesos. We fell short of the target collection by 23.8 billion pesos; the estimated expenses, we exceeded by 45.1 billion pesos.

The budget for 2010 is 1.54 trillion pesos.

Only 100 billion pesos, or 6.5 percent of the total, is freely available for the remaining six months of this year.

Only about 1 percent of the total budget remains for each month.

Where did the money go?

A Calamity Fund of 2 billion pesos was allocated in preparation for disasters that have not yet occurred. This fund is already quite small, but as we are just entering the rainy and typhoon season, 1.4 billion pesos, or 70 percent, has already been spent.

Out of a total of 108 million pesos for the province of Pampanga, 105 million pesos went to just one district. Meanwhile, the province of Pangasinan, which was hit by Pepeng, received only 5 million pesos for the damage caused by Typhoon Cosme, which occurred back in 2008.

The funds of Pampanga were released during the election month, seven months after Ondoy and Pepeng. What if a storm hits tomorrow? Its funds have already been exhausted for the typhoon that happened a year ago. The greed of the past will be paid for by the future.

That is also what happened with the funds of MWSS [Metropolitan Waterworks and Sewerage System]. Just recently, people were lining up just to get water. Despite this, the leadership of MWSS still chose to give themselves rewards even though the pensions of retired employees have not yet been paid.

In 2009, the entire payroll of MWSS was 51.4 million pesos. But this was not just their salary; they also had additional allowances and benefits amounting to 160.1 million pesos. In short, they received 211.5 million pesos last year. Only twenty-four percent of this was normal salary, and 66 percent was additional.

The average worker only receives up to 13th month pay plus a cash gift. In MWSS, the salary reaches the equivalent of more than 30 months including all the bonuses and allowances they received.

We have discovered that the compensation of their Board of Trustees is even more substantial. Let us look at the allowances they receive:

Just sit in the Board of Trustees and Board Committee meetings, and you already get 14,000. That amounts to 98,000 per month. They also have a grocery incentive of 80,000 per year.

That's not all: there's a mid-year bonus, productivity bonus, anniversary bonus, year-end bonus, and financial assistance. There's a Christmas bonus, and an additional Christmas package. Each of these is 98,000.

In total, all of this amounts to 2.5 million pesos per year for each member of the Board, excluding the car, technical assistance, and loans. I repeat. All of this was given to themselves while the pensions of their retirees remain unpaid.

Even the La Mesa Watershed was not spared. To have a proper water supply, we need to take care of the watersheds. Watersheds need trees. Even that area which should have been forested, they built houses on it for high-ranking MWSS officials.

They cannot be immediately removed from their positions because they are among the Midnight Appointees of former President Arroyo. We are already investigating all of this. If they have even a shred of shame remaining—hopefully they will voluntarily resign from their positions.

Let's talk about the funds for infrastructure. The DPWH [Department of Public Works and Highways] identified 246 priority safety projects to be funded by the Motor Vehicle Users Charge. This will require a budget of 425 million pesos.

Only 28 projects were funded. The 218 projects were forgotten and replaced with 70 projects that were not in the plan. The requested 425 million pesos became 480 million pesos, increasing further because the projects went to only a select few beneficiaries.

These projects are meaningless, unstudied, and unprepared for, so they sprout up like mushrooms.

The time for this is over. In our administration, there will be no quotas, no kickbacks; the people’s money will be spent for the people alone.

We have discovered more. Five days before the end of the previous administration’s term, they ordered the release of 3.5 billion pesos for the rehabilitation of those affected by Ondoy and Pepeng.

Eighty-six projects were supposed to be funded by this, which should not have gone through public bidding. Nineteen of these, worth 981 million pesos, almost slipped through. The contracts were signed even before the Special Allotment Release Order was issued.

Thankfully, this was discovered and stopped by Secretary Rogelio Singson of the DPWH. Now, the entire 3.5 billion pesos will go through a proper bidding process, and this fund can be used to provide assistance to those who lost their homes due to Ondoy and Pepeng.

Let's talk about what happened at NAPOCOR [National Power Corporation]. From 2001 to 2004, the government forced NAPOCOR to sell electricity at a loss so that prices would not increase. It seems the reason: they were preparing for the elections.

Because of this, in 2004, NAPOCOR's debt reached its limit. The national government was forced to shoulder its 200 billion peso debt.

What the people thought they saved on electricity, we are now paying for from the national treasury. We already have expenses for electricity, and we are still paying for the government's additional debt.

If the loans had been sound, our assurance of electricity supply would have increased. But the decision was based on wrong politics, and not on the needs of the people. The people, after making sacrifices, were further burdened.

This is also what happened with the MRT [Metro Rail Transit]. They tried to buy our affection again. The operator was forced to keep fares low.

Consequently, the guarantee given to the operator to recover their investment was not fulfilled. Because of this, the Land Bank and Development Bank of the Philippines were ordered to purchase the MRT.

The people’s money was exchanged for a losing operation.

Now, let's move on to the funds of the NFA [National Food Authority].

In 2004: The Philippines had a supply shortfall of 117,000 metric tons. They purchased 900,000 metric tons. Even if you repeat the shortfall more than seven times, they still purchased more than enough.

In 2007: The Philippines had a supply shortfall of 589,000 metric tons. They purchased 1.827 million metric tons. Even if you repeat the shortfall more than three times, they again purchased too much.

The painful part is that, because they buy so much every year, the rice just rots in warehouses, like what happened in 2008.

Isn't this a crime, allowing the rice to rot, despite 4 million Filipinos not eating three times a day?

As a result of this, the NFA's debt reached 171.6 billion pesos as of May of this year.

What was thrown away could have almost funded the following:

The entire Judiciary's budget, which is 12.7 billion pesos this year.

The Conditional Cash Transfers for the next year, amounting to 29.6 billion pesos.

All the classrooms that our country needs, amounting to 130 billion pesos.

This practice is despicable. Money that turned into stone.

You heard how the public funds were squandered. What is clear now: Any change will come from ensuring that luxury and wastefulness will end.

Therefore, from now on: we will stop wasting public funds. We will eliminate flawed projects.

This is the point of what we call a zero-based approach to our budget. The practice has been, year after year, to simply repeat a budget full of leaks. Just add a little, and it's okay.

Next month, we will submit a budget that correctly recognizes the problems, and will also focus on the right solutions.

These are just some of the problems we have discovered. Here are some examples of the steps we are taking.

There is the case of a pawnshop owner. He bought a vehicle estimated to be worth 26 million pesos.

If you can afford to buy a Lamborghini, why can't you afford to pay taxes?

This has already been filed as a case. Led by Finance Secretary Cesar Purisima, Justice Secretary Leila de Lima, BIR [Bureau of Internal Revenue] Commissioner Kim Henares and Customs Commissioner Lito Alvarez, we have a new case filed every week against smugglers and those who do not pay the correct taxes.

The perpetrators in the cases of Francisco Baldomero, Jose Daguio and Miguel Belen have also been identified, three of the six incidents of extralegal killings since we took office.

Fifty percent of the incidents of extrajudicial killings that occurred during our short term are on their way to resolution.

We will not cease pursuing prosecution of the remaining half until justice is achieved.

We will hold the killers accountable. We will also hold the corrupt in government accountable.

The formation of our Truth Commission has begun, led by former Chief Justice Hilario Davide. We will seek the truth regarding the alleged corruption that occurred during the past nine years.

Within this week, I will sign the first Executive Order designating the creation of this Truth Commission.

If the answer to the lack of justice is accountability, then the answer to our lack of funds is innovative and creative ways to address long-standing problems.

We have so many needs: from education, infrastructure, healthcare, the needs of the military and police, and many more. The funds will not be enough to meet all of these.

No matter how large the shortfall is to meet our list of needs, I remain optimistic because many have already shown renewed interest and confidence in the Philippines.

This will be the solution: Public-Private Partnerships. Even though no signatures have been made yet, I can say that the results of many discussions regarding this will be good.

Some have already shown interest, wanting to build an expressway from Manila, passing through Bulacan, Nueva Ecija, Nueva Vizcaya, all the way to the end of Cagayan Valley without the state spending even a single peso.

In the field of our Armed Forces:

We have 36,000 nautical miles of coastline. What we have: 32 ships. These ships are from the time of Gen. MacArthur.

Someone suggested to us, this is the proposition: they will rent the Navy headquarters on Roxas Boulevard and the Naval Station in Fort Bonifacio.

They will be responsible for the transfer of the Navy Headquarters to Camp Aguinaldo. Immediately, they will give us 100 million dollars. And on top of all that, they will also give us income from the businesses they will establish on the land they are renting.

In short: We will get what we need, we won't spend anything, and we will even earn.

Many have already offered and suggested to us, from local to foreign businessmen, to fund various needs.

From these Public-Private Partnerships, our economy will grow, and every Filipino will benefit. Many sectors will be helped by this.

We will now be able to build the infrastructure we need to grow tourism.

In agriculture, we can now build grains terminals, refrigeration facilities, proper road networks, and postharvest facilities.

If we can organize our food supply chain with the help of the private sector, instead of importing, we can perhaps dream of supplying the global market.

If the proposed railway systems are built, the price of goods will decrease. It will be cheaper, faster, more convenient, and travelers can avoid corrupt police officers and extortionist rebels.

Just a reminder: The creation of jobs is first on our platform, and jobs come from industrial growth. Industry will only grow if we make the process faster, cleaner, and more convenient for those who want to do business.

We will expedite the process of projects under Build-Operate-Transfer. With the help of all branches of government and the citizens, we will reduce the process from a year, or even a decade, to six months.

The DTI [Department of Trade and Industry], under the leadership of Secretary Gregory Domingo, has already begun to take steps:

We will reduce the endless back-and-forth in the company name registration process, which currently takes four to eight hours per visit, to 15 minutes.

We will reduce the previous list of 36 documents to 6. We will reduce the previous eight-page application form to one page.

I call on our LGUs [local government units]. While we look for ways to make it faster to open businesses, I hope they will also study their processes. These need to be made faster, and they need to be aligned with the reforms we are initiating.

Businessman, soldier, rebel, and the ordinary Filipino, everyone will benefit from this. As long as the Filipino is not disadvantaged, we will accept everything. We need to start working together to achieve this. Let's not make things difficult for each other.

The time is coming when we will no longer have to choose between the security of our citizens and the future of your children.

Once these Public-Private Partnerships are implemented, social services will be funded, in accordance with our platform.

We will now have funds to implement our plans for education.

We will be able to expand the basic education cycle from a very short 10 years to a global standard of 12 years.

We will increase the number of classrooms. We will fund the service contracting under GASTPE [Government Assistance to Students and Teachers in Private Education].

Even the conditional cash transfers, which will lessen the burden on the pockets of families, will also receive additional funding.

We will implement our plan for PhilHealth.

First, we will determine the true number of those who need it. Currently, the data does not match. According to PhilHealth on one hand, 87 percent supposedly have coverage. On the other hand, it’s 53 percent. According to the National Statistics Office, 38 percent have coverage.

Right now, Secretary Dinky Soliman and the DSWD [Department of Social Welfare and Development] are already working to implement the National Household Targeting System, which will identify the families most in need of assistance. An estimated 9 billion is needed to provide PhilHealth to 5 million of the poorest Filipino families.

We have a very bright future ahead of us. We are already with the private sector, and we are also with the League of Provinces, led by Governor Alfonso Umali together with Governors L-Ray Villafuerte and Icot Petilla. They are ready to cooperate to help fund the expenses. I also know that the League of Cities, led by Mayor Oscar Rodriguez, will not be left behind.

If the local governments are already sympathizing with our aspirations, I am sure I can also count on the Congress, from which I came.

The cabinet has already shown excellence in identifying our problems and launching solutions in just three weeks.

During Typhoon Basyang, the electricity franchisees told us there would be four days without power. But due to the quick action of Secretary Rene Almendras and the Department of Energy, power was restored to almost everyone in just 24 hours.

This alleged water shortage in Metro Manila was immediately addressed by Secretary Rogelio Singson and the DPWH. He did not wait for orders, which lessened the inconvenience.

We have also seen the brilliance of those we appointed to help in the Cabinet. It is reasonable to hope that they will no longer be subjected to a difficult confirmation process by the Commission on Appointments. If this happens, many more talented Filipinos will be encouraged to serve in the government.

Very soon, we will convene the LEDAC [Legislative Executive Development Advisory Council] and discuss the important laws that we need to pass. You can count on me to remain open-minded, and our relationship will remain sincere.

We are pushing for the Fiscal Responsibility Bill, where we will not pass laws that require funding if the source of funds has not yet been identified. We need 104.1 billion pesos to fund laws that have already been passed, but cannot be implemented.

We also need to organize the fiscal incentives given in the past. Now that we are tightening our belts, we need to review which of these should remain and which should be stopped.

Let us not allow another NBN-ZTE to happen. Whether the funds come from local or foreign sources, they must go through the proper process. I ask for your help to amend our Procurement Law.

According to the Constitution, it is the duty of the state to ensure there is no market dominance. Monopolies are prohibited, as are cartels that stifle competition. We need an Anti-Trust Law that will give life to these principles. This will give Small- and Medium-scale Enterprises the opportunity to participate and help in the growth of our economy.

Let us pass the National Land Use Bill.

The National Defense Act was also the first law of the Commonwealth, passed way back in 1935. It needs to be replaced with a law that responds to the current needs of national security.

I implore you to advance the Whistleblower’s Bill to continuously eradicate the culture of fear and silence.

The Witness Protection Program will be further strengthened. Let us remember that from 2009 to 2010, 95 percent of the cases where a witness underwent this program resulted in a conviction.

Our laws need to be reviewed. I appeal that we begin the codification of our laws, to ensure they are consistent and not contradictory.

These laws are the basis of order, but the foundation of everything we do is the principle that we will not achieve anything without peace and quiet.

We face two problems regarding peace: the situation in Mindanao, and the continuing rebellion of the CPP-NPA-NDF [Communist Party of the Philippines-New People’s Army-National Democratic Front].

Regarding the situation in Mindanao: Our position remains unchanged. Peace and quiet can only be achieved if all those affected talk: Moro, Lumad, and Christian. We have tasked Dean Marvic Leonen to oversee our negotiations with the MILF [Moro Islamic Liberation Front].

Let us avoid the mistakes of the previous administration, where the people of Mindanao were caught by surprise. We cannot turn a blind eye to the doubts that the process is politically motivated, and that the welfare of the people is not the sole interest.

We recognize the steps being taken by the MILF through disciplining their ranks. We expect negotiations to resume after Ramadan.

Regarding the CPP-NPA-NDF: Are you ready to offer concrete proposals, instead of mere criticisms?

If you also desire peace, we are ready for a comprehensive ceasefire. Are you also ready? Let us talk.

It is difficult to begin a conversation while the smell of gunpowder still lingers in the air. I appeal: let us not allow this very good opportunity to come together under a single purpose to be wasted.

Peace and tranquility are the foundation of development. As the shooting continues, so does our continued bondage to poverty.

We must also understand: This is a time of sacrifice. And this sacrifice will be the investment for our future. Along with our rights and freedoms comes our duty to our fellowmen and to the nation.

I expect our friends in the media, especially in radio and print, those who buy block-time, and in community newspapers, to monitor your own ranks yourselves.

May we bring to life the fundamental principles of your vocation: to clarify important issues; to be fair and truthful; and to elevate the level of public discourse.

It is the duty of every Filipino to focus on the leaders we ourselves placed in power. Let us move from meddling to participation. Because those who meddle endlessly complain. Those who participate contribute to the solution.

For too long, the view has prevailed that the key to progress is understanding oneself rather than understanding others. It is clear to me: How can we progress while taking advantage of others?

How can those who were not given the opportunity to study get a job? If there is no job, how can one become a consumer? How can one save in a bank?

But if we reverse our perspective—if we think, “I will increase the capacity of my fellow citizens”—it will bear fruit, and everyone will have an opportunity.

We have started something good. And we will reach something even better. But let us not forget that there are those who desire our failure. Because if we do not succeed, they can return to power, and to exploiting the people.

I believe that God and the people brought us to where we are today. As long as we focus on the welfare of our fellow citizens, blessings and guidance we can surely expect from Almighty God. And when we believe that God is our ally, is there anything we cannot do?

The mandate we received in the last election is proof that Filipinos still hope for change. The situation is really different now. We can dream again. Let us move towards the realization of our dreams.

Thank you very much. Good afternoon to all of you.
