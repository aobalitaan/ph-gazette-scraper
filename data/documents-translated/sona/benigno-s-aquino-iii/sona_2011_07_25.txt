Senate President Juan Ponce Enrile; Speaker Feliciano Belmonte; Vice President Jejomar Binay; former Presidents Fidel Valdez Ramos and Joseph Ejercito Estrada; Chief Justice Renato Corona and our honorable justices of the Supreme Court; honorable members of the diplomatic corps; members of the House of Representatives and the Senate; local government officials; members of our Cabinet; uniformed members of the military and police; my fellow public servants;

And to my beloved countrymen, my good Bosses:

I faced you during my inauguration and said: No sirens in our administration. And now, we continue to stop it. It became a signal and symbol of change, not only on the roads, but also in the mindset of society.

For a long time, the wangwang has become a symbol of abuse. Before, if government officials could counterflow, it was as if only their time mattered. Instead of serving the people, they seemed to become the kings of the people. The way their patrons and lackeys acted, you would think they were nobles clearing the road; they didn't care about those forced to pull over and left behind. Those who should be serving are the ones oppressing. Taking advantage after promising to serve—that is the wangwang mentality.

They have no right to do this. According to the law, only the President, Vice President, Senate President, House Speaker, Chief Justice, police, firefighters, and ambulances are authorized to use the wangwang for their official duties. If they can't even follow the law regarding traffic, how much more in matters where they can gain more, such as in projects funded by the public treasury?

Do you want all the corrupt to be imprisoned? I do too. Do you want the wangwang removed, not only from the roads, but from the mindset that caused the distorted system that we have endured for so long? I do too. Do you want everyone to be given a fair opportunity to succeed? Of course, I do too.

Here is an example of the result of our campaign against the wangwang in the system. This year, the people themselves said that the number of those who are hungry among them has decreased. From 20.5 percent self-rated hunger in March, it went down to 15.1 percent this June, equivalent to one million Filipino families who were hungry before, but now eat properly every day.

In the field of business, who would have thought we would surpass the stock market's all-time high seven times? The 4,000 index, once thought unreachable, or if reached, only temporary, is now routinely exceeded.

If previously the country was stuck with low credit ratings, Moody’s, Standard and Poors, Fitch, and Japan Credit Ratings Agency have raised our ranking, in recognition of our proper spending of funds and our creative financing. A high credit rating will lower interest on our borrowings. Compared to the first four months of last year, we saved 23 billion pesos more in interest payments from January to April of 2011. This could already cover two million and three hundred thousand beneficiaries of the CCT [conditional cash transfer] until the end of 2011.

Let me remind you, in the nine and a half years before we were appointed to office, we only experienced one ratings upgrade, and were downgraded six times by various ratings agencies. In just one year with us, we have been given four upgrades. We all know that it is not easy to be upgraded in these times. These ratings agencies were criticized for giving wrong advice before the American crisis, so now they are stricter in giving good ratings, and we see this in the successive downgrades of other countries. But they upgraded us. They all agree: the Philippine economy has improved and will continue to improve. We are just one step away from investment grade, and our economic team is relentlessly working to finally get us moving forward.

And there is more good news coming. Because there are no more sirens [wangwang] at the DOE [Department of Energy], confidence of investors in our energy sector has been revived. Proof of this is the one hundred and forty companies ready to invest in the exploration and strengthening of our oil and natural gas resources. In the last energy contracting round in 2006, only 35 participated. Just this Friday, a new agreement was signed for a new power plant on the Luzon grid so that by 2014, the country will have a cheaper and more reliable source of energy.

With confidence, with hope, and we are fulfilling our promises. I remember a woman I spoke with when I was first doing house-to-house campaigns for myself. Her complaint: “Whoever wins, the outcome will be the same. I was poor when they were campaigning; I was poor while they were in office, and I will likely still be poor when they retire.” In other words, the complaint of many is, “Our leaders yesterday didn’t care, they don’t care now. Tomorrow, they still won’t care.”

Isn't she justified in saying this, because of the extravagance in government agencies? Extravagance is buying a helicopter at a brand new price, but it turned out to be heavily used. Extravagance also includes the millions in bonuses received by officials of GOCCs [government-owned and -controlled corporation], such as the Philippine National Construction Corporation (PNCC), even though they did not provide decent service, and instead plunged their agencies into debt. Before they stepped down, the former leadership of the PNCC took 32 million pesos for themselves. As early as 2007, they no longer had a franchise; all earnings should have gone directly to the national government. They didn’t even remit any income, but took advantage of their position. Their bonuses from 2005 to 2009 were doubled in the first six months of 2010. They plunged their office into billions of pesos in debt, and still had the gall to give themselves a midnight bonus.

To prevent the extravagance in the national treasury, we have thoroughly reviewed and examined the programs. For two consecutive years, we have implemented zero-based budgeting, which will serve as a shield against wasteful spending.

In Laguna Lake, they were going to remove—they said—12 million cubic meters through dredging, but after three years, it was guaranteed to return; 18.7 billion pesos would be our debt just to play with mud. The debt wasn't even paid off, and the benefit had already expired. We stopped that. The food-for-school program, which randomly searched for beneficiaries, and other initiatives that were funded but led nowhere—we have removed them from the budget so that the freed-up money can be allocated to projects that are truly useful.

The budget is the clearest embodiment of our straight and narrow path. My message to all those who want to divert us from this path: If you will only harm the poor, do not even try. If you will only enrich yourself, do not even try. If it is not for the Filipino people, do not even try.

I hope we can already say that the era of sirens and ostentation is over, but we see the dregs of this mindset stubbornly trying to disrupt the clarity of our journey on the straight and narrow path.

It seems that many are also using sirens in the private sector. According to the BIR [Bureau of Internal Revenue], we have almost 1.7 million self-employed and professional taxpayers such as lawyers, doctors, and businessmen who paid only, in total, 9.8 billion pesos in 2010. An average of only 5,783 pesos in income tax was paid by each of them—meaning, if this is true, their total income reaches only 8,500 pesos per month. Lower than the minimum wage. Really.

You can see that your taxes are going to the right place, so there is no reason to avoid paying them. I appeal to you: It is not only the government, but also our fellow Filipinos who are deprived when the correct taxes are not paid.

We will hold accountable those involved in irregularities, wherever they may be within the government. It is painful, but even now, there are still those trying to get away with it. There is a district in Region IV-B with a project that will cost 300 million pesos. However, the district engineer can only approve up to 50 million pesos.

So they thought of breaking down the project into smaller parts so that the cost would not exceed 50 million pesos, and their papers would not reach the regional and central offices. What we found at the DPWH [Department of Public Works and Highways] was a situation where everyone had their own schemes and their own little kingdoms. They tried to continue their old habits. Often, because funds are given as a lump sum, there are no questions asked about the project's plan and details. Apparently—according to some—even if they were to build a spider's web, they would be given funds, as long as they have a sponsor.

Secretary Babes Singson did not allow this to happen. He removed the district engineer from his position. He also stopped the awarding of this project to investigate what other irregularities occurred. A thorough investigation is also underway on all those who conspired. Contractors who are proven to have colluded to profit from projects will be blacklisted.

Look at the damage caused by these irregularities in the system: The suffering of the people who should be benefiting from the projects of the nation continues.

It wasn't just in Region IV-B where this was discovered. But it has stopped now because patronage is no longer in play, and the proper process reigns at the DPWH. A work program is no longer optional; a well-thought-out plan must be presented to avoid conflicting project implementations. The bidding is now clean and transparent, and contractors have equal opportunities to participate.

Under the system now in place at the DPWH, we have already saved 2.5 billion pesos, and we hope to save 6 to 7 billion pesos this year. Most importantly, we can now rely on roads that are well-built, not ones that collapse or crack with just a little rain. We used to believe it was impossible for the DPWH to correct their system. It's not just possible; we are doing it in the first year alone.

Even in the farmlands, there were instances of oversupply. Before we took office in 2010, the country imported 2.3 million metric tons of rice. We only needed to import 1.3 million metric tons, but they exceeded that by 1 million tons. Because of this oversupply, we had to spend again on warehouses that were just used to store ship-loads of rice.

For how many years was the imported rice unnecessarily oversupplied? Because of this, the idea arose that we would be importing rice forever. Many thought there was nothing we could do about it.

But, in just one year, we proved them wrong. Now, our previous rice shortfall of 1.3 million metric tons has almost been halved; we only need to import 660,000 metric tons. Even if we increase that with a buffer against disasters and make it 860,000 metric tons—which we are already doing—it is still lower than the estimated annual shortfall of 1.3 million metric tons.

And the increase in our rice productivity wasn't just luck. It is a result of sound governance: the use of good quality seeds, and careful and effective spending on irrigation. Last year, we irrigated 11,611 new hectares of farmland. That's in addition to the almost 212,000 hectares that were repaired or restored after a period of abandonment. The result: our palay harvest increased by 15.6 percent last year.

What we want to happen: First, we will not import what is not needed, just to fill the pockets of those who want to profit from agriculture. Second: we no longer want to rely on imports; the rice that Juan dela Cruz eats will be planted, harvested, and bought here.

Let's also look back at the situation of our soldiers and police officers. Thirteen thousand pesos is the average salary of a PO1 in Metro Manila. Four thousand pesos of this goes to rent. It seems right that one-third of their salary goes directly to rent. One-third of that, for food. And the remaining one-third, for electricity, water, transportation, children's education, medicine in case someone gets sick, and others. It's good if his income equals his expenses. And when he falls short, he will likely resort to 5-6 lenders. And when the interest on their debts piles up, will they be able to resist the temptation to become corrupt?

Therefore, the housing solution we promised last February is now being fulfilled this July. We have already distributed 4,000 Certificates of Entitlement to Lot Allocation to our brave soldiers and police. This is just a part of our target of a total of 21,800 houses by the end of this year. The previous four thousand pesos paid for rent each month, is now only two hundred pesos, and for a house they truly own. The amount previously lost to monthly rent can now be used for other expenses.

There are still over 1,000 houses remaining, so for our police and soldiers who have not yet submitted their documents, this is a last call for this batch. But don't worry, next year, our housing program will expand, and not only police and soldiers in Luzon will benefit. The NHA [National Housing Authority] is preparing the land for construction in the Visayas and Mindanao, so next year, we can start building houses there. To our personnel from the Bureau of Jail Management and Penology and the Bureau of Fire Protection, I also have good news for you: you are included in this.

If we are talking about security, isn't that also connected to our national pride? Before, we couldn't even fight back when we were intimidated within our own territory. We are now sending a clear message to the whole world: What is in the Philippines is the Philippines; when you step on Recto Bank, it's like you're stepping on Recto Avenue.

Is the story about a standoff back then really true? They say our sailors were faced with cannons. What they did that night was cut down a coconut tree, paint it black, and point it at the enemy. That time is over. Capability upgrades and modernization of our Armed Forces' equipment are on their way. Our first Hamilton Class Cutter is literally traveling across the ocean to get here, a more modern ship that we can use to guard our coastlines. We may be able to acquire more ships like this. This will be added to the helicopters we are acquiring (plural that), patrol craft, and weapons that the AFP [Armed Forces of the Philippines], PNP [Philippine National Police], including the DOJ [Department of Justice] will be buying in bulk to get a large discount. All of this will be achieved through sound management; bought at the right price, without anyone needing to grease palms.

We have no intention of provoking conflict, but the world also needs to know that we are ready to defend what is ours. We are also studying the possibility of elevating the case in the West Philippine Sea to the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea, to ensure that in future instances, calmness and restraint will prevail during territorial disputes.

I know that our care for our civil servants focused on our security will bear fruit. Just imagine: in the first six months of 2010, 1,010 cars and motorcycles were stolen. Let's compare that to 460 cars and motorcycles stolen from January to June of this year. That's a big reduction, isn't it? I just got unlucky that one or two cases of car theft were headlined, and not the reduction in incidents or the higher percentage of stolen cars that were returned to their owners.

Here's another example of the change we are experiencing: The Anti-Trafficking in Persons Act was signed in May 2003, but because the state did not take its implementation seriously, only 29 individuals were convicted within seven years. We have surpassed that, because 31 human traffickers have been convicted under our administration. This is perhaps what Secretary of State Hillary Clinton of America calls a “sea change.” Because of this, we have been removed from their Trafficking in Persons Report's Tier 2 Watchlist. If we had not been removed from this watchlist, the grants we could have received from the Millennium Challenge Corporation and others would have been jeopardized.

Let's move on to employment. Creating jobs was our first promise to the Filipino people. The 8 percent unemployment rate in April of last year was lowered to 7.2 percent this April 2011. Let's remember: the number of unemployed is a moving target, because every year there are new graduates looking for jobs. In fact, this past year, the number of those seeking employment increased by thousands of campaign boys, banner hangers, and other Filipinos who earned temporary income from the election. Our result: 1.4 million jobs created last year, ending in April.

Previously, the ambition of Filipinos was fixated on working abroad. Now, they have a choice of employment, and as long as they meet their dreams with diligence and determination, they are sure to achieve them.

There is still a lot that can be added to the jobs being created in our country. According to our Philjobnet website, 50,000 jobs remain unfilled every month because the needs of companies do not match the skills and knowledge of job seekers. We will not allow this opportunity to be wasted; even now, the ideas of DOLE [Department of Labor and Employment], CHED [Commission on Higher Education], TESDA [Technical Education and Skills Development Authority], and DepEd are converging to address the issue of job mismatch. Curricula will be reviewed to focus on industries looking for employees, and students will be guided in choosing courses rich in job vacancies.

But what good are the numbers signaling the progress of a few, if many are still left behind? The first step: we identified those who truly need it; we invested in our most important asset: our people. Of the 2 million families registered in our Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program, 1.6 million are already receiving its benefits. As demonstrated by Secretary Dinky Soliman, an estimated more than 100,000 families—let me repeat: more than 100,000 families—are being lifted out of poverty each month. That is why I am confident that we will complete the 1.3 million additional families, from a total of 2.3 million target beneficiaries of the CCT before the end of this year. And with its compliance rate of no less than 92 percent, millions of mothers are now regularly having checkups at health centers, infants are being vaccinated, and children are not being kept out of school.

This is just the beginning, and with such clear results, I hope for the support of every Filipino, especially the legislature, for our proposal to further duplicate the funds for the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program. We hope that before the end of 2012, 3 million families will be given capital for their future.

We are giving these poor families the opportunity to rise in life, because their progress is also the progress of the entire nation. Who will patronize the products and services of entrepreneurs if the consumers themselves are barely surviving? When a father resorts to desperate measures to feed his family, and he steals or robs, who will be the victims of crime if not ourselves? If our countrymen have no proper food or shelter, are weak in health and suffer from serious illnesses, aren't we also at risk of contracting their diseases?

We are laying the groundwork for change to strengthen the foundation of a brighter future for all. For example, in health: isn't it noticeable how the number of PhilHealth beneficiaries increases during election season? Now, through the National Household Targeting System for Poverty Reduction (NHTS-PR), we have ensured that the 5.2 million Filipino families benefiting from PhilHealth are those who truly need it. Broad development and progress for all: That is our vow. No one will be left behind on the straight and narrow path.

Let's turn to the ARMM [Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao]. The old system: Crooked candidates just threw around false debts of gratitude. During national elections, those in power in the ARMM were free to manipulate the machinery in their region to ensure that their opponents received zero votes. When it was election time in the ARMM and the Mayor or Governor came to collect on those debts, the administration would then run the machinery to win their candidate.

According to what the COA [Commission on Audit] uncovered, in the office of the regional governor of the ARMM, from January 2008 to September 2009, eighty—I repeat: 80 percent of the disbursements went to cash advances that had no proper explanation. If that fund hadn't been lost, a child who now crosses a ghost bridge to attend a ghost school, where he would be taught by a ghost teacher, could have already finished his education. Poor child: endless suffering, and no hope of progress.

We want the ARMM to experience the benefits of good governance. That is why our proposed solution is: synchronization. Because of this, they need to focus on their respective campaigns; the competition will be fairer, and command votes will be diluted. Thank you to Congress for passing the law that will synchronize the ARMM elections with the national elections.

Some are asking, why is postponement necessary? In their desire, of course, to return to power, some are ready to repeat the usual tactics to win. Just imagine if we give in to the wishes of the opposition, and we continue with the election. They will do nothing for two years but prepare for the next election and cram their nonsense into a shorter period. As long as the empty-headed officials remain in power, the people will remain mired in the quagmire of despair.

I have no doubt about the outcome of the reforms we have already laid out. We are not just making empty promises; our principles have concrete results. When we say straight path, there is a corresponding road in Barangay Bagumbayan in Sta. Maria, Laguna. When we say clean governance, there will be clean water flowing to remote areas like in Barangay Poblacion, in Ferrol, Romblon. When we say light of change, we will ensure that there is light to illuminate communities that were previously groping in the flickering light of kerosene lamps, as we did in Barangay San Marcos, in Bunawan, Agusan del Sur. This is already happening in many other places; we are striving for this to happen throughout the Philippines.

Various government agencies are already focused; they are coordinating and working together to reach and expedite solutions to the problems that the nation has been carrying for so long.

Isn't it true that we have a problem with flooding, which we know is caused by the relentless and illegal cutting down of trees? The old solution: a photo opportunity of tree planting that only benefited politicians seeking to improve their image. They planted trees to combat floods, but didn't ensure that they would remain standing after they left.

One of the solutions being prepared and studied is to make tree guarding beneficial to communities. They will be given coffee and cacao seeds to plant and yield a livelihood. While waiting for the harvest, they can receive a stipend to guard the trees planted against floods. Informal settlers, who are now gathering in the city, can be beneficiaries of this program. We will invest in the people, while also investing in nature.

A year ago, did we ever think we could do this? Now, we are fulfilling our promises. Tomorrow, all our dreams will become a reality.

Many more creative concepts are being presented to us, and ones that we have already implemented. There's a mosquito trap that kills mosquitoes, which, I believe, is related to the almost 14 percent decrease in the incidence of dengue; there's coconut fiber that was supposed to be thrown away, but can actually be an affordable solution for roads that easily crack; there's a landslide sensor that will warn when the risk of a landslide increases; there are devices that will signal when rivers are about to overflow. All of this is made by Filipinos.

The DOST [Department of Science and Technology] and UP [University of the Philippines] are also studying the possibility of having a monorail system, to address the problem of large-scale transportation. With the creative thinking of fellow Filipinos, there is hope that we can build a light rail system for no more than 100 million pesos per kilometer. With the funds saved, more kilometers of rail can be laid and reach areas far from the commercial center. Those who used to crowd the city to look for work can now live a little further away, without hardship in their commute.

I repeat: this proposal comes from our fellow Filipinos, for the Philippines. Do you remember the time when our dreams could not even reach these kinds of projects? Now, I tell you: we dream it, we can do it, we will do it. Aren't we happy, being Filipinos living in these times?

Despite all of this, let us not forget: all our achievements will be wasted if we do not completely eradicate the culture of corruption that we inherited.

To my fellow civil servants, from the top to every corner of the bureaucracy: Isn't it honorable to work in the government now? Isn't it that now, instead of being ashamed, you sometimes want to wear your ID when riding a bus or jeepney to your agency? Will we waste the honor given to us by the people?

That is also my appeal to our local government units (LGUs). I am among those who agree that you are the ones who know best the needs of the people in your cities and municipalities. Our LGUs can count on greater freedom and capability, if we can also be confident that it will be used in a proper manner, and the welfare of the entire nation will be considered.

For example, some municipalities thought of imposing taxes on electricity transmission lines that pass through their areas. It would indeed generate income for their local treasury, but in return, the expenses of the majority of Filipinos for electricity will also increase. I am confident that we can balance the interests of your constituents with the interests of the nation.

Our programs must remain aligned, because the progress of the entire country will also yield results in your areas. Let us hopefully end the agenda focused only on the next election, and the mindset that we are islands and our own progress can be separated from the development of the country.

We must also help each other towards development. I am very grateful to Congress for passing laws regarding GOCC Governance, ARMM Synchronization, Lifeline Electricity Rates Extension, Joint Congressional Power Commission Extension, Children and Infants’ Mandatory Immunization, and Women Night Workers, and surely many more will follow.

Last year, Congress demonstrated efficiency in passing the budget before the year ended. Because of this, projects were immediately started and were not hampered by the rainy season. Tomorrow, we will submit to the legislature the budget for the next year. I hope that you will once again demonstrate efficiency, so that we can finally reap the fruits of the changes we have planted.

We have made a good start. But it is important for us to remember: this is just the beginning. We still have a lot to do. Allow me to present to Congress some laws that will strengthen the fulfillment of our promises to the nation.

We aim to provide appropriate compensation to the victims of Martial Law; the provision of just wages and benefits for domestic workers; and the implementation of a more orderly pension system for our soldiers. We also support the expansion of the scope of the scholarship granted by DOST to bright but underprivileged students; the promotion of enhanced general health; and the preservation of our environment and the facilities that will ensure the safety of citizens in times of disaster.

Also included in our agenda is the strengthening of the Bureau of Corrections (BuCor), the National Bureau of Investigation (NBI), the National Electrification Administration (NEA), and even PTV 4, so that instead of being left behind by knowledge and time, they can better fulfill their public service.

I was unable to include everyone who wanted to submit their advocacy for inclusion in the SONA. But the details are complete in the budget and budget message. For those interested, please just read the details there.

There are those who say that I am personalizing the pursuit of the corrupt. It is true: It is truly personal for me to do what is right, and to hold accountable those who do wrong—whoever they may be. And it should not only be me who personalizes this issue. It should be personal to all of us, because every Filipino is a victim of this.

What is wrong—no matter how long it has remained—is still wrong. It cannot be “Okay lang”; it cannot be “that’s nothing.” If we forget these things, the mistakes of the past will just happen again. If those who are guilty do not pay, it’s as if we ourselves have invited those who plan to do evil to repeat what they did.

In truth, we have uncovered many more absurdities—but I will not dwell on them, I will just give you one example. At PAGCOR [Philippine Amusement and Gaming Corporation]: coffee. The former leadership of the agency spent one billion pesos on coffee; at 100 pesos per cup, it turns out they consumed 10 million cups of coffee. Perhaps even now that PAGCOR has a new leadership, the eyes of those who drank this coffee are still wide awake. Let’s find them and ask: are you still able to sleep?

With the entry of the new Ombudsman, former Supreme Court Justice Conchita Carpio-Morales, we will have a protector of the people who will not be a paid guard of those who flaunt their power in government. I expect that this year, many who conspired in corruption will be charged, and were the cause of the situation we have reached. The time has also ended when the government files weak cases. When we file a case, the evidence is strong, the testimony is clear, and the guilty party is surely not able to escape.

We are focused on the fact that achieving complete justice does not end with filing a case, but with the imprisonment of the guilty. I have full confidence that the Department of Justice is fulfilling its large part to imprison the perpetrators, especially in cases related to tax evasion, drug trafficking, human trafficking, smuggling, graft and corruption, and extrajudicial killings.

There is no luck involved: honest and good governance produces good results as well. Think about it: we have implemented the government's promised services, and have even allocated sufficient funds for projects without needing to raise taxes.

That is really the plan: to ensure a fair fight; to stop the dominance of the powerful; and to ensure that the old system where only a few benefited will become a source of opportunity for all.

We are now putting an end to the sirens: on the roads, in the government, in the larger society. This will give birth to confidence that will attract business; this will also ensure that the people’s funds go to where they should: Infrastructure that will ensure the sustained improvement of the economy and the creation of jobs, and social services that will ensure no one is left behind. Many more economic opportunities will open up through tourism; strengthening agriculture will ensure that Filipinos will not go hungry. Those previously neglected will have their futures invested in.

This will initiate a cycle where there will surely be those who fill the jobs created, and consumers who will further grow businesses.

I know that until now, there are still a few complaining about our style of governance. You have seen our style, and the results that come with it. You have seen their style, and where it led us. For those with open eyes, it is clear what is right.

Now that we are steering the government, the direction our country is taking is clear. A nation where opportunity is within reach; where those in need are helped; where every drop of sweat, every moment of hardship, and every grain of sorrow we have gone through has meaning. If you do good, good will return to you. And if you do evil, you will surely be held accountable.

I certainly remember a woman who approached me during the campaign; her warning was, “Noy, be careful, you are colliding with many people.”

She was right: It is true that I sometimes had doubts. But I had no hesitation in taking the straight and narrow path: I was filled with courage because I knew you were behind me. Thank you.

Thank you to the priests and bishops who engaged in thorough dialogue with us, such as Cardinal Rosales and Vidal. Cardinal Rosales and I weren’t that close, but I believe he poured his all into reducing the misunderstandings between the government and the church. With the election of Archbishop Palma, defender of human rights and the environment, my confidence in the relationship, and not conflict, that will be built between the state and the church has further strengthened.

Thank you also to our Cabinet, who recognize no time for sleep or rest, just to implement the national agenda. Special mention to PAGASA [Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical, and Astronomical Services Administration], which is now truly providing reliable warnings.

To those who obstruct the path of honesty and integrity in governance, this is what I have to say: You have chosen the path where the people are oppressed. We have chosen the path to defend the people. We are right; you are wrong. To those who seek to return oppression to the people, you will not succeed.

To all our companions on the straight and narrow path: you have created the opportunity to change what we have inherited, and to make better what we will pass on to the next generation of Filipinos. You drivers who continue to ply your routes; you teachers and students on your way home from class; you who continue to create works that ignite the flame of our Filipino identity; you police, soldiers, linemen, and firefighters; you honorable workers, whether in the Philippines, in the middle of the sea, or in another country; you faithful companions in government, from any province or party; you Filipinos listening to me now—you have created this opportunity.

You have created a government that truly works for you. We have five years left to ensure that we do not return to our previous state. We will not derail now that the results of what we have started are so good.

When we see loopholes in the system, let us no longer attempt to exploit them. Let us no longer resort to pleading when hard work will suffice. Enough of getting ahead of each other, enough of pushing each other around, enough of taking advantage, because we will all reach the future we desire.

Let us end the culture of negativity; let us uplift our fellow Filipinos at every opportunity. Why do some people have a penchant for finding all sorts of ugliness in our country? And it is so difficult—almost a sin—to say something good? Do we even remember the last time we praised a fellow Filipino?

Let us stop dragging each other down. Let us cast aside this old industry of criticism that we cannot deny. Let us put an end to the “crab mentality”; can we not highlight the good things we have accomplished?

If you see something good, do not hesitate to praise it. When you see a police officer on the corner, directing traffic without a raincoat in the rain, please approach them and say, “Thank you.”

If you are sick and you see the nurse who cared for you, instead of serving foreigners for a higher salary, please also say, “Thank you.”

Before you go home from school, approach your teacher who chose to invest in your future rather than prioritize their own comfort; say to them, “Thank you”: Like my teacher on the screen now, thank you, Mrs. Escasa.

If you meet your representative on the road that was previously full of potholes (or nonexistent), and is now passable and smooth, approach them and say: “Thank you.”

Therefore, to the Filipino people, my Boss who steered us towards this day: thank you very much for the changes we are enjoying now.

The Philippines and the Filipino people are very much alive.
