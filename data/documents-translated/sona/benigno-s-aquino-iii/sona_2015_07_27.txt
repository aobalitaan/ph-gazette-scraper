This is my sixth State of the Nation Address. I once again face Congress and the people to report on the state of our nation. More than five years have passed since we stopped the sirens, not only on the roads, but throughout society; since we formally swore to fight corruption to eradicate poverty; and since the Filipino, our bosses, learned to dream again. My Bosses, this is the story of our journey on the Straight Path.

Just last Friday, we inaugurated the Muntinlupa-Cavite Expressway. This is the first of the Public-Private Partnerships that we approved, and which was opened to the public under our administration. To our predecessors: The government almost had to beg the private sector to participate in projects. Now they are courting us—here in the MCX, they even paid us a premium of P925 million for the privilege of building the infrastructure we need. With the high level of their confidence that the project will earn, the private proponent said that the first month of toll on the MCX will be free.

Indeed, we have come a long way. To put this into context, let us look back.

We inherited a people numbed by the relentless allegations of lies, fraud, and theft.

We were made to believe that we had sufficient classrooms. It turned out that classes were being held in four shifts. Some students went to school before dawn, and some went home after dark—but all of them were left in the dark because there wasn't enough time for learning.

“Uninterrupted growth” was touted by our predecessor in his final SONA. But upon review, a large part of it came from the remittances of people who had lost hope in the Philippines. As they say, “People were voting with their feet.” If I were to emulate this style, I would be horrified to claim success that came from driving our countrymen away.

As the 2004 elections approached, over P700 million was allegedly spent on fertilizer that was unsuitable for crops, overpriced, and in many cases, farmers never even saw it. Who do you think benefited from this fertilizer? Clearly not the crops or the farmers. You probably also remember the NBN-ZTE scandal. We investigated it in the Senate; someone said they were attempted to be bribed. When questioned, they refused to speak because they claimed executive privilege. The former president also couldn't be subpoenaed—so all that remained to be questioned were his minions accused of bribery. Naturally, he denied the accusations.

During that time, even children learned the word “scam.” You probably remember: Hello Garci, which was only answered with “I am sorry.” The real bank accounts of the fake Jose Pidal. The attempted Constitutional Assembly to stay in power indefinitely. EO 464, which attempted to suppress the truth. The declaration of a State of Emergency, to evade the checks and balances of the 1987 Constitution regarding Martial Law. Midnight appointments. The Calibrated Preemptive Response used against protesters. Even the language is wrong. How can the response come first? It's like saying you replied to someone who didn't even text [text] you.

This is the headline news we consumed every day before assuming office. Upon taking office, we successively discovered the foolishness of those we replaced. I have mentioned this in previous SONAs: In the NFA, the debt ballooned from P12.3 billion in 2001 to P176.8 billion in June 2010. This was accompanied by excessive rice imports that simply rotted in warehouses. In PAGCOR, one billion pesos for coffee. In MWSS: Layer upon layer of bonuses. In Laguna Lake: more than P18 billion was attempted to be squandered on mud games. I truly cannot fathom how those who initiated this could benefit from the suffering of our countrymen.

Every government official swears to be just to others and to follow the law. But it is clear: what those before us did was the opposite of this. We saw the worst example of this in November 2009, when 58 Filipinos were massacred in Maguindanao. Just imagine, that was already wrong. They did it, and it was even more wrong. But what is worse: they believed they could get away with it, because they were in power—so they continued. These are just a few examples that you are aware of; there are many more.

In this situation, can we blame our countrymen who have fled because they see no hope?

Like you, the thought of giving up also crossed my mind. When my mother passed away, our confidence was further diminished; we lost our leader and inspiration in pushing for change. At her wake, someone approached me and suggested that I run for president. My answer: I am not a masochist. I am among those who stop wrongdoing; I know the severity of the situation. I am also certain that there are details that were hidden from us, and that the problem is indeed much bigger than we know. When you called on me to serve, my question was: If the problems are not solved immediately, how long will the patience of my Bosses last, before they turn their anger towards me?

One of those who convinced me was Alex Lacson. He said: “Just starting to stop the abuse is enough. To stop the hemorrhaging would be enough.”

Let us listen to him:

In 2008, our Philippines was ranked by the World Bank as one of the most corrupt countries in the world.

At that time many of my friends and I were looking for a good man, not only a good politician to be our president in 2010.

When Cory died and Senator Noynoy spoke at her wake, that's when we focused on Senator Noynoy, in the same manner that Conrad de Quiros and Billy Esposo saw a great potential in Senator Noynoy.

Then my friends encouraged me to lead the public call to urge Senator Noynoy to run for president. My friends and I went to the house of Senator Noynoy in Times Street. We brought a yellow drum, and we encouraged the public to write letters.

Within 24 hours, there was an outpouring of public support for Senator Noynoy to run for public office.

I did not expect him to be Superman to solve all our problems in the country but I expected him to start the right path.

Mr. President, thank you for heeding the call of the people in 2010, and thank you, too, for respecting my beliefs, even as we disagreed on a number of issues.

Bring our country to the right path.

The message was clear: We are serious about change; the fight is fair. The result: confidence in our economy.
